From the outside, St Stephen Walbrook is easy to walk straight past — a modest Wren church wedged in behind the Mansion House. Go in. It's one of the most beautiful rooms in London, and it's a secret hiding in plain sight.

Look up. Floating above you is a dome — a great coffered dome, lifted on eight slender arches, flooding the whole space with even, calm light. It seems impossible that a building this plain outside could hold something this serene within. And here's why it matters: Wren built this dome in the 1670s, while he was still wrestling with the design for St Paul's, and many people believe he used St Stephen Walbrook as a try-out. A dome before the dome. The great one you'll finish the walk at was, in part, rehearsed right here, at small scale, in a side street.

So this little church is a working drawing you can stand inside — Wren thinking out loud in stone, testing whether he could float a dome on slim supports and fill a room with light, before he bet a cathedral on the answer.

*[beat]*

Two more layers worth holding. Under the dome sits a great rounded altar carved by the modern sculptor Henry Moore — smooth pale stone that startled people when it arrived, and now looks as though it was always meant to be there. And it was in this parish, in the 1950s, that a vicar named Chad Varah founded the Samaritans, the helpline for people in despair — quiet, serious, life-saving work that began behind these walls. A small church doing large things. When you've taken in the light, head out towards Cheapside. We're going to listen for some famous bells at St Mary-le-Bow.
