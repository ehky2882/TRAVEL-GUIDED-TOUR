Tip your head back, because this thing is two hundred and two feet of pure Baroque showing-off, and there's a number hidden inside it.

This is the Monument — just "the Monument," it doesn't need more — built by Christopher Wren and his brilliant friend Robert Hooke and finished in 1677 to commemorate the Great Fire and the rebuilding of the city. It's a single column of Portland stone, topped with a gilded urn of golden flames, and at two hundred and two feet it's the tallest free-standing stone column in the world.

Now the number. It stands two hundred and two feet from the spot in Pudding Lane where the fire began. If you could lay the column down on its side, the flaming urn at the top would come to rest exactly on the bakery. The height isn't arbitrary — it's a measurement, a line drawn in stone between the monument and the wound.

And it was never only a monument. Hooke was one of the great scientists of the age, and he designed the hollow core and the three hundred and eleven spiral steps to double as a giant scientific instrument — a fixed telescope pointing at the sky, a place to run experiments on air pressure and gravity, with a laboratory tucked in the base. A memorial that was also a piece of laboratory equipment.

*[beat]*

There's a darker footnote. For over a century, an inscription on the base blamed the fire on a Catholic plot — a falsehood, born of the religious fear of the time, finally chiselled away in the 1830s. Worth remembering that monuments record the feelings of the people who build them as much as the events themselves. Climb it if you've the legs — the view is the reward.
