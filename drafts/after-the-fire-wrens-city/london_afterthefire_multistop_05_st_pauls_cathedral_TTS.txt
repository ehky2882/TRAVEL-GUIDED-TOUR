And here it is — the building the whole walk has been climbing towards. Stand back far enough to take in the dome, because that dome is the point of everything we've seen today.

When the Great Fire destroyed the old medieval St Paul's, it handed Christopher Wren the commission of a lifetime: build a new cathedral, from nothing, for the rebuilt city. He spent the next thirty-five years on it — fighting committees, redrawing plans, watching it rise stone by stone — and finished in seventeen ten, an old man who lived to see his masterpiece complete, which almost no cathedral-builder in history ever managed.

The dome is one of the largest in the world, and it's a magic trick. What you see from outside is far too tall and too perfect to work as a ceiling inside — so Wren built three domes, one within another: an inner dome for the view from below, a hidden brick cone in the middle holding everything up, and the great lead outer dome you see on the skyline. Three domes pretending to be one. Every dome you saw rehearsed back at St Stephen wawlbrook was leading here.

Wren is buried downstairs, in the crypt, beneath a plain slab — no grand tomb. Instead, carved above him, a line in Latin that translates as: reader, if you seek his monument, look around you. He meant the cathedral. But after today you can read it wider. Look around the whole City — the steeples, the Monument, the churches, the skyline itself — and nearly all of it traces back to the four days of fire we started with, and the one man who answered them. That's the end of the walk. You're standing in his monument. So is everyone else, for half a mile in every direction.
