It's the second of September, sixteen sixty-six, and London is about to burn.

It starts just before two in the morning, in a baker's shop on a narrow lane near the river — a single oven, banked too hot, in a city built almost entirely of timber and pitch, packed so tightly that the upper floors lean towards each other across the streets. There's been a long dry summer and a steady east wind. By the time anyone takes it seriously, it's already past stopping.

For four days the Great Fire of London eats the city alive. When it finally burns out, around eighty per cent of the old City is gone — some thirteen thousand houses, the medieval cathedral, eighty-seven parish churches, the London of the Middle Ages erased in a long weekend.

And here's the turn this whole walk is about. Out of that catastrophe came the greatest opportunity any architect has ever been handed. The man who took it was Christopher Wren, and over the next fifty years he rebuilt the skyline of London in stone — fifty-one churches and one enormous cathedral, a whole city's worth of domes and steeples, out of a single mind.

This walk traces the fire from beginning to end: from the lane where the spark caught, to the column that commemorates it, through two of Wren's churches, and finishing at the masterpiece he spent his life building. And unlike a lot of London, this one really is a walk — it's all packed into the Square Mile, barely a mile end to end, the city rebuilt so tightly on its own ashes that you can cross three and a half centuries on foot in twenty minutes.
