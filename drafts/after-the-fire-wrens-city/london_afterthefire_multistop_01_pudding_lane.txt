You're standing on Pudding Lane, looking for a plaque, because there's almost nothing else to see here — and that, in a way, is the point.

This narrow, unremarkable City street is where the Great Fire began. In the early hours of that September morning in 1666, fire broke out in the bakehouse of Thomas Farriner, baker to the King, somewhere near the bottom of this lane. A small fire, in a small shop, of the kind that must have started a hundred times before. What was different this time was the wind, and the drought, and the timber, and the fact that the Lord Mayor, hauled out of bed to look at it, famously waved it away — a woman could put it out, he scoffed — and went back to sleep.

By the time the city understood what it had, the fire had jumped from the bakery to a riverside inn, into warehouses packed with oil and tallow and brandy along the Thames, and turned into something that made its own weather.

As for the name — don't picture desserts. Pudding Lane was named for the "puddings," the offal and guts that butchers carted down here on their way to the river. It was a working lane in a working city, and it has the dubious honour of being the most famous street in London for something that lasted a few hours before everything around it turned to ash.

Stand here a second and try to feel the scale of what started on this exact spot: one oven, one ordinary night, and four days later a medieval capital gone. Then look up the lane, because the city built a giant exclamation mark to mark it, and it's right around the corner. Walk to the Monument.
