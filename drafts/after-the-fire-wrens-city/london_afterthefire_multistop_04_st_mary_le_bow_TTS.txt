Listen. If the bells happen to be ringing while you're here, stop and pay attention, because these are the most famous bells in England — and they decide who counts as a Londoner.

This is St Mary luh boe, on Cheapside, one of Wren's churches, finished around sixteen eighty with a steeple so elegant that other architects spent the next century copying it. But it isn't the steeple people know — it's the sound that comes out of it. By old tradition, a true Cockney, a real native Londoner, is someone born within the sound of Bow Bells. Not within a boundary, not within a postcode — within earshot of this one particular peal. The bells draw an invisible map of who belongs, and they've done it for centuries.

The bells carry a story, too. In the old legend it was Bow Bells that called the young Dick Whittington back to London just as he was trudging away, broke and beaten — ringing out, the tale says, turn again, Whittington — back to a city where he'd go on to be Lord Mayor three times over. A discouraged young man, turned around by a sound.

There's a wartime coda. The bells you might hear aren't quite the originals — those were shattered when the church was bombed in nineteen forty-one, then recast and rehung in the nineteen sixties. But during the war, a recording of these bells was broadcast by the BBC into occupied Europe: the steady sound of London still standing, sent across the Channel to people in the dark. The name, by the way, comes from the bow-shaped stone arches in the crypt beneath you — older than Wren, older than the Fire. Go down and find them if it's open. Then we walk west for the finale.
