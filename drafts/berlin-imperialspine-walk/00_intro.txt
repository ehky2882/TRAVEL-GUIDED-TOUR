Every capital has one street where the state dressed up for company. This walk is Berlin's.

Unter den Linden — "under the linden trees" — runs dead straight for about a kilometre and a half, from the Brandenburg Gate to the site of the royal palace. For three centuries it was where Prussia, then the Empire, then every government since arranged the buildings they most wanted the world to see: opera house, university, armoury, cathedral, museums. Official Berlin, laid out in a single line. We'll walk it west to east in five stops, the way visitors were always meant to arrive.

The walking is easy — level and step-free the whole way, with no shortage of benches and cafés. One honest note before we set off: the boulevard is in the middle of a years-long refit. The central promenade is being rebuilt in stages and many of its famous lindens are young or still to be planted, so expect some hoardings, and don't expect a green tunnel. The street has been through far worse, as you'll hear.

We begin on Pariser Platz, at the foot of the Brandenburg Gate — the S-Bahn and U-Bahn station Brandenburger Tor puts you right there. Stand anywhere on the square, face the Gate, and when you're ready, we'll start at the front door.
