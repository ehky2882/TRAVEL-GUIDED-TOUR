No patch of ground in Berlin has changed costume as often as the lawn in front of you.

Stand at the edge of the grass. Ahead of you, a colonnade of eighteen columns: the Altes Museum. To your right, the great bulk of the Berliner Dom, the cathedral, its copper dome long since weathered green. Behind you, across the road, the palace facade you'll finish this walk beneath. This is the Lustgarten — the "pleasure garden" — and its name has spent four centuries being contradicted.

It began as the palace kitchen garden, growing cabbages and herbs for the royal table. Then it became a true pleasure garden, with fountains and rare plants. Then came the Soldier King, who considered pleasure a foreign extravagance and drilled it flat into a parade ground. His successors planted it again; the Kaisers paraded on it again; the Nazis paved it over for mass rallies; and after all of that, it is now a lawn where students eat lunch and visitors lie in the sun. Dinner, delight, drill, spectacle — every ruler ordered this square to perform something, and in the end it performs nothing at all, which feels like the city's verdict.

Two buildings deserve a look before we move on. The Altes Museum, finished in 1830, is Schinkel once more — a temple for art, placed squarely opposite the palace, which was a quietly radical thing to say in stone: culture, facing power as an equal. And the Dom, for all its Renaissance swagger, is younger than it plays — completed in 1905, one Kaiser's idea of what five hundred years of dynasty ought to look like.

When you're ready, turn your back on the museum and cross toward the palace facade. The final stop is the building this entire street was invented for — directly across the road, two minutes at most.
