You have been walking toward this building for the whole of this tour — and so, in a sense, has the street.

The boulevard exists because the palace did. In the middle of the sixteen hundreds, the Great Elector had lindens planted along the track that ran from his palace here to his hunting grounds in the teer gar ten — a shaded ride for the court. That bridle path became oon ter dain lin den, and every grand building you've passed today was pinned onto it later, along the king's own commute. So the walk you've just taken, west to east, is the ceremonial approach run in reverse: in through the front door, down the receiving line, and now — the house.

The house has led several lives. A royal palace stood here for around five hundred years, home to the hoh en tsol lern family, until war wrecked it and the g d r demolished the remains in nineteen fifty. In its place rose the pah lahst dair reh poo bleek, East Germany's parliament and people's palace — itself torn down in the two thousands. What stands now, opened this decade, is the hoom bolt forum: the old palace facades rebuilt in baroque detail on three sides, with a frankly modern fourth front facing the river. Walk around to the shpray side later if you want to see the honest seam. Some Berliners love the reconstruction and some cannot forgive it; that argument is the city's to have, not ours to settle this afternoon.

Inside are museums and free courtyards, and you can go up to the roof terrace, thirty metres above the street.

Before any of that, stand at the western face and look back the way you came — one straight line to the Brandenburg Gate. Three centuries of a state deciding exactly what to show you, and you've now read it in both directions. That's the walk. Find a bench, or the roof, and let the street settle. Everything else in this city fans out from here — and none of it is far.
