This is the front door. Not a figure of speech — an actual city gate, built in the 1780s in the customs wall that still ringed Berlin, and the only one of the city's eighteen gates left standing.

The square around you is Pariser Platz, which Berliners used to call the city's gute Stube — the good parlour, the room kept spotless for guests. It still is exactly that: the French and American embassies, the Academy of Arts and the Hotel Adlon face each other across stone that stays swept. The name arrived in 1814, when Prussian troops entered Paris — and that same year, the four-horse chariot on top of the Gate came home. Napoleon had carted the Quadriga off to France as a trophy after crushing Prussia; when Berlin got her back, the goddess was pointedly handed a new staff crowned with an Iron Cross. Look up at her. She drives her horses east, into the city — which is exactly where we're going.

Walk through the Gate — take the centre passage once reserved for royalty, if you like; nobody checks anymore — and stop on the far side. Now look straight down the street ahead of you. That is Unter den Linden, and at the far end, a little over a kilometre away, stands the palace. The Gate was never the destination; it was the doorway. Everything on this walk was arranged along that one line, in order to be seen from it.

Set off down the right-hand side of the boulevard. Ten minutes of easy walking — past embassies, past the state library, past a fair amount of building work — brings you to a wide square opening on your right, with a domed church at the back of it. That's Bebelplatz, and it's where a king shows us what he thought culture was for.
