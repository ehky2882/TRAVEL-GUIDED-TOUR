In the seventeen forties, Frederick the Great laid all this out as the forum free deh ree tsee ah num, his answer to the forums of Rome. The opera house came first, opened in seventeen forty two, among the first in Europe built to stand free rather than tucked into a palace wing — a public building for a public art. Saint hed vig's, modelled loosely on the Pantheon, was the first Catholic church raised in Prussia since the Reformation: a calculated gesture of tolerance from a king who had just acquired a great many Catholic subjects. A library, an opera, a church of the other faith — this square was the Enlightenment staged as state policy, tolerance and learning arranged like furniture in a display room.

Now find the pane of glass set flush into the paving near the middle of the square. Look down through it: white, empty bookshelves, with room for twenty thousand books. It remembers the night in May nineteen thirty three when students burned books by Jewish, liberal and other unwanted authors here, on this square built to celebrate learning. It asks for a quiet moment, and nothing more.

A square built as a showroom for enlightenment held the bonfire of it, two centuries apart, and bay bel plats simply carries both.
