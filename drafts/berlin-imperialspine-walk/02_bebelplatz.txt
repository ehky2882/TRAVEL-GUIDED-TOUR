Bebelplatz is what happens when a king decides that culture is a form of power.

Stand in the open square, with the boulevard at your back. On your left, the long, dignified front of the Staatsoper — the State Opera. Straight ahead, a green dome: St. Hedwig's Cathedral. On your right, a curved baroque facade that Berliners long ago nicknamed die Kommode — the chest of drawers. It was the royal library.

In the 1740s, Frederick the Great laid all this out as the Forum Fridericianum, his answer to the forums of Rome. The opera house came first, opened in 1742, among the first in Europe built to stand free rather than tucked into a palace wing — a public building for a public art. St. Hedwig's, modelled loosely on the Pantheon, was the first Catholic church raised in Prussia since the Reformation: a calculated gesture of tolerance from a king who had just acquired a great many Catholic subjects. A library, an opera, a church of the other faith — this square was the Enlightenment staged as state policy, tolerance and learning arranged like furniture in a display room.

Now find the pane of glass set flush into the paving near the middle of the square. Look down through it: white, empty bookshelves, with room for twenty thousand books. It remembers the night in May 1933 when students burned books by Jewish, liberal and other unwanted authors here, on this square built to celebrate learning. It asks for a quiet moment, and nothing more.

[beat]

A square built as a showroom for enlightenment held the bonfire of it, two centuries apart, and Bebelplatz simply carries both. When you're ready, go back to the boulevard and cross to its far side. A few minutes further east, set back among trees, you'll find a small building with a Greek temple front. That's the Neue Wache — our next stop.
