The smallest building on this walk does the heaviest work.

The noy uh vah khuh — the New Guardhouse — is the little temple-fronted block between the trees: built in eighteen eighteen by karl free drish shin kel to house the royal guard, and since then a memorial under four different Germanys, each of which refitted the room to mourn in its own manner.

If the door stands open, go in. One bare room, one figure: a bronze mother holding the body of her grown son, by kay tuh kol vits — an artist who lost her own son in the First World War and spent the rest of her life giving grief a shape. Above her, the roof opens to the sky in a bare circle. Rain falls on her. Snow settles on her shoulders. The room is never quite warm, and none of that is an accident. Whatever the state outside has called itself, she has sat here with the same weight in her arms.

Back outside, the long pink building beside it is the tsoyk hows, the old armoury and the oldest thing on the boulevard. Walk past it, cross the marble bridge — shin kel again — over an arm of the shpray, and you're on an island. On your left: a lawn, a colonnade, and a very large cathedral. That's the loost gar ten, where we're headed.
