You've arrived at the plassa day la armaireea, the broad courtyard with the palace on one side and the cathedral on the other. Stand where you can see both. This is where the walk has been heading, and it's where Habsburg Madrid ends — on the exact spot where it began.

Because there was a castle here long before this palace. The Habsburgs ran their empire from a sprawling old fortress on this hilltop, the alkathar — the building Philip the Second moved into when he made Madrid the capital. It stood for centuries. Then, on Christmas Eve in seventeen thirty-four, it caught fire and burned for four days. So much was lost that silver melted in the heat; one of the things carried out in time, reportedly thrown from a window to save it, was velaskess's las meneenass — which is why you can still see it in the Prado today.

By then the Habsburgs were gone, and a new dynasty, the Bourbons, held the throne. They built what you're looking at now: an enormous stone palace, deliberately fireproof, with no wood in its structure. It has more than three thousand rooms and is the largest royal palace in Western Europe. The king still works here and receives heads of state here — but he doesn't live here; the family long ago moved to somewhere smaller and more private. The palace is kept for the state, and for you to walk through.

Now turn to the cathedral, the almoodayna. Notice that it doesn't try to tower over the palace — its dome sits low, and its front echoes the palace's own pale stonework. That was on purpose: when it was finally finished, the designers held it back deliberately so it wouldn't outshine the king's house across the square. And "finished" is a generous word — Madrid argued about, began, and abandoned this cathedral for more than a hundred years, only consecrating it in nineteen ninety-three. A capital made overnight in fifteen sixty-one took until almost the twenty-first century to give itself a cathedral.

That's the whole story of this walk, standing in one courtyard: the austere Habsburg town that was handed an empire, and the grand Bourbon city that rebuilt on its ashes. Two dynasties, one hilltop. Stay a while — it's a good place to watch the light move across the stone, and you've earned the view.
