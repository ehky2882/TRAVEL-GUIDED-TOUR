Come through one of the archways into plassa mayor, and stop as soon as the square opens out around you. Notice that it's completely enclosed — nine arched entrances, and otherwise an unbroken wall of buildings four storeys high, wrapping the whole space. That's the key to the place. This isn't a square that simply happened over time; it's a room without a roof, designed all at once.

Philip the Third commissioned it, the architect hwan gometh day mora laid it out, and it was finished in sixteen nineteen. Look up: two hundred and thirty-seven balconies face inward. They face inward because the whole plaza was built as a stage, with the city as the audience. From those balconies Madrid watched everything that mattered — royal proclamations, the crowning of kings, markets, bullfights, plays. The grandest building, on the north side with the painted façade and two towers, is the kasa day la panadereea, the old bakery, and its central balcony was the royal box, where the king and queen sat above it all.

It wasn't only pageantry. During the Inquisition this square was also where the Church staged its public trials and sentences, in front of those same crowds. It's part of the history of the place, and worth knowing as you stand in it.

But mostly what this square was built to do was gather people and impress them — to make a not-very-old capital feel permanent and grand. Stand in the middle, turn once, and you can feel it still working.

The man on the horse in the centre is Philip the Third, the king who ordered all of it. When you're ready, leave by the southwest corner and head down the short slope — we're going to the market hall just outside the old walls, the mairkado day san migel.
