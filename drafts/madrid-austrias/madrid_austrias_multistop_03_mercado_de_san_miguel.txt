Just outside the southwest corner of Plaza Mayor, you'll find a market hall made of iron and glass — the Mercado de San Miguel. Walk up to it.

For most of Madrid's history this was an ordinary neighbourhood food market, one of dozens, the place you came for fish and vegetables. The building in front of you went up in 1916, an elegant cage of cast iron and glass in the style of the great covered markets of that age — and it's the last survivor of its kind in the city, the others long since pulled down.

By the early two-thousands it was nearly derelict. Then, in 2009, instead of knocking it down, the city did something cleverer: it reopened the market as a place to eat rather than to shop. Today the old iron stalls sell oysters and croquettes and small glasses of wine, and you graze your way around the room. It's frankly a tourist scene now, busy and not cheap — but the building itself is the real thing, and the easiest way to picture what every Madrid market once looked like is to stand under this roof and look up at the ironwork.

Have something if you like, or just pass through. When you come out, we leave the crowds behind — a short way west along Calle Mayor the noise drops, and we reach the quietest and oldest square on this walk: Plaza de la Villa.
