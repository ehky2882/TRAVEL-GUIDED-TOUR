Here, off the north side of Calle Mayor, is Plaza de la Villa — and the contrast with the market you just left is the whole point. It's small, quiet, paved in stone, easy to walk straight past. It is also one of the oldest squares in Madrid, and for roughly three hundred years it was the city's civic heart: this is where Madrid governed itself.

Three buildings frame you, each from a different century, and together they tell the story of a town becoming a capital. On your left, the brick tower — the Torre de los Lujanes — is fifteenth century, the oldest surviving civil building in the city, from back when Madrid was nobody's capital at all. The building in the middle, with the fine carved stonework, is sixteenth century: the Renaissance arriving as the town began to swell. And the heavier one with the dark slate spires is the old town hall, seventeenth century, built by Juan Gómez de Mora — the same architect who gave you Plaza Mayor a few minutes ago.

So you've now met Gómez de Mora twice on one short walk, which tells you how small the circle of Habsburg Madrid really was: a handful of squares, a handful of builders, one compact town carrying an empire.

The city ran itself from here until 2007, when the government finally moved across town. Then this square went quiet — which is how you find it now.

From here, Calle Mayor runs the last stretch west, gently downhill, to where the whole walk has been heading: the royal palace, and the cathedral facing it. Follow it down to the open courtyard, the Plaza de la Armería.
