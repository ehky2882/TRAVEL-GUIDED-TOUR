Now you're at the foot of the Champs-Élysées, looking up a wide avenue that rises gently for almost two kilometres to the arch at the top. This is the stretch everyone has heard of — and the name is grander than you might guess. Champs-Élysées means the Elysian Fields: in Greek myth, the paradise where heroes went after death. Somebody named a Paris street after heaven.

What's strange is how little there was here when the name was coined. The lower end, where you're standing, was once marshy open ground and market gardens, well outside the city. Le Nôtre — the same man who drew the garden behind you — simply extended his sightline straight through it in the 1660s, planting a double row of trees to carry the view onward toward the horizon. So the Champs-Élysées was a line on a plan, a direction, long before it was a place. It was a view before it was an address.

You'll feel the avenue change as you climb. The lower half is still mostly that original idea — trees, gardens, room to breathe. Then it crosses a roundabout and becomes the famous part: the broad upper avenue of glass-fronted shops, café terraces, car showrooms and flagship stores, packed shoulder to shoulder with people from everywhere on earth. The city still calls it the most beautiful avenue in the world. Up close it's also, frankly, a very expensive shopping street — and both of those things are true at once.

*[beat]*

Keep climbing, and keep your eye on the arch. It's been growing in front of you the whole way, and it only really gives up its scale when you're nearly under it.

At the top, the avenue ends at a vast roundabout with the arch marooned in the middle. That's the Arc de Triomphe.
