You've reached the top: the ark duh tree-omf, standing alone in the middle of a roundabout where twelve avenues meet like the spokes of a star. Don't try to cross to it through the traffic — there's an underpass. But before you go to it, stop and turn around, because this is the best view on the walk. From up here the whole line you've climbed runs back east — avenue, square, obelisk, garden, the distant loov — every monument you've passed, strung on one thread. You can see the entire afternoon in a single look.

Now the arch. Napoleon ordered it built in eighteen oh six, at the height of his power, to honour his armies after a great victory. It was meant to be the ultimate triumphal gesture — soldiers marching home beneath it. But it was enormous and slow, and it wasn't finished in his lifetime. Napoleon fell, was exiled, and died on a distant island in eighteen twenty-one, with his arch still half-built. It was finally completed in eighteen thirty-six, fifteen years after his death — and the only time he ever passed beneath it was in eighteen forty, as a coffin, when his remains were brought back to Paris and carried under the arch he never saw standing.

And there's a quieter thing underneath it now. After the First World War, an unknown soldier — one unidentified body, standing in for all the missing dead — was buried beneath the arch, and a flame was lit over the grave. It has been relit, by hand, every single evening since. So the monument built to glorify one man's victories now keeps watch, in the end, over a single nameless soldier. The grandest arch in Paris finishes on the most modest possible note.

That's the end of the walk on foot. But the line doesn't stop here. Look west, past the arch, and on a clear day you'll see another arch far off on the horizon — a vast, square one. To reach it you'll want the metroh. That's la dayfonss, and the grond arsh.
