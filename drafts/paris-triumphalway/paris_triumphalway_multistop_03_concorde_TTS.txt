The garden opens out, and suddenly you're on the edge of a vast, traffic-ringed square with a single tall stone needle at its centre. This is the plass duh la konkord, the midpoint of the whole line — and the most loaded ground you'll cross all day.

Start with the needle, because it's the easy part. It's an Egyptian obelisk, around three thousand three hundred years old, that once stood at the temple of looksor and was given to France in the eighteen thirties. It was raised on this spot in eighteen thirty-six, and much later, in nineteen ninety-eight, capped with a thin pyramid of gold. It is, by some distance, the oldest thing you'll see all walk — and it was put here, very deliberately, to fill a space the city wanted to stop thinking about.

Because this square has worn three names. It opened as the Place loo-ee the Fifteenth, with a statue of that king at its centre. The Revolution tore the statue down, renamed it the Place of the Revolution, and for a couple of years made it the central stage of the Terror: this is where the guillotine stood, where the king and queen were executed, and where well over a thousand people lost their lives in a matter of months. And then, when it was over, the city renamed it a third time — konkord, meaning agreement, reconciliation — and eventually planted a neutral, ancient, foreign monument exactly where the blade had been. Not a memorial. The opposite of a memorial: a way of changing the subject.

It's worth standing here a moment with that in mind — that the calmest, grandest square in Paris is calm by design, built to let the city forget what happened on it. Whether that's wisdom or evasion, people still argue.

When you're ready, turn west again and face the long, rising avenue lined with trees. That's the shonzaylee-zay.
