You've come up out of the Métro at La Défense, and the thing in front of you doesn't look like the rest of the walk at all. No carved stone, no emperors — just glass towers, open plazas, and one enormous, impossibly clean white cube, hollow in the middle, big enough to see sky straight through. This is the Grande Arche, and it's where the line you've been following finally runs out — on purpose.

First, where you are. La Défense is Paris's business district, a forest of towers built from the 1960s onward and kept deliberately outside the historic centre, so the old city could stay low while the offices went high out here. The name is older than the towers: it comes from a statue that once marked where Parisians defended the city in the war of 1870.

Now the cube. It went up in 1989, for the two-hundredth anniversary of the Revolution, designed by a Danish architect as a near-perfect hollow cube — so large that the whole of Notre-Dame, spire and all, could sit inside the opening. And here's where it talks back to everything behind it. The old arch on the hill is a solid block of stone, raised to glorify an army. This one is open — a window, not a wall — and it was dedicated not to a victory but to human brotherhood. A triumphal arch you can see straight through.

*[beat]*

Remember the secret bend, back at the start? The Grande Arche is turned a few degrees off the axis — the very same angle as the Louvre, all those kilometres east. The architects did it on purpose, a quiet nod from the newest end of the line to the oldest. And notice what they didn't do: they didn't close the line off. Stand in the opening and look west, and the axis simply carries on, out past the cube, toward nothing in particular — left open, handed to whatever comes next.

Which is the right way for this walk to end. You started in a king's courtyard, on a line drawn to make a city point at power, and you finish here, at an open square cube that points at no one and lets the line run on. From a palace to a window. That's most of the story of Paris, in one straight road.
