You're in the Cour Napoléon, the great courtyard of the Louvre, standing at the glass pyramid. Before you start down the line, it's worth knowing what you're starting from — because the building wrapped around you on three sides has been almost everything a building can be.

It began as a fortress. In the 1190s a king threw up a defensive castle here to guard the western edge of Paris, and its foundations are still down there — you can walk around the base of the medieval keep in the basement, beneath the museum. Over the centuries the fortress became a palace, growing wing by wing into the largest royal residence in Europe, until Louis the Fourteenth grew bored of the city, decamped to Versailles in 1682, and left this enormous place half-empty.

Then the Revolution did something pointed with it. In 1793 the palace the kings had abandoned was reopened as a public museum — the nation's art, hung in the nation's house, free for the citizens who'd just got rid of the king. That's still what it is, and it's now the most-visited museum on earth.

The pyramid is the newest layer: glass and steel, dropped into the centre of all that old stone in 1989, and hated at first for exactly that contrast. It's since become as much a symbol of Paris as anything standing behind it.

Now turn your back to the pyramid and face west. Through the smaller arch ahead — the pink one, the Arc du Carrousel — a straight line opens up: a path through gardens, a distant obelisk, and, far off, a faint arch on a hill. That's the whole walk, visible in a single glance. The line technically begins right here.

Walk toward that little pink arch and through it into the gardens beyond. That's the Tuileries.
