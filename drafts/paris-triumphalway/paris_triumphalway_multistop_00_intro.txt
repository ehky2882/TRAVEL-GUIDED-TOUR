Stand in the right spot in Paris and the whole city seems to line up.

This walk follows that line. It's called the Axe historique — the historic axis — and it runs dead straight for kilometres, from the courtyard of the Louvre, through a royal garden, across the square where the guillotine once stood, up the most famous avenue in the world, to the great arch on the hill. Five monuments, one sightline, laid down over more than three hundred years by kings, emperors and republics who each wanted their stamp on the same straight road.

It is, in the most literal sense, power drawn as a line. Every regime that ruled France added to it, pushed it further west, and tried to make the whole city point at whatever it most wanted to be.

A word on the walk itself. This is the long one — about four kilometres from the Louvre to the Arc de Triomphe, more or less flat, almost perfectly straight, and busy the whole way. You can do it comfortably in an afternoon, but it's a real walk, so wear the right shoes. At the end, if you've still got the legs, the line keeps going past the Arc to a giant modern arch out in the business district — we'll point you to it, but that last stretch is a short ride on the Métro, not a walk.

One thing to carry with you. The line looks ruler-straight, but it isn't quite: the Louvre sits at a slight angle to the rest of it, a few degrees off, and that small kink gets echoed deliberately at the far modern end. Keep it in mind. The axis has a secret bend in it.

Start in the great courtyard of the Louvre, at the glass pyramid. Put the pyramid behind you and look west, the way the line runs.
