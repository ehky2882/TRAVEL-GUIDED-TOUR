You've come through the pink arch into the tweelree — a long formal garden of gravel paths, clipped trees and round ponds, running straight ahead toward the obelisk in the distance. Keep that obelisk in your sights; you're walking the line now, and this garden is where it was first drawn.

The man who drew it was ondray luh notr, in the sixteen sixties — the same gardener who laid out vairsye. He took an older royal garden here and straightened it into this: a central avenue, dead level, pulling the eye west to a vanishing point. That was a new idea about what a garden was for. Not a place to wander and get lost, but a line of sight — a way of standing at the palace and watching your power run, uninterrupted, all the way to the horizon. Everything west of here, right up to the arch on the hill, is luh notr's idea, extended by everyone who came after.

There's something missing, though, and the gap is the point. For centuries a palace closed off this end of the garden — the tweelree Palace, where kings and emperors actually lived. It burned during the violent end of the kommewn in eighteen seventy-one, and what was left was pulled down. That's why the loov behind you now looks out over open sky instead of meeting another building: the home this garden was made for is simply gone, and most people walking here never know it stood.

These days the garden belongs to everyone. The green metal chairs scattered round the ponds are free; drag one to the water, point it west, and you're sitting in a king's private view that nobody owns any more.

Carry on toward the obelisk. The garden ends at a wide, busy square wrapped around it — the plass duh la konkord.
