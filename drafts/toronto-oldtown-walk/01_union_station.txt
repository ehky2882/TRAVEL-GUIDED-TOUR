You're looking at a long row of limestone pillars, each one carved from a single block of stone, running the length of Front Street like the front of a Roman temple. That's the point. When Union Station opened in the 1920s, the railway was the thing that made or broke a city, and Toronto wanted anyone stepping off a train to understand immediately that they'd arrived somewhere that mattered.

Step inside if it's open — it's worth it — into the Great Hall: a long, echoing room with a vaulted ceiling far overhead, tall arched windows at each end, and the names of the towns the railways once served carved in stone around the top. The scale is deliberate. This was built to feel like a cathedral, because for a while the train was the closest thing a growing country had to a shared religion.

Here's the quiet irony, though. By the time this opened, the automobile was already coming for the railway's crown — the grand age of rail it was built to celebrate was nearly over before the doors swung open. And yet it never became a relic: it's still the busiest transport hub in the country, moving more people now than it did in its supposed heyday. A temple built at sunset that somehow kept its congregation.

When you've had your look, come back out to Front Street and head east, a few short blocks, toward a strange, narrow, wedge-shaped building where the streets come together at an angle. That's the Gooderham Flatiron — and there's a little park at its foot.
