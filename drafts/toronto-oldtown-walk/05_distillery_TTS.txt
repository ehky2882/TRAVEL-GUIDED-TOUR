You've arrived at the end of the walk, and at the other end of a story we started an hour ago. Remember the Flatiron — the wedge built as the head office of the gooderum family? This is where their money came from. gooderum and werts began distilling here in eighteen thirty-two, and by the late nineteenth century this was one of the largest distilleries in the world: a small city of brick buildings turning Ontario grain into whisky by the barrel.

Look around at what that left behind — dozens of Victorian industrial buildings, widely called the best-preserved collection of their kind in North America, all in the warm red brick and cobblestone the era ran on. And the reason it survived, when so much of industrial Toronto was flattened, is almost aggressively simple: after the distillery closed, instead of tearing it down, the whole district was handed over to people on foot. No cars, at all. Cobblestone lanes, galleries, cafes, and studios in the old still-houses and warehouses.

It's a neat closing argument for the whole walk. Toronto spent a long time as a city that knocked down its own past without much sentiment — and Old Town is the counter-example, the founding core that got kept: the rail cathedral, the market that governed a town, the church that marked its centre, and the industrial engine that paid for it, all still here, still legible, block by block.

So take your time here at the end. Get a coffee, walk the lanes, look up at the brick. You've just walked the length of the city Toronto used to be — from the grandest door it ever built to the works that made it rich — and it's all still standing, which, for this city, is rather the whole point.
