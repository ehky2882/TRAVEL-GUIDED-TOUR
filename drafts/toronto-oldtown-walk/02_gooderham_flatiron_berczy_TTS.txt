Here's the building — five storeys of red brick tapering to a rounded point, wedged into the triangle where Front and Wellington streets meet at an angle. This is the gooderum Building, though everyone calls it the Flatiron, and it's worth knowing it went up in eighteen ninety-two, which makes it more than a decade older than the far more famous Flatiron in New York. Toronto got there first, and rarely mentions it.

It was built as the head office of the gooderum distilling family — hold onto that name, because we'll end this walk at the distillery that made their fortune — and the rounded prow gave the executives on the top floor the best possible view down the street toward their empire.

Now turn and look at the building's flat back wall, the one facing the park. Someone has painted the entire thing as a giant optical illusion — a tromp loy of windows and a great sail-like facade peeling away from the brick. It's one of the most photographed walls in the city, and it exists for the simple reason that a blank Victorian back wall was too good a canvas to waste.

And the park you're standing in, bairskee Park, has a secret of its own: that central fountain is covered in cast-iron dogs — dozens of them, every one a different breed, all gazing up at a single golden bone on top. Look closely and you'll find exactly one cat, pointedly ignoring the whole affair. It's the least serious monument in Old Town, and locals adore it.

When you're ready, walk north up Church Street, a block or two uphill, toward the tall Gothic spire you can probably already see. That's Saint James Cathedral.
