Two buildings, one market, two hundred years of history. Start with the long grey South building, the one with the arched central hall — a market has stood on this spot since 1803, which makes it older than almost anything else you've seen today. But here's the part people miss: this building was once City Hall. The upstairs held the council chamber and the mayor's office; the basement held the city jail. For a stretch of the nineteenth century, Toronto bought its vegetables directly beneath the room where it governed itself, and not far from the cells. Commerce, democracy, and punishment, all under one roof.

Inside today it's a food market, six days a week — and if you try one thing, make it a peameal bacon sandwich, a Toronto institution this market is credited with making famous.

Now look across the street at the new building in glass and orange steel. That's the rebuilt North Market, reopened after years of the Saturday farmers' market camping under a tent while it went up — and it was designed by the same British architect behind the famous inside-out Pompidou Centre in Paris, which is why it wears its structure on the outside like that. It also quietly took in the courts that moved out of Old City Hall, so the market's old sideline in civic business isn't entirely gone.

For the last stop, we leave the old core behind. Walk southeast, about fifteen minutes, toward the lake and Parliament Street, to a cluster of Victorian brick buildings and cobblestone lanes closed to cars. That's the Distillery District.
