Most cities keep their history in a museum. Toronto keeps a good chunk of its founding self out on the sidewalk, in about a square kilometre east of the downtown core — and that's where we're going.

This is Old Town: the patch of the city where the original street grid, the first market, the first big church, and the industrial engine that paid for a lot of it are all still standing, close enough to walk between in an easy hour or so. The ground is flat, the blocks are short, and unlike some walks that ask you to cross half a city, this one genuinely holds together — you can usually see the next stop from the last one.

Here's the through-line to keep in your head. Everything on this walk was built when Toronto was young and unsure whether it would amount to much, and each stop is the city betting that it would: a railway station built like a temple, a market that doubled as the seat of government, a wedge-shaped office building meant to look modern in eighteen ninety-two, and a whole Victorian distillery that grew into one of the largest of its kind in the world. Read together, block by block, they tell you how a muddy colonial town talked itself into becoming a city.

We start at the grandest front door it ever built. Head to Union Station, on Front Street — stand across from it on the south side if you can, and look up.
