This is the Cathedral Church of Saint James, and that spire is the tallest in the old city — for a long time the tallest structure in Toronto, full stop, and still one of the tallest church spires in the country. In a town this age, the church steeple was the skyline, the thing you steered by from the lake.

There's been a church on this corner since the seventeen nineties, though the earlier ones kept burning down; the building you're looking at went up in the eighteen fifties, its great spire finished a couple of decades later, in the confident Gothic style a young colonial city used when it wanted to look permanent. Step inside if the doors are open — it's quieter than you'd expect for a downtown corner, tall stained glass and a hush the traffic outside never quite reaches.

The thing to notice is where it sits. This was, for the early town, the centre of everything — the church, the market, and the seat of government all within a few minutes of each other, which is more or less the walk you're on right now. Old Town isn't a scatter of separate landmarks; it's the actual core of the original city, still arranged the way it always was.

Head back down toward Front Street and turn east. Two blocks along, at the corner of Front and Jarvis, you'll come to a long grey building with arched windows — and, across the street, a striking new one in glass and orange steel. That's the Saint Lawrence Market.
