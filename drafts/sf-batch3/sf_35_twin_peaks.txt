You've come up about nine hundred feet, and the reward is the whole city at once. From up here, near the geographic center of San Francisco, you can see almost all of it — the grid of streets running out to the bay, the downtown towers, the two bridges, and on a clear day the hills of the East Bay beyond. This is one of the highest points in the city, and the single best place to understand how San Francisco is actually put together.

Because that's the thing to look at from here — not just the pretty bits, but the pattern. Look at the streets. They run in a dead-straight grid, block after block after block, marching right over the tops of hills and down the other side as if the hills weren't there. San Francisco was laid out on paper by people who loved a clean grid and didn't much care what the land underneath was doing. So you get streets that climb at impossible angles, intersections perched on slopes, that whole vertical rollercoaster the city is famous for. From the ground it feels like chaos. From up here you can see it's the opposite — it's a stubbornly rational plan, slammed down on top of stubbornly irrational terrain, and neither one gave an inch.

Watch the weather, too. If there's fog, this is a great seat for it. The fog comes off the cold Pacific to the west and spills over the hills in slow motion, and Twin Peaks sits right in its path. You can stand up here in bright sun and watch a gray river of cloud pour silently across the neighborhoods below.

*[beat]*

One practical note: it's almost always windier and colder up here than down in the city, sometimes dramatically so, so whatever you're wearing, you probably want one more layer. But it's worth it. Most viewpoints just give you a nice picture. This one gives you the logic of the place — the grid against the hills, the fog against the sun — the argument San Francisco has been having with its own landscape since the day it was drawn. Take a minute. Find your hotel. Trace your day. It's all down there.
