When the nineteen oh six earthquake brought down grander, newer buildings all over town, this old adobe just sat tight. Step inside if it's open, and look up: the ceiling is painted in patterns based on ohlohnee designs, the work of the Native people who actually built this place.

Because here is the harder history, and it's worth telling honestly. The mission was founded in seventeen seventy-six, part of a chain the Spanish strung up the California coast. It was built by the ohlohnee, the people who had lived around this bay for thousands of years before any European arrived. The mission system brought them into its orbit to convert and to labor — and it brought European diseases they had no defense against. Thousands of Native people died in these mission years, and many of them are buried right next door, in the old cemetery beside the church. For a long time their graves went largely unmarked.

The big ornate basilica next door, by the way, is the new building — barely a century old. This little adobe is the original.

So stand here a moment with both things at once, because the place asks you to. This is a survivor, the seed the entire city grew from and took its name from, genuinely moving in its plainness. And it's also a monument to a meeting that cost the people who built it almost everything. The oldest building in San Francisco holds the city's beginning and its first great loss in the same four-foot walls. It's worth a quiet minute in the cemetery before you go.
