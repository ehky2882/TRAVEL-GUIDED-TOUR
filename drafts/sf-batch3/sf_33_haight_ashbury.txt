You're standing at the corner of Haight Street and Ashbury Street, and there's not much to see. A couple of street signs people line up to photograph, some shops, a clock that's often stopped, the steady traffic of an ordinary city neighborhood. That's the first thing to understand about this place: it's a real corner, and for one strange summer it became a synonym for an entire era.

The summer was 1967 — the Summer of Love. For reasons that had a lot to do with cheap rent, the neighborhood had filled up with young people, musicians, dropouts, and seekers, drawn by big Victorian flats you could split a dozen ways for almost nothing. The Grateful Dead lived in a house just up Ashbury. Janis Joplin lived nearby. Jefferson Airplane was around the corner. And in that one summer, the word went out across the country that something was happening here, and tens of thousands of mostly very young people poured into these few blocks looking for it.

But here's the part the legend leaves out. It didn't really work, and it didn't last. The neighborhood couldn't absorb the crowds. What had been a small, idealistic scene got swamped — too many people, not enough food or housing, harder drugs moving in. By the fall of that very same year, the original residents had had enough of being a tourist attraction for their own utopia. So they staged a mock funeral, right here in the streets, complete with a coffin. They called it "The Death of the Hippie." The idea was to declare the whole thing over, to kill the label, and to get their neighborhood back.

*[beat]*

So this corner is honest in a way the postcards aren't. The Summer of Love was real, and brief, and messier than the myth — a genuine flowering that curdled almost as fast as it bloomed, and that the people living it tried to bury before it had even cooled. Wander up Haight a few blocks, past the vintage shops and the record stores, and you can still feel the tail of it. Just don't expect it to match the legend. The people who were here knew better, early.
