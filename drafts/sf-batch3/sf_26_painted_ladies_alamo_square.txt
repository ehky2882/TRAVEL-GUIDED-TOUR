Find the bench, or just a patch of grass on the east slope of Alamo Square, and face downhill. In front of you is one of the most photographed views in America: a row of pastel Victorian houses, prim and gabled and trimmed like wedding cakes, with the glass towers of downtown rising right behind them. You've almost certainly seen this exact lineup before — it's turned up behind the opening credits of more than one TV show. They call these the Painted Ladies, and the postcard you're looking at is doing more work than you'd think.

Start with the houses themselves. This row on Steiner Street went up in the early 1890s, ordinary middle-class homes built by a developer, six of them, more or less identical. San Francisco built tens of thousands of houses like these before 1906 — it was a Victorian boomtown. Then two things came for them: the earthquake and fire of 1906, which destroyed huge swaths of the city, and then the twentieth century itself, which decided Victorians were fussy and old-fashioned. Thousands were torn down. Thousands more were "modernized" — stripped of their trim and slapped with gray paint. For a while, gingerbread like this was an embarrassment.

The color is the surprise. These houses weren't always candy-colored. The look you're photographing comes from a movement that started in the 1960s and seventies, when a new generation fell back in love with these old places and began repainting them in three, four, five colors to bring out all that woodwork. The term "Painted Lady" itself only dates to the late seventies. So the postcard is younger than it looks.

*[beat]*

But the real reason to sit here is the contrast, which is the whole city compressed into a single frame. Gingerbread Victorians from the 1890s, lovingly restored, against a wall of twenty-first-century glass — old San Francisco and new San Francisco, in one glance, with a park full of dogs and picnics in between. These six houses survived the quake, survived the wrecking ball, survived being painted gray. Now they're the first thing the world pictures when it pictures this city. Not bad for a row of spec houses.
