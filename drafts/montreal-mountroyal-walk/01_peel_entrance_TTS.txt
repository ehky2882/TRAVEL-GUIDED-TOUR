This park was laid out by Frederick Law Olmsted, the landscape architect who designed Central Park in New York, and Mount Royal — opened in eighteen seventy-six — was one of the last great projects of his career. He took it on one condition, more or less: that the mountain be treated as a mountain, not as a garden with a hill in it. His conviction was that a city park should undo the city in you, gradually, and that the medicine only works if you take it on foot.

So the route ahead is deliberate. The stairs and the winding gravel path don't take the shortest line to the top because they were never meant to — the switchbacks stretch the climb so the trees thicken, the traffic fades, and the city lets go of you by degrees.

Head up the first flights of stairs, and where the steps meet the wide gravel path, you have a choice: more stairs, straight up, or the longer switchbacks to the right. Take the switchbacks — that's Olmsted's line, and the next part of the story belongs to him. I'll meet you on the path.
