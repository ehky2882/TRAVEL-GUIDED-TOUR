Walk out onto the plaza, up to the stone balustrade, and collect what you've earned: all of Montreal at once, about two hundred and thirty metres below your feet — downtown stacked in the foreground, the Saint Lawrence behind it, the bridges reaching across to the far shore, and on a clear day, hills on the horizon that are another country.

Find the cross-shaped tower in the middle distance — plahs veel mah ree, whose underground corridors you may have wandered downtown. From up here you can read the whole trick of this city's skyline: nothing in it rises above where you're standing. That's law, not luck, and the mountain has held the high ground for as long as anyone's been building here.

Behind you stands the shah lay, the grand stone pavilion with the sweeping windows. It went up in the early nineteen thirties as a Depression-era public works project — the city putting hundreds of men to work on the mountain in the hungriest years of the century — and it repaid the effort with interest: step inside if it's open and look up at the large canvases around the walls, a painted history of Montreal hanging over the heads of everyone warming up with a coffee. The lookout itself is named for kon dee ah ronk, the wehn daht chief and peacemaker — a story that belongs to the old city's founding ground, and one worth chasing down there if you haven't already.

Most visitors turn around here. Don't — the actual end of this walk is a five-minute stroll away, and it settles a promise I made you at the bottom of the mountain. Facing the shah lay, take the path that leads left and east along the summit, following the signs for the cross. It's the finish line, and it was the finish line long before there was a park.
