As you settle into the switchbacks, notice what's happening to the sound. Somewhere in the last few minutes the city hum dropped away, and what's left is gravel underfoot, wind in the canopy, and your own breathing — which is exactly the sequence Olmsted wrote into the ground you're walking on.

His original plan was even more elaborate than what you're experiencing: a single long, gentle ascent where the landscape would shift character zone by zone, growing wilder and quieter the higher you went, so that arriving at the top felt like reaching somewhere genuinely apart from the city below. Montreal, in the middle of an economic depression, cut his budget before the vision was fully built — a fate Olmsted took hard — but the great curving path is still his line on the mountain, and the essential trick still works. You can feel it working right now.

And through the trees, when the gaps allow, the city reappears in fragments — a rooftop here, a grey stone shoulder of a mansion there — always a little farther below than you expected. The mountain is dealing the view out slowly, one card at a time, saving the whole hand for the top.

Stay with the path until you reach a junction where a long zig-zagging staircase climbs the slope to your left, with a signpost pointing the way to the shah lay. Those stairs are the last effort. At the top of them is the kon dee ah ronk belvedere — and the payoff.
