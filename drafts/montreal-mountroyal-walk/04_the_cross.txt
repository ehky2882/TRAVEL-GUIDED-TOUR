The steel cross rising out of the trees ahead of you is about thirty metres tall, it lights up every night, and on a clear evening it can be seen from tens of kilometres away. It was raised in 1924 by the Société Saint-Jean-Baptiste. But the reason it stands on this particular mountain is the promise I asked you to hold onto at the bottom of the climb.

In December of 1642, in the settlement's very first winter, the river rose and flood water crept toward the little colony of Ville-Marie. Maisonneuve — the founder, the man in bronze at Place d'Armes — made a vow: if the settlement was spared, he would carry a cross up the mountain himself. The water stopped. And so in January of 1643, he shouldered a wooden cross and climbed this slope through the snow, more or less the way you just did, minus the staircases and the gravel and the coffee waiting at the chalet. The cross you're looking at is the distant descendant of the one he planted — steel where his was timber, electric where his stood dark, but the same statement on the same summit.

[beat]

Which is what this whole walk has been. Every person who climbs Mount Royal — the joggers taking the stairs two at a time, the families on the switchbacks, you, today — is repeating the city's oldest deliberate act: going up the mountain because something at the top feels worth the effort. Olmsted understood that when he refused to make the climb easy. Maisonneuve invented it, three hundred and eighty-odd years ago, in the snow.

Take the descent gently — downhill is harder on the knees than anyone admits — and give the city a last long look on the way. It earned the view of you, too.
