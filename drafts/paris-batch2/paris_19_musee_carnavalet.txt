You're outside the Musée Carnavalet, spread across two linked mansions in the heart of the Marais, and it may be the most generous museum in Paris — because it's devoted entirely to the story of the city you're standing in, and it costs nothing to enter.

Start with the building, because its name is a small joke history played. The main mansion was once owned by a Breton noblewoman whose surname Parisians found unpronounceable, so they mangled it — and the mangled version, Carnavalet, stuck so hard it became the building's name, and then the museum's. A garbled name is the most Parisian thing imaginable: the city heard something foreign and simply reshaped it to suit itself.

Inside is the city's autobiography, told through the things it couldn't bear to throw away. Old painted shop signs that once hung over streets long since rebuilt. Carved panels rescued from demolished ballrooms. Relics of the Revolution. A model of the city as it looked centuries ago. And, reconstructed room by room, the actual cork-lined bedroom where Marcel Proust wrote much of his great novel, sealing himself off from noise and daylight to chase his own memories.

That's really the thread of the whole place. This isn't a museum of masterpieces brought in from elsewhere — it's a hoard of the city's own discarded pieces, the fragments Paris saved as it endlessly rebuilt itself. Every shop sign and staircase and bedroom here is something the city outgrew but couldn't quite let go of.

So if you want to understand how Paris became Paris — how the medieval town became the Revolutionary capital became the modern city — this is the place, assembled out of its own leftovers. And the city, fittingly, lets you in for free. Walk through it slowly. You're reading the autobiography of the ground beneath your feet.
