You're at the Musée de Cluny, the museum of the Middle Ages, and before you even go in, look at the building, because it's two completely different eras stacked on top of each other. The lower portion — those thick stone walls and rounded arches — is Roman: the remains of a vast public bath-house built here nearly two thousand years ago, when this was the Roman town of Lutetia. On top of it sits an elegant late-medieval mansion, raised fifteen hundred years later for a powerful abbot. The museum of the medieval world is housed, literally, in the medieval world built on top of the Roman one.

Inside, you can still walk into the great cold room of the Roman baths — a soaring stone chamber, the best-preserved Roman interior in Paris. But most people come for what hangs upstairs.

In a darkened circular room hang six tapestries known as The Lady and the Unicorn, woven around 1500 and among the most beautiful things to survive from the medieval world. Five of them illustrate the five senses — touch, taste, smell, hearing, sight — each shown as a graceful scene with a noblewoman, a lion and a unicorn. The sixth is the mystery. It shows the lady before a tent bearing the words "To my only desire," and no one has ever fully agreed what it means: a sixth sense, the heart, free will, the giving up of desire. The greatest medieval artwork in France ends on a question nobody can answer.

One more thing to find, near the entrance. A row of large stone heads, battered and noseless — the original heads of the Kings of Judah, hacked off the front of Notre-Dame during the Revolution by a crowd who mistook them for French kings. They were thought lost forever. Then, in 1977, they turned up by accident, buried in the basement of a Paris bank.

So you've got Rome under your feet, a riddle on the wall, and the lost faces of Notre-Dame found under a bank. Few museums pack so much time into one building.
