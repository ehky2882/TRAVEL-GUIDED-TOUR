Stand in the open square facing the main facade — a long, ornate building bristling with statues, turrets and a high central pavilion, looking every bit the grand Renaissance palace. Here's the catch: almost none of what you're looking at is as old as it appears. This is the Hôtel de Ville, the seat of Paris's city government, and it has stood on this exact spot for more than seven centuries — but the building in front of you is essentially a careful copy of itself.

The original Renaissance city hall stood here for three hundred years. Then, in 1871, in the violent final days of the Paris Commune — the radical uprising that briefly seized the city — it was deliberately set alight as the rebels retreated, and it burned to the ground. When the city rebuilt it, between the 1870s and the 1890s, it chose not to modernise but to recreate: a near-replica of the lost Renaissance facade, only larger and more elaborate. What you see is a nineteenth-century reconstruction wearing a sixteenth-century face.

Now look at the open ground you're standing on, because it has a hard past and a strange legacy. For centuries this was the Place de Grève, the city's main square for public executions. But the word grève — which simply meant the sloping, gravelly riverbank here — gave the French language something unexpected. Out-of-work labourers used to gather on this open ground waiting to be hired, and to this day the French phrase for being on strike, en grève, comes straight from the name of this square.

So you're standing on the spot where, for hundreds of years, the city punished people, hired people, and governed people — all in the same place.

One more. In August 1944, when Paris was liberated, it was from a window of this building that General de Gaulle addressed the freed city. The square that had seen so much of Paris's hardest history was also where it celebrated its freedom.
