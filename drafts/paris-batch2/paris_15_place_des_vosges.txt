Walk into the middle of the square and turn slowly all the way around, because the whole point of this place is that every side looks the same. Thirty-six houses of warm red brick and pale stone, identical pitched roofs, a continuous covered arcade running around all four sides at ground level — and a quiet garden of clipped trees and fountains in the centre, where you're standing. Pick a bench.

Here's what you're looking at: the first of its kind. Before this, Paris had no planned squares — it grew as a medieval tangle of streets. Then King Henri the Fourth decided, around 1605, to impose a single unified design on one open space: every facade matched, built to one plan, around a central garden. It was called the Place Royale, and it worked so well that it became the template — every grand square laid out in Paris and across Europe in the two centuries that followed owes something to this one. You're standing in the original.

Look at the two taller pavilions facing each other across the garden, one on the north side and one on the south — those were the King's and the Queen's, slightly grander than the rest, marking the axis.

Now, the name. It isn't "Royale" anymore — it's Place des Vosges, and the reason is wonderfully unromantic. In 1800 the new revolutionary government renamed it to honour the Vosges, a region in eastern France — specifically because the Vosges had been the first department in the country to pay its taxes in full. The most beautiful square in Paris is named, in effect, after the most punctual taxpayers in France.

One corner holds another draw: at number six, the house where Victor Hugo lived for sixteen years and worked on Les Misérables. It's a museum now, and it's free.

So: the square that invented the square, renamed for an accountant's gold star. Sit a while under the arcades. It has looked almost exactly like this for four hundred years — which, in a city that keeps tearing itself down and starting again, is its own quiet miracle.
