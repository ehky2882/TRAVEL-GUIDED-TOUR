You're in the Latin Quarter, the oldest student district in Paris, and the first thing to know is why it's called that — because the answer is hiding in plain sight. For centuries, from the Middle Ages onward, this was the home of the university, and the students and scholars here all spoke and argued and studied in one shared language: Latin. Not because they were Roman, but because Latin was the common tongue of learning across all of Europe — a German, an Italian and an Englishman could all understand one another in it. The quarter is named for a language that nobody has spoken on these streets in two hundred years.

These are some of the oldest, narrowest streets in the city — a tangle of medieval lanes that the great nineteenth-century rebuilding of Paris mostly spared. Wander them and you're walking the same crooked paths students walked seven hundred years ago.

Now find the bookshop facing the river, across from Notre-Dame: Shakespeare and Company, green-fronted, books spilling from every shelf. It's famous — and it's also, in a sense, a loving tribute act. The original Shakespeare and Company was run by an American, Sylvia Beach, in the 1920s; it was the heart of the expatriate writers' scene, and it was she who first published James Joyce's Ulysses when no one else would touch it. That shop closed during the war and never reopened. The one you're looking at was opened later by a different American, George Whitman, who borrowed the name in homage.

He ran it as something more than a shop. Aspiring writers were allowed to sleep among the books — in beds tucked between the shelves — in exchange for a little work and a promise to read. Thousands have, over the years.

So the whole quarter runs on homage: named for a dead language, kept alive by a bookshop that took a dead shop's name. It's a place that honours the past by impersonating it — and somehow stays completely alive doing so.
