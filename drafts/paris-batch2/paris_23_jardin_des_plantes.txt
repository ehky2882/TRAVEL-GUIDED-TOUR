You're at the edge of the Jardin des Plantes — the Garden of Plants — which sounds like a pleasant city park and is, in fact, one of the most important pieces of ground in the history of French science. It began, four centuries ago, as something very specific: a medicine garden.

In 1626 the king's physicians laid it out to grow medicinal herbs and teach their properties — a working pharmacy in plant form, the royal garden of healing herbs. But over the next two centuries it grew into something far larger. The greatest French naturalists worked here — the people who first tried to classify all living things, to puzzle out how life was organised and whether species might change over time. The arguments that shaped modern biology in France happened on these paths and in the buildings around you. A garden planted to grow remedies became the place where people worked out the order of life itself.

Walk in and, among the formal flowerbeds, you'll find a small zoo — the menagerie. It's one of the oldest in the world, and its origins are pure Revolution. When the monarchy fell, the exotic animals of the royal court at Versailles, along with creatures seized from travelling fairs, had nowhere to go — so in the 1790s they were brought here, and the menagerie was born from the wreckage of the old regime.

It has seen hard times. During the harsh siege of Paris in the winter of 1870, when the city was cut off and starving, some of its animals were lost to the kitchens — a grim chapter the garden doesn't hide.

Today there's also a grand glass-roofed gallery telling the story of evolution and the staggering variety of animal life. But the garden itself is the real exhibit: four hundred years of curiosity, laid out in neat rows, still growing. Find a bench among the old trees and sit in the middle of it.
