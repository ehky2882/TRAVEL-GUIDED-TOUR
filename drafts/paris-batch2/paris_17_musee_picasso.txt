You're in front of the Hôtel Salé, one of the grandest private mansions in the Marais — a great seventeenth-century townhouse with a deep courtyard, carved stonework, and a sweeping staircase inside. It holds the largest public collection of Picasso's work anywhere in the world. Before you go in, two stories about how all this came to be — and both of them, oddly, are about tax.

Start with the house. It was built around 1660 for a man who had grown rich collecting the salt tax — the deeply unpopular levy on salt that the French crown imposed for centuries. The nickname stuck: locals called it the Hôtel Salé, the "salted mansion," a little dig at the source of the money. So the building itself is a monument to a tax.

Now the collection. When Picasso died in 1973 he left an enormous body of work — thousands of paintings, sculptures and drawings, the pieces he'd kept for himself across a seventy-year career. His heirs faced a vast inheritance-tax bill. And French law allowed something unusual: instead of paying in cash, they could pay the state in artworks of equivalent value. So they did — and the French nation acquired, as a tax payment, one of the greatest concentrations of Picasso anywhere on earth. The museum opened here in 1985 to house it.

So look at what you have. A mansion built by a salt-tax collector, now holding a collection assembled entirely from a tax paid in art. The whole place exists because of money owed to the government — twice over, three centuries apart.

There's something fitting in that. Picasso spent his life turning ordinary things — a bicycle seat, a sheet of newspaper, a scrap of metal — into art the state would one day chase. And in the end, his own art became the currency that settled the bill. Go in and find the bull's head made from a bicycle: handlebars and a seat, nothing more, and unmistakably a bull.
