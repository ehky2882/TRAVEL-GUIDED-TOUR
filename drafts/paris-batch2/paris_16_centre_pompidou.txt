Stand back on the sloping open square in front of the building and just take in the facade, because there's nothing else in Paris that looks remotely like it. You're looking at a giant glass-and-steel box with all its insides on the outside: pipes, ducts, struts, and a glass tube of escalators climbing diagonally across the front — all of it painted in bright colours. That's the Centre Pompidou, and right now that facade is the whole show. The building closed in 2025 for a long renovation and won't reopen until the end of the decade, so we're here for the idea, not the art.

And the idea is genuinely radical. When two young, almost unknown architects — Renzo Piano and Richard Rogers — won the competition to design it in the early 1970s, they did something nobody had dared at this scale: they turned the building inside out. Every pipe, every duct, every stairway and structural beam was pushed onto the exterior, so that the inside could be left completely open — vast clear floors with nothing in the way, free to be rearranged however an exhibition needed.

Now look at the colours, because they aren't decoration — they're a code. Blue is for air, green for water, yellow for electricity, and the bright red is for movement: the escalators and the lifts that carry people. Once you know that, you can read the building like a diagram of its own organs.

When it opened in 1977, plenty of Parisians hated it — they said it looked like an oil refinery dropped into the medieval Marais. Half a century later it's one of the most loved buildings in the city, and the open plaza in front, sloping down toward the entrance, has become exactly the kind of public gathering place its architects hoped for.

There's a small irony in the timing. A building designed to be endlessly flexible, easy to adapt and change — and here it is, shut for years for a top-to-bottom overhaul. Even the most adaptable buildings, it turns out, eventually have to close for repairs.
