Stand back in the open square to take in the front, because it's pretending to be something it isn't. A row of huge Greek-style columns, a triangular pediment, an enormous dome behind — it looks like an ancient temple, but it's barely two and a half centuries old, and it has spent that whole time unable to decide what it's for.

It began as a church. King loo-ee the Fifteenth, gravely ill, vowed that if he recovered he would build a magnificent church to Saint zhuhnvyev, the patron saint of Paris. He recovered; the church went up. But it was finished in the late seventeen hundreds, just as the Revolution arrived — and the Revolution had no use for a church. So it was converted into a secular temple to honour the nation's great citizens, and carved across the front went the words: To the great men, the grateful homeland. Then it became a church again. Then a temple again. In its first century it flipped back and forth between God and country something like five times.

It settled, finally, as a temple to the great. Down in the crypt lie voltair and roosso, the writers oogoh and zoh-la, the scientists Marie and Pierre kyooree — Marie the first woman laid here for her own achievements. Above them, under the dome, hangs the real gift of the place.

In eighteen fifty-one a scientist named layon fookoh hung a long pendulum from the centre of the dome and set it swinging. Over hours, the direction of its swing slowly turned — not because the pendulum changed, but because the Earth was rotating beneath it. It was the first time anyone had directly, visibly shown that the planet spins, without looking at the sky. People came and watched the floor of the world turn under their feet.

So this is a building that set out to honour God, kept changing its mind, and ended up honouring people — and proving, with a weight on a wire, that the Earth itself moves. A pendulum still swings there today. Stand under it a while and watch the world rotate.
