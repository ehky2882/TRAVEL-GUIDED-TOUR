Before anything else, breathe in. If the wind is right, what you're smelling is smoked meat — the delicatessen a few steps up the boulevard has been curing brisket the same way since 1928, and the scent is as much a landmark as anything made of stone.

You're standing on the Main, and for more than a century this street was Canada's gangway. Newcomers came up from the harbour and simply kept walking north, each wave settling a little further along the boulevard — and the street still reads like rings in a tree trunk. Walk it and the storefronts change languages on you: the old Jewish garment blocks, then Portuguese grocers and rotisseries, with Greek, Hungarian, Italian and a dozen other chapters layered in and over one another. A few blocks up on your right you'll pass Parc du Portugal, a small square with a bandstand — and the grey house facing it was Leonard Cohen's home for more than forty years, a man who could have lived anywhere and chose a poet's distance from the noise of the Main. He'll cross our path once more before the end.

This next stretch is the walk's one honest slog — a good twenty to twenty-five minutes straight north up the boulevard. Treat it as the point rather than the price: window-read the shopfronts, count the languages, watch the murals go by.

[beat]

When you reach Rue Saint-Viateur, turn left. Within a block, the air will change again — wood smoke this time, and something like warm sesame. Follow your nose. That's Mile End, and that's the finish.
