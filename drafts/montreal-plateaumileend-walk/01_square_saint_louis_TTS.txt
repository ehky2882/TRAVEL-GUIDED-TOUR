Stand by the fountain and look at the houses around the square — the turrets, the crested rooflines, the painted trim in colours no committee would ever approve. These were built in the eighteen eighties by the French-Canadian upper middle class, and they weren't being modest: this square was where a rising francophone bourgeoisie announced, in mansard and gingerbread, that it had arrived.

But notice what kind of showing off it is. The grandeur all faces the park. The square works like a shared front parlour — the fountain is the hearth, the benches are the furniture, and the houses arrange themselves around it like guests at a salon. Poets and playwrights have haunted this square for over a century — a few of them are here in bronze, if you care to circle the paths and meet them — and it's no accident they gathered at a park rather than in private clubs. In this part of the city, the room where things happen has always been outdoors.

That's the idea this whole walk is built on, and the square is only the first and most formal version of it. It gets looser, funnier, and more delicious from here.

Leave the square along avenue lah vahl, the leafy street running north from its west side — worth a slow look in its own right — and keep going into the residential blocks beyond. Watch the buildings as you go, and especially watch the staircases. At the corner with avenue doo loot, pause. That's where I'll explain what you've been walking past.
