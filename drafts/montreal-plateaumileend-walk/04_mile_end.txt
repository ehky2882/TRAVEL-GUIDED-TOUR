The smell brought you here, so let's honour it first. Somewhere close by, a wood-fired oven is turning out bagels by hand — rolled, boiled in honeyed water, and baked over maple wood — and the two bakeries that rule this neighbourhood have been at it since 1919 and 1957 respectively, in a rivalry conducted entirely without a ceasefire. The ovens barely rest; one of the shops never closes at all. Pick a side, join the sidewalk line, and order a half dozen straight from the fire. Leonard Cohen, who you passed on the Main, was a regular in one of these line-ups for decades — the safest possible name-drop, since both camps claim him.

While you wait, look around, because Mile End is where this walk's whole idea comes home. The line you're standing in is a public living room. The stoops and staircases are occupied. Around the corner, a church built by Irish hands in the style of Istanbul now serves a Polish congregation, and its bells share the air with three languages of street hockey. Nothing here happens behind closed doors that could plausibly happen in front of them.

[beat]

So that's the walk, start to finish: a bourgeois square arranged like a parlour, staircases that turned thrift into theatre, laneways gardened by committee, a boulevard that absorbed the world one block at a time — and now supper, eaten warm out of a paper bag, standing on the curb with everyone else. Other cities keep their best rooms inside. This one voted for the fire escapes, remember — it knew exactly what it was doing.

Stay for the evening if you can. The balconies are best after seven.
