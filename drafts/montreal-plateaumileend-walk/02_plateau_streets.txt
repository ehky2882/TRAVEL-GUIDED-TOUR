By now you've passed dozens of them: the outdoor staircases, spiralling and zig-zagging up the fronts of the triplexes, wrought iron curling like handwriting, and famously no two quite alike. Locals once voted them the city's best-loved symbol — ahead of the mountain, ahead of Old Montreal — which tells you something about a place that chose its fire escapes over its cathedrals.

They exist because of rules and thrift: bylaws pushed buildings back from the street, and putting the stairs outside meant not heating them and not wasting a single square metre of the flat inside. But what began as economy became a stage. Every staircase delivers you to a balcony, and the balcony is where the Plateau actually lives — supper out there in July, hockey radio drifting down, neighbours conducting entire friendships one storey apart. There's an old local answer to "where are you summering?" — "Balconville" — and a playwright from the other end of town liked the word enough to make it the title of the first bilingual play in Canadian theatre. The joke stuck because it's true.

And the life runs out the back as well. Behind these streets is a hidden network of laneways that residents have turned into gardens — the ruelles vertes, the green alleys, hundreds of them across the city now, and the movement started right here in the Plateau in the 1990s. If a laneway entrance tempts you along the way, it's public. Wander in.
