Look up at the rainbow flag flying over the plaza at Castro and Market. It's enormous, deliberately so, and it marks this corner as the symbolic capital of gay America. The flag itself was designed here, in this neighborhood, in 1978 — and the reason this corner became a capital at all comes down largely to one man who ran a camera shop a few blocks up the street.

Walk up Castro to number five-seventy-five. Today it's a storefront with a plaque, but in the nineteen-seventies it was Castro Camera, and behind the counter was Harvey Milk. He hadn't planned to be a politician. He came to San Francisco, opened a shop, and watched gay people pour into this neighborhood from all over the country — drawn here because here, finally, they could live openly. Milk turned his store into an unofficial community center and himself into its loudest advocate, and people started calling him the Mayor of Castro Street long before he held any office.

In 1977 he won a seat on the Board of Supervisors, becoming one of the first openly gay people elected to public office anywhere in the country. He served barely eleven months. In November of 1978 he was assassinated inside City Hall, together with Mayor George Moscone — a loss this neighborhood has never really stopped feeling, and one it marks every year with a candlelight march down Market Street.

[beat]

But here's what Milk understood, and why this corner matters more than his short time in office would suggest. His whole strategy was visibility. He believed the single most powerful political act a gay person could perform was simply to be known — to come out, to families, to coworkers, to neighbors — because it's harder to fear or legislate against people you actually love. He pushed people to do it relentlessly, and he led by example, living his life completely in the open at a time when that took real courage.

That's the idea this giant flag is really flying for. Not a man — though he's worth remembering — but a conviction: that being seen, plainly and without apology, changes the world more than almost anything else. Stand under it a moment. The whole neighborhood below you is what happens when a lot of people decide to stop hiding.
