Stand in front of the de Young, in the heart of Golden Gate Park, and the first thing to make peace with is the color. The whole building is sheathed in copper — dimpled, perforated, and embossed all over its surface — and when it opened in 2005 a lot of people thought it looked like a battleship that had wandered into the trees. Give it time. The architects, the Swiss firm Herzog and de Meuron, knew exactly what they were doing, and they were playing a long game.

Look closely at that copper skin. It's punched and textured to mimic the pattern of light falling through a forest canopy — leaves, dapples, shadow. And copper does one thing reliably over time: it weathers. The architects designed this building to corrode on purpose, so that as the decades pass the bright metal turns a soft, mottled green and the whole museum slowly dissolves, visually, into the surrounding park. It's a building engineered to disappear.

The museum holds American art, and art from across the Pacific, Africa, and the Americas — well worth a few hours. But there's one part you can enjoy for nothing, and it's the reason a lot of locals come. At one end the building twists upward into the Hamon Tower, and the observation floor at the top is free. No ticket, no gallery admission. You ride up, step out, and get a glassed-in, three-hundred-sixty-degree view over the whole park, the avenues, and out to the ocean.

[beat]

That free tower is the generous heart of the place. A museum could easily charge for the best view in the park; this one decided the view belonged to everybody, members and passersby alike, and just gave it away.

So take the elevator up first, before you decide anything else. Get the lay of the land — the green canopy you're standing in, the city beyond it, the Pacific at the edge. Then come back down and look at that copper one more time, and picture it fifty years from now, gone soft and green, the building finally keeping its promise to vanish into the trees it was always trying to become.
