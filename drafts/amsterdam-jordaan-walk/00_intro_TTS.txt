The grand canal ring has a shadow twin. When the planners drew the seventeenth-century city — those calm, expensive arcs of merchants' houses — they drew this quarter too, on the cheap side of the prin sen grahkt: narrower streets, no imported stone, the lanes following the old farm ditches instead of elegant new curves. This was the yor dahn, the workers' annex — tanners, brewers' men, dockhands, and wave after wave of newcomers with more hope than money. One telling derives the name from the French zhar dan, garden, though the truth is nobody's quite sure, which suits the place.

This walk is about how the city's poorest streets became its most loved — and about what the quarter remembers. Fair warning: the yor dahn carries some of Amsterdam's heaviest memory and its lightest pleasures within a few hundred metres of each other — a hidden annex and an apple-pie rivalry, a riot memorial and cafés that still sing on weekends. The mix is not an accident. The mix is the yor dahn.

Practical notes: the route is flat and short — about two kilometres, an hour at a stroll. We'll pass the Anne Frank House early on; the walk treats it respectfully from the outside, and if you want to go in, know that tickets are online-only, released weeks ahead, and sell out — that visit needs planning of its own.

Start on ves ter markt, the square at the foot of the tallest church tower in the city. You can't miss it. That's rather the point of it.
