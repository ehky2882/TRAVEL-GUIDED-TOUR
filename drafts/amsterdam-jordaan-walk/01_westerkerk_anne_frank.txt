Look up first. The Westerkerk tower — the Westertoren — is the tallest church tower in Amsterdam, about eighty-five metres of it, and it wears something odd at the top: a great blue imperial crown. That's the crown of Maximilian of Austria, granted to the city's coat of arms centuries ago in thanks for services rendered — a Habsburg emperor's hat, gleaming over a Calvinist church, on the edge of a workers' quarter. Amsterdam collects ironies the way other cities collect statues.

Inside the church, somewhere under the floor, lies Rembrandt. Nobody knows exactly where. He died in 1669, poor, a short walk from here, and was buried in a rented grave — the kind whose contents could be cleared when the rent lapsed. The most famous painter the city ever produced, and the city cannot point to his bones. It can only point to this building and say: somewhere.

Now walk to the canal side of the church, along the Prinsengracht, and stop. A few doors along the far quay stands a narrow dark house, number 263, with a queue that forms in every season. Behind its street face, in a concealed annex at the back, Anne Frank and seven others hid for twenty-five months during the German occupation, until they were discovered and deported in the summer of 1944. Anne died in Bergen-Belsen at fifteen. Her father, Otto, was the only one of the eight to survive; helpers had saved her diary, and he gave it to the world.

She could hear these bells from the hiding place — the same carillon of the Westertoren that will likely ring while you stand here — and she wrote of finding a kind of steadiness in them. The tower has become part of how the world remembers her.

Take a moment here; there's no hurry. Then come back around to the square beside the church. Set into the ground and the canal bank there are three pink granite triangles — easy to walk past, impossible to forget once you understand them. The Homomonument is next.
