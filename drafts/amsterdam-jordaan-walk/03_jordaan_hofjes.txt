Notice how quickly the scale changed. The canals here are narrower, the houses smaller and less regular, the streets bending where the ring's arcs were serene — because under these lanes run the old drainage ditches of the farmland this quarter was thrown up on, cheaply and fast, while the money went into the grand canals behind you. Wander a few streets at will here — Bloemgracht and Egelantiersgracht are as lovely as any — and keep an eye out for two things in particular.

The first: carved stone tablets set into house fronts — a leaping hare, a sower, a ship, a pair of scissors. These are gevelstenen, gable stones, and they're the quarter's original addressing system. Before house numbers existed, you lived "at the Hare" or "at the Golden Scissors"; the stones named the house, advertised the trade, and told the illiterate city who was who. Hundreds survive, lovingly restored — a directory written in sandstone.

The second: unassuming doors that open into hidden courtyards. These are hofjes — almshouses, built around gardens by wealthy benefactors from the sixteen-hundreds onward, mostly to house elderly women of small means, on the theory that charity should come with a garden. Dozens hide in the Jordaan. Some open their outer door to respectful visitors; if you find one ajar, you may step in — quietly, please, because every hofje is still exactly what it was built to be: somebody's home.

[beat]

The quarter itself has needed rescuing more than once. By the mid twentieth century it was overcrowded and crumbling, and there were serious plans to raze whole blocks; the residents objected, loudly and long, and won. The reward for their loyalty is that the Jordaan is now some of the most coveted property in the country — which is not entirely a happy ending for the people who loved it first.

Head north through the quarter, keeping roughly parallel to the Prinsengracht, until the streets open onto a square with a church in the middle. That's Noordermarkt, our next stop.
