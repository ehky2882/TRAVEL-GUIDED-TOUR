The church in the middle of this square is the nor der kairk, finished in sixteen twenty-three by hen drik duh kye zer — the same hand that topped the Mint Tower — and it was something new: a church built specifically for the workers of the yor dahn. Look at its shape. No long nave, no grand processional axis; it's built on a cross plan that's nearly a church-in-the-round, with the pulpit at the centre and every seat more or less equal. The merchants on the ring got their soaring towers. The yor dahn got democracy in brick, which the quarter would probably tell you is the better deal.

The square around it has always been where the quarter gathers, and market mornings still fill it — produce and cheese one day, fabrics and second-hand treasure another — while two celebrated cafés within sight of this spot have spent decades in polite dispute over who bakes the best apple pie in Amsterdam. The only responsible research is to try both.

But this square remembers harder gatherings too. A memorial here marks the yor dahn riots of nineteen thirty-four, when, in the depths of the Depression, cuts to unemployment relief brought the quarter into the streets for days, and several residents were killed. And it was on this square, in February nineteen forty-one, that crowds gathered in the meetings that launched the February Strike — when Amsterdam's workers laid down their tools in open protest at the persecution of their Jewish neighbours, one of the very few such acts in occupied Europe.

A workers' church, a market, an apple-pie feud, and two monuments to the days this quarter stood up. That's the yor dahn on one square.

The finish line is thirty seconds away. Walk to the north edge of the square, where it meets a canal running crossways — broader, lined with warehouses. That's the brow ers grahkt, and it's the last stop.
