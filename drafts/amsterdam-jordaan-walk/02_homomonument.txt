Three triangles of pink granite: one flush with the square, one raised like a low podium, one descending in steps into the canal itself, where the water laps over its point. Together they form the corners of a larger triangle you're standing inside. This is the Homomonument, unveiled in 1987 — the first monument anywhere in the world to the gay men and women persecuted and killed for who they were.

The pink triangle was the badge the Nazi camps forced on gay prisoners; the designer, Karin Daan, took the mark of persecution and laid it into the city's stone in granite, at the foot of the national church tower, in the open, unmissably public — everything the badge's inventors intended homosexuality never to be.

And the triangles point. The one at street level aims toward the Anne Frank House, a few hundred metres away. The one at the canal aims at the National Monument on the Dam, the country's memorial to its war dead. The raised one points toward the former headquarters of the COC, one of the oldest organisations of its kind in the world — persecution, national memory, and the long work of organising, held in one quiet geometry. On the steps by the water, people still leave flowers, often for reasons of their own. Carved into the monument is a line by Jacob Israël de Haan — a Jewish and openly gay Amsterdam writer, decades ahead of his time — speaking of a boundless longing for friendship.

If this walk's city has a famous ledger of tolerance — everything permitted, with conditions — then this monument marks its missing page: the people the old arrangement never protected at all, remembered here first, before anywhere else on earth.

When you're ready, cross the Prinsengracht by the bridge near the church and walk straight into the quarter. The streets narrow at once, and they're named for flowers and trees. The Jordaan itself is next.
