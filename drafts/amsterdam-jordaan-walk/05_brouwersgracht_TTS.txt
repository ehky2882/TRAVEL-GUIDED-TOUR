Find a spot on one of the bridges and look down the water, either direction. Houseboats moulded to the quays, shutters in oxblood and cream, warehouse gables reflected in the canal, trees leaning companionably over all of it. Readers of a local paper once voted the brow ers grahkt the most beautiful canal in the city, and standing here it's hard to build the case against them.

Which is funny, in the yor dahn's favourite way, because nothing here was built to be beautiful. This is the Brewers' Canal — the working waterfront where the breweries and their warehouses stood, storing grain, spices, whale oil, whatever the ships brought in. The shutters were for hoisting sacks, not for charm. And the brewers themselves needed one commodity above all, the one thing this city of water never actually had: clean water. The canals of Amsterdam were, not to put too fine a point on it, undrinkable — so fresh brewing water was shipped in by barge from the rivers outside town. A city floating on water, importing water. The yor dahn has always appreciated a good joke at the planners' expense.

Today the warehouses are flats that trade for fortunes, their loading doors turned into picture windows — the last word in the story this walk has been telling. The quarter built cheap, for the people the grand plan priced out, is now the view everyone wants.

Stay on the bridge a while. Watch a boat slide under your feet, listen for the ves ter tor en on the quarter hour, floating over the rooftops from where we started. The prettiest view in Amsterdam, by popular vote — from the quarter the planners built cheap, for the people who made it worth loving. Four hundred years is a long time to wait for the last laugh, but the yor dahn got it.
