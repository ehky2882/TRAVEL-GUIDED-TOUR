From the street, the monument reads as a low labyrinth — walls of brick, with polished steel floating above them, catching the sky. Walk in. It works at close range.

Every brick carries a name, a date of birth, and an age. There are one hundred and two thousand of them — the Jewish, sin tee and Roma victims of the Netherlands, murdered in the camps or lost without a grave. Seen from above, the steel shapes spell out four Hebrew letters that read "in memory of." dan yel lee bes kint designed it, and it opened in twenty twenty-one, eighty years after the raids on this quarter began.

A number like one hundred and two thousand is not something a person can really hold, and the monument knows that — so it refuses to try. It gives the number back one brick at a time: a name, a birthday, an age. Some of the ages are in single digits. Families appear together, brick after brick along a single row, and that, more than any figure, is where the scale of it lands.

Find one brick. Read the name, and the age, and stand with it for a moment. That is the entire idea — one person, remembered by name, on a wall built so the number could never go abstract again.
