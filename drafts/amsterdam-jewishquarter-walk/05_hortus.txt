THE JEWISH QUARTER — SEGMENT 05: THE HORTUS BOTANICUS

It's deliberate that this walk ends in a garden.

The Hortus Botanicus is one of the oldest botanical gardens in the world. The city founded it in 1638 as the Hortus Medicus — a medicinal herb garden, planted in plague years so the city's doctors and apothecaries would have something reliable to work with. Then the trading ships got hold of it. Dutch vessels brought back plants from every coast they touched, and the medicine garden became a world collection — cycads, palms, a giant water lily, an agave older than most countries. It's a beautiful inheritance, and an honest eye sees where it came from: the same empire that made this city rich carried these plants here, and the garden doesn't pretend otherwise.

One story to take with you. By the most-told version, a coffee plant raised in this garden supplied a cutting presented to the King of France — and from that line descend a great share of the world's coffee plantations. If that's even half true, your morning coffee may trace back to this ground.

The garden nearly didn't make it to the present. In 1987, with its funding gone and closure on the table, it was Amsterdammers — ordinary citizens, organising, donating, volunteering — who saved it. The city that planted it kept it.

[beat]

The Plantage has always been where this part of town came to breathe — the green edge of the old Jewish quarter, a district of slow walks and shade. After everything this route has asked you to carry, that's why it ends here: at a place that has spent nearly four centuries doing one thing, quietly and stubbornly — keeping things alive.

Go in if you can. Find a bench under something older than you, and stay as long as you like. The garden is good at that.
