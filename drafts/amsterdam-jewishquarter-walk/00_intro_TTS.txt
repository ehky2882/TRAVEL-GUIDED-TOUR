Some walks in this city are about buildings, and some are about water. This one is about memory.

For more than four hundred years, the streets east of the centre were the heart of Jewish Amsterdam — suh far dik families from Spain and Portugal first, then ash kuh nah zee families from central and eastern Europe. Their markets, synagogues and printing houses shaped the sound of this city for centuries. The war took almost all of them. What stands here now is a neighbourhood that holds both truths at once — the presence and the loss — sometimes on opposite sides of the same street.

A few practical notes before you set off. The walk is short, flat and unhurried: five stops, all within a few minutes of each other, ending in a botanical garden. Four of the sites along the way — the Jewish Museum, the Portuguese Synagogue, the hol lant suh skow burg and the National Holocaust Museum — share a single ticket, valid for a week. The Holocaust Museum uses timed entry and often books out, so if you want to go inside, reserve ahead. The Names Monument is open air, free, and always there.

This is the quietest walk in the city, and it asks a little more of you than the others do. It gives more back.

We start at vah ter loh pline — the square with the flea market, and the house where rem brant lived. Head there now.
