THE JEWISH QUARTER — SEGMENT 04: THE HOLLANDSCHE SCHOUWBURG & THE NATIONAL HOLOCAUST MUSEUM

Plantage Middenlaan is a gentle street — plane trees, tram tracks, café tables — and that gentleness is part of what makes this stop land the way it does. On one side stands the pale facade of a former theatre; directly across from it, a sober brick school building. Between them, this street tells the hardest part of the quarter's story, and its most hopeful, at the same time — which is why we take both sides together.

The theatre first. The Hollandsche Schouwburg opened in 1892 as a popular playhouse. Under the occupation it was made a Jews-only theatre, and from the summer of 1942 it was turned into an assembly point: families were ordered to report here and held in the auditorium — for hours, for days, sometimes longer — before being taken away. Today the facade stands, and behind it a quiet memorial courtyard and a wall carrying family names. It is free to enter, and it is kept deliberately still.

Now the other side of the street. Small children were held separately, in the crèche opposite the theatre, and the woman who ran it, Henriëtte Pimentel, decided not to accept what was happening. With her staff, students from the teacher-training college next door, and resistance workers across the country, she had children smuggled out — passed over the garden wall, carried in bags and baskets, walked out when a passing tram blocked the view from the theatre — and hidden in homes across the Netherlands. Around six hundred children were saved this way.

[beat]

In 2024, the National Holocaust Museum opened inside that school building — the first museum to tell the whole story of the persecution of the Jews in the Netherlands, housed, deliberately, in the building children escaped through. One street. Both truths.

When you're ready, walk back along Plantage Middenlaan the way you came, to the garden gates you passed earlier. The Hortus Botanicus is the end of this walk.
