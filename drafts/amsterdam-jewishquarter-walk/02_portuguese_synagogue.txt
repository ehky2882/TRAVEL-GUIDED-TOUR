THE JEWISH QUARTER — SEGMENT 02: THE PORTUGUESE SYNAGOGUE

The building in front of you is a great, plain box of dark brick, ringed by a low run of small houses hugging its base. It doesn't announce itself. It didn't need to.

When it opened in 1675, this was the largest synagogue in the world — built by a community of refugees. The Sephardic Jews of Amsterdam had fled the Inquisitions of Spain and Portugal, and this city let them in and let them build, though its tolerance, as ever here, came with conditions: most guilds stayed closed to them for generations, so they made their living in trade, in printing, in medicine. Within a century of arriving, they raised this.

Inside, almost nothing has changed since the seventeenth century. The synagogue was never wired for electricity — services are still lit by hundreds of candles in the great brass chandeliers — and the wooden floor is still strewn with fine sand, an old Dutch trick to soften footsteps and drink up the damp. In the low buildings around the courtyard sits Ets Haim, the oldest functioning Jewish library in the world. This is not a preserved monument; it's a working house of prayer, and has been for three and a half centuries.

On the square beside it stands a bronze statue of a dockworker, solid and bare-armed. In February 1941, when the first raids fell on this quarter, Amsterdam's dockworkers called a general strike in protest — one of the very few mass public protests against the persecution of Jews anywhere in occupied Europe. He stands here as its memory.

[beat]

The building came through the war almost untouched. Most of the people it was built for did not. Candles are still lit here every week.

Walk on now toward Weesperstraat, the wide street a few minutes south. Beside it you'll find low brick walls topped with mirrored steel — the National Holocaust Names Monument.
