You should be on vah ter loh pline, the wide square with the daily flea market — stalls of vinyl records, army coats, bicycle parts, and the occasional genuine find. At the market's edge, on the street called yoh den bray straht, stands a tall brick house with red-and-green shutters.

If you've walked this city with me before, you've been meeting rem brant in pieces — the anatomy lesson he painted for the surgeons at the vahg, the Night Watch at the rikes mew zay um, an unmarked grave somewhere under the ves ter kairk floor. This is where he actually lived. He bought the house in sixteen thirty-nine, at the height of his fame, and he bought it here, in the middle of the Jewish quarter, on purpose, many think: his neighbours turn up in his paintings and etchings, and the faces of his biblical scenes were the faces of this street.

It didn't end well, and that's the gift. In sixteen fifty-six rem brant went bankrupt, and the clerks handling the sale walked through the house writing down everything he owned, room by room. That inventory survived — and it's the reason the museum inside could be furnished the way it has been. His misfortune became the floor plan.

Now turn back to the square itself. vah ter loh pline was made in eighteen eighty-two, when two canals were filled in, and its market is the descendant of the Jewish market that fed and clothed this quarter for generations. Trading stopped in the war, along with everything else, and started again after — which makes this square the whole walk in miniature: ordinary life, carried on, on ground that remembers.
