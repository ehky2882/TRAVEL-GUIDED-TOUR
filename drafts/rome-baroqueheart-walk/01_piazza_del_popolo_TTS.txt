The gate behind you is the por tah del poh poh loh, where the main road from the north — the old vee ah flah mee nee ah — entered Rome, which meant that for centuries almost everyone who reached the city overland arrived exactly here: pilgrims, merchants, ambassadors, painters, and eventually the Grand Tourists with their sketchbooks. Rome had one front door, and this was it.

Now look at the three streets. The one on the far left leads toward the Spanish Steps. The one on the right runs down toward the river. And the one in the middle, vee ah del kor soh, drives straight as a ruler for a kilometre and a half into the heart of the city. Together they're known as the Trident, and they turn this square into something much cleverer than a grand entrance — they make it a sorting machine. Rome received you in one magnificent oval room, gave you a moment to be impressed, and then dispatched you down the correct corridor for wherever you were going. We remember the Baroque city for its drama, but underneath the marble it was crowd management: the popes were handling the largest visitor flows in Europe centuries before anyone invented the term, and they did it with architecture instead of barriers.

The obelisk you're standing beside, for the record, is Egyptian — carved for a pharaoh, shipped here by an emperor, and older than the city around it.
