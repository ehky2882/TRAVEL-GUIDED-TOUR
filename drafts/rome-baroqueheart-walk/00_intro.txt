Rome spent the seventeenth century turning itself into a show. The empire was ancient history, the banking fortunes had moved north, and the popes who ran this city understood that what Rome could still do better than anywhere on earth was spectacle — so they hired the best architects alive, pointed them at the medieval tangle, and commissioned wonder: fountains the size of palaces, staircases that pour downhill like water, squares built less for traffic than for applause.

This walk links five of those showpieces. We start at Piazza del Popolo, climb through Piazza di Spagna and the Spanish Steps, squeeze through the lanes to the Trevi Fountain, cross to the Pantheon, and finish in Piazza Navona. It's a bit over two kilometres, flat almost the whole way, on cobbles and busy shopping streets — and these are some of the most visited places in Europe, so treat the crowds as part of the scenery. Early morning and evening are gentler, but the route works at any hour.

One thing to listen for as we go: very little on this walk is quite what it claims to be. A Spanish square that turns out to be French. A fountain that's really the wall of a palace. A temple signed by the wrong man. Baroque Rome was theatre, and theatre runs on illusion — and the trick is that knowing how it's done makes it better, not worse.

Head for Piazza del Popolo, and make for the obelisk at its centre.
