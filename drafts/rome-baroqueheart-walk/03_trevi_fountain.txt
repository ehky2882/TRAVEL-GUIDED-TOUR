You hear it before you see it. Somewhere in these last narrow streets the sound begins — a low, steady roar underneath the crowd noise — and it thickens with every corner until you take one final turn and the whole end of the street is water, rock and marble.

Stop wherever you first get a clear look, and consider what you're actually looking at. The Trevi is not a fountain standing in a square. It's the front wall of a palace — Palazzo Poli — seized top to bottom and turned into a cliff: sculpted boulders spilling forward into the basin, a screen of columns rising the full height of the building, and striding out of the centre of it all, the sea god Oceanus, his chariot hauled through the spray by winged horses. The fountain doesn't stand in front of the architecture; it is the architecture, nearly as tall as the palace whose face it borrowed.

And the little square was never enlarged to give it room — which, by accident or by design, is the secret of the whole performance. There is no long view of the Trevi, no gradual approach. It's always too big for the space it's in, always arriving all at once, a colossus kept in too small a room. The Baroque understood something every theatre director knows: surprise isn't about the object. It's about the framing.

These days, a small ticket gets you down the steps to the water's edge in daytime hours; the view from street level, where you're standing, costs nothing at any hour.
