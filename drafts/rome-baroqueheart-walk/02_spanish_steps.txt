Via del Babuino ends and the space opens out — this is Piazza di Spagna, and the staircase rising in tiers to your left, up to the church with the two bell towers, is the one you know from a thousand photographs. Work your way toward the sinking-boat fountain at its foot and find somewhere to stand.

Start with the name. The Spanish Steps are, in fact, French. The square is named for Spain — the Spanish embassy to the pope has stood here for some four hundred years — but the staircase was paid for with French money, left in the will of a French diplomat, and it climbs to a French church: Trinità dei Monti, up there behind the obelisk. And for decades the French wanted more. The plan was to crown this hillside with a statue of the King of France on horseback, gazing down over the city — and the popes, who had no intention of hosting a foreign monarch above their own rooftops, kept refusing. The staircase, finished in the 1720s, was the eventual compromise: all the sweep and grandeur of a royal monument, with nobody on top. What reads as pure romance is really one of the most elegant diplomatic settlements in Europe — two rival powers, one slope, and the whole argument resolved in travertine.

Notice, too, how the thing behaves. It splits, curves, gathers itself at terraces and spills on again — a staircase designed less for climbing than for lingering. Which makes the modern rule a quiet irony: to protect the stone, sitting on the steps is now banned, so the world's most famous place to sit is standing room only.

The next showpiece hides in the alleys about ten minutes south of here: the Trevi Fountain. Take your time through the shopping streets — it will announce itself.
