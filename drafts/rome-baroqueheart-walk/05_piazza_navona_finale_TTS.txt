It's a Saturday in August, sometime in the seventeen hundreds, and the square you're about to enter is underwater. The fountain drains have been stopped, the piazza has flooded from end to end, and the carriages of the Roman nobility are wheeling slowly through the shallows while half the city leans out of windows to watch, cheer, and be seen.

Come into the square and find a spot near the great fountain at its centre.

For more than two centuries — from sixteen fifty-two until eighteen sixty-six — that was the August ritual here: on summer weekends, pee aht sah nah voh nah became a lake, part cooling-off, part carnival, part rolling display of who mattered in Rome. And it tells you what this square was built to be. The long oval you're standing in follows the footprint of an ancient Roman stadium buried beneath your feet, and when the pahm fee lee family produced a pope in the sixteen forties, they rebuilt the whole space as their personal showcase — palace, church and fountains — then threw it open as the city's grandest room. The centrepiece is the Fountain of the Four Rivers, bair nee nee's marble mountain, four colossal figures for the four continents then known, with an obelisk balanced improbably over the hollow grotto between them; the church rising behind it, sahnt ahn yeh zeh, wears a facade by bor roh mee nee. And around them, the square carries on much as it always has — painters, buskers, café tables, Romans cutting through — the cast changes daily, but the run is permanent.

This is where the walk ends, and it's the right place to end it. Everything we've passed today was built to persuade: a sorting machine dressed as a piazza, a treaty dressed as a staircase, a palace wall dressed as the sea, a signature borrowed for the ages. Illusion, all of it — and none of it any less true. So stay a while. Find a coffee at the edge of the oval, watch the water, and enjoy being exactly what this square has always wanted: an audience.
