Nineteen hundred years, give or take, and nobody has ever built a bigger dome out of plain, unreinforced concrete. Not the Renaissance, not the industrial age, not us. The record holder is in front of you, behind a porch of granite columns — wearing a name that isn't its builder's.

Walk up to where you can read the bronze letters across the front. M. AGRIPPA, they begin — Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius, three times consul, made this. It's the most famous inscription in Rome, and it isn't true — or rather, it's out of date by about a hundred and fifty years. Agrippa did build a temple on this spot, but it burned. What you're looking at is a complete rebuild from the early second century, under the emperor Hadrian — new porch, new rotunda, new dome, the whole miracle — and when it was finished, Hadrian put the old name back on the front.

He did this all over Rome: rebuilt other men's monuments and kept their names on them. It's worth pausing on, because Hadrian did not lack ego. He seems to have grasped something subtler — that in this city, authority came from continuity, and a name two centuries old carried more weight than a fresh one ever could. It's the same instinct you meet everywhere in Rome, where nothing old is discarded and everything gets kept in service; here, what was reused was the signature itself. The most influential building in the history of western architecture, copied by domes from Florence to Washington, is signed by the wrong man — on purpose.

There's a ticket to go inside these days; the porch and the square are free. If you do go in, look at nothing until you've looked up.
