The name means the marsh — and that's exactly what this was. Wet, low ground on the edge of the river, drained by monks in the Middle Ages, useless for anything grand until, in the sixteen hundreds, the kings and nobles decided this was where they wanted to live. They built mansions here, dozens of them, and for a while the maray was the most fashionable address in Paris.

Then fashion moved on, as it does. The aristocrats drifted off to newer quarters and to vairsye, and the maray came down in the world — its grand houses carved up into workshops and crowded tenements, a dense, poor, working quarter, and for a long stretch the heart of the city's Jewish community along with many others who'd found their way to cheap rooms in old streets. By the twentieth century, the city looked at the maray and mostly saw a slum to clear.

Here's the twist that makes this walk worth taking. When Baron ohssmun tore up most of Paris in the eighteen hundreds and replaced it with wide straight boulevards, he largely left the maray alone — too poor, too tangled, not worth the trouble. So the very neglect that brought it low also saved it. This is the largest surviving piece of old Paris: narrow medieval streets and Renaissance mansions that exist precisely because nobody thought them worth knocking down. In the end the city changed its mind, protected the whole quarter, and the maray came back — not as an aristocrats' enclave this time, but as everyone's. The galleries, the boutiques, the cafés, the crowds: this is the city's living room now.

The good news for your feet: it's flat, and it's dense, with everything packed close together. We start at the city's grand front door on the river — the otel duh veel.
