Here's our last stop, and the right one to end on: the muzay karnavalay, the museum of the history of Paris itself, set inside two old maray mansions joined together. Before anything else — it's free, always has been, one of the great no-cost pleasures of the city.

Even the name is a little Paris story. It comes from a long-ago owner with a Breton surname that French tongues couldn't be bothered with, so over the years they mangled it into karnavalay. The wrong name simply stuck, the way so much in this city did, and now it's the right one.

What's inside is the reason it belongs at the end of this walk. This isn't a museum of masterpieces; it's the city's attic. Paris has spent centuries saving bits of itself in here — carved shop signs from streets that no longer exist, whole panelled rooms rescued from mansions before they were demolished, keys and paintings and revolutionary relics, even the actual cork-lined bedroom where the writer Marcel proost sealed himself away to work. Walk through it and you're walking through the physical leftovers of the city you've spent all afternoon in.

Which closes the loop on the maray. You've spent this walk in the one quarter that survived — the old Paris that escaped the wrecking ball by being overlooked, then was loved back to life. And here at the end is the building where the city deliberately keeps the pieces of everything it didn't manage to save anywhere else. The marsh that became the address, that became the slum, that became the living room — remembering itself, for free, in a borrowed mansion with a mangled name. Take your time in here. It's the whole city, in one house.
