This is the otel salay, and it's now the muzay peekasso — and the story of how those two things came together is really a story about taxes, twice over.

Start with the mansion. It was built in the sixteen fifties for a man who'd grown rich collecting the tax on salt — which in those days was a big, hated tax, since everyone needed salt and the crown squeezed it hard. The house he built with the proceeds was so showy that locals nicknamed it the otel salay — the salted mansion, a dig at exactly where the money had come from. So before any peekasso ever hung here, the building itself was already a monument to tax.

Now the art. When peekasso died, his heirs faced an enormous bill in French inheritance tax. France has a clever law that lets you settle a tax debt by handing over works of art instead of cash — and so, rather than money, the state took peekassos. A flood of them: paintings, sculptures, sketches, the artist's own private collection. That windfall is the core of this museum, which holds the largest public collection of his work anywhere. The whole place exists because of a death and a tax bill.

If you go in, hunt for a small bronze called the Bull's Head. Look at it once and it's a bull; look again and it's exactly what peekasso built it from — a bicycle seat for the face, the handlebars for the horns, joined together and cast in bronze. He liked the thought that one day someone might find it, not knowing, melt it down, and turn it back into a bicycle.
