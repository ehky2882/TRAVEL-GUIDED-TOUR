You're standing in front of the Hôtel de Ville — the city hall of Paris, and one of the grandest buildings you'll see all day: a long ornate facade bristling with statues and turrets, facing a wide open square by the river. It looks like it has stood here, untouched, for five hundred years. It hasn't.

This has been the seat of the city's government since the 1300s, when the merchants who effectively ran Paris first set up here. But the building you're looking at is younger than it pretends to be. The grand Renaissance city hall that stood on this spot was burned to the ground in 1871, in the violent collapse of the Commune, when much of central Paris went up in flames. Rather than build something new and modern, the city rebuilt its city hall almost exactly as it had been — a careful, slightly grander copy of what the fire had taken. So this is a nineteenth-century replica wearing a sixteenth-century face, and most people walking past never notice.

Now the square in front of it, because there's a word hiding in it. For centuries this was the Place de Grève — grève meaning the strip of sandy ground along the river. It was where men without work came to stand and wait, hoping to be hired for the day. Out of that grew a phrase the whole world now borrows: to be en grève, "on the strand," came to mean to stop work, to strike. Every strike, everywhere, quietly carries the name of this square.

*[beat]*

It's also, it should be said, a square with a darker past — for a long time the city's public punishments were carried out here, in front of crowds. That's part of its history too.

When you're ready, head north, a few streets back from the river, toward a low modern building wrapped in coloured pipes that could not look more out of place. That's the Centre Pompidou.
