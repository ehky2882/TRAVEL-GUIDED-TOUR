Step out of the lanes and into the Place des Vosges, and let it open up around you: a large garden square, framed on all four sides by identical houses of rose-coloured brick and pale stone, with covered arcades running along the ground all the way around. It's calm, symmetrical and complete in a way nothing else in the Marais is — and that's because it's the original. This is the oldest planned square in Paris, and arguably the model for the elegant city square anywhere.

It was laid out under Henri IV and inaugurated in 1612 as the Place Royale — royal by name and by intent, a piece of perfect order dropped into the medieval muddle, where the nobility could parade and duel and show themselves off. Every matching house, every arch, was built to a single design. The idea of a square as one unified composition — rather than just a gap where streets happen to meet — more or less starts here, and you can trace its descendants in grand squares right across Europe.

The name changed later, and it's the third tax story of this walk. After the Revolution, the new Republic needed money, and it renamed this square to honour the Vosges — the region of France that was first to pay its share of a new tax. A perfect aristocratic square, renamed as a gold star for prompt taxpayers.

One door is worth knowing: number six, in the corner, was Victor Hugo's home for sixteen years — the years he was writing toward Les Misérables. It's now a small museum, and it's free; you can stand in the rooms where he worked.

Our last stop is just around the corner, a couple of minutes away — the place where this whole quarter keeps its memories. The Musée Carnavalet.
