And here it is — the building that looks like it's still under construction, or has been turned completely inside out. The pompeedoo. After streets of old stone, it's a deliberate shock: a great glass-and-steel box with all its skeleton and plumbing on the outside.

That's the whole idea, and it was radical when it opened in nineteen seventy-seven. Two young architects won the competition with a building that wore its insides on its outside — every pipe, duct, beam, even the escalators, bolted onto the exterior, so that the entire interior could be left open and empty, free to be rearranged for any kind of art. And they colour-coded the outside like a diagram, so you can read the building from the street. The blue pipes carry air, the green ones water, the yellow ones electricity, and the red ones are for movement — the escalators that run up the outside, carrying people through it. Stand back and the whole facade explains itself.

It was hated at first — Parisians thought someone had abandoned a refinery in the middle of their old city — and then, slowly, loved, until it became one of the most visited buildings in the country.

One honest note. Right now you can admire it only from out here. The pompeedoo closed in twenty twenty-five for a deep, years-long renovation and isn't due to reopen until around twenty thirty; its modern-art collection has been scattered to other museums in the meantime, with a good part of it on show over at the gron palay. So today this is an architecture stop — the radical idea, read off the facade — rather than a visit inside.

From here we walk east, deeper into the old maray and its narrow streets, to a grand mansion that now holds the work of one man. The muzay peekasso.
