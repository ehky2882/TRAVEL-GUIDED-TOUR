You've just crossed from the medieval city into the plan. The first canal you came over back there — the sing ul — was the old moat; the one beside you now was dug fresh, on purpose, starting in sixteen thirteen. And the little street you're standing in is one of the seams that hold the whole design together.

The planners drew three grand canals, but grand canals are for facades, and a city also needs backs — somewhere to buy bread, mend shoes, cure leather. So between the arcs they threaded short cross-streets, nine of them in this quarter in three rows of three, and gave them over to trade. You can still read the original tenants in the names: how den straht is Hide Street, run straht is named for the oak bark tanners used, and vol ven straht and bay ren straht — Wolf Street and Bear Street — dealt in pelts. The smelly work went in the seams while the canal fronts kept their dignity. Zoning, seventeenth-century style.

Four centuries later the leather is gone and the seams have become the destination — small shops, cafés, vintage dealers, one-room boutiques — which makes the Nine Streets arguably the most pleasant aimless half hour in the city. Take it. Wander a row or two; the grid is small enough that getting lost isn't really an option.

When you're done, find the hair en grahkt again and turn right along it, keeping the water on your left, and simply follow the canal south. In ten minutes or so it begins a slow, stately curve, and the houses grow noticeably grander. That's the Golden Bend — next.
