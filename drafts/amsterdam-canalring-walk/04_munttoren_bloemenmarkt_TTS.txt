The tower ahead of you, with the clock and the crowned spire, is the munt tor en — the Mint Tower — and it's the oldest thing you've seen since the Dam. It is also, if you look at it honestly, a stump.

This was once the ruh goo leers poort, a medieval gate in the city wall: twin towers and a guardhouse holding Amsterdam's southern edge. Then the plan you've been walking through made the wall obsolete — the city simply stepped over it — and in sixteen eighteen a fire took most of the gate anyway. What survived was the base of one tower, so the city, being this city, topped the ruin with an elegant clock spire by hen drik duh kye zer and carried on. Everything you've walked since the Nine Streets lay outside the old walls. This is where you come back in — through a gate with no wall left to guard.

The name arrived later, and it's a good story. In sixteen seventy two — the year the Dutch still call the Disaster Year — French armies overran the country and cut Amsterdam off from the mints it normally used, so for a while the city simply struck its own coins here, in the guardhouse of the old gate. The Mint Tower: named for an emergency.

Now the barges. Along the sing ul beside you floats the bloo men markt, the flower market, trading on the water since eighteen sixty two, when growers sailed their stock in each morning from the fields. The barges stopped moving generations ago — they sit fixed on piles now — and these days the stalls sell as many souvenirs and packaged bulbs as fresh flowers, so take it as atmosphere rather than errand. It photographs better than it shops.

When you've had your fill, walk past the tower to the open water just beyond it. That's the river am stel — the one the Dam dammed — and it will carry us to the finish. Follow the river away from the centre, keeping the water on your left, for ten minutes or so. The white wooden drawbridge ahead is the mah guh ruh broog, the last stop.
