End of the walk, and the city has saved its gentlest trick for last.

You're on the bank of the am stel — the river this whole story began with. An hour or two ago you stood on the dam that blocked it and gave the city a name; here it runs unblocked, wide and unhurried, flowing straight out of the countryside into the middle of everything. And crossing it in front of you is the mah guh ruh broog, the Skinny Bridge: white-painted timber over a run of low arches, a double drawbridge worked by balance beams, looking more or less as bridges here looked three centuries ago.

That's deliberate. A bridge has stood on this spot since the sixteen nineties, and it has been rebuilt and widened more than once — but always, stubbornly, in wood and in the old pattern, long after every other crossing in the city went over to steel and stone. It still opens several times a day, swinging its two leaves skyward to let boats through; if your timing is lucky, you'll catch it mid-bow. The name comes with a legend attached — two sisters called mah ger, living on opposite banks, said to have paid for a bridge so they could visit each other. The duller truth is probably that the first bridge was simply skinny. The city keeps the legend anyway.

Stay for the light if you can. At dusk, more than a thousand small bulbs come on along the arches and cables, and the whole structure doubles itself in the river.

The dam started this city, and the ring you've just walked is what let it breathe. It seems right to finish on the water — the one thing here that was never planned, and the reason for everything that was.
