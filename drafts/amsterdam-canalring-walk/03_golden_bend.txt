Somewhere in the last few hundred metres, the houses started showing off. That's how you know you've arrived.

This stretch of the Herengracht — the slow curve between Leidsestraat and Vijzelstraat — is the Golden Bend, and in the second half of the seventeenth century it was the most expensive address in the city, which made it very nearly the most expensive address on earth. The plots along here were sold double-width, and the men who bought them — merchants, bankers, directors of the great trading companies — built accordingly.

Stand on one bank and read the far side like a ledger. Everywhere else on the ring, the houses follow the local code: narrow brick fronts, tall windows, a shaped gable on top like a signature. Here the code breaks. The fronts stretch wide, the brick gives way to imported stone, and the stepped gables flatten into cornices borrowed from Paris — because past a certain point of wealth, you stop building like Amsterdam and start building like Versailles.

Two details to catch while you're reading. Nearly every house still carries a hoist beam jutting from the top of its facade — a hook for winching furniture up the outside, since the staircases within were too narrow and steep for anything bigger than a person. And look closely: many fronts lean deliberately out over the street, built at a tilt so the winched goods wouldn't scrape the windows on the way up. The lean is original. These houses were designed to loom.

[beat]

One more line in the ledger. Some of these fortunes were made in trade that ran through colonies, and some through slavery — a fact the city's own museums now research and state plainly. The bend doesn't hide it; you just have to know you're reading it.

Follow the canal to the end of the curve, turn left up Vijzelstraat, and walk the short stretch to the tower with the clock and the crowned spire. That's the Munttoren, and the flower market floats just beside it.
