This square is older than the plan — older than almost everything here. Stand anywhere on the open cobbles and you're standing on the dam itself: the barrier of earth and timber that a few hundred fishing families threw across the river Amstel in the 1270s, and that gave the settlement its name. Amstelledamme — the dam on the Amstel. Say it quickly for a few centuries and you get Amsterdam.

The river is still here, by the way; it runs beneath the square in culverts and pipes. You are, quite literally, standing on top of the reason the city exists.

Now look at the Royal Palace, the great grey block filling the western side. Here's the thing to hold onto for the rest of this walk: it wasn't built as a palace. It went up in the 1650s as the town hall — a city hall so enormous and so expensive that visitors of the day talked about it as the eighth wonder of the world. No king paid for it; merchants did. That's the Amsterdam we're about to walk through — a city where the money was civic, the ambition was municipal, and even the megalomania was somehow practical.

Because by the time this building opened, the city had already burst its walls more than once, and the answer was the thing this walk is really about: a whole new Amsterdam, drawn on paper and dug as three rings of water.
