Most cities grow the way coral grows — one street at a time, nobody in charge. This walk is about the great exception.

In 1613, Amsterdam was bursting out of its medieval walls, rich on shipping and getting richer, and the city did something almost no city had done before: it drew the next Amsterdam on paper first. Three broad canals laid out in calm concentric arcs around the old town — Herengracht, Keizersgracht, Prinsengracht — with the plots marked, the rules written, and the whole thing sold off lot by lot. The world's first planned boom suburb, four centuries before anyone used words like that. Today the canal ring is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it still works the way it was drawn.

This walk crosses it. We start on the Dam, the square the city is named for, then head west into the Nine Streets, follow the ring south to the Golden Bend — where the money got loudest — then come back in past the Mint Tower and the floating flower market, and finish on the river Amstel at the Skinny Bridge, with the lights coming on if you've timed it kindly.

The route is flat — everything here is flat — compact, and genuinely walkable: about three kilometres, an hour and a half at an easy pace, longer if the shops in the Nine Streets get hold of you. They usually do.
