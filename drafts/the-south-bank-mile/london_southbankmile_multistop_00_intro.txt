For five hundred years, this was the side of the river London was ashamed of.

The south bank sat outside the City's control, and so it became the place for everything the City wanted at arm's length — the theatres, the bear-baiting pits, the brothels, the prisons, the tanneries that stank. If you wanted to see a play or misbehave, you crossed the Thames to the wrong side, and then you crossed back.

Look at it now. Over a single lifetime, this exact stretch has flipped from London's back-yard into its cultural front garden — a near-continuous mile of concert halls, theatres, galleries, and food, strung along the river on a flat, open riverside path. It's one of the best walks in the city, and it tells the story of how a whole side of London changed its mind about itself.

We'll walk it west to east, river on your left, the City glittering across the water. We start where the south bank looks straight across at the seat of power it once thumbed its nose at — by Westminster Bridge, under the gaze of Big Ben. Then we'll move through the buildings that turned this place around, one by one, finishing somewhere that's been feeding Londoners for the better part of a thousand years.

Begin at the river's edge by Westminster Bridge, looking across at Parliament.
