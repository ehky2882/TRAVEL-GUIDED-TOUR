Stand at the south end of Westminster Bridge, at the railing, and look across the water — because this is the best free view in London.

Right in front of you, across the Thames, are the Houses of Parliament and the great clock tower with Big Ben inside it, the whole golden Gothic pile reflected in the river. People pay for a lot worse than this.

Notice the colour of the bridge: it's green. That's not random — it's painted green because it sits at the end of the river nearest the House of Commons, whose benches are green. Walk upstream to Lambeth Bridge and you'll find it painted red, for the red benches of the House of Lords. The bridges are quietly colour-coded to the Parliament they sit beside.

*[beat]*

Two centuries ago the poet William Wordsworth stood on an earlier bridge here at dawn and wrote that nothing on earth could be more beautiful than London at that moment — the city asleep, the river glittering, the whole view open and silent. Come early enough and you can still catch the version he meant, before the crowds arrive. Then turn and walk along the riverside, away from the bridge, towards the giant wheel and the long curved building behind it. We're heading for County Hall.
