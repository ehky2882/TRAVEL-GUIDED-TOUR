We finish with your nose. Borough Market is a riot of food under a canopy of Victorian iron — cheese and charcuterie, fresh bread, sizzling pans, fruit stacked in pyramids, the smell of it reaching you before you arrive.

And it is properly, almost absurdly old. There's been a market trading around here for something close to a thousand years — this was the gateway to old London Bridge, for centuries the only crossing into the City, so everyone and everything funnelled through here, and where there's traffic, there's trade. The handsome ironwork you're standing under is Victorian, but the market itself is medieval in spirit, doing now what it has always done: feeding the people crossing the river.

Just beside it stands Southwark Cathedral, quietly one of the oldest church sites in London, and the whole knot of streets here — Borough, Bankside, Southwark — is where the south bank's long story began, the old "wrong side" of the river.

*[beat]*

So this is the end of the mile. You started across from Parliament, on the side of the river London was once ashamed of, and you've walked through the concert hall, the theatre, the power station turned gallery, the resurrected playhouse — the whole reinvention — to land in a market that was thriving here long before any of it, and will probably outlast all of it too. Buy something delicious. Find a spot by the river. You've earned it, and you've walked the South Bank Mile.
