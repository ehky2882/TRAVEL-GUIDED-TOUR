That vast brick building with the single square chimney is Tate Modern — and like a lot of the best things in London, it began life doing something else entirely. This was Bankside Power Station, built to burn oil and make electricity for the city.

Here's a thread worth pulling, if you've done some of these walks: it was designed by Giles Gilbert Scott — the same architect behind Battersea Power Station upriver, and the red telephone box you've photographed all over town. The man had a gift for making industrial monsters look monumental, and when the power station closed, that gift is exactly what saved it from demolition.

In the year 2000 it reopened as Tate Modern, converted by the Swiss architects Herzog and de Meuron, who left the raw industrial bones on show. Walk in through the Turbine Hall — the colossal sloping space that once held the generators, now empty enough to swallow giant artworks whole. It's free to get in, it's one of the most visited art museums on earth, and the view from the upper floors back across the river is one of the best in London.

*[beat]*

So this is the flip in its purest form: a dirty, roaring power station, made obsolete, reborn as a free cathedral of modern art that pulls in millions. Spend as long as you like inside. When you come out, head for the slender silver footbridge reaching across the river towards St Paul's — and mind your step, because it has a reputation. That's the Millennium Bridge.
