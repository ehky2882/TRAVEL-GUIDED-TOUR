The long, curved, columned building ahead, with the London Eye turning in front of it, is County Hall — and for most of the twentieth century it was the seat of London's own government.

This is where the people who actually ran London worked: first the London County Council, then the Greater London Council. And its position is pure cheek — it sits directly across the river from Parliament, staring the national government down. In the nineteen eighties that stand-off turned real: the Greater London Council, led by Ken Livingstone, was locked in a bitter feud with Margaret Thatcher's government, and at one point hung an enormous banner on this facade, broadcasting London's unemployment figures straight across the water at the politicians opposite.

Thatcher's response was blunt: in nineteen eighty-six she abolished the council altogether. London then went without any city-wide government for fourteen years. Whether that was sound economics or a political hit, people still argue.

So this grand building — now full of an aquarium and hotels and tourists queuing for the Eye — is also a monument to a real fight about who gets to run a city. Look up at that facade and picture the banner. Then carry on east along the river, because the next building is national morale made solid. We're going to the Royal Festival Hall.
