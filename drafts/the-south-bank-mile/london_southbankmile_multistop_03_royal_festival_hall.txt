The clean, horizontal, glass-fronted building ahead is the Royal Festival Hall, and it was built to cheer the country up.

Rewind to 1951. Britain was exhausted, broke, and grey, still living with rationing six years after the war. The government's answer was the Festival of Britain — a huge exhibition along this stretch of river, designed, in the phrase of the time, as "a tonic to the nation": a promise that the future could be bright, modern, and worth building. The Royal Festival Hall was its centrepiece concert hall, and it's the one major building from the whole Festival meant to last — everything else was temporary and swept away afterwards.

Go inside if it's open, even just into the foyers. They're deliberately democratic — open, free to wander, full of people reading and meeting and drifting through. That was the idea: high culture without a velvet rope, a concert hall that doubled as a public living room.

*[beat]*

This building is where the rebirth of the whole South Bank began. Everything else on this walk — the theatre, the galleries — followed the lead of this one optimistic hall, built to tell a beaten-down country that it still had a future. From here, the cluster of grey concrete terraces just ahead is the next chapter, and it's the most divisive building on the river. We're going to the National Theatre.
