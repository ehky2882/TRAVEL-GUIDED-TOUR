Step out onto the Millennium Bridge, the slim steel walkway running dead straight from Tate Modern across the temz to St Paul's. Look up as you cross: the cathedral's dome sits perfectly framed at the far end. The whole bridge was designed as a blade of light, low and sleek, deliberately kept thin so as not to block that view.

It opened in the year two thousand, London's big millennium footbridge — and on the very first day, with thousands of people crossing, it started to sway. Not a little: a real, alarming, side-to-side wobble, as the crowd's footsteps and the bridge's own natural movement fed off each other. It was nicknamed the wobbly bridge within hours, closed within days, and stayed shut for almost two years while engineers fitted dampers to steady it. It reopened in two thousand and two, and has been rock-solid ever since.

It's a lovely little parable, this bridge: the grandest, most modern gesture in town, humbled on day one by the simple physics of people walking together. Cross it for the St Paul's view if you like, then come back to the south side — because right next door is a wooden theatre that, by rights, shouldn't exist at all. We're going to Shakespeare's Globe.
