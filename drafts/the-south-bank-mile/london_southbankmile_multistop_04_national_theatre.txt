Here it is — the building Londoners can't agree on. The National Theatre, a great stack of raw grey concrete terraces stepping up from the riverside, is either a masterpiece or an eyesore, depending entirely on who you ask.

It was designed by Denys Lasdun and opened in 1976, in the style we now call Brutalism — a name that comes from the French for raw concrete, not from "brutal," though plenty of people think the second meaning fits too. Lasdun didn't think of it as a building so much as a landscape: he wanted those concrete terraces to read like geological strata, like cliffs and ledges, a piece of man-made scenery along the river.

Not everyone was charmed. The future king, then Prince of Wales, famously savaged a lot of modern architecture, and this building drew some of his sharpest lines — he likened it, more or less, to a clever way of dropping a power station into the middle of London without anyone objecting.

*[beat]*

And yet. Come back after dark, when the foyers glow gold through the glass and the terraces are lit, and the whole thing softens into something genuinely beautiful — and inside, it's one of the great theatres in the world. Whether you find it brutal or brilliant is part of the fun; this is a building that refuses to let you feel nothing about it. Walk on east, and the scale drops right down to something small and human. We're stopping at Gabriel's Wharf.
