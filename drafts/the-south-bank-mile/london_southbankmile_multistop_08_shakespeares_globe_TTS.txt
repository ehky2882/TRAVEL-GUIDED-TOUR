This round, white, timber-framed building with the thatched roof is Shakespeare's Globe — and it's a kind of resurrection. The original Globe, where Shakespeare's plays were first performed, stood near here in the late fifteen hundreds: an open-air playhouse on the disreputable south bank. It burned down in sixteen thirteen when a stage cannon set the thatch alight, was rebuilt, and then was pulled down decades later. For three centuries, there was no Globe at all.

That there's one here now is down to one stubborn man — an American actor named Sam wonamaker, who came to London, was dismayed to find only a plaque where the theatre had stood, and spent the rest of his life raising the money to build a faithful reconstruction. He died in nineteen ninety-three, four years before it opened. He never saw it finished.

Look at that thatched roof, and here's a detail that ties this walk to the rest of London: it is the only thatched roof in the whole of central London. Thatch was banned after the Great Fire of sixteen sixty-six, for fairly obvious reasons — so the Globe needed special permission, and a hidden modern sprinkler system, to be allowed one at all.

Inside, it works much as it did four hundred years ago: open to the sky, no microphones, the groundlings standing in the yard, rain and all. It's one stubborn man's gift to the city, and the truest link on this whole walk back to the rowdy, theatrical south bank we started with. From here it's a short walk inland to finish — at a place that's been feeding London for the better part of a thousand years. We're going to Borough Market.
