After all that grand culture, here's a deliberate change of scale: Gabriel's Wharf, a little courtyard of small shops, craft studios, and cafés, with painted-up building fronts and not a monument in sight.

It's easy to stroll past, but it's worth a pause for what it represents. In the nineteen eighties, developers wanted this whole stretch of riverside for office towers. The local community fought them — and, remarkably, won. The land was handed to a community group, and instead of corporate blocks you got affordable homes, gardens, and this cluster of independent traders. Gabriel's Wharf exists because ordinary residents refused to be cleared off their own riverfront.

So between the great cultural cathedrals on either side, this scruffy, friendly little corner is the South Bank's reminder that a riverside is also somewhere people live. Grab a coffee, poke around the studios. Then carry on east towards the unmistakable brick chimney rising ahead — one of the most famous art galleries in the world, in a building that used to make electricity. We're going to Tate Modern.
