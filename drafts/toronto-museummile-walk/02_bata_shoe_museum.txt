You've walked a couple of blocks west, to the corner of Bloor and St. George, and here's a very different kind of museum building. Look at its shape: a low box of pale limestone with the roofline tilted, just slightly, as though a lid had been set down on top and not quite pushed flush. That's deliberate. The whole building is designed to look like a shoebox with the lid a little askew.

This is the Bata Shoe Museum, and the joke of its architecture is also its subject. It holds the largest collection of footwear in the world — something like twelve thousand pieces, spanning four and a half thousand years, from ancient sandals to the shoes of the famous. It grew out of one woman's private collection: Sonja Bata, of the global shoe-making family, who spent decades gathering shoes as a way of reading whole cultures through what they put on their feet.

The architect, Raymond Moriyama, was one of the finest this country produced, and rather than build a temple, he built a container — even the limestone was chosen because it has the warm, soft look of leather. Where the ROM up the street shouts for your attention, this one makes a quiet, witty little bow: a museum disguised as the most ordinary object in your closet.

From here, cross the street and head south, down into the tree-lined heart of the University of Toronto, toward the wide green circle at its centre. That's King's College Circle.
