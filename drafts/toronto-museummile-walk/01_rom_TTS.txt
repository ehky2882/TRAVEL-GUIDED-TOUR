Stand back on the corner of Bloor Street and Avenue Road and look at what's happened to this building. A stately old stone museum — and bursting out of its side, tilting over the sidewalk at angles that look almost dangerous, a huge jagged form of glass and brushed aluminum, like a shard of ice driven into the old stone. That's the michael lee chin crystal, and it is probably the most argued-about building in the country.

The museum behind it is a century old — the Royal Ontario Museum opened in nineteen fourteen, Canada's largest, with something like eighteen million objects: dinosaurs, meteorites, mummies, and one of the world's great collections of fossils. For most of that century it looked exactly like what it was, a dignified temple of learning. Then, in two thousand and seven, came the Crystal.

The architect was daniel leebeskind, and the story goes that he sketched the first version on a paper napkin at a wedding held inside the museum — five interlocking crystal forms, inspired, he said, by the gem collection downstairs. When it opened, a great many Torontonians hated it, loudly and at length; one American critic called it the worst building of its decade. Others defended it just as fiercely, and both camps are still going.

Here's the thing to notice, and it's the key to the whole walk. By the time the Crystal went up, museums all over the world had figured out that a wild building could put them on the map overnight — and the rom wanted in. So it let a famous outsider drive a shard through its heritage wall and dare the city to look away. Nobody has managed to look away yet.
