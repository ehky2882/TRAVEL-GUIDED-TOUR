You're standing at the foot of University Avenue, looking up at a long, heavy palace of pink sandstone — turrets, round arches, deep-set windows. This is Queen's Park, home of the Ontario Legislature, and it closes off the top of the avenue like a full stop at the end of a very grand sentence.

It opened in 1893, built from pink stone quarried west of the city and carved, block by block, right here on the lawn. The style is all rounded arches and rough stone, and at the time plenty of locals grumbled that it looked too American. But the building you see isn't quite the one that opened. On a September afternoon in 1909, a spark from a workman's fire-pot on the roof set the west wing alight; staff formed a human chain at the doors of the legislative chamber and saved it, but the wing and the library behind it — a hundred thousand books — were lost.

The man brought in to rebuild the west wing was E.J. Lennox, the same architect who left his fingerprints all over Victorian Toronto, including the old courthouse downtown. He rebuilt it a little taller and lined the inside in pale Italian marble — so today, if you climb the grand staircase within, the east wing is dark Victorian oak and the west wing is bright stone, a fire-line running right through the middle of the building.

[beat]

Like the university behind you, this place burned and rose again — and it's about to be remade one more time: the whole building is due for a deep restoration, with the legislators to be moved, temporarily, somewhere the province has been oddly quiet about naming.

For the last stop, walk back down the length of University Avenue and turn west onto Dundas Street, to a gallery wrapped in glass and wood. That's the Art Gallery of Ontario.
