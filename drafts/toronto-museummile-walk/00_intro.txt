If the last walk was about money and power, this one is about knowledge — and it runs in a rough line north to south, from a museum full of dinosaurs to a gallery remade by one of the most famous architects who ever lived.

You're starting up at Bloor Street, in the cluster of museums and university buildings that Toronto has quietly packed into a few leafy blocks around Queen's Park. Five stops: two museums, the provincial legislature, the old university at the heart of it all, and a great art gallery to finish. It's the gentlest walk of the set — flat, shaded, mostly through a university campus that most of the city forgets is even here.

Here's the thread to carry. Almost every building on this walk is an old institution that decided, at some point, to remake itself — and the two that bookend the route show the opposite ways a city can do that. It opens with a museum that had a giant glass crystal smashed into its side by a famous outsider, a building half of Toronto openly hated. It ends with a gallery that a local boy — who first fell in love with art and buildings inside its walls as a child — came home, decades later, to rebuild gently, from the inside, in wood and glass, and that almost everyone adores.

Rupture at one end, homecoming at the other. Start with the rupture.
