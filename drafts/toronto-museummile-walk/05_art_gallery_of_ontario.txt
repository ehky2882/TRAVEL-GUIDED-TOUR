You've come down University Avenue and west along Dundas to the corner of McCaul, and running above you is a long, curving wall of glass and warm wood — like the upturned hull of a wooden boat stretched the length of a city block. Step under it. This is the Art Gallery of Ontario, and it's where the walk's argument lands.

The gallery grew, more than a century ago, out of a single donated house behind it — the Grange — and has expanded many times since. But the version you're standing in was shaped, in 2008, by Frank Gehry: one of the most famous architects who ever lived, the man behind the wildest, shiniest buildings of his era. This one is different from all of them, for one reason — Frank Gehry was born in Toronto. As a boy he came to this very gallery, and later said it was here he first felt the pull between art and building. This was his first work in his own country: a homecoming.

So instead of a titanium spectacle, he did something quieter. That wooden gallery over the street is the Galleria Italia — glass and Douglas fir, close to two hundred metres of it — and inside, a sculptural staircase spirals up and bursts through the roof. Critics who braced for Gehry to shout called it, instead, his most relaxed and graceful building: restraint, from the least restrained architect alive.

[beat]

Frank Gehry died in late 2025, at ninety-six, which leaves this building as a kind of farewell — the local boy's last gift to the city that first showed him what a building could do. And it's the right place to end. The walk began at the ROM with a rupture: a stranger driving a glass shard through an old wall and daring you to look. It ends here with a homecoming: someone remaking the place he loved from the inside, in wood, so gently you could almost miss it. Two ways to keep an old city alive — and Toronto, being Toronto, kept them both, a few blocks apart.

Stay a while, and walk back and forth beneath the wood. It's the warmest ceiling in the city.
