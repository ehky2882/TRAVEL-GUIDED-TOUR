You've come into King's College Circle, a big oval of lawn ringed by old stone buildings, and the first thing to notice is how quiet it is — you're in the geographic centre of downtown, and you can hear birds. This is the historic heart of the University of Toronto, almost two hundred years old, and in a very Toronto way it spends most of its time hiding in plain sight behind the office towers.

Nearly everything on this walk grew out of this campus. The university leased the land the provincial legislature now sits on; it ran the ROM in its early years; the whole strip of learning you're walking is, in a sense, the university's overflow. And it has a long habit of remaking itself: University College, the grand building on the west side, burned nearly to the ground in a fire in 1890 and was rebuilt.

It just did it again, underground. The lawn near you was recently torn up, the cars sent below ground, and in their place the university drilled hundreds of wells straight down into the bedrock — some two hundred and fifty metres deep, half the height of the CN Tower — to build the largest urban geothermal field in the country, quietly heating the century-old buildings around you.

[beat]

No shard of glass, no famous name on it — the oldest institution here reinvented itself by digging down and going silent. Beneath it all runs a buried creek the campus is slowly bringing back to the surface, a nod to the older meeting place this land has always been.

Now walk east, out of the circle and across to the pink stone palace at the foot of University Avenue. That's Queen's Park, the Ontario Legislature.
