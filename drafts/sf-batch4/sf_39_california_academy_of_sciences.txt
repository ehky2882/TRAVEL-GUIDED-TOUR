Step back far enough to see the roof, because the roof is the whole idea. It rolls and humps in a series of low green hills, planted edge to edge with living plants, with round skylights set into it like bubbles. That is a living roof — about two and a half acres of native California plants growing on top of the building — and it tells you exactly what kind of museum this is: one that would rather be part of nature than just describe it.

This is the California Academy of Sciences, and the trick of it is what's packed underneath that roof. Most cities make you choose. You go to the aquarium, or the planetarium, or the natural history museum, or the rainforest, on four different days. Here they're all in one building, under one roof. There's a full aquarium below your feet, a planetarium dome you can sit back and watch the universe in, a natural history collection, and — the showstopper — a four-story rainforest sealed inside a giant glass sphere, warm and humid and full of free-flying butterflies and birds, that you climb on a spiral ramp.

The institution itself is old — founded in 1853, which makes it one of the oldest scientific bodies in the West — but this building is new. It was rebuilt in 2008 by the architect Renzo Piano, and that roof isn't just for looks. The hills are shaped to draw warm air up and out through those round skylights, cooling the building without much air conditioning, and the plants soak up rainwater that would otherwise run off. The roof is doing real work.

*[beat]*

There's a nice honesty to it, when you think about what a science museum is for. This one doesn't lecture you about the environment from behind glass and then guzzle power doing it. It tries, in its own structure, to behave the way it's telling you to behave — a building that grows a meadow on its head, cools itself with hot air, drinks the rain, and keeps a rainforest breathing inside a bubble. Go in. Look up through the glass sphere at the canopy, then go down and watch the same water in the tanks below. It's the rare museum that's also an argument.
