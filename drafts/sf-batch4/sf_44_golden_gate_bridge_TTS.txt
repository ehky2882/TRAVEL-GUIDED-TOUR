Here it is — the one you came for. And the first thing worth knowing about the Golden Gate Bridge is that almost everyone said it couldn't be built. The strait it crosses is deep, the current is ferocious, the water is freezing, and the fog and wind come howling in off the open Pacific. For years the conventional wisdom was that no bridge could stand here. They built it anyway, and it opened in nineteen thirty-seven.

Look at the towers first. They rise about seven hundred and forty-six feet above the water, and the main span between them stretches a little over four thousand two hundred feet — for nearly thirty years after it opened, the longest suspension span in the world. Everything hangs from those two great cables sweeping between the towers; the whole roadway is essentially dangling from steel rope.

Now the color, because the color was a fight. It's a specific shade called International Orange, and it was not the plan. The Navy wanted the bridge painted in black and yellow stripes so ships could see it. Others pushed for ordinary gray. But the consulting architect, Irving Morrow, noticed that the steel was arriving coated in a burnt-orange primer, and argued hard that this was the color — warm, glowing against the cool blues and grays of the water and sky, and oddly easy to see in fog. He won. The most famous color in American engineering started as a rust-proof undercoat that one man refused to paint over.

One more thing, looking toward the south end. Tucked underneath the bridge down there is Fort Point, a brick fortress from the Civil War era. The bridge's engineers could have simply demolished it. Instead, the chief engineer, Joseph Strauss, threw a special steel arch over the top of it so the old fort could stay. So the bridge actually bends around a building older than itself.

So stand here for a while and take it in. The fog may be pouring through the cables, or the whole thing may be glowing clear in the sun — both are the real bridge. And know that nearly everything you're admiring was an argument someone had to win: that it could be built at all, that it should be orange, that the old fort beneath it was worth bending steel to save. It's not just beautiful. It's a stack of stubborn good decisions, six hundred feet tall.
