It looks like a Roman ruin that somehow drifted up against a San Francisco lagoon — a great domed rotunda the color of old sand, a sweeping colonnade, all of it weathered and romantic and clearly very old. Here's the joke: it was built to look that way on purpose, and it isn't old at all.

This is the Palace of Fine Arts, and it went up in nineteen fifteen for the Panama-Pacific International Exposition — a world's fair San Francisco threw partly to celebrate the opening of the Panama Canal, and partly to announce to the world that the city had fully recovered from the nineteen oh six earthquake. The fair was a temporary fantasy city of palaces along the waterfront, and this was its most beloved building. The architect, Bernard maybek, didn't want it to feel triumphant. He wanted it to feel a little sad — the beautiful melancholy of a classical ruin, the sense of something grand slowly decaying. So he designed a brand-new building to look like a magnificent wreck.

Look closely at the colonnade and you'll catch the detail that gives the game away. On top of the columns are large urns, and around them, women carved with their backs turned outward, leaning over as if weeping into the planters. maybek meant them as a meditation on the sadness of life without art. A fake ruin, built to mourn.

And here's the lovely twist. The whole fair was meant to be torn down when it ended, and almost all of it was. But San Franciscans loved this melancholy ruin so much they couldn't bear to lose it. It was patched and propped up for decades, and finally, in the nineteen sixties, completely rebuilt in permanent materials so it would last. A building designed to look like it was crumbling refused to actually crumble, because the city wouldn't let it.

So walk the colonnade, sit by the lagoon with the swans and the wedding photographers, and enjoy the strangeness of it. You're standing in a deliberate illusion — a hundred-year-old monument to the idea of ruins, made to look ancient, built to be temporary, and saved out of pure affection. It's the most sincere fake in San Francisco, and one of the most beautiful spots in the city to do nothing at all.
