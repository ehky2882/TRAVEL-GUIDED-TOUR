Down below you, at the edge of the surf, are the ruins. Roofless concrete walls, a grid of rectangular pools now filled with green tidewater and gulls, the bones of something enormous half-returned to the sea. People wander through them today as if they were ancient. They're a little over a century old, and what stood here was one of the most ambitious public pleasures any American city ever built.

This was the Sutro Baths. In 1896 a man named Adolph Sutro — a self-made mining millionaire who would go on to be mayor — opened on this spot a glass-and-iron palace the size of a stadium, filled with swimming pools. Not one or two. Seven of them, most fed straight from the ocean by the tides, ranging from cold seawater to heated, under a vast greenhouse roof. The place could hold something like ten thousand people at once. And Sutro's whole point was that it should be for everyone — cheap enough that an ordinary working family, not just the rich, could come spend a day swimming at the edge of the Pacific.

It never quite paid for itself, the public's tastes changed, and in 1966, as it was being demolished for redevelopment, the whole structure burned. What's left is what you see: the concrete footprint, slowly losing its argument with the ocean.

Just up the bluff is the Cliff House, the big pale building that for generations was the restaurant out at the end of the world. It's been closed since 2020 and its future is uncertain, so don't count on a meal — but the setting is still extraordinary. And the real treasure is the trail from here: the Lands End path, winding along the cliffs through cypress trees, with the Golden Gate Bridge swinging into view and the wrecks of old ships sometimes showing at low tide.

*[beat]*

It's a melancholy, beautiful corner, and worth the wind. Stand at the railing over the ruins and picture it full — the glass roof, the steam, ten thousand people laughing in the surf, a rich man's gift to everyone who couldn't afford the sea. Then look at the tide working through the empty pools. Few places hold the whole arc of a grand idea — the dream, the crowds, the slow return to water — as plainly as this one does.
