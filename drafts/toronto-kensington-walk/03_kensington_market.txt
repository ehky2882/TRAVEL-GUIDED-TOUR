You've turned off Spadina into Kensington Market, and you can feel the scale change immediately: the streets narrow, the awnings drop low, and the shops spill their produce and racks and crates straight onto the sidewalk.

Read the market as layers and it opens right up. It began in the early 1900s as a Jewish market — pushcarts and front-room shops — and two synagogues from that era still stand in the streets around you. Then came the Portuguese, then Caribbean, then Vietnamese and Latin American and East African arrivals, each adding a layer of food and shopfronts and language without erasing the one underneath. The fishmonger, the taqueria, the vintage rack, and the cheese shop end up sharing the same half-block, and somehow it works.

Two things have kept it that way. First, the streets themselves: they were laid out for handcarts and foot traffic, with almost no parking, which makes the market structurally hostile to chain stores and towers — they simply can't fit. Second, people fought for it. In the 1960s a planning proposal called for flattening the small houses and putting up apartment blocks, and the neighbourhood organised and killed it. It was later named a National Historic Site in 2006, and then, in 2025, a Heritage Conservation District — with the city's own plan naming the market's sense of anarchy, inclusivity, and experimentation as the very thing worth protecting.

There's a lovely contradiction in that: rules written to protect a place whose whole character is that it resists being tidied.

Kensington is the purest version of everything on this walk — a neighbourhood made entirely by addition, layer over layer, and never once declared finished.

When you've had your fill, make your way back down to Spadina and south toward Queen. Just below Queen, between Spadina and Portland, is a lane that took this same habit and made it literal. They call it Graffiti Alley.
