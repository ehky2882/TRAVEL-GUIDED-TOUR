You've come down to the lane officially named Rush Lane, running parallel to Queen just south of it. If the subway works around Queen and Spadina have the usual way in blocked off, you'll have come at it from the side, off Richmond — the city has been digging a new line through here, and the access shifts with the construction.

Now look at the walls. Three blocks, about a kilometre, of near-continuous murals, floor to roofline, and the key thing to understand is that almost none of it is permanent. The lane is repainted constantly, new work going up over old, so the wall in front of you didn't look like this a year ago and won't a year from now. One long mural crams the whole city onto a single wall — the tower, the streetcars, the raccoons that treat this place as theirs.

Here's the twist that makes the lane worth the detour. It isn't a legal wall. It's tolerated. For years the city ticketed graffiti here as vandalism, and then, in 2011, it reversed course, designated the lane an area of municipal significance, and set up a program to fund murals outright. The thing the city once fined, it now protects and pays for — a sanctioned wall of the unsanctioned.

Which is the walk's habit again, only now you can watch it happen in paint: layer over layer, nothing finished, the newest arrival painted right on top of the last.

Climb back up to the daylight of Queen Street West and turn west. The last stop is that same habit stretched thin over a couple of kilometres.
