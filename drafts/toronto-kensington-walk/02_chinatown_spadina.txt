You've reached the corner of Dundas and Spadina — Spadina, when it's the avenue, said spa-dye-na — one of the widest streets downtown, streetcars running down the centre, Chinese signage in every direction.

This is Chinatown, but it's worth knowing it isn't the first one. Toronto's original Chinatown sat east of here, in the old immigrant district called the Ward, near where City Hall stands today. In the late 1950s and through the 1960s, roughly two-thirds of it was expropriated and cleared to build New City Hall and the square in front of it — the same square you may have crossed earlier on this trip.

The community did something quietly remarkable: it moved west, to Spadina and Dundas, and rebuilt. And when the city floated plans to clear that too, a restaurateur named Jean Lumb organised the Save Chinatown Committee and fought it to a standstill. Lumb went on to become the first Chinese-Canadian woman appointed to the Order of Canada, in part for that work.

So the reframe here is almost the whole walk in one story. A neighbourhood was erased in one place and simply re-grew two blocks over — subtraction answered, stubbornly, with addition. It's still changing today, as it always has; newer arrivals, rising rents, the usual pressures of a downtown that everyone wants a piece of. Chinatown has never once stopped being remade.

From here, walk north up Spadina and duck west into the narrow streets — Augusta, Baldwin, Kensington Avenue. That's Kensington Market.
