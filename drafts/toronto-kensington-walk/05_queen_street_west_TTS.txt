You're up on Queen Street West now, the streetcar rattling past — the Queen car, one of the longest streetcar routes in North America — with boutiques and cafes and record shops running west as far as you can see.

Queen West runs on one simple, ruthless pattern: cool migrates. A stretch gets discovered, gets interesting, gets expensive, and the genuinely interesting stuff moves one block further west to where the rent is still cheap — and then that block gets discovered, and it happens again. This street has been doing it for decades, block after block, which is how a former garment district ended up being named, at one point, the second-coolest neighbourhood in the world by a fashion magazine — a ranking Toronto accepted with its usual faint embarrassment.

And yet it hasn't fully smoothed out. There's a grittiness gentrification keeps failing to scrub off: the live-music dive bars, the stacked vintage shops, the independent bookstore near Trinity Bellwoods holding its ground. The next layer arrives, but the last one doesn't entirely leave.

Which is the whole walk, in the end. Step back and look at what you've crossed: a museum that grew around a two hundred year old house, a chinatown that regrew after it was cleared, a market built of stacked newcomers, a lane repainted over and over, and now a street where cool keeps adding the next block west. Five versions of a single stubborn habit.

Most of downtown gets torn down and rebuilt. Out here, the city just keeps getting added to, and you've spent the last hour walking the proof.

So stay on the corner a moment before you go. Watch the streetcar, watch the street. Whatever it is right now, it isn't finished — and out here, that has always been the point.
