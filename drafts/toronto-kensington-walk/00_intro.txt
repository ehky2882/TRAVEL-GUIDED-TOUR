Most of downtown Toronto was built the way you'd expect a city to build itself: clear the old thing, put up the new one, and repeat until the skyline looks modern. The stretch you're about to walk did almost the opposite.

You should be standing on Dundas Street West at the corner of McCaul, with the long, low galleries of the Art Gallery of Ontario in front of you and the office towers of downtown at your back. Everything ahead — west, and a little north — is what the city loosely calls the immigrant west: Chinatown along Spadina, the tangle of Kensington Market, a painted lane below Queen, and the long mile of Queen Street West beyond it.

Here's the thread to hold onto for the next hour. This is the part of Toronto that grew by addition rather than replacement. Wave after wave of newcomers arrived and added a layer without scraping off the one before, so the neighbourhoods out here read less like finished places and more like something still being assembled, layer over layer, in public.

The good news for your feet: it's flat, dense, and genuinely walkable, maybe forty-five minutes of actual walking, with one short stretch back south in the middle. If you tire, the Queen and Spadina streetcars cover most of it.

We'll go from the gallery west to Chinatown on Spadina, up into Kensington Market, back down to the lane they call Graffiti Alley, and out onto Queen West to finish.

Start where the habit is easiest to see, with a museum that grew exactly the way the neighbourhoods around it did. Turn to the Art Gallery of Ontario.
