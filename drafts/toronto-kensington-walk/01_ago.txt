You're standing in front of the Art Gallery of Ontario, its entrance set back from Dundas behind a long glass-and-wood front. Most people come for what's inside. For this walk, the interesting thing is how the building got here.

Walk your eye to the older brick house tucked behind and within the complex. That's the Grange, built in 1817, and it's the oldest surviving brick house in Toronto. It was left to the gallery in 1909, and here's the part worth carrying with you: the entire museum grew outward from that one house. Expansion after expansion wrapped around it over more than a century, and rather than tear the old house down, the gallery kept it, folded inside the galleries like a seed still sitting in the fruit.

The collection was assembled the same way, layer by layer. The gallery holds the most complete collection of the Group of Seven anywhere on earth, the largest collection of Henry Moore's sculpture in the world — Moore chose to send it here himself — and a major gift of Canadian and European art from the Thomson family. There's a dedicated centre where Indigenous and Canadian works are hung to speak to each other. None of it replaced what came before; it was all added to it.

Which makes this gallery a small model of the walk ahead. It didn't reinvent itself by clearing the ground. It accreted, around one stubborn old house, keeping every layer.

When you leave, head west along Dundas for two blocks, until the signage turns to Chinese and the street opens out at Spadina. That's Chinatown.
