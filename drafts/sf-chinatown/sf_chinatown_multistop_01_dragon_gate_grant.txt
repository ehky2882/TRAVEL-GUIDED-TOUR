You should be standing in front of the Dragon Gate, looking north up Grant Avenue into Chinatown. Three openings, a green tiled roof curling up at the corners, and a pair of stone lions standing guard. This is the front door, and it's worth a second look before you walk through it.

Start with the gate itself. It's younger than you'd think — it went up in 1970, a gift from Taiwan, modeled on the ceremonial gateways you'd find at the entrance to a Chinese village. Read the inscription up top if you like; it's a line attributed to Sun Yat-sen, the founder of modern China, and the gist is that everything under heaven is for the good of all. Grand words for a tourist arch — but they suit a neighborhood that has always been about more than it looks.

Now look up the street. Grant Avenue is the oldest street in San Francisco — this was the main road of the original Yerba Buena settlement, back before the city had a name. The Chinatown strung along it, the lanterns between the buildings, the pagoda cornices, the dragon-wrapped lamp posts, almost all of it dates from after 1906. Remember the idea from the start of the walk: this look was chosen. Before the fire, Chinatown was mostly plain brick. The version you're walking into was rebuilt to be seen.

[beat]

One small thing to carry with you. The dragon lamp posts down Grant were installed in the 1920s, and they were genuinely bold for their moment — a Chinese-American neighborhood insisting, in public, in the middle of an era of brutal exclusion laws, that it belonged here and intended to stay.

When you're ready, walk a few blocks up Grant and turn right onto Waverly Place — a narrow side street that's about to ask you to look up.
