You're in Ross Alley now, a narrow, slightly crooked lane that's the oldest alley in Chinatown. It's barely wide enough for the light to reach the ground, which is exactly why filmmakers love it — it's stood in for old Chinatown in more movies than its residents would probably like. But you didn't come down here for the scenery. You came for the smell: sweet, warm, vanilla, drifting out of a doorway on your right.

That's the Golden Gate Fortune Cookie Factory, and it's tiny — a couple of women at small round griddles, folding cookies by hand, the way it's been done for decades. Step in if there's room. You'll watch a flat, hot disc of batter get a paper fortune laid across it and folded into that familiar shape in about two seconds, faster than you can follow.

Now here's the twist, and it's a good one. The fortune cookie — the single most "Chinese" thing in the whole American imagination — almost certainly isn't Chinese at all. The best evidence traces it back to Japanese rice crackers, and to Japanese-American families in California who were serving a version of it around the early nineteen hundreds. San Francisco and Los Angeles both claim to have invented it, and in nineteen eighty-three the two cities staged a mock trial to settle the argument; San Francisco "won," which tells you about as much as a mock trial should.

So the cookie in your hand is really an immigrant story folded into a snack — invented by one community, adopted by another, sold to everyone, and claimed by a city. Very San Francisco.

Eat the cookie. Then we'll walk the last short stretch over to portsmuth Square — the spot where this whole city began.
