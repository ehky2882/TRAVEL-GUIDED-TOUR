And here we are at the end of the walk, at portsmuth Square — except, right now, you're standing at the edge of a construction site. Fences, hoarding, the hum of machinery. The square closed in June twenty twenty-six for a two-year rebuild and won't reopen until twenty twenty-eight, so I'm going to ask you to do something a little strange: stand here at the fence, on karnee or up at the Clay Street corner, and picture it.

Because of everywhere we've walked today, this is the most important ground. This is where San Francisco began. It was the central plaza of a small settlement called yairba bwayna, and in eighteen forty-six a U S Navy captain came ashore and raised the American flag right here, the first time it flew over the town. The place was renamed San Francisco the next year. California's first public school opened on the southwest corner in eighteen forty-eight. The whole city radiated outward from this one square.

And then it became something else — the heart of Chinatown. Chinatown's living room, people call it. For generations this was where elders played mahjong and Chinese chess in the sun, where kids ran the play structure, where the neighborhood gathered to celebrate and, when it had to, to protest. It's the civic center of the city within the city we've been walking through.

That's what the rebuild is protecting, not replacing. They're pulling down the ugly nineteen sixties footbridge over karnee to open the views back up, adding a stage, a new clubhouse, canopies built to carry lanterns at Lunar New Year. The old monuments are in storage, waiting to come home. So picture the square full again — because it will be. You've just walked the oldest Chinatown in North America, a neighborhood that was told to move and refused, and you're ending where the city itself first stood up. It's still here. It's just catching its breath.
