And here we are at the end of the walk, at Portsmouth Square — except, right now, you're standing at the edge of a construction site. Fences, hoarding, the hum of machinery. The square closed in June 2026 for a two-year rebuild and won't reopen until 2028, so I'm going to ask you to do something a little strange: stand here at the fence, on Kearny or up at the Clay Street corner, and picture it.

Because of everywhere we've walked today, this is the most important ground. This is where San Francisco began. It was the central plaza of a small settlement called Yerba Buena, and in 1846 a U.S. Navy captain came ashore and raised the American flag right here, the first time it flew over the town. The place was renamed San Francisco the next year. California's first public school opened on the southwest corner in 1848. The whole city radiated outward from this one square.

And then it became something else — the heart of Chinatown. Chinatown's living room, people call it. For generations this was where elders played mahjong and Chinese chess in the sun, where kids ran the play structure, where the neighborhood gathered to celebrate and, when it had to, to protest. It's the civic center of the city within the city we've been walking through.

That's what the rebuild is protecting, not replacing. They're pulling down the ugly 1960s footbridge over Kearny to open the views back up, adding a stage, a new clubhouse, canopies built to carry lanterns at Lunar New Year. The old monuments are in storage, waiting to come home. So picture the square full again — because it will be. You've just walked the oldest Chinatown in North America, a neighborhood that was told to move and refused, and you're ending where the city itself first stood up. It's still here. It's just catching its breath.
