Before you take a step, here's the idea that makes this walk worth doing. You're about to walk through the oldest Chinatown in North America — and almost everything you'll find charming about it, the curved roofs, the dragon lamps, the color, was a deliberate act of self-defense.

Here's why. In nineteen oh six, the earthquake and the fire that followed leveled this neighborhood completely, and a lot of powerful people in San Francisco saw an opportunity: clear the Chinese residents out, push them to the edge of the city, and take this valuable downtown-adjacent land for themselves. The community fought back — and they fought back, in part, with architecture. Merchants and their architects rebuilt Chinatown on the same ground, but flashier, more theatrical, more "Chinese" than it had ever actually looked, on purpose. The bet was simple: make it a place the whole city wants to visit, an attraction too valuable to bulldoze, and you make it permanent. It worked. What looks like decoration is really a survival strategy a century old.

So as we walk, look past the souvenir shops and read the neighborhood as what it is: a city within the city that refused to be moved.

The good news for your feet: this is one of the flattest, tightest walks in San Francisco. Four stops, a handful of blocks, no real hills until the very end. We'll start at the grand front door, work our way up Grant and into the back alleys where the real life is, and finish in the square where the whole city was born.

Let's begin at the Dragon Gate, at the corner of Grant Avenue and Bush Street, the southern entrance to Chinatown. Make your way there, and meet me at the gate.
