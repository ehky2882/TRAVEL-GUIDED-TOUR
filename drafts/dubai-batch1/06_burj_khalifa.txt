The tallest structure human beings have ever built is directly ahead of you — and the strange part is how your eye keeps losing the top of it.

You should be in Burj Park, the small island of green in the middle of Downtown's lake, with the water between you and the base of the Burj Khalifa. This is the vantage the photographers use, because it's one of the few places close enough to feel the scale and still far enough back to see all of it at once.

Let your eye climb it. The tower rises in a spiral of setbacks — a three-lobed plan, modelled loosely on a desert flower, that narrows as it goes. Eight hundred and twenty-eight metres, opened in 2010, and the spiral is not decoration: at that height the wind will organise itself into rhythmic gusts strong enough to shake a building, so the shape keeps changing to confuse it. The tower is, quite literally, arguing with the sky.

It has held the world title since 2010, and a taller one is perpetually promised somewhere — one is rising across the peninsula now. The record will pass eventually, because records do. What won't pass is the idea underneath, and the idea is older than the tower. In the late nineteen fifties, before the oil money, the ruler Sheikh Rashid borrowed heavily to dredge the creek — to make the harbour deeper than the day's trade required, betting the trade would come. It came. Build the thing before it's needed, and the need arrives: that doctrine has run through the port, the airline, and the islands ever since, and it reached its full height here, in a tower planned before anyone was asking for it.

And notice the water you're standing beside. On the creek, the water came first and the city grew out of it. Here, the city came first and built itself the water. The order reversed; the bet, exactly the same.

[beat]

Before you leave, find the very top one more time — the spire, where the building finally lets go of the ground. It's a hard tower to finish looking at. That may be the point.
