The best purchase in Dubai is not in a mall. It costs a coin or two, lasts about five minutes, and comes with a wooden bench, a diesel heartbeat and the best view of the old city that money can buy — because almost no money is involved.

You should be at the ah brah station on the boor doo bye bank of the creek, beside the Textile sook, watching low wooden boats swing in and out of the dock. Each ah brah carries about twenty passengers, seated back to back on a single bench down the middle, open to the water on all sides.

Watch the boats work first. There's no timetable — an ah brah leaves when its bench is full, which at busy hours means every minute or two. You pay the driver directly, coins passed hand to hand once the boat is moving, while he steers with one arm draped over the tiller. This is public transport older than every road in the city, and it operates on a system that has never needed improving: turn up, sit down, cross.

Out on the water, look both ways. day rah on one bank, boor doo bye on the other — the two halves of the original town, facing each other across the reason either of them exists. This creek is why there's a city here at all: a safe anchorage on a harsh coast, deep enough for trading boats, defensible, fishable. Everything you can see from the middle of this crossing — and everything you can't, every tower and highway and man-made island for fifty kilometres — is downstream of this water.

That's what you're really buying for your coin. Not a ferry ride; a seat at the city's point of origin, on a boat that has outlasted every skyline built around it.

When you land on the far side, the sooks of day rah are waiting. But there's no law that says you have to be going anywhere. Some people ride back and forth a few times just to stay on the water — and honestly, they've understood the place better than most.
