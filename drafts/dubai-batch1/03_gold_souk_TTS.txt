There's a gold ring in a window near the main gate that weighs more than most of the people photographing it.

You should be standing at the wooden entrance arch on the day rah side of the market, where the sign overhead reads Dubai City of Gold. Ahead of you, a covered arcade runs deep into the block — a latticed timber roof, ceiling fans turning, and beneath them shopfront after shopfront of yellow metal, window glass packed floor to ceiling.

Find the ring first. A few doors in, at a shop called Kanz Jewels, the naj mat tie bah — the Star of tie bah — sits behind glass with its Guinness certificate propped beside it. Sixty-four kilograms of twenty-one carat gold, worked by fifty-five goldsmiths over forty-five days, and it has never been for sale. It exists purely to be looked at, which in this market counts as honest work.

Now look at everything else. The windows hold necklaces built like armour, bangles stacked by the hundred, chains sold off the spool by length, bridal sets heavy enough to anchor a rowing boat. But here is the quiet fact underneath the sparkle: the gold itself is priced by weight, at the day's rate, and the rate is the same in every window — posted, public, not up for discussion. What you negotiate is the making charge, the craftsmanship laid on top. For all the theatre, this is a commodities market wearing a bazaar's clothes.

And the trade is older than the skyline. Dubai was moving gold across the sea — to Iran, to India — long before the oil and the towers, and it earned the name City of Gold decades before there was a skyline to hang it on. Lately the city has made the nickname official: this whole quarter is now branded the Dubai Gold District, with plans announced for a street finished in real gold. You could call that investment, or you could call it costume — the market underneath carries on either way.

Come back at dusk if you can. That's when the arcade fills, the windows blaze, and the whole lane turns the colour of the thing it sells.
