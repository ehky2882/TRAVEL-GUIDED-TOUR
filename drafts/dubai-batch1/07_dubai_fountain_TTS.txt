Every half hour after dark, this lake goes to work.

You should be on the waterfront promenade between the mall and the water, with the boorj khah lee fah rising on the far side and the lake spread flat in front of you. It looks ornamental. It's an instrument — one of the largest choreographed fountain systems anywhere, resting between performances — and the lake itself covers about twelve hectares of what was open desert within living memory.

Face the water. Under the surface, arcs of nozzles run the length of the lake, and when the show starts they send columns of water as high as one hundred and fifty metres — higher than most towers in most cities — timed to music and wrapped in light. Each performance lasts about five minutes, and in the evening they come every half hour, each one choreographed differently: Arabic classics, opera, film scores. If you don't like the programme, wait thirty minutes. There are a couple of afternoon performances as well, but the fountain is a nocturnal creature — the show is water and light in equal measure, and it needs the dark to do its full work.

The studio behind it is the same Californian team that made the fountains at the beh lah jee oh in Las Vegas — and this was the bigger commission. The engineering is theatrical on purpose: the tallest shots are launched with compressed air, so the water moves faster than the eye expects water to move, which is why a crowd gasps before it has decided to.

Now look away from the lake for a moment and look at the railing. Every language on the promenade is standing at it. In a city that sells tickets to nearly everything — the tower behind the lake included — the best five minutes in Downtown are free, public, and repeated all evening. That's rarer here than it sounds, and the crowd is the proof: for the length of one song, several thousand strangers are watching exactly the same thing, and nobody paid.

Stay for two shows if you can. The water is the same and the choreography never is — and the second time through, you'll know to watch the crowd as much as the lake.
