Most buildings you look at. This one you can read.

You should be on the raised walkway along Sheikh Zayed Road, near the Emirates Towers, looking at a silver ring standing on a green hill — an oval of polished steel with seven storeys' worth of empty space in the middle, its whole surface flowing with Arabic calligraphy. This is the Museum of the Future, and it is its own first exhibit.

Take the shape in first. The whole thing stands seventy-seven metres high, though it refuses to read as a tower; it reads as an object, set down. It's a torus — a ring — with no columns holding it up; the steel skin is the structure, a hidden lattice carrying the whole curve. And the hole in the middle is deliberate. The designers were plain about what it means: the building stands for what we know, and the void stands for what we haven't imagined yet. Dubai built a monument with the future left blank on purpose.

Now the writing. The calligraphy isn't applied to the windows — it is the windows. The letters are cut straight through the steel, and daylight enters the building through its own sentences. The lines are by Sheikh Mohammed, the ruler of Dubai, on his favourite theme — that the future belongs to the people who can imagine it, design it and build it, and that it doesn't wait around for anyone. After dark the building glows through the script, and the message runs in silver light.

There's an old rhyme in this, if you know the city. Dubai's first great buildings were engineered around air — wind towers above the creek, built to catch a breeze and pull it down into the rooms. Its newest landmark is engineered around emptiness. The technology changed completely; the habit of building an idea, rather than just a building, did not.

[beat]

You can circle the whole thing from out here, and outside is honestly where the architecture lives. Come at dusk if you can, when the sky goes violet and the sentences light up stroke by stroke — a building reading itself aloud to the motorway.
