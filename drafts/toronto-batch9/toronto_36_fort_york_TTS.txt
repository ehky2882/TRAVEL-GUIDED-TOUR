Stand close to the old walls, with the Gardiner Expressway roaring on its concrete legs just overhead, and you're standing on the exact spot where Toronto began. Not a market, not a meeting hall — a fort. The city started as a military decision.

In seventeen ninety-three, wary of the new United States next door, the British sent a hundred soldiers to build a garrison here on the lakeshore, and moved the colonial capital to the sheltered harbour behind you. They renamed the place York. The town grew up around the fort, fed by a creek the garrison was named for — Garrison Creek, the same buried stream that shaped the ground under Trinity Bellwoods, up the hill.

Twenty years later, the war they'd feared arrived. In eighteen thirteen, an American fleet landed to the west and overwhelmed the outnumbered defenders — British regulars, local militia, and missisawga and ojibway warriors fighting alongside them. As they fell back, the garrison did something drastic: they blew up their own gunpowder magazine rather than surrender it. The blast was devastating, and among the many it killed was the American commander. The Americans took the town for a few days, looted it, and burned the parliament buildings of Upper Canada. The next year, the British answered in kind — marching on Washington and burning the White House. A quiet line runs from this muddy fort to that.

The British rebuilt here at once, and remarkably, seven of those two hundred year old buildings still stand — the largest collection of War of eighteen twelve buildings anywhere in Canada.

What almost finished the fort in the end wasn't an army. In the nineteen fifties the city nearly ran the Gardiner straight through it, and Torontonians had to fight to save the place where their city was born. They won. So it sits here still, a green pocket of eighteen thirteen pinned beneath the roar of the modern city — the oldest thing in Toronto, hiding under one of the newest.
