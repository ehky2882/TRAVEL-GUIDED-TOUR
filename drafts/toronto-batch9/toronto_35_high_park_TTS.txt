Most big-city parks were bought, or expropriated, or carved out of land nobody else wanted. This one was a gift — and like a lot of gifts, it came with conditions.

You're in High Park, four hundred acres of woods, ponds and gardens in the west end. It was the country estate of John Howard, Toronto's first professional architect, and his wife Jemima, who built a small cottage on the hill above the lake in the eighteen thirties and named the whole property for its height. In eighteen seventy-three, the Howards handed it to the city, the deed spelling out that it stay "for the free use, benefit and enjoyment of the citizens of Toronto" — and that it always be called High Park.

Free, forever. And Howard attached one more condition the city still honours: no alcohol, anywhere in the park. High Park is the last dry patch in Toronto — the cafe by the pond, more than a century later, still can't sell you a beer. One man's temperance, quietly enforced from beyond the grave.

Then there's a gift laid on top of the gift. Every spring the hillside above Grenadier Pond turns pale pink with cherry blossom — Japanese sakura trees, the first of them sent from the citizens of Tokyo in the nineteen fifties, in thanks for the city taking in Japanese-Canadians who'd been relocated after the war. For a few days each spring the crowds grow so thick the park closes to cars. A gift given in gratitude for a decency that had been a long time coming.

One last thing, if you walk down to the Howards' tomb near the pond. The ornate iron railing around it isn't Canadian at all — it once stood around St Paul's Cathedral in London, designed by Christopher Wren, and Howard had it shipped across an ocean to fence his own grave. Even in death, the man had taste, and conditions. Stay a while. It's free — he made sure of it.
