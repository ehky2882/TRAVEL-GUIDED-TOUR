Most people don't think of Toronto as a city with cliffs. But you're standing at the edge of nearly ninety metres of them — the height of a twenty-five-storey building — dropping almost straight down to Lake Ontario, and they run for fifteen kilometres along the eastern shore. It's one of the strangest things the city has, and it's giving itself away the whole time.

Find a spot behind the fence — and stay behind it, because this edge is genuinely not to be trusted — where you can see the cliff face falling away below and the lake opening out flat to the horizon. That pale, layered wall of sand and clay is the whole show. If instead you've come down to Bluffers Park at the base, look up: the cliffs rise right over you, and the water along the shore is often a strange milky colour — hold that thought.

Start with what you're standing on.

These cliffs are the shoreline of a lake that no longer exists. At the end of the last ice age a much larger, higher lake sat here, held in place by a wall of ice damming the St. Lawrence; when that ice gave way, the water dropped by something like thirty metres, and the old underwater edge was left standing in open air. So the cliff in front of you is a beach — a very old one, roughly twelve thousand years old, lifted up and left behind. It's built from loose sand and clay layered over tens of thousands of years, and you can almost read it: those bands record the ice advancing and retreating, again and again, which is why geologists come from around the world to study one of the clearest ice-age cross-sections on the continent.

Now, here's the part that matters. These cliffs are falling down — wind, water, and the lake at their base chew away at them every year, sometimes gently, sometimes in sudden collapses, one of which gave way near a lookout not long ago with a sound like an explosion and a cloud of dust. And all that material doesn't vanish: the lake carries it west along the shore, where it becomes the sand of the Beaches and, further on, the ground the Toronto Islands stand on. The Bluffs are quietly taking themselves apart and rebuilding the rest of the city's shoreline with the pieces — and that milky water at the bottom is simply the cliff, dissolved, on its way there.

Even the name is a bit of an accident. When Elizabeth Simcoe sailed past in 1793, the white cliffs reminded her of the chalk cliffs back home at Scarborough, in Yorkshire, and she noted it in her diary — and that offhand comparison ended up naming not just these cliffs but the entire eastern third of the city. There's a quieter irony at the top, too: the view is so good that people built houses right along the edge, which is exactly what hurries the edge along. The thing that makes the clifftop worth having is the thing that slowly takes the clifftop away.

[beat]

So stand here a while — behind the fence — and take it in. It looks like the most permanent thing for kilometres, this great pale wall above the lake, and it's actually the least: you're watching the slowest landslide there is, the floor of a vanished lake going back into the water it came from, one quiet year at a time — and somewhere off to the west, becoming a beach.
