Museum Row starts with a hole in the ground — several of them, black and bubbling and older than human memory — and it ends with lunch. In between: the largest art museum in the western United States, a museum for the movies, a museum for the automobile, and one flat stretch of Wilshire Boulevard called the Miracle Mile.

The name was a sales pitch. In the 1920s, a developer named A. W. Ross bought a strip of dusty road west of downtown and bet on a strange new idea: that Angelenos would rather shop from their cars than ride the streetcar downtown. He built the whole boulevard for drivers — parking lots behind the stores, bold storefronts you could read at thirty miles an hour. People called it Ross's Folly, right up until it worked. Then they called it the Miracle Mile.

Here's the thread to hold onto as you walk: everything on this stretch came up out of the ground. The tar that trapped the mammoths, the oil that made the fortunes, even the boulevard itself, which rose out of empty ranch land on one man's bet. Keep that in your pocket — it pays off at the very end, at a stop you'll smell before you see.

The route is simple. We start at the La Brea Tar Pits, walk west along Wilshire past LACMA to the Academy Museum and the Petersen, then turn north up Fairfax to the Original Farmers Market — about a mile and a half, flat the whole way, with a Metro station at Wilshire and Fairfax. Start at the pits. They're the oldest thing in Los Angeles.
