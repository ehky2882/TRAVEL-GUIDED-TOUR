The movie business is more than a century old. It did not get around to building itself a museum until 2021 — and when it finally did, it chose this corner.

The building you're facing began life in 1939 as the May Company department store, a streamline moderne landmark whose gold-tiled cylinder was designed to catch the eye of passing drivers — a storefront for the car age, planted on the Miracle Mile's best corner. The architect Renzo Piano restored it, then did something stranger: he attached an enormous concrete-and-glass sphere to the back, holding a thousand-seat theater, with a glass-domed terrace on top. Ride up and you can see the Hollywood Sign framed in the hills — the industry's oldest advertisement, viewed from its newest temple.

Inside are the artifacts of more than a hundred years of moviemaking — costumes, cameras, screenplays, a gallery of golden statuettes — presented with the industry's full production polish. Which raises the obvious question: why did the town that films everything wait so long to preserve itself? Part of the answer is money and politics — decades of stalled plans. But part of it is the business itself. In Hollywood, the next picture has always mattered more than the last one. This is a city that sells the future; keeping the past was nobody's department, until the industry finally decided it had better become its own.

The sphere has been collecting nicknames since the first renderings went public — locals compare it to a soap bubble, a golf ball, and a few things less polite. Judge for yourself from the terrace, or from right here on the sidewalk. Then cross Wilshire to the building that looks like it's moving while standing still — the red one, wrapped in ribbons of steel. That's the Petersen.
