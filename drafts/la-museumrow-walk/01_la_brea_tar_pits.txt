You'll smell this stop before you see it. Find the Lake Pit — the big fenced pond near the corner of Wilshire and Curson — and watch the surface for a moment. Those slow black bubbles are natural asphalt, seeping up from an oil field deep beneath the park, the same way it has for tens of thousands of years. The life-sized mammoth family at the water's edge, one of them caught in the muck, is a sculpture from the 1960s — but it's an honest reenactment, because that is exactly how it happened here, again and again, for millennia.

The trap worked like this: a few inches of sticky tar under a film of rainwater or fallen leaves was enough to hold a mammoth fast, and everything that came to eat the mammoth — dire wolves, saber-toothed cats — sank right alongside it. Dire wolves are the most common large animal ever pulled from these pits; the collection holds thousands of them. And the pulling hasn't stopped. Walk into the park and you'll find fenced excavation sites where paleontologists are still at work, still making discoveries — which makes this the only place on earth where an active Ice Age dig sits in the middle of a major city, wedged between a boulevard and an art museum.

Long before any of it was science, the Chumash and Tongva people gathered this same tar to seal their plank canoes — the first Angelenos to find a use for what this ground gives up. The museum beside the pits is being rebuilt, due back before the 2028 Games, but the pits themselves don't close. They never have. The asphalt doesn't take a day off.

One more look at the Lake Pit before you go — the bubbles keep their own slow time. Then walk west through the park. The long gray building floating over Wilshire on concrete legs is our next stop: LACMA.
