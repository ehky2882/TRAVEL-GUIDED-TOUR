Around 1900, a dairy farmer named Arthur Gilmore drilled for water on this land and hit oil instead. That accident explains everything you're about to eat.

The Gilmore dairy became the Gilmore Oil Company, the pastures filled with derricks, and the family got rich on what came out of the ground — the same buried oil field, incidentally, that feeds the tar pits where you started this walk. By the 1930s the derricks had thinned out and the Depression had arrived, and the corner of Third and Fairfax was mostly idle land. Then, in July 1934, two promoters talked Earl Gilmore into an experiment: let local farmers park their trucks here and sell produce off the tailgates, fifty cents a day for the space. A dozen farmers showed up. By October they were moving into permanent stalls, and they have never left. What you're standing in is a Depression stopgap that simply refused to end — low stalls, narrow crooked aisles, more than a hundred vendors, open every day of the week. The white clock tower, the market's landmark since 1948, still keeps the time.

So here's the walk's whole story, finished. This boulevard runs on things that came up out of the ground. Tar, and the Ice Age it preserved. Oil, and the fortunes it built. A boulder and two hundred lampposts raised up as art, a gold corner built to catch a driver's eye, a red museum for the machine that ran on Gilmore's gasoline. And at the end of it all, the ground's last and best offering — tomatoes, doughnuts, gumbo, hot corned beef — sold from stalls on an old oil field.

[beat]

That's the end of the walk. Go get something to eat, and take your time choosing — nobody in here is in a hurry, and after a mile and a half of Wilshire, neither are you.
