The gray colossus hovering over Wilshire ahead of you is the David Geffen Galleries — nine hundred feet of curving concrete, the new heart of the Los Angeles County Museum of Art, and the most argued-about building in the city. It opened in April 2026, after two decades of planning by the Swiss architect Peter Zumthor, and it does something almost no museum anywhere does: it steps clean over the boulevard, so the collection literally crosses the street above the traffic.

Inside, everything sits on a single level — no wings, no grand staircase, no prescribed route — art from every era and culture laid out side by side, with floor-to-ceiling glass that lets the famous Los Angeles light do the lighting. Angelenos spent twenty years arguing about this building before it opened and haven't stopped since: some see a concrete freeway overpass with paintings in it, others the calmest room in the city. Both camps stood in the same line to get in, and around here, that's how you know a building matters.

Two things at street level, both free. First, the forest of lampposts on the Wilshire sidewalk — that's Urban Light, two hundred and two of them, and every single one is a real street lamp that once lit Los Angeles, most from the 1920s and 30s, gathered and restored by the artist Chris Burden. They still switch on at dusk. Second, in the park behind, a three-hundred-and-forty-ton granite boulder parked above a walkway — Levitated Mass — which you can stroll directly beneath. A boulder on a pedestal, lampposts as a monument: this stretch of the city keeps taking ordinary ground and holding it up to the light.
