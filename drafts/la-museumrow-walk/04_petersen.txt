From across the street, the Petersen Automotive Museum looks like it's doing thirty miles an hour at a standstill — a hot-rod-red body wrapped in swooping ribbons of stainless steel. Here's the gift: underneath all that motion is an ordinary department store from 1962. When the museum wrapped the old box in steel in 2015, it pulled the most Los Angeles move there is — take something plain, give it a dramatic new body, and sell the speed.

The museum itself was founded in 1994 by Robert Petersen, the publisher who built an empire on Hot Rod magazine — a man who understood better than almost anyone that in this town, the car was never just transportation. Inside are three floors of proof: race cars, concept cars, gleaming machines you've seen on screen, and below it all a vault holding hundreds more of the rarest vehicles anywhere, from world leaders' limousines to one-of-a-kind prototypes.

But stand here a second before you go in, because the location is the real exhibit. This is a museum to the machine that built and cursed this city — the car gave Los Angeles its shape, its sprawl, its freeways, and this very boulevard, laid out from the start for people arriving on four wheels. And it sits, with a completely straight face, one crosswalk from the tar pits: the gasoline age and the ancient seep it grew out of, facing each other across Wilshire.

When you're done, walk north up Fairfax — about ten minutes. You're looking for a white clock tower, and for the last block you can trust your nose. That's the Original Farmers Market: the end of the walk, and the payoff of the whole story.
