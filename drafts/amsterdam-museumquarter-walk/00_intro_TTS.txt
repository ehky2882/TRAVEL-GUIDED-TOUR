A hundred and fifty years ago, everything on this walk was pasture. Cows, ditches, wet Dutch sky — the unglamorous edge of a city that had spent two centuries getting rich and remarkably little of that time asking what any of it had been for.

Then, in the space of one generation, the question got answered. Between eighteen eighty-five and eighteen ninety-five this field sprouted a palace for the nation's paintings, a concert hall with almost supernatural sound, and a museum for art so new it hadn't been argued about yet — and beside them all, a park the citizens had already built for themselves. This walk is about that burst of deciding: a trading nation working out what, of everything it had accumulated, was actually worth keeping.

A practical warning, kindly meant. The museums on this route can each swallow half a day, and the two big ones want timed tickets booked well ahead — the van goh sells out weeks in advance in high season. This walk is the connective tissue, not the interiors: it works beautifully as a way to read the quarter from outside, with one museum visit folded in if you've planned it, or none at all. The route itself is short and flat — under two kilometres, comfortably done in an hour, ending on the grass.

Start at the rikes mew zay um — specifically, at the great arched passage that runs straight through the middle of the building. Stand near it, out of the way of the bicycles. You'll see why.
