Through the gates, and the city fades with impressive speed — winding paths, long ponds, big lazy lawns, herons standing around like retired officials. This is Vondelpark, Amsterdam's green living room, and here's what ties the whole walk together: it's older than everything you've just seen. The park came first.

In 1865 — twenty years before the Rijksmuseum opened its doors — a group of private citizens looked at their booming, treeless city, formed an association, bought this stretch of soggy pasture, and hired landscape designers to conjure an English-style park out of it: hills where there were none, a winding stream, vistas arranged like paintings. It was the citizens'-committee trick you've now seen three times, and this was the original. A few years in, they raised a statue to the poet Joost van den Vondel — the city's Shakespeare, more or less — and the public renamed the park after him before officialdom had much say in the matter.

One last gift, and it's very Amsterdam. The park is sinking. It sits below sea level on soft peat, and parts of it settle year by year — paths are re-laid, trees are hoisted, whole sections have been rebuilt while the joggers detour politely around the works. Even the countryside here is invented ground, and keeping it requires the same quiet, perpetual effort as keeping the canals. Nothing in this city stands still by itself; everything is held up on purpose.

Find some grass and sit down — you've earned it, and sitting on it is what this place is for. It's a fair summary of the whole quarter, in the end. A nation of traders spent its great century accumulating, then spent a later one deciding what to keep: the paintings, the letters, the music — and an afternoon on the grass, held gently above the water, on purpose.
