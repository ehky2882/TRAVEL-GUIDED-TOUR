You see it, of course. The nineteenth-century building — red brick, gabled, perfectly respectable, opened in 1895 — and docked on top of it, the vast smooth white form that the whole city immediately and permanently named the bathtub. The extension arrived in 2012, the nickname arrived roughly a day later, and the museum has long since surrendered to it.

But the bathtub is only the loudest example of what this institution has always done, which is to get there embarrassingly early. While the Rijksmuseum across the lawn was busy canonising the seventeenth century, the Stedelijk collected the art nobody had agreed on yet — the moderns, the abstractionists, the difficult and the possibly-ridiculous — and in 1938 one of its directors did something quietly radical: he painted the ornate interiors white, top to bottom, creating one of the first of the blank white galleries that every modern art museum on earth now treats as natural law. The white cube, more or less, started here, inside a gabled Dutch palazzo.

That's the neat division of labour on this lawn. One museum keeps the argument the nation already won; this one keeps the arguments still running. A city needs both — a memory and a dare — and Amsterdam, characteristically, put them a hundred metres apart and let them stare at each other.
