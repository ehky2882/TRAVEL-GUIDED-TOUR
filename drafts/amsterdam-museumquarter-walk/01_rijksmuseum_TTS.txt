The arched tunnel running through the heart of the rikes mew zay um is a working bicycle path — thousands of Amsterdammers pedal daily straight through the middle of the national museum, between the galleries holding rem brant and vair meer. When the building was renovated some years ago, there were serious proposals to close the passage and give the space to the museum. The cyclists won. Of course the cyclists won. It remains the most Dutch hundred metres in the country: the nation's treasure house, with a commuter route through its chest.

Now step back and take in the building itself, all brick and spires and stained glass. This is pee air kow pers' work, opened in eighteen eighty-five, and if you've stood in front of sen trahl Station you know his style — the same architect gave the city both its gateways, and he made each of them look like a cathedral. Which, in this case, is close to the truth. A cathedral is exactly what this is: built by the state to house the relics of the Golden Age, with the Night Watch enshrined at the far end of a long ceremonial axis like an altarpiece — which is precisely how it hangs.

Here's the pivot for the whole walk. For two hundred years the Dutch had been better at making money than at making monuments; the paintings that ended up here were commercial products, bought by merchants for their parlours. It took until the eighteen eighties for the nation to look at that inheritance and decide it amounted to something — that a republic of traders had, almost by accident, produced one of civilisation's great arguments for itself, and that it deserved a building this unembarrassed.

The rest of the quarter answers this building, one institution at a time. Walk through the passage — or around, if the bells unnerve you — onto the great lawn, and cross it, bearing right. The museum at the far side, the one with the glass entrance between two older wings, holds the world's largest collection of a painter who sold almost nothing while he lived.
