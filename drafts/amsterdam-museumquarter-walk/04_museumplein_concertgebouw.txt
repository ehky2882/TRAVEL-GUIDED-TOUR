First, the ground you're crossing. Museumplein — the museum square — is really the museum lawn: a long green rectangle that serves as the nation's front garden. This is where the city comes to celebrate its football teams, protest its governments, mourn its losses, and sunbathe between grievances. Most squares in Europe are stone and statuary; Amsterdam's grandest public space is, characteristically, grass you're allowed to sit on.

Now the building closing off the far end, pale and formal with a golden lyre on the roof: the Concertgebouw — literally, the concert building — home to one of the most admired orchestras in the world, in one of the most acoustically perfect halls ever constructed. And here is the part musicians tell each other with a straight face: nobody fully intended that. The architect, by his own family's account, had no particular ear for music, and the science of acoustics barely existed in 1888; yet the hall he produced is still ranked by acousticians among the two or three finest on earth. Even the house's own directors have been known to credit a measure of luck. Some cathedrals are designed; some are conjured.

But the detail worth carrying home is who built it. Not a king, not a ministry — a committee of citizens, who decided their city was embarrassingly short of a proper concert hall and raised the money themselves, by subscription. The Stedelijk behind you began the same way: civic-minded Amsterdammers, passing the hat at the grandest possible scale. Of the temples on this lawn, the state built exactly one. The citizens built the rest — which may be the most seventeenth-century thing about this thoroughly nineteenth-century quarter.

[beat]

One stop remains, and it's the oldest thing here — the citizens' first gift to themselves. Take the busy street running north beside the Concertgebouw, Van Baerlestraat, and follow it a few hundred metres to the park gates on your left. Vondelpark, to finish.
