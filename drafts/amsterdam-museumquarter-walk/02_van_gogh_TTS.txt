The numbers first, because they're the point. Inside this building: some two hundred paintings, five hundred drawings, and seven hundred and fifty letters — the largest van goh collection in the world, by a distance. And it exists for one reason, which almost nobody in the queue knows: the family never sold.

Vincent died in eighteen ninety, aged thirty-seven, having sold famously little. His brother tay oh — the art dealer who had funded him, believed in him, and received nearly every canvas and letter — died just six months later. Which meant that the entire estate of an unknown, unsellable painter landed on a twenty-eight-year-old widow with a baby: tay oh's wife, yo van goh bong er.

She could have sold the lot for very little, and been advised to. Instead she spent the next three decades running the longest, shrewdest campaign in the history of art: lending paintings to exactly the right exhibitions, selling a few strategically to seed museums and collectors, keeping the core intact, and — her masterstroke — editing and publishing the brothers' correspondence, which turned Vincent from a painter into a story the world could not put down. By the time she died, the unknown brother-in-law was on his way to being the most beloved painter alive or dead. Her son gave the collection to a foundation, the state built this museum around it, and the doors opened in nineteen seventy-three.

So when you hear that van goh was unrecognised in his lifetime, remember the second half of the sentence, the half that matters here: recognition was manufactured, patiently, by a woman history nearly left as a footnote. This building is her monument as much as his.

Next door — the building that looks like an elegant brick palazzo wearing an enormous white bathtub — is the stay duh like, the city's museum of modern art. Thirty seconds' walk. The bathtub is not an insult, by the way. Locals mean it fondly. Mostly.
