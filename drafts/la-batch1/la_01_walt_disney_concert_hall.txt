Walt Disney Concert Hall

You're standing in front of the building that finally gave a car city a room to hear itself think. Walt Disney Concert Hall opened in 2003, and its stainless-steel sails — billowing, colliding, catching the afternoon sun like something about to leave the ground — have been the face of downtown ever since. Which is a little funny, because for most of its life this project was famous for going nowhere.

[beat]

It began in 1987, when Lillian Disney, Walt's widow, gave fifty million dollars for a concert hall in her husband's name. Frank Gehry won the design competition the next year, and then the whole thing stalled — budgets, politics, and a parking garage so expensive the county built it first, which left downtown with a garage for a concert hall that did not exist. It took the worldwide success of Gehry's museum in Bilbao, Spain, to persuade Los Angeles that the architect it already had — he lived here — was worth finishing a building for. Sixteen years after the gift, the doors finally opened.

Inside, all that cold steel gives way to Douglas fir, warm and ship-curved, wrapped around one of the finest concert rooms on earth. This is home to the Los Angeles Philharmonic, and the room was tuned like an instrument: you can hear a bow lift off a string from the back row. Behind the stage stands the organ Gehry designed with its pipes splayed in every direction — locals call it the french-fry organ, and once you've seen it, so will you.

[beat]

Two details to carry with you. First, some of the shiniest panels had to be sanded down after opening, because they focused sunlight onto the sidewalk and nearby apartments hard enough to roast both — Los Angeles, it turns out, has plenty of sun without architectural assistance. Second, if you follow the outdoor stairs, there's a public garden hidden up among the sails, free to anyone, where a fountain shaped like a rose sits covered in broken Delft porcelain. Lillian Disney collected Delft, and never lived to see the hall finished. The building remembers her anyway.

Walk the full perimeter before you go. The building never repeats itself, and neither will your photographs.
