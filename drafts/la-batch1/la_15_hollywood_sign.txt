The Hollywood Sign

The most famous sign on earth started life as a real-estate ad. In 1923, developers selling homes in the hills below put up thirteen enormous letters reading HOLLYWOODLAND — the name of their subdivision — lit with thousands of light bulbs and intended to last about a year and a half. That's the entire origin: no vision, no symbolism, just billboard money. The ad simply refused to die, and then it outlived the thing it was advertising, which may make it the most perfectly Los Angeles monument in existence.

[beat]

The letters stand on Mount Lee, each forty-five feet tall, the row stretching some four hundred fifty feet across the scrub. By the 1940s the sign was collapsing and the developers were done with it, so in 1949 the city's chamber of commerce struck a deal: repair the sign, and drop the last four letters. HOLLYWOODLAND, the housing tract, became HOLLYWOOD, the idea. By the 1970s it had rotted all over again, and this time celebrities and businessmen sponsored individual letters to fund a complete rebuild — the version standing today went up in 1978, sturdier than the original ad ever was.

[beat]

Now, the practical truth, delivered with love: you cannot visit the sign. It sits behind fences, cameras, and a very attentive security operation, and no path puts you at the letters. What you can do is see it beautifully, and you're likely standing in one of the best places to do exactly that. The lawn at Griffith Observatory frames it across the canyon; Lake Hollywood Park gives you the classic straight-on view from a grassy field; the upper terraces at Ovation Hollywood work nicely if you're already down on the boulevard. And hikers can take the fire roads of Griffith Park up behind Mount Lee to stand above the letters, looking out over the city the sign has spent a century facing.

One last detail: it goes dark at night. There have been no lights since the billboard days, partly out of consideration for the neighborhood below, so the most photographed sign in the world keeps farmer's hours. For a piece of advertising that accidentally became a civilization's logo, that feels about right.
