The brohd

First things first: it rhymes with road. The brohd is named for ee lie and ee dith brohd, and mispronouncing it is the fastest way to out yourself as a visitor, so consider that sorted.

This is a billionaire's private art collection turned into a free public museum, which is a very Los Angeles transaction. ee lie brohd made fortunes in homebuilding and insurance, spent decades collecting contemporary art, put his name on buildings all over this city, and then in twenty fifteen opened this one — general admission free, forever — to hold some two thousand works by the biggest names of the last seventy years.

The building tells you exactly what it is, if you know how to read it. The architects, dill er skoh fee dee oh and ren froh, called their design "the veil and the vault." The veil is what you're looking at: that white honeycomb skin of fiberglass and concrete panels, lifted at the corners like a curtain being raised. The vault is the massive concrete storage block hidden inside, holding the majority of the collection that isn't on view. Most museums bury their storage in a basement somewhere; this one makes you ride an escalator straight up through the middle of it, and on your way back down the stairs there's a window where you can peer into the vault itself and watch the art waiting its turn.

Upstairs, the top floor is one enormous column-free room washed in filtered skylight — koons balloon animals, warhols, bahs kee ahz, the whole gang. The famous one is smaller: yah yoy koo sah mah's Infinity Mirror Room, a chamber of lights and mirrors stretching to forever in every direction. You get about a minute inside, it books up fast, and reserving early is the closest thing this museum has to a rule.

Across the street sits moh kah, the contemporary museum brohd helped found back in the day and later helped rescue; next door rise the sails of Disney Concert Hall. He planted his own museum in the middle of the cultural district he'd spent a lifetime funding, which tells you the collection came with a point of view.

Free art, first come, first served, under a veil on top of a hill. Rhymes with road. You're ready.
