Hollywood Walk of Fame

The Hollywood Walk of Fame is the sidewalk that turned fame itself into a tourist attraction — more than twenty-eight hundred coral-pink terrazzo stars set into the pavement, each rimmed in brass, each carrying a name, stretching for blocks in every direction. It's one of the most visited attractions on earth, and it is also, famously, just a sidewalk. Both things are true, and the gap between them is the most honest thing in Hollywood.

[beat]

The idea arrived in the 1950s, when local business owners wanted to glamorize the boulevard, and the first permanent stars went into the ground starting in 1960. Under each name sits a small bronze emblem telling you what the fame was for: a movie camera, a television set, a phonograph record, a radio microphone, or the twin masks of live performance. A few names appear more than once — the true completists of American entertainment collected a star in every category they could get.

Here's how a star actually happens, because it's not how most people assume. Anyone can nominate a celebrity — fans included — but the honoree has to agree, a committee selects only a couple dozen a year from hundreds of nominations, and a sponsor pays a fee running to tens of thousands of dollars to cover the making, the ceremony, and the upkeep. Fame here is part honor, part transaction — which, again, may be the most honest thing in Hollywood. And once a star is in the ground, it's considered part of the historic fabric of the Walk and cannot be removed. Reputations rise and fall; terrazzo is forever.

[beat]

A suggestion for walking it: find two stars. First, someone you love — that's the easy one, and there's a directory online if you want to hunt one down. Then find a name you have never heard of in your life, because the Walk is also a graveyard of enormous fame — radio stars and matinee idols who once stopped traffic and now stop no one. That second star is the real monument here. Every single name under your feet was, for a moment, the biggest name there was.

The lesson is free, and it runs the length of the boulevard.
