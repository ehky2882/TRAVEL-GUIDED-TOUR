El Pueblo & Olvera Street

This is where Los Angeles actually began — not Hollywood, not the beach, but right here, on September 4, 1781, when forty-four settlers founded a farming village with a long Spanish name historians still argue about: El Pueblo de la Reina de los Ángeles, the town of the Queen of the Angels.

[beat]

Here's the founding fact worth carrying with you around this whole city: those forty-four settlers, recruited from what is now northern Mexico, were mostly of Indigenous, African, and mixed heritage — only two identified as Spanish. Los Angeles was multicultural at birth, by roster, in 1781. Everything the city later became was built on top of a village founded by people most history books took two centuries to get around to mentioning.

The old plaza in front of you was the center of that world — Spanish, then Mexican, then, after 1848, American, the flags changing over the same dusty square. Around it survive the city's oldest buildings, including the Avila Adobe on Olvera Street, a thick-walled house from 1818 that is the oldest standing residence in Los Angeles. You can walk into it for free and stand in rooms older than the state of California.

[beat]

Olvera Street itself needs one honest footnote. By the 1920s the district had slid into neglect, and a determined civic booster named Christine Sterling campaigned to save it — by reopening the alley in 1930 as a romantic "Mexican street of yesterday," a marketplace of puestos and crafts and candle-lit restaurants built partly on nostalgia for a Mexico that never quite existed. So yes, it's a bit of a stage set. But it's a stage set that saved the real buildings around it, and the stalls have now been run by some of the same families for generations — meaning the invented tradition became, over nearly a century of actual use, simply a tradition. Taquitos were reportedly invented here, a claim the city has shown no interest in fact-checking.

Wander slowly. The plaza still hosts festivals the way it has for some two hundred years, the little church on the square still fills, and this compact district does something Los Angeles almost never does: it stays put.
