El pweh bloh and ohl vair ah Street

This is where Los Angeles actually began — not Hollywood, not the beach, but right here, on September fourth, seventeen eighty one, when forty-four settlers founded a farming village with a long Spanish name historians still argue about: el pweh bloh deh lah ray nah deh lohs ahn heh lehs, the town of the Queen of the Angels.

Here's the founding fact worth carrying with you around this whole city: those forty-four settlers, recruited from what is now northern Mexico, were mostly of Indigenous, African, and mixed heritage — only two identified as Spanish. Los Angeles was multicultural at birth, by roster, in seventeen eighty one. Everything the city later became was built on top of a village founded by people most history books took two centuries to get around to mentioning.

The old plaza in front of you was the center of that world — Spanish, then Mexican, then, after eighteen forty eight, American, the flags changing over the same dusty square. Around it survive the city's oldest buildings, including the ah vee lah ah doh bee on ohl vair ah Street, a thick-walled house from eighteen eighteen that is the oldest standing residence in Los Angeles. You can walk into it for free and stand in rooms older than the state of California.

ohl vair ah Street itself needs one honest footnote. By the nineteen twenties the district had slid into neglect, and a determined civic booster named Christine Sterling campaigned to save it — by reopening the alley in nineteen thirty as a romantic "Mexican street of yesterday," a marketplace of pwess tohs and crafts and candle-lit restaurants built partly on nostalgia for a Mexico that never quite existed. So yes, it's a bit of a stage set. But it's a stage set that saved the real buildings around it, and the stalls have now been run by some of the same families for generations — meaning the invented tradition became, over nearly a century of actual use, simply a tradition. tah kee tohs were reportedly invented here, a claim the city has shown no interest in fact-checking.

Wander slowly. The plaza still hosts festivals the way it has for some two hundred years, the little church on the square still fills, and this compact district does something Los Angeles almost never does: it stays put.
