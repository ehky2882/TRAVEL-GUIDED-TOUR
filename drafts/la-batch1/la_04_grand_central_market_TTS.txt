Grand Central Market

Grand Central Market has been feeding downtown Los Angeles since nineteen seventeen, without interruption, through every single version of this city — and if you want to understand Los Angeles in one room, this is the room.

It opened on the ground floor of the Homer laff lin Building back when Broadway was the grandest shopping street west of Chicago, all streetcars and department stores and movie palaces. The market was the daily-needs engine underneath the glamour: produce stalls, butchers, fishmongers, most of them run by European immigrant families selling to the well-off residents of Bunker Hill, who rode the little incline railway across the street down to do their shopping.

Then the city changed, the way this city does — completely, and without asking anyone. Bunker Hill's mansions came down, the money moved west, and by mid-century Broadway had become the main street of Mexican and Central American Los Angeles. The market changed with it, stall by stall: chee lehs and moh lehs, kar nee tahs by the pound, dried shrimp and fresh mah sah under the same old neon. And when downtown reinvented itself yet again in recent decades — lofts, galleries, people actually living here — the market absorbed that wave too. A generations-old Mexican deli now shares an aisle with an oyster bar and whatever stall currently has a line out the door.

Here's what makes this place genuinely rare, though: the new arrivals didn't replace the old ones. The neon signs stacked overhead — some decades old, some designed last year to look it — hang over legacy vendors and newcomers side by side, and that almost never happens. In most cities, a market either stays frozen and slowly dies, or gets renovated into a food court with a memory problem. This one just kept adding layers, which is the most Los Angeles survival strategy there is.

Come hungry, obviously, and bring patience at lunchtime. Then take whatever you're eating out the doors on the Hill Street side: directly across the street is Angels Flight, the tiny fyoo nik yuh ler railway that's been hauling people up Bunker Hill since nineteen oh one. A ride costs about the same as adding guacamole. Do both.
