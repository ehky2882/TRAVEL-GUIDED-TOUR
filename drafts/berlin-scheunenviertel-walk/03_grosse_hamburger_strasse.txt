Grosse Hamburger Strasse is four hundred metres long, and quiet. Berliners once called it the street of tolerance: a Protestant church, a Catholic hospital and the city's oldest Jewish cemetery share this one short stretch. Take it slowly, and let the street show you what it keeps.

Watch for a gap between two buildings, small plaques climbing the bare side walls. This is The Missing House. The house here was destroyed by bombing in the war's last months and never rebuilt. In 1990 the artist Christian Boltanski traced everyone who had lived in it and mounted twenty-four plaques — a name, a profession, the years they were here. Many of the residents were Jewish. The house is missing; the work insists the people are not.

A few steps away, behind a railing, lies the Old Jewish Cemetery, laid out in 1672. The Enlightenment philosopher Moses Mendelssohn was buried here in 1786 ; the stone marking his grave is a later copy, standing where the original likely did. The cemetery was wrecked on Gestapo orders in 1943. Beside its entrance, where a Jewish old people's home stood, the Gestapo ran the collection point from which Berlin's Jews were sent to the trains. A bronze group of gaunt figures stands there now, facing the street.

[beat]

One more thing, and it's underfoot. Small brass squares sit in the ground before doorways — here, and all over Europe. Stolpersteine: stumbling stones. Each marks the last home a person freely chose, and carries their name and what became of them. The artist Gunter Demnig began laying them in 1992; there are well over a hundred thousand now, every letter still stamped into the brass by hand, one at a time — the deliberate opposite of the way these lives were taken. You've been walking past them all day. From here on, you'll notice.

At the street's end, turn right onto Oranienburger Strasse. The gold rising ahead is the Neue Synagoge — the last stop.
