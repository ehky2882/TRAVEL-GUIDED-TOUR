If the Hackesche Höfe show what money and care can do, this passage shows what the whole quarter looked like before either arrived — and it looks this way on purpose.

Go in. The courtyard is raw: bare, pocked brick, layer upon layer of street art and paste-ups, welded-metal creatures, a bar and a small cinema tucked into the corners. After the Wall fell, when investors were snapping up the neighbourhood, a group of artists and tenants dug in here, took the building on, and refused to let it be smartened. Every polished district needs one room left honest. This is Berlin's.

And the rough walls hold the quarter's most tender history. Just inside the passage is a small museum in a former workshop. In the early 1940s a brush maker named Otto Weidt ran his business here, employing blind and deaf Jewish workers — and he used the workshop to protect them: hiding some in a back room, bribing officials, buying time for as many as he could. The workbenches are still there. Alongside it now stand a centre named for Anne Frank and a memorial to the Berliners who quietly hid their neighbours — the silent heroes, as the city has come to call them.

Think about the arrangement for a moment. The Scheunenviertel keeps this memory not in marble but in a scuffed courtyard between a street-art gallery and a bar — remembrance and everyday life sharing a doorway, neither asked to make way for the other. It may be the most Berlin arrangement imaginable.

When you're ready, the simplest route onward is back the way you came: cut through the Hackesche Höfe again, courtyard by courtyard, and take their far exit, which lands you on Sophienstrasse — a short street of restored shopfronts and gas lamps. Follow it away from the Höfe to its end, where it opens beside the churchyard of the Sophienkirche. The quiet street in front of you is Grosse Hamburger Strasse — the next stop.
