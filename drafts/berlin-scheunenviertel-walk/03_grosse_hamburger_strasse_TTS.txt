groh suh hahm boor ger shtrah suh is four hundred metres long, and quiet. Berliners once called it the street of tolerance: a Protestant church, a Catholic hospital and the city's oldest Jewish cemetery share this one short stretch. Take it slowly, and let the street show you what it keeps.

Watch for a gap between two buildings, small plaques climbing the bare side walls. This is The Missing House. The house here was destroyed by bombing in the war's last months and never rebuilt. In nineteen ninety the artist kree stee ahn bol tahn skee traced everyone who had lived in it and mounted twenty-four plaques — a name, a profession, the years they were here. Many of the residents were Jewish. The house is missing; the work insists the people are not.

A few steps away, behind a railing, lies the Old Jewish Cemetery, laid out in sixteen seventy-two. The Enlightenment philosopher moh zes men dels zohn was buried here in seventeen eighty-six ; the stone marking his grave is a later copy, standing where the original likely did. The cemetery was wrecked on geh shtah poh orders in nineteen forty-three. Beside its entrance, where a Jewish old people's home stood, the geh shtah poh ran the collection point from which Berlin's Jews were sent to the trains. A bronze group of gaunt figures stands there now, facing the street.

One more thing, and it's underfoot. Small brass squares sit in the ground before doorways — here, and all over Europe. shtol per shty nuh: stumbling stones. Each marks the last home a person freely chose, and carries their name and what became of them. The artist goon ter dem nikh began laying them in nineteen ninety-two; there are well over a hundred thousand now, every letter still stamped into the brass by hand, one at a time — the deliberate opposite of the way these lives were taken. You've been walking past them all day. From here on, you'll notice.

At the street's end, turn right onto oh rah nee en boor ger shtrah suh. The gold rising ahead is the noy uh zeen ah goh guh — the last stop.
