Every city has a district that wears its history lightly. Berlin's is the shoy nen feer tel — the barn quarter — and the name is your first clue that nothing here is quite what it seems.

You should be at hah kesh er markt, the small square beside the red-brick arches of the es bahn station, with trams crossing and café tables spread out under the trees. Three hundred years ago this ground lay just outside the city walls, and because hay barns were a fire risk, the barns were banished out here. The barns are long gone. The name stayed.

What grew up in their place was Berlin's old Jewish quarter — and, since reunification, some of the most fashionable streets in the city. This walk threads those two truths together. It is short and level, well under a kilometre, moving from courtyard to courtyard: first a polished one, then a rough one, then a quiet street that carries more memory per metre than almost anywhere in Berlin. The walk will tell you plainly when the ground turns heavy. And it ends somewhere golden — you'll see it long before you arrive.

Cross to roh zen tah ler shtrah suh and look for the arched entrance at number forty, with people streaming in and out. That's the way into the hah kesh uh hur fuh.
