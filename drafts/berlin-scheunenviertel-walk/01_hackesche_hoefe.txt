Fair warning: the famous entrance is dressed in scaffolding at the moment. Thirty years after its last great restoration, the Hackesche Höfe are having their roof and facade seen to — which, as it happens, is exactly how this place has always survived.

Step through the passage — the shops and courtyards stay open through the works — and into the first courtyard. Even with repairs underway, look up where the walls are clear: glazed brick in waves and bands of colour, rising all the way to the roofline, the work of August Endell, opened in 1906. Eight courtyards link together from here, one opening into the next, clear through to Sophienstrasse — culture at the front, workshops in the middle, apartments at the back, a whole city block arranged like a set of nested rooms.

Now the part the facades won't tell you. Under the GDR these courtyards belonged to the state and were left to moulder; by 1989 the glory was mostly grime. The restoration that followed in the nineties cost eighty million marks, and the craftspeople re-fired the glazed bricks in more than fifty colours to match Endell's originals — not repainted, remade. What you're standing in is devotion with a budget line.

That's the real lesson of the Höfe, and it's why the scaffolding outside shouldn't put you off. Beautiful buildings don't simply last; they are kept — by owners, tenants and tile-makers who decide, decade after decade, that the effort is worth it. This courtyard has now been rescued twice in a single lifetime, and you're visiting in the middle of the second rescue.

Wander deeper through the linked courtyards if you like, then come back out to Rosenthaler Strasse. Just next door, at number thirty-nine, there's another passageway — no glazed brick, no polish. That's Haus Schwarzenberg, and it's the other half of this story.
