You've been able to see it since you turned the corner: a gilded dome floating above Oranienburger Strasse, flanked by two smaller ones, catching whatever light the day has to offer. Walk toward it. This is the Neue Synagoge, and being seen was always the point.

When it opened in 1866, this was the largest synagogue in the country, and nothing about it apologised. The congregation chose a Moorish style — banded brickwork, arched windows, that extraordinary dome — architecture with no intention of blending into a Prussian streetscape. Bismarck attended the opening. Berlin's Jewish community was prosperous, confident and at home here, and it built accordingly: not a discreet prayer house tucked into a courtyard, but a landmark on a main street.

The best place to take it in is from across the street. And it's worth knowing what you're looking at: the building was attacked in the pogrom of November 1938, gutted by wartime bombing, and the ruined main hall behind was cleared away in the 1950s. The front and the dome are a rebuilding, begun in the last years of the GDR and finished in 1995. Today the building holds a museum, a working prayer room and the community's archives. You may also notice the barriers and the police post out front. Synagogues in Germany are guarded, and this walk promised to tell you plainly.

[beat]

End on what the builders wanted you to feel. The quarter you've just walked keeps every register of its history within a few hundred metres — the polished courtyards, the rough one, the quiet street, the brass squares underfoot — and it saves its gold for this. On a clear evening the dome is still the brightest thing in the Scheunenviertel: a building made to be seen, wrecked for being seen, and then, patiently, made to be seen again.

The walk ends here. Stay across the street a moment longer, and give the dome the long look it was built for.
