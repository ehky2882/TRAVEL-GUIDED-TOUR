Step inside the lobby — the ground floor is open to visitors during business hours, free — and give your eyes a second to adjust.

From the street, the Bradbury Building is a plain brick box. Inside, it's a canyon of light: a five-story atrium under a glass roof, marble stairs, French wrought-iron railings running like lace along every landing, and two open-cage elevators rising and falling in plain view, counterweights and all. It was finished in 1893, and it is, by a comfortable margin, the most filmed interior in Los Angeles — science fiction especially keeps coming back, most famously a certain 1982 vision of this city's future, because nothing else on earth looks quite like this.

How it got built is stranger than the movies. Lewis Bradbury, a mining millionaire, rejected an established architect's design and handed the job to George Wyman — a thirty-two-year-old draftsman in that architect's own office, with no formal training. Legend has it Wyman consulted a ouija-style planchette board and received a message from his dead brother telling him to take the commission — and that is a legend, told for decades and impossible to check. What's documented is his inspiration: a utopian novel of the day that imagined the commercial buildings of the year 2000 as vast halls filled with light. Wyman read a description of the future and built it, in brick and iron, seven years before the century turned.

So the most filmed building in the world's film capital was itself science fiction first — a Victorian's guess at 2000, which the movies later borrowed to imagine 2019.

Back on Broadway, turn right and settle in — this is the long leg, about fifteen minutes north on foot, past the marquees of the old movie palaces, with the white tower of City Hall rising ahead of you. We're leaving the nineteenth century now and walking to 1781: El Pueblo, the plaza where Los Angeles began.
