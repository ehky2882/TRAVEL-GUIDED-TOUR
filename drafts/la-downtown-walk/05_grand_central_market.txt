You're inside a market that has been open, through every version of Los Angeles, since 1917. Neon signs overhead, forty-some vendors, the smell of a dozen cuisines negotiating. The building is older still — the Broadway side went up in 1897 as one of the first fireproofed, steel-reinforced structures in the city — and the market runs straight through the block, Hill Street to Broadway, which is exactly how we're using it.

Take your time in here; nothing about this stop rewards hurrying. But as you wander, read the market as a census. In the twenties there were ninety-odd stalls — greengrocers, fishmongers, delis, butchers — serving the crowds that rode Angels Flight down from the Hill. When downtown emptied out in mid-century, the market carried on, selling produce and chiles and menudo to the neighborhoods that stayed. And when downtown came back over the past couple of decades, the new wave moved in — the egg sandwiches with lines out the door, the natural wine — folding in beside legacy vendors whose families have worked these counters for generations.

That's the reframe worth carrying out the door: people call Grand Central Market a food hall, but food halls are invented all at once, by developers. This one accreted, era by era, like the city outside its doors — and every layer is still serving lunch.

When you've eaten what needs eating, head out the far side onto Broadway. Cross at Third Street, angling left, and look for a modest brick office building on the corner, five stories, giving away absolutely nothing from the sidewalk — which turns out to be the whole point. That's the Bradbury Building, finished in 1893, and it's hiding the most famous interior in Los Angeles.
