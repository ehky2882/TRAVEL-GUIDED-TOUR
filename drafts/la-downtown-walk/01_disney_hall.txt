You're at the top of the hill, and the building in front of you is doing something buildings aren't supposed to do — billowing. Stainless-steel sails, curved and stacked and catching the light, no two panels quite alike. This is Walt Disney Concert Hall, home of the Los Angeles Philharmonic, opened in 2003, designed by Frank Gehry — and it took sixteen years to get here.

The story starts in 1987, when Lillian Disney, Walt's widow, gave the city fifty million dollars for a concert hall in her husband's name. Then came the part LA does best: delays, budget fights, a stalled site. The county built the six-story parking garage first; then the money ran out, and for most of the nineties the garage sat under an empty lot with nothing on top of it. It took a civic rescue campaign in the late nineties to finally get the steel out of the drawings.

Walk toward the entrance and look at the surface as you go. Those curves aren't just for show — inside, the hall is lined in Douglas fir and shaped around the orchestra, and musicians rank it among the finest rooms for sound anywhere in the world. A car city, famous for listening to everything through a windshield, finally built itself a room to hear.

One gift before you move on: there's a public garden hidden up on the roof terrace, free to enter, with a fountain shaped like an enormous rose and covered entirely in broken Delft porcelain — Lillian Disney collected it, and the builders smashed and reset thousands of pieces in her memory.

When you're ready, walk south along Grand Avenue, one block, staying on this side of the street. The building coming up ahead looks like a sheet of white coral pulled taut over a box — that's The Broad, and it's twelve years younger than the steel behind you. The clock has started running backward.
