MOCA Grand Avenue is easy to underestimate — it's low where its neighbors are loud, red sandstone and green pyramids, most of it sunk below the street. The architect was Arata Isozaki, and this was his first American commission, finished in 1986. He buried the galleries deliberately: you descend into them, and the pyramids overhead are skylights, feeding daylight down onto the art.

Inside is one of the country's great collections of postwar art — Rothko, Rauschenberg, Pollock — and like its neighbor across the street, general admission is free. But the thing to understand, standing here, is the date. In 1986, Grand Avenue was not a destination. Bunker Hill's Victorian neighborhood had been razed in the sixties — an entire hilltop of houses cleared for a redevelopment that then took decades to actually arrive — and for years this museum sat among the parking lots and the wind, betting the city would eventually show up around it.

The city did. Disney Hall in 2003, The Broad in 2015, the towers and plazas filling in between. MOCA anchored the hill before the hill was anything, which makes it less the quiet sibling on this street and more the reason the street exists.

[beat]

Now for the best transition in Los Angeles. Continue past the museum into California Plaza — the broad courtyard among the towers just beyond, with a watercourt at its center. Make for its far edge, where the hill drops away, and you'll find a small station house and two vintage railway cars waiting on a steep track. That's Angels Flight, built in 1901, and it's how we get off Bunker Hill.
