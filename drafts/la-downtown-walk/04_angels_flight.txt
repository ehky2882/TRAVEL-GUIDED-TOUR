Two cars, one track, two hundred ninety-eight feet — this is Angels Flight, billed for over a century as the shortest railway in the world. The fare is a dollar fifty; pay at the station or tap a Metro card. The cars are named Sinai and Olivet, they ride the same cable, and they counterweight each other — one goes down because the other comes up. They've been doing this, on and off, since 1901.

The "off" is the story. Angels Flight was built when Bunker Hill was a neighborhood — Victorian mansions gone genteelly to seed as rooming houses — and it hauled residents up from the shops on Hill Street for a penny a ride. When the city leveled the entire hilltop in the sixties, the railway was dismantled and put in a warehouse, where it sat for twenty-seven years before being rebuilt half a block from its original line. It has closed and reopened more than once since; Angelenos treat a running Angels Flight as a small civic victory, because history says it is one.

Board when a car's ready, and as you ride, think about what the ride is. Everything you've seen so far was built after this railway — the concert hall, the veil, the museum. You're descending out of the city's newest layer in its oldest machine.

[beat]

At the bottom, the doors open onto Hill Street. Directly across from you is a doorway into a long brick market building — that's the back entrance of Grand Central Market, which has been feeding downtown without a break since 1917. Cross carefully and walk straight in; we'll come out the other side, on Broadway.
