The white honeycomb ahead of you is The Broad — rhymes with road, named for Eli and Edythe Broad, who filled it. The skin is called the veil: two thousand five hundred panels of fiberglass-reinforced concrete, lifted at the corners like a curtain caught mid-raise. Underneath it sits the vault — the concrete core where most of the collection actually lives. The architects, Diller Scofidio and Renfro, put the storage at the heart of the building and then let you look at it; ride the escalator inside and you tunnel straight through the vault on your way up.

Here's the number that matters: zero. That's the admission. Eli Broad made billions building tract homes and selling insurance, spent decades collecting postwar and contemporary art — Warhol, Basquiat, Kusama, more than two thousand works — and then built a museum downtown and made it free, permanently. Whatever you make of billionaires putting their names on things, the line down the block most mornings is real, and it's mostly Angelenos.

You'll notice the construction alongside — the museum is adding a second building, an expansion meant to open before the 2028 Games, with admission staying free. The veil got a vault; now the vault is getting a companion.

And the timing tells you something about this hill. When The Broad opened in 2015, it joined a street that already had a concert hall and a contemporary museum — Grand Avenue as a cultural district was a finished argument. It wasn't always. The next stop was here when none of this existed.

Cross Grand Avenue when you get the chance, and continue south half a block. The low building faced in red sandstone, with copper-green pyramids on its roof, is the Museum of Contemporary Art — MOCA — and it has been holding this corner since 1986, back when Bunker Hill was mostly parking lots.
