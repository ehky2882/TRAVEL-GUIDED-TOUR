Cross into the old plaza — round, tree-shaded, a bandstand at its center, historic buildings ringing it on every side. However you came in, aim for the middle and stop.

You are now standing at the bottom of the walk, in every sense. In September 1781, forty-four settlers — men, women, and children, most of them of African, Indigenous, and mixed heritage, recruited from what is now northern Mexico — established a farming pueblo near this spot, beside a river, close to Yaanga, a village of the Tongva people who had lived on this land for thousands of years. That sentence deserves its weight: the second-largest city in the United States was founded by four dozen people, and the ground you're on is where its whole story starts.

The plaza around you took shape in the 1820s. Facing it stands the Avila Adobe, built in 1818 and the oldest house still standing in the city, and just off it runs Olvera Street — the brick-paved lane of puestos, market stalls, many run by the same families across generations. The marketplace opened in 1930, after a preservation campaign led by Christine Sterling saved this block from demolition, and like a great deal of Los Angeles it's part genuine article, part staged romance — a distinction that stops mattering somewhere around the ninety-year mark.

One more thing to find: above Olvera Street, on an upper wall, the ghost of a mural painted in 1932 by the Mexican artist David Alfaro Siqueiros. Its political message got it whitewashed almost immediately; conservators spent decades recovering what survived. The city censored it, then saved it — both impulses, same wall.

[beat]

Now turn and look back the way you came. City Hall, and beyond it, up on the hill, the towers and the steel sails where you started. You've walked from 2003 to 1781 — from the newest room in the city to its founding ground — in about a mile and a half. Every city has layers. Los Angeles, of all places, turns out to be the one that lets you walk straight down through them.

That's the walk. Stay in the plaza a while — it's a good place to end at the beginning.
