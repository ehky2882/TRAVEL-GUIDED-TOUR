Everyone will tell you Los Angeles is not a walking city, and mostly they're right — the places worth seeing sit ten or twenty miles apart with a freeway in between. This walk is the exception. Seven stops, about a mile and a half, one hill, and you never need your car once.

Here's the trick of it: the walk runs backward. You'll start at the top of Bunker Hill among the newest architecture in the city — stainless steel from 2003, a honeycomb veil from 2015 — and every block you cover, the clock unwinds. A funicular from 1901. A food hall from 1917. A skylit Victorian office building from 1893. And at the bottom of it all, the plaza where the city was founded in 1781, when Los Angeles was forty-four settlers and a river.

Most cities make you work for this — the layers scattered, the origins paved over. Downtown Los Angeles stacked everything on one slope, oldest ground at the bottom, newest money at the top, and then built a tiny railway so you don't even have to walk down the steepest part.

[beat]

The route: south along the crest of the hill, then down its face by rail, then north through the old commercial district to the plaza. Flat where it matters, with ninety-six feet of drop handled for a dollar fifty. Start at the corner of First Street and Grand Avenue, in front of the building that looks like it's setting sail — Walt Disney Concert Hall.
