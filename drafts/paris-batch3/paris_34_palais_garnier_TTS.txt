You're in front of the palay garnyay, the great opera house, and from the outside it already looks like it's showing off — gold, marble, columns, statues, a riot of ornament. That's no accident. When the architect Charles garnyay built it in the eighteen seventies, the building wasn't really designed around the opera. It was designed around the audience.

Step inside, if you can, and you'll see it at once. The grand staircase is enormous, a sweep of marble built so that society could descend it — so that ladies in gowns and gentlemen in tails could see and, more to the point, be seen. The foyers glitter like the halls of a palace. For the wealthy of nineteenth-century Paris, a night at the opera was theatre in both directions: there was a performance on the stage, and there was the performance of the audience itself, dressed and arranged across that staircase. The real show, half the time, was the crowd.

Now, the part you've probably heard of. This building is the setting of The Phantom of the Opera — and the eeriest details of that story are real. There genuinely is water beneath the opera house: a large underground reservoir, built because the ground was so wet during construction that the engineers couldn't drain it, so they enclosed it instead. And in eighteen ninety-six, during a performance, one of the great counterweights of the chandelier broke loose and crashed down into the audience, killing a woman. A writer named gaston luhroo took the underground water and the falling chandelier and spun them into a novel about a phantom haunting the cellars. The legend grew straight out of the facts.

Look up at the ceiling inside, too, if you get the chance — the colourful painting overhead was added in the nineteen sixties by Marc shagal, a dreamlike modern ceiling floating above all that old gold.

So: an opera house built mostly so the audience could perform to itself, with a real lake in the basement and a real falling chandelier in its past. The fiction barely had to invent a thing.
