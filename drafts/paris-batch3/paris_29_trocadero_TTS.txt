You're standing on the trokadairoh terrace, and you came here for one reason: this is the spot. Look across the river and there it is — the eyefel tower, head to foot, perfectly framed, the single best place in the whole city to see it whole. This wide stone terrace, with its two great curving wings of a building sweeping away on either side, was practically designed to make you turn your back on it and face the tower.

Which is the quietly funny thing about trokadairoh: it's a grand building, the palay duh shy-oh, raised for a World's Fair in the nineteen thirties — and in most people's minds it doesn't exist as a building at all. It exists as a viewpoint. People arrive, photograph the tower, and leave, having never once looked at the thing they were standing on. It's a monument defined entirely by what it looks at.

But it has had its own moments. On this terrace, in nineteen forty-eight, the nations of the world gathered and adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights — the founding statement that all people are born free and equal in dignity and rights. There's an inscription here marking it. So the place tourists treat as a photo backdrop is also where one of the most important documents of the modern age was proclaimed to the world.

Take your photograph — everyone does, and the view earns it. But then turn around for a second and look at the building behind you, and the wide plaza under your feet. Just so you can say you actually saw the place, and not only the view across the river.
