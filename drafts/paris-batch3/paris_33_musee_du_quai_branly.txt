You're outside the Musée du quai Branly, and even before you go in, the building is doing something unusual — it's trying to disappear into a garden. Look at the wall facing the street: a large part of the facade is alive, a vertical garden of thousands of plants growing straight up the side of the building, a green wall climbing several storeys. The architect, Jean Nouvel, wanted the museum to feel less like a stone monument and more like something half-swallowed by nature, glimpsed through trees.

Inside is a different kind of departure. Where the Louvre and the Orsay tell the story of European art, the quai Branly is devoted to almost everything else — the arts and cultures of Africa, Asia, Oceania and the Americas. Masks, sculptures, textiles, instruments and sacred objects from across the non-Western world, shown not as curiosities but as great art in their own right. When it opened in 2006, that was a central part of its purpose: to put these works on the same footing as anything hanging in the grand European galleries.

It also carries a question it doesn't hide from, and neither will we. Many objects in museums like this one left their original homes long ago, in the age of empire, and how they came to be in Paris — bought, gifted, or taken — is a live and sometimes painful debate. In recent years France has begun returning some pieces to the countries they came from. The museum sits squarely in the middle of one of the real open questions of our time: who should hold the cultural treasures of the world, and where do they truly belong?

You don't have to settle that to be moved by what's inside. Go in, look closely, and let the objects speak for themselves. Then come back out and look at the green wall one more time — a museum slowly being reclaimed by the living world, which feels, somehow, like the right note for everything it holds.
