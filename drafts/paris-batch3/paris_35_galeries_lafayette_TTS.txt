You're inside galree lafayet — and if you've just walked in off the street expecting a department store, stop and look straight up, because you're standing under one of the most beautiful ceilings in Paris. High overhead is an enormous dome of coloured glass and gilded ironwork, glowing like the rose window of a cathedral, with tiers of balconied shopfronts rising all around you toward the light. It's breathtaking — and it's selling handbags.

That's the whole idea, and it was a radical one when this dome went up in nineteen twelve. The department store was still a fairly new invention then — the notion of gathering every kind of merchandise under one roof and making the act of shopping itself a glamorous experience. And the people who built these places understood something: if you want buying to feel important, even sacred, you build it like a temple. So they gave it a dome worthy of a church, a soaring central hall, light pouring down from above — all to make purchasing a scarf feel like a visit to somewhere grand. A cathedral of commerce, quite deliberately.

It worked. Crowds came as much for the spectacle as the goods, and the great glass dome became one of the sights of the city in its own right — which is why you'll see plenty of people here, like you, just looking up, not shopping at all.

Before you go, there's a reward most people miss. Head for the rooftop — it's free, and open to anyone. From up there you get a wide, sweeping view across the rooftops of Paris: the opera house just below, and the eyefel tower off in the distance. One of the best free views in the city, sitting on top of a shop.

So look up at the dome, then go up to the roof. The store would love to sell you something in between — but it won't insist.
