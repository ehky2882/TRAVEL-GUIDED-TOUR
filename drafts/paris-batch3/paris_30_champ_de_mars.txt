You're on the Champ de Mars, the long green lawn that runs from the foot of the Eiffel Tower to the elegant old buildings at the far end. Today it's the most relaxed place in Paris — people sprawl on the grass with wine and bread, the tower rising over them. But the name gives away a very different past: Champ de Mars means the Field of Mars, the Roman god of war.

It was a parade ground. Those grand buildings at the far end are the old military academy, and for centuries this lawn was where soldiers drilled and marched — and among the cadets who once trained here was a young officer named Napoleon Bonaparte. The picnic blanket you might be sitting on is spread over a former field of war.

It saw the Revolution up close, too. In 1790, on the first anniversary of the fall of the Bastille, a vast crowd — hundreds of thousands of people — gathered here for an enormous festival of national unity, one of the great hopeful moments of the early Revolution. But the very next year the same ground saw bloodshed, when troops fired on a crowd of protesters gathered here. The Field of Mars lived up to its name.

Now it's grass and tourists and the tower's evening sparkle. There's something to that — that a ground named for war, drilled by armies and marked by the Revolution, has ended up as the gentlest lawn in the city, where the only thing anyone points at the sky is a phone camera. Find a clear patch, lie back, and look straight up at the iron.
