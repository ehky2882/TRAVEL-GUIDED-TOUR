You're looking at Les Invalides, and the gold dome rising over it is one of the brightest things in the Paris skyline — covered in real gold leaf, it catches the sun from kilometres away. But the gold is the smaller story here. Look at the long, severe buildings spread out beneath it, because that's where this place begins.

Les Invalides was built in the sixteen seventies by Louis the Fourteenth, the Sun King, for a genuinely humane reason: to house and care for soldiers who had been wounded or grown old in his wars. Before this, broken-down veterans often ended up begging in the streets. This was, in effect, one of the first great veterans' homes and hospitals — a place for the men the wars had used up. Parts of it still serve old soldiers today, alongside a vast military museum.

Now, the dome. The golden church beneath it holds the most famous resident of all: Napoleon. After he died in exile on a remote South Atlantic island, France eventually brought his body home, in 1840, in a solemn procession that passed through the Arc de Triomphe and across the city to here. And then they buried him in extraordinary fashion.

Go in and look down into the open circular crypt at the great reddish-brown stone tomb in the centre. Inside that single massive sarcophagus the emperor's body rests not in one coffin but in six, nested one inside another like a set of Russian dolls — tin, mahogany, two of lead, ebony, and oak. A man who spent his life pushing outward, conquering Europe, sealed at the end inside six layers of box beneath a dome of gold.

There's a hard irony in standing here. The gold dome was raised to shelter the soldiers war had wounded; and at its very centre now lies the general who, more than almost anyone, sent men to be wounded. A home for the broken, watched over by the man who broke so many. Stand at the rail and look down at the tomb a while before you go.
