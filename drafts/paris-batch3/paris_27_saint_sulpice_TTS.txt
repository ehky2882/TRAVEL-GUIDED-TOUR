You're in front of san soolpees, one of the largest churches in Paris — nearly the size of notruh dam. Look up at the two towers on the front and you'll notice they don't match: one is plainer, one more ornate. They were built by different architects, decades apart, and the second was never brought into line with the first. The church simply gave up and left them mismatched.

Inside, though, are two genuinely surprising things. The first is set into the floor. Walk toward the north side and you'll find a thin brass strip running across the stone, leading to a tall white marble obelisk. This is a nohmon — an astronomical instrument. A small hole high in a south-facing window lets a single ray of sunlight in, and at noon it falls onto this brass line; where it lands marks the date. The priests built it for a practical reason: to fix the date of Easter each year, which shifts with the sun and the moon. A scientific instrument, built into the floor of a church, to keep the religious calendar honest.

You may have heard of this brass line for the wrong reason. A hugely popular novel claimed it marked an ancient secret meridian tied to a grand conspiracy — none of which is true, and the parish grew annoyed enough to post a notice inside saying so. It's a sun-clock, nothing more, and that's plenty.

The second treasure is in a chapel just inside the door on the right: a set of murals by Eugène duhlakrwah, among the last great works he ever painted. One shows Jacob wrestling the angel — two figures locked together in a struggle, set against real trees. He worked on them at the very end of his life, and you can feel it; they have the urgency of a painter racing the clock.

So: a machine for tracking the sun set into the floor, and a dying master's final paintings on the wall. Two kinds of light, really — one let in through a pinhole, one mixed on a palette. Stand in the quiet and find them both.
