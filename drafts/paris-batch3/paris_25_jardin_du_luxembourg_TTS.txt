You're in the zhardan dew looksomboor, the most beloved garden on the Left Bank, and the surprising thing about it is that it was built out of one woman's homesickness.

Look toward the large palace at the north end — that's the looksomboor palace, home today to the French Senate. It was built in the early sixteen hundreds for maree duh maydeesee, the Italian-born queen of France. She was from Florence, and after her husband died she was lonely in Paris, missing the city of her childhood. So she had a palace and gardens built deliberately to recall the Florentine ones she'd grown up among — a piece of Tuscany rebuilt on the far bank of the senn. The garden you're standing in began as a cure for one queen's longing for home.

Now look at how people use it, because it has a quiet genius. There are almost no fixed benches. Instead, scattered everywhere, are hundreds of those green metal chairs — loose, movable, yours to drag wherever you like. Want sun, shade, the edge of the pond, a spot near friends? Move your chair. It sounds small, but it's why the garden feels less like a park you pass through and more like a vast shared living room you arrange to suit yourself.

Find the big octagonal pond in the centre. On weekends, children push old wooden sailboats out across it with long sticks, the same simple pleasure their great-grandparents had here. Off to one side, half-hidden, is the maydeechee fountain — a shaded, romantic grotto maree built to remember Italy, the most Florentine corner of the whole place.

So this is a homesick queen's Florence, opened up and handed to the city — a private cure for loneliness that became everyone's favourite room. Grab a chair, drag it somewhere that suits you, and stay a while. That's exactly what it's for.
