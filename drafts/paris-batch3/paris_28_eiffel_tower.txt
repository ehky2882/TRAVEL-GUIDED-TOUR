You're at the foot of the Eiffel Tower, looking up through the iron at the most recognisable structure on earth — so here's the thing almost nobody believes: it was supposed to be temporary, and plenty of Parisians wanted it gone.

It was built for the World's Fair of 1889, to mark a hundred years since the Revolution. Gustave Eiffel's company won the right to put up the tallest structure in the world — three hundred metres of iron, far taller than anything ever made — and the permit ran for twenty years. After that, it was to be taken down and sold for scrap.

And the cultured classes of Paris hated it. A group of the city's most famous artists and writers signed a furious public protest while it was still going up, calling it a monstrous iron eyesore that would disgrace the city. There's a story that the novelist Guy de Maupassant ate lunch in the tower's restaurant every day — because it was the one place in Paris from which he couldn't see the tower itself.

So why is it still here? Not because the city fell in love with it; that came later. It survived because it turned out to be useful. As the twenty-year deadline approached, the tower had become the perfect mount for the brand-new technology of wireless telegraphy — a giant antenna the military didn't want to lose. Radio saved the Eiffel Tower. Sentiment had nothing to do with it; a transmitter on top did.

For forty years it stayed the tallest structure in the world, until New York's skyscrapers finally passed it. It's repainted by hand every few years, and on a hot day it grows — the iron expands and the whole tower stands a little taller in summer than in winter.

So look up again. The symbol of Paris, of France, of romance itself, is a temporary structure that the tastemakers despised and a radio aerial rescued. It only became beloved once it was safe — which may be the most human thing about it.
