You're looking at the Musée d'Orsay, and the first thing to understand is that it was never meant to be a museum at all — it was built to be a railway station. Stand back and you can still read it: the great arched roof, the vast hall meant for platforms, and high on the facade two enormous round clocks that once told travellers when their trains were leaving.

It went up for the World's Fair of 1900 — the grandest, most central station in Paris, right on the river facing the Louvre. But it had a fatal flaw: its platforms were too short for the longer trains that came later, and within forty years it was effectively useless as a station. For decades it sat half-abandoned, nearly torn down more than once. Then someone had the idea to turn the empty hall into a museum — and it opened, reborn, in 1986.

And here's the lovely fit. The art they filled it with is the art of roughly the same moment the station was built: the second half of the eighteen hundreds, the age of the Impressionists. Monet, Manet, Degas, Renoir, Van Gogh, Cézanne — the painters who walked away from grand historical scenes and started painting the modern world instead: train stations, riverbanks, dancers, ordinary afternoons. They painted the railway age, and now they hang inside a monument to it.

So the building and its contents are the same era talking to each other. The painters captured the new world of steam and leisure; the station was a piece of that new world; and when the trains outgrew it, it became the perfect frame for the pictures.

Don't leave without going up to one of those giant clocks. From behind, the clock face is translucent — you can stand inside the mechanism and look out through the great glass dial at the rooftops of Paris, the city framed inside the very instrument that once governed the timetables. It's the best view in the building, and almost nobody rushes to find it.
