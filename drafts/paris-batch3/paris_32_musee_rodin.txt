You're at the Musée Rodin, and it might be the most pleasant museum in Paris, because a good half of it is outdoors. This was the Hôtel Biron, a graceful eighteenth-century mansion with large gardens, and in his final years the sculptor Auguste Rodin lived and worked here. He struck a deal with the French state: he would give the nation all of his works, on the condition that the mansion become a museum devoted to them. He died in 1917; the museum opened two years later, and it has been here ever since.

Go into the garden first — that's the point of this place. Set among the roses and clipped hedges and gravel paths are Rodin's great bronzes, out in the open air, weathering and catching the light exactly as, in his view, sculpture was meant to. There's something right about meeting them under the sky rather than in a white room.

Find The Thinker. You know the figure — seated, hunched forward, chin on hand, lost in thought. But here's what most people don't know: he wasn't conceived as a standalone everyman. He began as a small figure meant to sit at the very top of an enormous sculpted doorway Rodin laboured over for decades, called The Gates of Hell, based on Dante's Inferno. And the thinker at the top was Dante himself — the poet, looking down over the damned souls writhing in the gates below, contemplating the whole of human suffering. Only later was he enlarged and set on his own pedestal, where he quietly became a symbol of thought itself.

So the most famous statue of a man thinking is really a poet staring down into Hell. Once you've seen the Gates here in the garden — and you can — you'll never quite see The Thinker the innocent way again.

Take your time among the bronzes. Few museums let you wander the masterpieces with birdsong and open sky around them. This one does, and that's the gift.
