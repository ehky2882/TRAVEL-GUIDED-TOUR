You're standing in front of Saint-Germain-des-Prés — and that stout stone tower ahead of you is, quite possibly, the oldest thing still standing in Paris. There's been an abbey on this spot since the sixth century, and the tower itself goes back about a thousand years, to a time when this was open country outside the city walls — des Prés means "of the meadows." For centuries this was one of the most powerful monasteries in France.

Step inside if it's open; it's plain and ancient and worth a moment. But the strange twist of this place is what happened just outside its doors, much more recently.

In the years after the Second World War, the cafés on the corner here — two in particular, the Café de Flore and Les Deux Magots, almost next door to each other — became the headquarters of French intellectual life. The philosophers Jean-Paul Sartre and Simone de Beauvoir worked at these tables for hours, day after day; the writer Albert Camus was part of the same circle. They sat and wrote and argued over coffee, and out of that café world came existentialism, the philosophy that defined a generation. Down the side streets, jazz clubs filled the cellars. For a couple of decades, this small corner was the centre of the thinking world.

So look at what shares this one intersection: a thousand-year-old abbey church, and the café tables where twentieth-century philosophers reinvented what it meant to be free. The sacred and the secular, faith and philosophy, separated by a few metres of street.

The cafés are still here, still serving — though now you pay handsomely for a coffee at the same tables, and you're more likely to share the room with tourists than philosophers. Sit anyway. The view of that ancient tower, from a café table where modern philosophy was hammered out, is a very Parisian kind of layered.
