You're in front of la madlen, and your first question is probably the right one: is this even a church? It doesn't look like one. No spire, no cross on the front, no visible dome — just a huge rectangular box wrapped on every side by fifty-two towering columns, looking for all the world like a Greek or Roman temple lifted out of the ancient world and set down in the middle of Paris. And yet it is a church, a working Catholic one. The disguise is the whole story.

It had a famously confused life. Building started, stopped, and started again over the better part of a century, and during that time nobody could agree what it should be. It began as a church. Then Napoleon halted the work and declared it would instead be a Temple of Glory dedicated to his armies — a monument to soldiers, not to God. Plans were even floated, at various points, to turn it into the city's first grand railway station, or a bank, or a library. Only after Napoleon's fall was it finally finished — as a church again, but keeping the pagan-temple shell it had been given along the way.

So what you're looking at is a building that spent decades unsure whether it honoured a god, an emperor's army, or a train timetable — and ended up a Christian church that looks defiantly like a temple to no god in particular.

Climb the steps and look back the way you came. la madlen sits at the head of a straight line that runs across the river to the national parliament — two matching temple-fronts staring at each other across the city, a deliberate piece of grand planning. And inside, behind that severe exterior, it's surprisingly rich and dim and gold; the composer Frédéric shoh-pan's funeral was held here, with Mozart's Requiem filling the space.

A temple on the outside, a church within, and very nearly a railway station. Only in Paris.
