Those rings and bars in the sand, just south of the pier — that patch of beach invented the American gym.

In 1934, in the middle of the Depression, the Works Progress Administration installed exercise equipment here as a public works project: bars, rings, a platform, free to anyone. What the government got for its money was a spectacle nobody ordered. Gymnasts, acrobats, circus performers, Hollywood stunt people, and a new species entirely — the bodybuilder — turned this stretch of sand into an open-air theater of the human body, with crowds packing in on weekends to watch. The regulars here became the founding fathers and mothers of the entire fitness industry: Jack LaLanne, decades before television made him America's trainer; Joe Gold, before there was a Gold's Gym; and Pudgy Stockton, the barbell-pressing star who proved to a skeptical country that strength belonged to women too.

Then it ended. Santa Monica shut Muscle Beach down in 1959 and bulldozed the platform, and the lifters did the only sensible thing — they picked up and moved down the beach to Venice, along the same path you're walking today, where the city of Los Angeles had a modest weight pen waiting. The fitness boom didn't die; it just changed addresses. You'll pass its second home later on this walk.

[beat]

Santa Monica eventually thought better of the whole affair, and rededicated this spot as the original Muscle Beach — rings, ropes, and bars restored, still free, still busy with acrobats most afternoons. Watch for a minute if someone's flying. Then keep south on the path. The next stretch is a long, easy one, and it ends at the loudest quarter mile in Los Angeles: the Venice Beach Boardwalk.
