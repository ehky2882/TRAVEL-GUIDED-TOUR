Walk out onto the deck, past the carousel building, until you can feel the boards flex a little under your feet. You're standing on the first concrete pier on the West Coast, opened in 1909 — and it was built, gloriously, to carry a sewer pipe. The city needed to run treated waste out past the surf, engineers needed a structure to hold the pipe, and Santa Monica decided that if it was building a pier anyway, the public might as well be allowed to stroll on it. The pipe is long gone. The strolling stuck.

A century of pleasure got grafted onto that piece of plumbing. The carousel you passed spins inside a 1920s hippodrome building, one of the oldest structures out here. The Ferris wheel at Pacific Park runs on solar power — it makes its own light for the postcard. And somewhere near the deck you'll find the marker for Route 66, because this pier is the symbolic end of the Mother Road: two thousand-plus miles from Chicago, finishing in the Pacific air.

Here's what makes this pier matter, though. It isn't that it's the best of its kind — it's that it's the last. A hundred years ago this coastline was crowded with pleasure piers, each gaudier than the next, stacked with ballrooms and roller coasters, and one by one they burned or fell or were torn down. This is the survivor. When you stand here, you're standing on an entire extinct species of American fun, down to its final specimen — still open every single day.

When you've had your fill of the view, head back to the sand and turn south on the beachfront path. Within a few hundred yards you'll see rings and bars rising out of the beach on your right. That's the original Muscle Beach — and yes, original is doing real work in that sentence.
