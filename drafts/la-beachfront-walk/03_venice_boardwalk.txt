You'll know you've arrived by ear before anything else — drums, boom boxes, sales pitches, somebody's electric guitar. This is Ocean Front Walk, the Venice Beach Boardwalk, and it is the single strangest outcome of a business plan in the history of Los Angeles.

Because here's what this was supposed to be. In 1905, a tobacco millionaire named Abbot Kinney opened "Venice of America" on this marshland — canals, imported gondoliers, arcaded buildings, an amusement pier — conceived as a cultural resort where Americans would come for lectures, concerts, and moral improvement by the sea. The public skipped the lectures and stampeded to the roller coasters, and Kinney, to his credit, took the hint and gave them a carnival — the Coney Island of the Pacific. He died in 1920; his grand pier burned weeks later; the canals were mostly paved; and by the 1950s his dream city had decayed into what the papers called the slum by the sea. Which is exactly when it got interesting — cheap rent by the ocean pulled in the poets, the artists, the misfits, and the boardwalk grew into what surrounds you now: an unbroken line of street performers, vendors, muralists, fortune tellers, and one of the most famous skate plazas on earth.

As you walk it, look for the outdoor weight pen with the concrete barbell on its roof — Muscle Beach Venice, where the Santa Monica lifters landed after 1959, and where bodybuilding grew from beach curiosity into an industry.

[beat]

Kinney built a stage for high culture and got the greatest low-culture promenade in America instead — and if his record is any guide, he'd have counted the crowd and called it a win. When you reach Windward Avenue, turn inland, cross the main road, and follow the signs a few short blocks to the water you can hear nothing from: the Venice Canals.
