The last stop on this walk is a street that sells one product, and the product is the idea of cool.

Abbot Kinney Boulevard runs about a mile on the diagonal, lined with boutiques, galleries, and restaurants you've read about — and for most of its life it was nothing of the kind. This was West Washington Boulevard, a scruffy strip of repair shops, storefront churches, and cheap studio space, which is precisely why artists moved in when Venice was still the slum by the sea. Around 1990, locals had it renamed for the neighborhood's founder, and then the strip did the most Venice thing imaginable: it got discovered. Tech money arrived one street over, the boutiques followed, and in 2012, GQ crowned it the coolest block in America — after which, longtime tenants will tell you, the rents did what rents do. Some people here will say the street was saved; others, that it was bought. Both can point at the same storefronts to make their case, and that argument — who gets to keep a place once the world falls in love with it — is the most honest thing on the boulevard.

So end the walk with the man himself. Abbot Kinney spent his fortune building a dream city that failed at nearly everything it planned — the culture flopped, the pier burned, the canals were paved — and succeeded, wildly, at everything it never intended. The carnival boardwalk. The body-builders. The artists in the cheap studios. And now a street that trades on his name, selling the one thing his Venice actually produced: the feeling that life could be lived differently, closer to the water.

[beat]

A sewer pipe became a postcard, a relief project became an industry, a failed resort became a way of life. That's the invented seaside — invented, mostly, by accident. Go find a coffee on Abbot Kinney's street. You've walked his whole imagination, end to end.
