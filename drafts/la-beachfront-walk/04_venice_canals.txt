Stop on one of the little arched footbridges and listen. Two blocks from the loudest promenade in Los Angeles, you can hear ducks.

These are the Venice Canals — the surviving handful of Abbot Kinney's waterways, dug in 1905 when this whole district was laced with them and gondoliers poled visitors past brand-new cottages. They were the heart of the sales pitch, the thing that made "Venice of America" more than a name. And they lost. The car arrived, residents wanted streets instead of water, and in the paving campaigns that followed Venice's annexation by Los Angeles in the mid-twenties, most of the canal network disappeared under asphalt. Traffic drives over Kinney's grand canals today without knowing they're there. This small grid south of the main road survived largely because it wasn't worth the cost of filling — the losing remnant of a bet against the automobile, in the city where the automobile won everything.

Then the strangest reversal on this whole walk: losing turned out to be the smartest thing the canals ever did. Left as a backwater, they slid into decades of neglect — and were finally restored in the early 1990s, sidewalks rebuilt, water clean, bridges repaired. Now the quietest real estate in Venice is the part that progress skipped, and the houses along these walkways are some of the most coveted on the Westside. No cars can reach the front doors. That's the amenity.

[beat]

Take one slow loop — over a bridge, along a walkway, back over the next bridge. Then head inland a few blocks to the diagonal street full of people carrying shopping bags and expensive coffee. It's named for the man who dug these canals, and it's the final stop: Abbot Kinney Boulevard.
