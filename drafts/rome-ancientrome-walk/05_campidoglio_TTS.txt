Take the kor doh nah tah slowly — it was designed to be climbed slowly, a ramp rather than a staircase, wide enough for a procession. At the top, between the two colossal marble horsemen, step into the piazza and stop.

You're standing on the summit of the Capitoline Hill, in a square designed by Michelangelo, on the spot where Rome kept its most important temple for a thousand years. And here's the thing worth knowing at the end of this walk rather than the beginning: you've just retraced, more or less, the last stretch of a Roman triumph. When a victorious general paraded into the city, the route ran past where the Colosseum now stands, through the Forum along the Sacred Way, and ended up here — on this hill, at the temple of Jupiter, where the parade stopped and the sacrifice was made. Emperors walked the mile you just walked, in the other direction of history.

Now cross the piazza, keeping the trapezoid of Michelangelo's paving underfoot, and find the gap to the left of the palace at the back — a narrow lane called vee ah del kahm pee doh lyoh. Thirty seconds down it, the city falls away.

There it is: the Roman Forum, from above this time, and beyond it, at the far end of everything, the Colosseum where you started. The whole walk, in one frame.

A kilometre and a half, and look what it held — a stadium the modern world still copies, an arch built from older arches, a dictator's road being handed back to walkers, a shopping centre nineteen centuries old, and a hilltop where the parades ended. None of it preserved under glass; all of it reused, relabelled, still in service. That's been the argument of this walk, and it's the one thing about Rome worth carrying home: this city doesn't keep its past. It keeps using it.
