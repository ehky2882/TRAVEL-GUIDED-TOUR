You should be on the north side of the Colosseum, the flat open stretch between the amphitheatre and the Metro station, looking at the great outer wall where it still rises to its full height.

Look above the ground-level arches. Carved over each one, if you find the right stretch of wall, is a Roman numeral — XXIII, XXIV, XXV, still legible after nineteen centuries. Those are gate numbers. The building had eighty entrances, seventy-six of them numbered, and your ticket — a shard of pottery or bone — told you which gate, which staircase, which level. Some fifty thousand people could find their seats in minutes and empty out even faster, through passages the architects called vomitoria, which meant exactly what it sounds like: the building disgorging its crowd.

That's the thing to hold onto here, because everyone talks about what the Colosseum staged and almost no one talks about how it worked. This was crowd engineering, solved in the first century — the numbered gates, the ticketing that matched them, the one-way circulation — and it worked so well that when the modern world started building stadiums again, it didn't improve on the system. It copied it. The arena you last watched football in is running Roman software.

And it was fast. The whole thing went up in less than a decade, opened in 80 AD, and stayed in business for the better part of four hundred years — longer than most countries have existed.

Before you move on, look at the wall one more time and find a numbered arch for yourself. It's a small thing, a gate number carved in travertine — and somehow more transporting than the whole silhouette.

Now walk to your right, keeping the Colosseum on your left, and round its flank. In a couple of minutes a three-bayed marble arch appears on the open ground just beyond — the Arch of Constantine. That's our next stop.
