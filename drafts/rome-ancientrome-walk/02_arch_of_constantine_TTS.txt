The arch in front of you is the largest Roman triumphal arch still standing — twenty-one metres of marble, three bays, raised by the Senate in three fifteen to celebrate the emperor Constantine's victory over his rival at the Milvian Bridge. Stand back far enough to take in the whole face of it.

Here's what the Senate didn't advertise: most of what you're looking at is second-hand. The round hunting medallions were carved for Hadrian, two hundred years earlier. The statues of captive day shuns along the top were lifted from a monument to Trajan. The big rectangular panels up on the attic came from a monument to Marcus Aurelius. And on nearly all of them, the sculptors did one small, remarkable thing — they recut the emperor's face. Look closely at the figures and you're watching Hadrian and Marcus Aurelius wear Constantine's head.

Only the thin horizontal frieze above the smaller arches was carved new, and you can see the difference: the older panels are deep, muscular, alive; the new band is flatter, stiffer, more like a diagram. Historians have argued for centuries about why — whether the great workshops had declined, or whether a new emperor from the frontier was deliberately dressing himself in the wardrobe of the good emperors everyone still trusted. Both can be true at once. Either way, this is the boldest piece of recycling in a city built on it: legitimacy, cut and pasted.

One more thing this arch has seen. In nineteen sixty, the Olympic marathon finished right here, under floodlights — and an Ethiopian runner named ah beh beh bih kee lah crossed the line barefoot, the first Black African to win Olympic gold — in the capital of the country whose armies had occupied his own only a generation before.

When you're ready, put the arch at your back and walk toward the wide boulevard that runs away from the Colosseum in a dead straight line. That's vee ah day foh ree eem peh ree ah lee — and the next stretch of this walk happens along it, with the Roman Forum opening up on your left.
