This walk covers about a kilometre and a half, and roughly a thousand years. It begins at the largest building the Romans ever put up and ends on the hill where their city was born — and along the way, it makes one argument over and over: Rome doesn't preserve its past so much as reuse it, relabel it, and keep it in service.

The route is simple. From the Colosseum you'll round its flank to a marble arch, then walk the long straight boulevard called vee ah day foh ree eem peh ree ah lee, with the Roman Forum opening up beside you, to a column carved like a comic strip — and finish with a climb up a ramped staircase to the top of the Capitoline Hill. The ground is flat until that final climb, and the climb is gentle. One honest note: the boulevard still carries buses and taxis along part of its length, so use the crossings; the pavements on both sides are wide, and the walking is easy.

Give it an hour and a half at a stroll, longer if the Forum railings catch you — they usually do.

Start on the north side of the Colosseum, the side facing the Metro station, far enough back that you can see the full curve of the wall. That's where we begin: with the building every stadium on earth is still quietly copying.
