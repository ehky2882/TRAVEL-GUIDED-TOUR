You're walking now, up the long straight boulevard with the Colosseum shrinking behind you. Keep to the left-hand side, and take this one slowly — the railings along here are the best free view in Rome.

Below you on the left, the Roman Forum unrolls: the three great vaults of the Basilica of Maxentius, the Arch of Titus up the slope, and beyond them the columns and broken floors of what was, for centuries, the centre of the known world. You saw it — or will see it — from the terrace on the Capitoline; from down here at the railing it's less a panorama and more a neighbour, close enough to read.

Now, the street you're standing on. It looks eternal. It's not even a hundred years old. In nineteen thirty-two, Mussolini carved this boulevard through the middle of the ancient city so his parades could run from his balcony to the Colosseum in one straight line — flattening a hill, erasing a whole packed neighbourhood, and burying parts of four imperial forums under the asphalt you're walking on. Romans have never stopped arguing about it, and the argument has finally turned into action: the city is now, piece by piece, handing the road back to walkers. You're strolling down the middle of an open question.

While you walk, look down as well as around. The iron drain covers underfoot are stamped ess pee kyoo ar — the Senate and People of Rome, the same four letters the legions carried. The republic fell about two thousand years ago. The plumbing didn't get the memo.

Up ahead, where the boulevard opens out toward pee aht sah veh neh tsee ah, a single white column rises across the street on your right, a spiral of carvings winding up it like a ribbon. That's Trajan's Column — cross at the crossing, walk to the railing nearest it, and I'll meet you there.
