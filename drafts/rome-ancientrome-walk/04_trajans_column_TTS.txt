You're at the railing now, looking at the column — and behind it, climbing the hillside in a curve of brick, a honeycomb of arched openings stacked three storeys high.

The column marks the centre of what was once the grandest of all the imperial forums, built by Trajan in the early hundreds with the loot of his wars. The spiral ribbon of carving that winds to the top tells the story of those wars in more than a hundred and fifty scenes — but you had, or will have, a longer look at that on its own. What this walk wants you to see is the brick curve behind it.

That's Trajan's Markets: a multi-level complex of shops, offices and halls, terraced into the hill, often called — with a wink and an asterisk — the world's first shopping centre. Whatever you call it, it's nineteen hundred years old and structurally sound enough that you can still walk its corridors today. The emperor built his forum for glory; his architect quietly built the thing next door for errands. Guess which one stayed in one piece.

Now for the finale. Face away from the column, cross back over the boulevard, and walk toward the enormous white marble monument ahead — you can't miss it; it's the size of a hillside. Skirt around its left flank, and just behind it you'll find a long, gently ramped staircase rising between two stone lions. That ramp is called the kor doh nah tah, and at the top of it is pee aht sah del kahm pee doh lyoh — the summit of the Capitoline Hill, and the end of our walk.
