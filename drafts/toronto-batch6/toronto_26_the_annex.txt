Step off the noise of Bloor Street onto one of the side streets running north — Admiral, Brunswick, Walmer — and the city changes in half a block. Big red-brick houses, deep porches, turrets and rounded arches, all under a canopy of old trees. This is the Annex, and it's worth knowing two things about it: it invented a house, and it once saved itself.

Start with the name, which is gloriously unromantic. In 1887 the city of Toronto literally annexed this subdivision — absorbed it across the city limits — and the bureaucratic word just stuck. The developers had gambled that Toronto's rich would pay for big lots on leafy streets away from downtown, and they were right; the Eatons and the Gooderhams built their mansions up here.

Now look at the houses. Many are built in what's actually called the Annex style — a local invention, blending heavy American Romanesque arches with British Queen Anne turrets and big show-off attics. The architect usually credited with starting it is E.J. Lennox — yes, the Old City Hall and Casa Loma man, again. His fingerprints are all over this part of the city.

[beat]

But the Annex's real claim is a fight. In the 1960s, the city planned an expressway that would have torn straight through here. The residents organized and stopped it cold — and one of the leaders was Jane Jacobs, the writer who had already beaten a highway through her own neighbourhood in New York before moving to Toronto and doing it all over again. The expressway died. The houses stayed.

That's the thread that runs through this whole city, once you start looking for it — the market that refused to be replaced, the distillery saved by going quiet, the church the mall had to build around. The Annex is where Toronto got good at saying no. Walk a block further and look up. All of this was supposed to be a highway.
