Right next door to the castle, set back behind a garden on the same high ridge, is a large brick house that most visitors walk straight past on their way to Casa Loma. Don't. This is Spadina — and if the castle next door is a fantasy of wealth, this is the real thing.

First, the name. Spadina comes from an Anishinaabe word meaning hill, or a sudden rise of land — which is exactly what you're standing on. This ridge is the ancient shoreline of a prehistoric lake, Lake Iroquois, and it runs right under the castle too. There's a quiet bit of Toronto class history buried in how you say it: the grand old house is Spa-dee-na, while the busy street down the hill is Spa-dye-na. Same word, two accents — once a marker of which side of the money you were on.

The house was home to a single family — the Austins — for well over a century, from 1866 all the way to 1982. James Austin was a banker, and the bank he founded eventually grew into the one whose black towers you'll find downtown today. When the last Austin left, the family gave the house and everything in it to the city, on the condition that it be kept rather than sold off.

[beat]

So the city froze it — and not as a museum of one grand era, but specifically in the 1920s and 1930s, the years between the wars. And it's told from both ends of the house: the family in their drawing room, and the staff on the back stairs who actually ran the place.

That's the gift of Spadina. The castle next door sells you a dream. This house, quietly, shows you how people really lived in one — all of them.
