You're standing on the Prince Edward Viaduct, most people just call it the Bloor Viaduct, the long steel arch that carries Bloor Street across the Don Valley. Look down the line of the bridge: nearly five hundred metres of three graceful arches, forty metres above the valley floor, the city skyline opening to the south and the ravine running green below you. It's one of the great views in Toronto. It's also a monument to believing in a future nobody else could see.

Go back to when it was built, finished in nineteen eighteen. The man behind it was the city's commissioner of works, R.C. Harris, and Harris did something that struck a lot of people as madness. He ordered a second deck built underneath the road, a complete lower level, engineered to carry a subway. Toronto had no subway. Voters had rejected the idea at the ballot box, more than once. There were barely any trains, and the land east of the valley was mostly empty fields. People grumbled about spending wartime money on a bridge for trains that didn't exist, to carry people who weren't there yet.

The lower deck sat empty for forty-eight years. Then, in nineteen sixty-six, the Bloor-Danforth subway finally crossed the Don Valley, running straight along the deck Harris had built half a century earlier, and saving the city millions. The trains you can feel rumbling beneath your feet right now are running on a bet placed before they were invented.

There's a second layer to this place. In nineteen eighty-seven, the writer Michael on dah chee set part of a novel here, In the Skin of a Lion, and gave the bridge back to the immigrant workers who actually built it, the men whose names the official records never bothered to keep. His idea, roughly, was that a city has to be imagined before it can be seen. This bridge is exactly that.

And the fine screen of rods beside you is called the Luminous Veil, added in two thousand three, the city's quiet way of looking after the people who cross here. Stand a moment. You're standing on something that was dreamed long before it was ever real.
