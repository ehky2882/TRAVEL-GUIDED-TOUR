Look up. The grey stone towers and battlements above you belong to Casa Loma — Spanish for "hill house" — and it is, by any reasonable measure, a castle, dropped onto a ridge in the middle of a North American city. It's the largest house ever built in Canada. It's also a monument to a fortune that was already draining away as the walls went up.

The man who built it was Sir Henry Pellatt, and you've met his architect already, in a sense: E.J. Lennox, the same man behind Old City Hall and the rebuilt wing of the legislature at Queen's Park. Pellatt handed him a near-blank cheque, and between 1911 and 1914 Lennox spent it — ninety-eight rooms, secret passages, a tunnel running underground to the stables, an oven big enough to roast an ox, and one of the first private homes in the city wired throughout for electric light.

That last detail is the whole story. Pellatt made his fortune on electricity. He was one of the men who harnessed Niagara Falls and lit up Toronto, and at his peak he was worth something like seventeen million dollars in the money of the day. He poured it into this hill.

Then two things happened. The First World War froze his plans, and the grand basement, with its planned swimming pool, was never finished. And Ontario decided that power this important shouldn't belong to one rich man at all. A public-electricity movement, riding the idea that hydro should be as free as the air, took the private companies into public hands. The thing Pellatt had built his life on became a public utility.

[beat]

He lived here less than ten years. The taxes climbed, the debts mounted, and in 1923 he walked away from the castle and auctioned off its contents for a fraction of their worth. He died, years later, with almost nothing.

So stand here and read it for what it is — not just a rich man's folly, but a kind of headstone for the idea that one person should own the light. The view from up here, out over the city he helped electrify, is still one of the best in Toronto. He just didn't get to keep it.
