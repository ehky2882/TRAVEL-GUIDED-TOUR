Stand back far enough to see the roof, because the roof is the whole point. You're looking at the Grand Palais — a long stone building on the outside, but under that masonry a single vast hall roofed entirely in glass and iron, a great curved canopy held up on a web of pale steel, with bronze chariots driven by winged figures galloping along the rooflines at the corners. Find the glass; that's what you came for.

Here's the thing worth knowing. The Grand Palais was never meant to last. It was thrown up for the World's Fair of 1900 — the Exposition Universelle, the same kind of event the Eiffel Tower had been built for eleven years earlier — as a temporary showpiece, a hall to display the art and industry of the age for one summer and then, in theory, come down.

It didn't come down. Like the tower a short distance away, the temporary thing turned out to be too good, too useful and too loved to remove, so it stayed, and became one of the great exhibition halls of Europe. The glass roof is among the largest of its kind on the continent, and on a bright day the whole interior fills with daylight in a way no electric hall ever quite matches.

And there's a neat twist to what's going on inside it now. The Pompidou Centre, the big modern-art museum across the river in the Marais, closed in 2025 for years of renovation, and a good part of its displaced programme has moved in here. So the building made to be temporary, the one that refused to leave, has become the temporary home for a museum that had to.

There's something very Parisian in that — a hall built for a single summer in 1900, still standing a hundred and twenty-odd years later, quietly taking in the homeless masterpieces of a younger museum until that one sorts itself out. If you can get in under that glass, for a show or an event, look up: the roof is the real exhibit, and always was.
