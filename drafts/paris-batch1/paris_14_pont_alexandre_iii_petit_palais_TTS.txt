You're on what's generally agreed to be the most extravagant bridge in Paris, so take a second to look at it rather than across it. At each of the four corners stands a tall stone column topped with a gilded winged figure catching the light; the whole deck is crusted with cherubs, nymphs, sea-creatures and garlands in bronze and gold; and lamps on curling iron stems run the length of it. Walk to the middle and look back at one of the gold figures on its column.

Now, the question this bridge quietly answers: why is it quite this much? The answer is politics. The pon alexondr is named for a Russian zar, Alexander the Third, and it was built to celebrate a brand-new alliance between France and Russia in the eighteen nineties. The foundation stone was laid by the zar's son, Nicholas the Second, and every gilded flourish on it is, in effect, a diplomatic toast — a friendship between two nations cast in metal and laid across the river. The most romantic bridge in Paris is a piece of statecraft.

It's also a piece of clever engineering. Look at how flat it is — it crosses the whole river in one low, shallow arch of steel, with almost no hump in the middle. That was deliberate. The bridge sits on a straight line between the gold dome of the anvaleed on one bank and the great glass halls on the other, and it was built low on purpose so it wouldn't block that view. All that decoration sits on top of a very disciplined idea.

And look across to the smaller of the two glass-roofed palaces on the far bank — the puhtee palay. It went up for the same World's Fair, in nineteen hundred, as the bridge and its bigger neighbour; today it's the city's own fine-art museum, and unlike almost everything else around here, walking in to see the collection costs nothing.

So this corner of Paris is essentially one very good year — nineteen hundred — frozen in iron and gold: a bridge built to flatter a zar, two palaces built to show off to the world, all of it arranged around a single straight view. Lean on the rail a while. Few cities ever spent quite this lavishly on a river crossing, and fewer still left it standing.
