You could spend an entire day inside this building, see one of the great art collections on earth, and still have missed the biggest exhibit — which is the building itself.

You're standing in the main courtyard, the Cour Napoléon, ringed on three sides by the long ornate wings of a Renaissance palace, with a glass pyramid rising out of the paving in front of you. Start with the pyramid, because it's the argument.

When it opened in 1989 — glass and steel, designed by the architect I.M. Pei, dropped right into the middle of a centuries-old royal palace — much of Paris hated it. It was called a scar, a joke, a giant diamond in the wrong setting. Today it's as much a symbol of the city as the building behind it. The same thing happens here again and again: this year's outrage becomes next year's icon.

Now wind the building back. The Louvre began, eight hundred years ago, as a fortress — a defensive castle with a moat, guarding the edge of medieval Paris. You can still visit the stone foundations of it, down in the basement; the museum kept its own buried past. Over the centuries the fortress became a palace, expanded by king after king, until Louis the Fourteenth tired of it, moved the whole court out to Versailles in the sixteen eighties, and left this enormous place half-empty.

So what do you do with an abandoned royal palace? After the Revolution, France had an answer: you give it to the public. The Louvre opened as a museum in 1793 — the king's private treasures, now everyone's.

That's the gift to carry inside. The Mona Lisa is in there, and the Venus de Milo, and the crowds around them are enormous. But the real story is the one you're standing in: a war fortress that became a king's palace that became the most-visited museum in the world. Pei's glass pyramid is simply the seam — the visible joint where a palace finally admitted it belonged to everyone.
