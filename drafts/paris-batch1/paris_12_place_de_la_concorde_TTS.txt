You're standing in the largest square in Paris, and one of the most beautiful — open on every side, with fountains, long views down to the river and the gardens and the avenue, and a gold-tipped Egyptian column at the centre. Find that tall stone needle in the middle; it's your anchor for this one.

The square has had three names, and the order of them tells the whole story. It opened in the seventeen fifties as place loo-ee the fifteenth, laid out around a statue of the king on horseback — a royal square, named for a living monarch. During the Revolution the statue came down, the square was renamed place duh la rayvoluusyon, and for a few years in the seventeen nineties it became the main stage of the Terror: the guillotine stood here, roughly where the column now rises, and more than a thousand people were executed on this ground, the king and queen among them.

When the killing was over, the square was given its third and final name — place duh la konkord. Konkord means harmony, agreement, reconciliation. It was chosen deliberately, to close the wound: a name meant to make people forget what had happened on the stones under their feet.

Now look at what stands on the exact spot. Not a French king, not a Revolutionary hero — a three-thousand-year-old Egyptian obelisk from the temple at looksor, given to France in the eighteen thirties and raised here in eighteen thirty-six. It is the single most neutral object you could possibly put in this place. It belongs to no side in any French quarrel; it predates the whole argument by millennia.

And that, almost certainly, is the point. Faced with the one patch of ground in Paris that nobody could agree how to mark, the city imported something so old and so foreign that it carried no French meaning at all — and set it down precisely where the guillotine had stood.

So the prettiest square in the city is also its quietest act of forgetting. Stand by the obelisk a moment. The fountains, the gold, the open sky — all of it arranged, very deliberately, over a place the city decided it would rather not remember.
