Here's a question to hold as you go in: what does a building look like when its entire purpose is to be a container for something precious?

You're at Sainte-Chapelle, tucked inside a courtyard of the old royal palace on the Île de la Cité — these days ringed by the city's law courts, which is why you came through security to reach it. There are two chapels stacked one on top of the other. The lower one, where you start, is low and dim, and was for the palace servants. Don't linger. Take the narrow spiral stair up.

Because the upper chapel is one of the great rooms in Europe, and walking into it does something to people. The walls are essentially gone. In their place: fifteen windows, each more than fifteen metres tall, almost the whole surface of the room turned to stained glass — over a thousand small scenes, glowing, with barely any stone visible between them. On a bright day it feels less like a room than like standing inside a lantern.

Now the gift, and it explains everything. This was built in the twelve hundreds by King Louis the Ninth to house a single object: the Crown of Thorns, the relic he believed Christ had worn, which he had bought from the emperor in Constantinople. And here's the detail nobody forgets — he paid more than three times as much for the relic as he spent building this entire chapel to keep it in. The container cost a fraction of what went inside it.

[beat]

That's the reframe. Sainte-Chapelle isn't a church that happens to have beautiful windows. It's a reliquary — one of those jewelled gold boxes made to hold a holy object — except scaled up to the size of a building, with the gems swapped for coloured light. You're not standing in a chapel admiring the decoration. You're standing inside the jewellery box. Find a seat, look up, and let it work on you.
