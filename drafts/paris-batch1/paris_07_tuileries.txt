This is the oldest and grandest public garden in Paris, and it's built as a straight line — one you can follow with your eye for kilometres.

You're at the Louvre end of the Jardin des Tuileries. Stand on the central path and look away from the museum, west, down the long gravel avenue with its clipped trees and round ponds. Keep your eye going: that line runs out through the gateway, across the great square beyond, all the way up the Champs-Élysées, and dead-centre into the Arc de Triomphe on the horizon. It's no accident. You're looking down the spine of Paris.

The man who drew that line was André Le Nôtre, the royal gardener, around the sixteen sixties. He took an older garden and reshaped it into the strict, symmetrical, geometric style we now think of as the formal French garden — everything balanced, everything ordered, the sightlines running clean to the horizon. Then he took the same idea and scaled it up into the gardens of Versailles. This was the prototype.

Here's the gift, and it's an absence. For three hundred years there was a palace here — the Tuileries Palace — closing off the end nearest the Louvre. In 1871, during the upheaval of the Paris Commune, it was burned, and later cleared away entirely. That's why the Louvre now opens straight onto the garden with nothing in between: you're standing where a royal palace used to be, looking at the empty space it left behind.

That's the reframe. This garden was designed as a corridor of power — a dead-straight line from the king's palace out to the edge of the world, meant to make anyone standing in it feel the reach of the crown. The palace is gone, the king is gone, and the grand axis they built is now where Parisians drag a green metal chair to the edge of a pond and fall asleep in the sun. The line outlived everything it was meant to glorify.
