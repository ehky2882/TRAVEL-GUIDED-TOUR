Stand on the wide stone terrace at the base of the arch, with the twelve avenues spinning out around you and the long straight pull of the Champs-Élysées falling away to one side. If you can hear traffic coming from every direction at once, you're in the right place — this is the centre of a roundabout that twelve roads feed into, which is why you reached the arch through a tunnel under the road, not across it.

Look up first. Napoleon ordered this arch built in 1806, the year after his great victory at Austerlitz, as a monument to his armies — a Roman triumphal arch scaled up to a size Rome never attempted, nearly fifty metres tall. He wanted his soldiers to march home through it.

They never did. The arch took thirty years to finish, and Napoleon was dead fifteen years before the last stone went in — exiled, defeated, buried on a rock in the South Atlantic. The only time he ever passed beneath it was in 1840, when France brought his body home and the funeral procession carried his coffin through the arch on its way to the tomb. The emperor's triumphal arch received him exactly once, as a corpse.

Walk in under the vault and look at the walls. They're carved with the names of victories and the generals who won them — hundreds of names, a stone index of the empire's wars. On the outer face, toward the avenue, a group of figures surges forward behind a winged woman with her mouth open in a shout: that's the relief everyone calls La Marseillaise, the volunteers of 1792 marching off to war.

And then, on the ground, at the centre of all that carved glory, there's a plain slab and a low flame. That's the grave of the Unknown Soldier — one unidentified French body from the First World War, buried here just after it ended, to stand for all the missing. Every evening at half past six, someone relights the flame.

So the grandest monument to one named man's ambition ended up keeping watch over the most deliberately nameless grave in France. Stay until the flame is rekindled if you can — it has burned without going out since 1923, longer than almost anyone now standing around it has been alive.
