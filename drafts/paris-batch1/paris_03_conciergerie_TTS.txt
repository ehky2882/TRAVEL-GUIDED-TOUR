For a thousand years, this riverbank has been the address of power in Paris — and this building is the oldest piece of it left.

You're looking at the konsyairzhuree from across the water, or from the embankment beneath it: a long pale wall on the eel duh la seetay, with round towers and pointed, cone-shaped roofs rising over the senn. It doesn't look like much from the outside. Inside is one of the largest medieval halls anywhere in Europe.

This was the royal palace — the seat of the kings of France for some four hundred years, before they moved on to the Louvre and beyond. When they left, the building stayed in the business of the state: it became a courthouse and a prison. Step inside and you'll find the Hall of the Men-at-Arms, a vast vaulted Gothic space the size of a cathedral nave, built to feed and house the royal staff — thousands of them.

But that's not what most people come for, and it's the gift, though a heavy one. During the French Revolution, in the worst stretch known as the Terror, this prison became the last stop before the guillotine. The revolutionary court sat upstairs; the cells were down here; and over a few months, something like twenty-six hundred people passed through on their way to execution. The most famous was the queen, maree antwanett, held here through the autumn of seventeen ninety-three. The cell where she spent her final weeks is now a small, quiet chapel.

Here's the reframe to stand with. The palace of kings became the place a queen was sent to die. The same walls, the same riverbank, the same idea — that this is the spot where France decides things — carried straight through from monarchy to revolution without missing a beat. Power kept its address; it only changed who held the key. Today, fittingly, the buildings alongside are still the nation's law courts.

This is a sombre place — treat it gently. When you step back out onto the embankment, the river and the islands are still there, beautiful and indifferent, which is its own kind of relief.
