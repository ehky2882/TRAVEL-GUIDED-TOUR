In April 2019, much of the world watched this building burn on live television. The spire fell. The roof — eight hundred years of oak — went up like a bonfire. For one night it looked as though Notre-Dame might not survive at all.

It did. And that's the strange thing you're standing in front of: a cathedral that is at once more than eight hundred years old and only a few months old.

You're on the wide square facing the west front — the two great towers, the round rose window between them, the three deep carved doorways below. Before you walk up, look down. Set into the ground near the centre of the square is a small brass marker: Point Zéro. Every road distance in France is measured from that exact spot — when a sign says Paris is so many kilometres away, this is the dot it means. You're standing on the centre of the country.

Now the building. It was begun in 1163 and took the better part of a century to finish — one of the first cathedrals anywhere to use flying buttresses, those stone arms bracing the walls from outside, which let the builders go higher and thinner and fill the gaps with glass. The three great rose windows are the result, and here is the gift: when the fire tore through in 2019, the rose windows survived. The thing everyone feared losing is the thing that lived.

The roof was another story. The medieval framing was known as "the forest" — around thirteen hundred oak trees, each beam shaped by hand. It burned completely. So they grew it back: felled new oaks, cut them with medieval tools, and rebuilt the forest exactly as it had been, along with the spire, reopened at the end of 2024.

That's the reframe to carry inside with you. People call Notre-Dame ancient, and it is — but it has been half-destroyed and remade more than once: in the Revolution, in the nineteenth century, and again in our own time. What you're looking at isn't a thing that survived unchanged. It's a thing that keeps being saved. Take your time on the square before you go in.
