You're standing on the most famous avenue in the world, which is a strange thing for a road to be, so it's worth asking what you're actually looking at. Face uphill, toward the arch on the horizon — that long, dead-straight rise ahead of you is the Champs-Élysées, almost two kilometres of it, running from the obelisk down at one end up to the arch at the other.

Start with the name. Champs-Élysées means the Elysian Fields — in Greek myth, the paradise where the souls of heroes go after death, the fields of the blessed. It's a grand thing to call a street. And for a long time it wasn't a street at all: this was marshy ground and market gardens outside the city, until the royal gardener André Le Nôtre extended the avenue out from the Tuileries in the 1660s, planting a long straight promenade of trees so the view from the palace would run on forever.

That's the thing to hold onto here — the Champs-Élysées was designed as a view before it was ever a destination. It's the middle stretch of a single line the French call the historic axis, running from the Louvre through the gardens and straight out to the arch and beyond. You're not standing on a road so much as inside a sightline.

Now look at what fills it. The lower half, nearer the obelisk, is still mostly green — gardens and trees with a couple of grand glass-roofed halls tucked among them. The upper half, where you probably are, is the famous part: wide walkways, café terraces, flagship stores selling cars and clothes and perfume to people from everywhere on earth.

So you've got an avenue named for paradise that began as a swamp, was built as a view for a king, and is now lined with shops selling luxury to the world. Every layer is still here if you look for it.

It earns its fame twice a year, when the whole width of it clears: once in July, when the cyclists of the Tour de France come sprinting up to the finish, and once on the national day, when the military parade comes down the other way. The rest of the time it's just a very long, very busy walk uphill — toward a paradise that turns out, when you reach it, to be a roundabout.
