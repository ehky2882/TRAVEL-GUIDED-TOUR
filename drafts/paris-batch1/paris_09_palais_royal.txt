The calmest courtyard in central Paris was once its loudest room — the place where, more or less, the French Revolution was talked into happening.

You're at the Palais-Royal, just across from the Louvre. Walk into the inner courtyard and you'll meet the surprise first: a grid of black-and-white striped columns, of varying heights, set into the stone like a field of candy-striped stumps. People sit on them, children leap between them, visitors photograph their feet on them. When the artist Daniel Buren installed them in the nineteen eighties, the city was furious — another modern outrage dropped into a historic setting, just like the Louvre pyramid. And just like the pyramid, the city came round.

Walk on through into the garden behind — arcades on all four sides, lime trees, fountains, one of the quietest green spaces in the middle of the city. Which is the gift, because it's so hard to believe what this place used to be.

In the seventeen eighties, the owner lined these arcades with cafés, bookshops, gambling rooms and theatres. And because this was private royal property, the king's police were not allowed inside — which made it the one place in Paris where you could say anything at all. Radicals, pamphleteers and gossips packed it day and night. In July 1789, a young man named Camille Desmoulins climbed onto a café table here and called the crowd to arms — and two days later, the Bastille fell.

[beat]

That's the reframe to stand with in all this quiet. This peaceful garden, where people now read on benches in the shade, was the free-speech engine of a revolution — a shopping arcade that helped bring down a monarchy. The stripes that once scandalised the city are simply part of the calm now. Give it long enough, and Paris absorbs almost anything.
