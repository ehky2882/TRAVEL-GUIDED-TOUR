Step through, and the volume drops. You're on the riverbank now — a strip of grass and trees between the back of the Wall and the shpray, with swans idling and the towers of the oh ber bowm brew kuh downstream. Officially this is East Side Park. Unofficially it's one of the most quietly astonishing places in Berlin, because everything pleasant about it is standing in what used to be the border strip.

Look at the back of the Wall first — this side is mostly bare, grey, patched. Then look at the ground you're on. During the division, this riverbank was sealed territory: cleared, patrolled, off-limits, a city turned away from its own water. The houses that once stood along the bank were demolished to make room for the border installations. No one strolled here, because no one could. Today it's picnic blankets and evening beers, and the traffic on the water is tour boats.

This is where the walk has been heading all along, so give it a minute. Berlin's real achievement isn't that it removed its border — most of it is simply gone, and elsewhere in the city you'd never know. Its achievement is what it did with the pieces it kept: a bridge reopened and made the emblem of a joined-up borough, a wall handed over to painters, a forbidden riverbank handed back to anyone with a free afternoon. Nothing here pretends the division didn't happen. It just refuses to let the division have the last word.

The walk ends here — and so, if you've been following this city's story from the Gate onward, does a much longer one. Find a spot on the grass, face the river, and wait for the U1 to cross the bridge on its way between the two halves of one city. It's a small sight. It took a century to arrange.
