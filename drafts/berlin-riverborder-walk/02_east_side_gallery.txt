The East Side Gallery runs from the bridge behind you almost to Ostbahnhof — more than a kilometre of Berlin Wall, painted end to end. And here's the friendly advice this walk owes you: do not try to see all of it. There are more than a hundred works. Treat it like any great gallery — walk the full length, but let three or four pieces actually stop you.

First, though, understand what the canvas is. These are the real segments — prefabricated concrete, roughly three and a half metres tall, standing exactly where the border installations sealed off the eastern bank. The border itself was the middle of the river; the Wall stood here to keep everyone away from the water. Through the decades of division, this concrete was blank, and no one could stand where you're standing now. Then, in 1990, with the border open but the Wall still on its feet, artists from all over the world were invited to paint it — and the most forbidding surface in the city became, within months, its most photographed.

So walk, and read as you go. Some works are jokes, some are grief, some are hope painted very fast in a year when anything seemed possible. You'll pass the famous kiss — you'll know it when you see it, and so does everyone else, so the crowd will find it for you. The paintings have been restored and argued over in the years since; the concrete weathers, and the city keeps deciding how much to intervene. That argument is ongoing, and it isn't ours today. Yours is simpler: keep walking.

About halfway along, watch for the openings cut through the Wall. Pick one and step through it — the river side is the last stop, and it's the quiet ending this loud kilometre has been hiding.
