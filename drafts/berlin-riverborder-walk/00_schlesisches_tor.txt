For most of its course through Berlin, the Wall was a line drawn across land. On this walk, it was water.

You should be at Schlesisches Tor, under the elevated tracks of the U1, in the thick of Kreuzberg — kebab shops, late-night bars, the railway rumbling overhead on its brick arches. During the division this was a corner of West Berlin pressed so hard against the border that the city simply ended a few streets away. Elsewhere, a double row of cobblestones traces where the Wall once ran; here the river did that work, and the cobblestones give way to the Spree.

This walk is about two kilometres, flat the whole way, and it saves its show for the end: first a bridge that looks like it escaped from a fairy tale, then the longest surviving stretch of the Berlin Wall — painted end to end — and finally a riverbank where the border strip is now a lawn. One honest note: the stretch along the gallery is narrow and can be crowded, especially near the bridge. Take your time anyway.

Walk down Oberbaumstrasse toward the river — you'll see twin brick towers rising ahead. That's the Oberbaumbrücke, and it's where we cross.
