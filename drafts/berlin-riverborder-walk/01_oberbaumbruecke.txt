Stop mid-bridge if the crowds allow, between the two brick towers. You are standing on the best-loved bridge in Berlin, and on one of its better jokes: the Oberbaumbrücke looks medieval — turrets, arcades, crenellations — and it is younger than the telephone. It went up in 1896, romantic dress-up for what was really modern infrastructure: the upper deck was built to carry the city's first elevated railway across the Spree, and the U1 still rattles over your head today. And the name is older still: the Oberbaum was the wooden boom that once closed the river here at night, back when this was the city's toll gate on the water.

Now look down at the water, because the water is the point. The border between East and West ran down the middle of this river. The bridge touched both worlds — Friedrichshain on the far bank was East, Kreuzberg behind you was West — and for most of the division it carried no traffic at all. For years it served as a crossing where West Berliners with the right paperwork could walk into the East, one at a time, and back again before the day ended.

[beat]

Here's the detail worth keeping. After reunification, the two districts that had faced each other across this border were merged into a single borough: Friedrichshain-Kreuzberg, one name, one hyphen. Berliners still joke about the arranged marriage, but look at the geography — the borough's two halves face each other across the river, stitched together by a few bridges, and this is the grandest of them by a mile. The bridge isn't just in the hyphenated borough. The bridge is the hyphen.

Cross to the Friedrichshain side and turn left along Mühlenstrasse, keeping the river on your left. The long run of concrete ahead, covered in paint and people photographing it, is the East Side Gallery — the next stop, and the last stretch of this walk.
