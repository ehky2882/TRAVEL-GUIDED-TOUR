The governor who built this house called it the most beautiful in all of Canada. He was biased. He also wasn't entirely wrong.

You should be on Rue Notre-Dame Est, directly across from City Hall, looking at a long, low fieldstone house with a steep silver roof and dormer windows — Château Ramezay. Next to the grand civic architecture all around it, it looks almost humble. It's older than nearly everything in sight: built in 1705 as the residence of Claude de Ramezay, governor of Montreal, and one of the last true pieces of New France still standing in the old city.

What makes this house remarkable is its résumé. After the Ramezay family, a French fur-trading company ran its empire from these rooms. When Britain took Canada, British governors moved in. And in 1775 an American revolutionary army occupied Montreal and made this building its headquarters — which is how, in the spring of 1776, Benjamin Franklin came to stay here, sent north to charm Canada into joining the revolution as a fourteenth colony. Montreal heard him out and declined. Franklin went home; the printer he brought along stayed, and the newspaper that printer founded evolved into the Gazette, which is still publishing.

By the 1890s the house had run out of empires to serve and was headed for demolition, until a society of local citizens stepped in, bought its future, and opened it as a museum in 1895. It later became the first building in Quebec ever classified as a historic monument — the province literally invented its heritage protection starting here.

[beat]

That's the quiet power of this place: French governors, fur barons, British administrators and American generals all used this house and moved on, and the house outlasted every one of them. Three empires came through these doors; the doors are still here. Inside you'll find period rooms and the story of the city told at human scale — and behind the building, the Governor's Garden, a recreated New France garden of orchards, vegetables and medicinal herbs, free to enter. If the day is warm, that garden is one of the best-kept secrets in Old Montreal. Go sit under the pear trees for a moment. The eighteenth century keeps them pruned.
