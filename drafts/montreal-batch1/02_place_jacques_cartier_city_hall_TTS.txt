The oldest war monument in Canada honours a British admiral — in a city that speaks French.

You should be on plahs zhak kar tyay, the wide cobbled square sloping downhill from roo noh truh dahm toward the river, lined with terraces and street performers. At the top of the slope stands a grey stone column with a figure at its summit: Horatio Nelson, hero of Trafalgar. Behind him, across the street, the grand green-roofed building with the clock tower is City Hall.

The column went up in eighteen oh nine, more than thirty years before the famous one in London — and here's the twist: French Montrealers helped pay for it. Napoleon was no hero in Catholic Quebec, and when news of Trafalgar arrived by messenger from New York, interrupting a New Year's ball, English and French merchants subscribed together. The peace didn't hold. In the eighteen nineties someone tried to dynamite the column, and in nineteen thirty francophone Montrealers answered its presence by raising a statue of zhahn vohk lan, a French naval officer, in the small square directly across the street. Look north and you can find him. Two admirals from opposite sides of an old war, facing each other over roo noh truh dahm — the city's long argument, conducted in bronze, at a polite distance. As recently as nineteen ninety-seven, a proposal to move Nelson elsewhere was defeated. He stays. So does vohk lan. That, in one intersection, is Montreal.

One small secret: the figure on the column today is a replica. The original, after nearly two centuries of winters, was moved indoors in nineteen ninety-seven to save it.

Now City Hall. Built in the eighteen seventies, gutted by fire in nineteen twenty-two, then rebuilt inside its own surviving stone shell — and it's free to walk into. But its history turns on a single balcony, the one facing you. In July nineteen sixty-seven, French President sharl duh gohl stepped onto it before an enormous crowd and ended his speech with four words: veev luh kay bek leebr. Long live free Quebec. It thrilled the crowd, scandalized Ottawa, and people here still debate what he meant and what it set in motion.

Walk down the slope when you're ready. Buskers, caricaturists, terraces charging a little too much — this square has been selling something since it was a market in the early eighteen hundreds. These days, mostly, it sells the pleasure of being exactly here.
