Everything in front of you exists because of a stretch of white water you can't see.

You should be on the waterfront promenade of the Old Port, the Saint Lawrence spread out ahead, the low stone skyline of Old Montreal at your back. Somewhere off to your right, upstream, the river breaks into the lah sheen Rapids — and for three centuries, that white water was the whole point of Montreal. Ships coming up from the Atlantic simply could not go any further. Everything — furs, grain, timber, immigrants — had to stop here, unload here, change hands here. The city is, at its root, a traffic jam that got rich.

Look left along the water and you'll find the Clock Tower at the end of its pier, pale and slender. It reads as ornamental, but it's a war memorial, raised in the early nineteen twenties for the sailors lost at sea in the First World War. This harbour ran on their work, in both directions.

Across the basin to the south, that improbable stack of concrete cubes, piled like a child's blocks, is Habitat sixty-seven — a housing experiment left over from Expo sixty-seven, which people either love instantly or need a minute with. It gets better the longer you look.

And notice what's missing: ships. In nineteen fifty-nine the Saint Lawrence Seaway opened — locks and channels that finally let ocean freighters climb past the rapids — and the working port moved east down the river. The old harbour fell quiet, nearly derelict, until the city did something genuinely unusual. Instead of selling its waterfront, it turned the whole thing into public space: quays, lawns, a beach, a Ferris wheel turning slowly over the water.

So this is what you're standing in — the reason the city exists, retired. The rapids still churn upstream and the river still carries the country's grain, just not from here. What remains is one of the rarest things a port city can own: a working waterfront handed back to the public, with nothing to sell you but the view. Find a bench facing the water. It has been earning its keep for nearly four centuries.
