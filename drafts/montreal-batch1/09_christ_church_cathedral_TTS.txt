In nineteen eighty-seven, the cathedral in front of you spent months standing on stilts.

That's not a figure of speech. You're looking at Christ Church Cathedral, on sant kat reen Street — a proper Gothic Revival church, modelled on the medieval parish churches of the English countryside, pointed arches and gargoyles and a dark spire, with a thirty-four-storey glass tower rising directly behind it. Hold that pairing in your eye, because the two buildings are more entangled than they look.

The trouble started with the ground. When the cathedral was finished in eighteen fifty-nine, it sat on land so soft the district was known as the Marshes, and the stone tower — well over a thousand tonnes of it — began to sink and tilt almost immediately. By nineteen twenty the steeple leaned more than a metre off true. The fix, in nineteen forty, was quietly brilliant: the stone spire came down and an aluminum replica went up, cast to look exactly like its predecessor. The spire you see is a lightweight impostor, and almost nobody walking past has any idea.

Then came the bolder operation. In nineteen eighty-seven, engineers excavated the entire block beneath the church, holding the building aloft on concrete pillars sunk to bedrock while they built a shopping mall underneath it — the prom nahd ka tay drahl, whose corridors also stitched together the eastern and western halves of Montreal's underground city. Montrealers called it the floating church. Today the cathedral literally rests on the mall's columns, and the lease on the ground below helps keep the congregation's lights on.

So look again at the postcard contrast — old church, new tower. It isn't a contrast at all. The skyscraper, the mall, and the cathedral are one interlocking structure, and the deal that built them is the reason the church still stands. This city's landmarks survive less by being protected than by being useful.

If the doors are open, step inside — entry is free, and the quiet up here runs directly above the food court. Somewhere under your feet, someone is buying socks beneath a Gothic cathedral, and neither of you will hear the other at all.
