For the better part of a century, the money in Canada lived on this hillside. From the eighteen forties to the nineteen thirties, the merchants who ran the country's banks, railways, shipping lines, and fur companies built their mansions on the slope between downtown and the mountain — and locals still repeat the claim that the families of this one square mile controlled nearly seventy percent of Canada's wealth. Take the number with a grain of salt; take the greystone evidence around you seriously.

Stand at the Roddick Gates, on Sherbrooke Street, looking into McGill University. The campus runs uphill from here, its buildings climbing the lower slope of Mount Royal, and behind you McGill College Avenue frames the view back down toward the glass towers.

The university came first. James McGill was a Scottish fur trader who made his fortune here and, when he died in eighteen thirteen, left his estate and his country orchard for a college — which opened its doors in eighteen twenty-one and never stopped growing. What it grew into, oddly enough, was the neighbourhood itself: as the great families faded after the First World War, their mansions proved too vast to heat and too costly to keep, and roughly a third of what survives now houses university faculties and institutes. The fortune built the college, and then the college absorbed the fortune's houses.

One more thing about the name. Nobody in the neighbourhood's heyday called it the Golden Square Mile — the golden was added in the nineteen fifties, by real estate developers polishing the past to sell the present. Even the nostalgia here was a business decision.

And beneath all of it lies something far older. Somewhere on or near this ground stood hosh eh lah gah, the Iroquoian village of perhaps a few thousand people that zhak kar tyay visited in fifteen thirty-five — centuries before any fur fortune. A boulder on the lower campus marks the general spot. The oldest address in Montreal has no house on it at all.

Before you move on, look up McGill College Avenue and hold the frame a moment: the mountain at one end, the towers at the other, and this green campus in between — the hinge where the city's old money quietly turned into its institutions.
