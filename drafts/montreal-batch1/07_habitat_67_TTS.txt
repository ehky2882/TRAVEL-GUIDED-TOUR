Every architecture student has a thesis project their professors gently talk them out of. This one got built.

You should be on see tay doo ahvr, the long artificial peninsula between the Old Port and the river, near the foot of avenue pyair doo pwee — though plenty of people meet this building first from across the water, where it reads as a mirage. Up close it resolves into what it actually is: three connected pyramids of concrete boxes, three hundred and fifty-four of them, stacked twelve storeys high as if a giant had been interrupted mid-game. This is Habitat sixty-seven.

It began as a master's thesis at McGill by an architecture student in his early twenties named moh shay saf dee, who worked out his early models in Lego bricks. His idea was to rescue density from the apartment tower: stack identical factory-made modules so that every home gets light on multiple sides, its own front door, and a private garden — each terrace sitting on the roof of the home below. For everyone, a garden, as saf dee framed it. When Montreal won the nineteen sixty-seven world's fair and made housing one of its themes, the student project became a national pavilion — the only structure at Expo sixty-seven built to be permanent, and its official legacy today. Even the ground under your feet belongs to the same dream: this peninsula was raised partly with rock excavated for the city's brand-new métro.

Then came the honest part of the story. The system was meant to make good housing cheap, but building the prototype cost a fortune, the modular revolution never scaled, and the great experiment in affordable living slowly became some of the most coveted real estate in Montreal — the residents themselves bought the complex in nineteen eighty-five. As housing policy, it failed.

As an argument, it won. Look at any new development with stepped terraces, rooftop gardens and prefabricated parts, and you're looking at Habitat's descendants. saf dee went on to build landmarks on three continents, but he kept his penthouse here, restored it, and gave it to McGill — and in season you can see it on a guided tour, booked ahead online; the rest is private homes, so the walkways aren't open to wanderers. From out here, though, the view is free. Stand back and squint. It still looks like the future — a future now pushing sixty.
