For a hundred years, sailors coming up the Saint Lawrence knew they had reached Montreal when they saw two things: a silver dome, and a lady with her arms open to the water.

You should be on roo san pol, one of the oldest streets in the city, near its eastern end. The long grey neoclassical building crowned with the shining dome is bon suh koor Market. Just beside it, the small stone church with the copper statue on its roof is noh truh dahm duh bon suh koor — the sailors' chapel.

Take the market first. Finished in eighteen forty-seven, it was built to announce to every arriving ship that Montreal was a serious city, and for a stretch it practically was the city: it housed City Hall for a quarter of a century, staged concerts and grand banquets upstairs, and sold produce on the ground floor the entire time. That dome was never about keeping rain off vegetables. It was advertising.

The chapel is older, smaller, and the better story. mar guh reet boor zhwah, the colony's first schoolteacher, had it founded in sixteen fifty-five; the building you're looking at dates from seventeen seventy-one and stands on the foundations of the first stone chapel. Beneath it, archaeologists found traces of an Indigenous camp some two thousand four hundred years old — this small rise above the water has been drawing people for a very long time. boor zhwah herself went on to become Canada's first female saint, and she rests inside, in the chapel she began.

Step in if it's open. Hanging from the ceiling you'll see little wooden ships — votive models, left by sailors giving thanks for surviving the crossing. This was the first stop after the docks: up from the harbour, through the doors, a candle lit. And on the roof stands the statue you spotted from the street, known as Our Lady of the Harbour — arms wide, facing the river rather than the city. She's there for the ships, not for us.

If she sounds familiar, it may be because Leonard Cohen wove her into Suzanne, one of the most famous songs ever written about this city — he walked this waterfront, and the chapel drifted into the verses. His trail runs all through Montreal. For now, the lady keeps her watch, the little ships hang quietly, and the harbour she blesses has softened into a park.
