Stand in the centre of plahs darm and turn a slow circle. Almost everything this city has ever cared about is looking back at you.

You should be in the cobbled square at the heart of Old Montreal. On the south side, two flat-topped stone towers rise over a grand Gothic front — that's noh truh dahm Basilica. Directly opposite, a domed building with a Roman temple portico: the head office of the Bank of Montreal, built in eighteen forty-seven. And in the centre of the square, a bronze founder on a pedestal — pol duh shom day duh may zoh nuv, who started all of this in sixteen forty-two.

Begin with the basilica. It went up in eighteen twenty-nine, and the architect was a surprise: James O'Donnell, an Irish-born Protestant working out of New York, hired by Catholic Montreal to build the largest church on the continent at the time. The commission changed him. He converted to Catholicism at the end of his life, and he's buried in the crypt beneath the church — the only person who is. The two towers have names, Perseverance and Temperance, and the western one carries the groh boor dohn, one of the largest bells in North America. When it rings, you feel it in the pavement.

Inside — and it's worth the ticket — the vault is deep midnight blue, scattered with thousands of gold stars, the whole interior carved and gilded like the inside of a jewel box. Most of that is the later work of veek tor boor zhoh, and it's the reason people gasp at the door.

Now turn around. Faith on one side of the square, money on the other, staring at each other since eighteen forty-seven — and for a century, the church was winning. It ran the schools, the hospitals, the rhythm of the week. Then, in a single generation starting in the nineteen sixties, Quebec became one of the most secular societies in North America. The pews emptied, but the building endures — as a monument, a concert hall, an evening light show.

That arc — from a city the church built to a city that keeps its churches the way you keep love letters — is the deep story of modern Quebec, and you'll see it told all over Montreal. Here is where it's easiest to read. Go in and look up. The stars are still there.
