There's a river running under your feet.

You should be at Place Royale, on the western edge of Old Montreal, beside the sharply modern museum building with the angled tower — Pointe-à-Callière. The name means Callière's Point, after a governor who built his residence here in the 1690s, but the point itself came first: a narrow tongue of land between the St. Lawrence and a much smaller river, the Saint-Pierre, which flowed into it right about where you're standing.

That little river is why this is the exact spot where Montreal began. On the seventeenth of May, 1642, a small party of French colonists came ashore on this point and founded a mission settlement they called Ville-Marie. Two names to hold onto: Paul de Chomedey de Maisonneuve, the soldier who led them, and Jeanne Mance, the nurse who arrived with them, founded the colony's hospital, and is now formally recognized as the city's co-founder. And they were not the first. People had been living, meeting and trading on this ground for well over a thousand years before any European saw it — the island was a crossroads long before it was a colony, and the museum beneath you takes that longer history seriously.

As for the Saint-Pierre, the city it created eventually swallowed it. By 1832 the little river had become an open drain, so Montreal buried it inside a great stone tunnel — one of the first works of its kind in Canada. It's still down there. Inside the museum you can descend below street level and walk through it: a river turned into architecture, running through the dark beneath the birthplace of the city.

[beat]

Most cities keep their founding site under glass, or under a plaque in a parking lot. Montreal's is under your shoes, layered like strata: an Indigenous crossroads, a French mission, a governor's house, a buried river, a museum — each one built directly on top of the last. Go inside and you can stand on glass floors above the actual foundations. Or simply stay here a moment and consider the ground. Very few street corners anywhere are carrying this much.
