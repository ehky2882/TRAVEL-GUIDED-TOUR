Below you: the centre of the world. That was the claim, anyway — and for roughly eight hundred years it was close to true.

You should be on the small terrace tucked behind Rome's city hall. From pee aht sah del kahm pee doh lyoh, take the lane down the right-hand side of the central palace — vee ah del kahm pee doh lyoh — and it opens onto a balcony with the Roman Forum spread out beneath you. It's free, it's never closed, and it is the best single view of ancient Rome there is.

Start with the arch directly below you, the massive one with three openings — the Arch of sep tim ee us seh veer us, from the year two oh three, still crisp enough to read. The road passing beside it, paved in those huge flat stones, is the vee ah sah krah, the sacred way, the street victorious generals paraded down for a thousand years. To your right, the eight grey columns standing closest to the terrace belong to the Temple of Saturn, which doubled as the state treasury — Rome kept its money in the basement of a god. And out in the open ground, one column standing entirely alone: the Column of foh kas, raised in six oh eight for a Byzantine emperor, on a second-hand shaft. It was the last monument ancient Rome ever put up in its Forum, and a thousand-year habit ended there, quietly, with recycled stone.

Then comes the strangest chapter, the one this view doesn't show. When the empire fell away, the Forum didn't collapse so much as sink — flood silt and rubble buried it metres deep, and for centuries the place carried another name entirely: the kahm poh vah chee noh. The cow field. Cattle grazed here; column tops poked out of the grass like fence posts; artists came to sketch the ruins with the herds drifting through. The centre of the world became a pasture, and Rome, busy elsewhere, barely noticed. Nearly everything below you was dug back out within the last two hundred years.

That's the real subject from up here — not how the Forum died, but how long its afterlife has run. Longer now, by a comfortable margin, than the life.

If you can manage it, come back after dark. The ruins are floodlit, the terrace is nearly empty, and the cows, at long last, are gone.
