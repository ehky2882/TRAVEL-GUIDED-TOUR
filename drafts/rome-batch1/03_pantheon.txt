The inscription over the entrance is a very generous credit. "Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius, made this," it says — but Agrippa's building burned, twice, and what you're looking at is a complete rebuild by the emperor Hadrian, finished around the year 125. Hadrian kept the old name on the front and left his own off entirely, and two thousand years of visitors have been thanking the wrong Roman ever since.

You should be in Piazza della Rotonda, facing the portico — sixteen columns of grey and rose granite, each one a single piece of stone, quarried in Egypt and shipped across the Mediterranean because Rome wanted columns that the world's biggest empire couldn't produce at home.

But the portico is a curtain-raiser. Go inside when you can, because the room behind it is the reason this building matters: the dome is over forty-three metres across, still the largest unreinforced concrete dome ever built, and it is exactly as high as it is wide — a perfect sphere would fit inside the space, resting on the floor and touching the top. The engineering is Roman pragmatism at its best: the concrete gets lighter as it climbs, finishing in featherweight volcanic stone around the oculus, the open eye at the crown. And it is genuinely open. Rain falls straight into the building, onto a marble floor that has sloped, from the beginning, toward twenty-two discreet drains. The Romans didn't fight the sky; they plumbed for it.

The Pantheon is the best-preserved ancient building in Rome, and the reason is hiding in plain sight: it never closed. It became a church in 609 — Santa Maria ad Martyres, still active, still holding Mass — and a building in continuous use for two thousand years never gets quarried, never gets forgotten, never sinks under the silt. Raphael understood; he chose to be buried here, and here he is. The Pantheon isn't preserved, exactly. It's just still open.

[beat]

If you're lucky enough to be here in the rain, don't take shelter — go in. Watching a column of weather fall through the roof of a Roman temple, onto drains cut by Roman plumbers, is one of the quietest astonishments this city has to offer.
