Nothing about the Spanish Steps is Spanish.

The money was French — a diplomat's bequest, left specifically to build a grand stairway up the hill. The church at the top, Trinità dei Monti, is French too, and still is. The design is by an Italian, Francesco de Sanctis, who built the great sweep in the 1720s. The only Spanish thing here was the Spanish embassy, which happened to sit in the square below and quietly lent its name to the whole ensemble. One of the most famous landmarks in Italy is, on inspection, a Franco-Spanish diplomatic misunderstanding — and it works anyway.

You should be at the bottom, in Piazza di Spagna, beside the low fountain shaped like a sinking boat. That's the Barcaccia, and it's by Gian Lorenzo Bernini's father, Pietro. The sunken design isn't a whim — the aqueduct feeding this square arrives with almost no pressure, so a conventional fountain would barely have dribbled. He carved a half-submerged barge instead, and turned a plumbing problem into the most relaxed fountain in Rome. There's a story that a real boat washed up here in a great flood and inspired the shape; it's a legend, but Romans are fond of it.

Now look up at the staircase itself: a hundred and thirty-odd steps in warm travertine, splitting and rejoining around terraces like a slow waterfall in stone. It was built as urban theatre — a place to loiter, promenade, be seen — and for three centuries that's exactly what people did, until the city, deciding the steps were being loved to death, banned sitting on them. So the steps have ended up as pure sculpture: a grandstand you can't sit in, and somehow still the best people-watching in the city.

One quieter thing. The rose-coloured house at the bottom right of the steps is where the poet John Keats came hoping the Roman climate would heal his lungs. It didn't; he died there in 1821, at twenty-five, in a small room overlooking all of this. The house is now a museum to him, in the middle of the least melancholy square in Rome.

Climb to the first terrace and turn around. The view down Via dei Condotti — that straight canyon of shopfronts — is the same axis travellers have admired for three hundred years. The steps were always a stage. You're on it.
