The most photographed face in Rome is, in all likelihood, a drain cover. That's the leading theory, anyway — and somehow it makes the whole thing better.

You're in Piazza della Bocca della Verità, on the flat ground between the Tiber and the Circus Maximus. The medieval church with the tall, elegant bell tower is Santa Maria in Cosmedin, and the queue along its porch is waiting for the Mouth of Truth — a great marble disc, nearly two metres across, carved with the face of a bearded god, mouth open.

The mask first. It's ancient — most scholars read the face as Oceanus, god of the sea — and it probably began life flat on the ground, swallowing rainwater in the cattle market that once filled this square. Some time in the Middle Ages it picked up a better job and a legend to go with it: put your hand in the mouth and tell a lie, and the mouth bites it off. That's folklore, not history — a story medieval Rome told itself — but it stuck, and in 1953 a scene in Roman Holiday sent it around the world. The disc has been propped in this porch since 1632, taking hands ever since.

While the queue shuffles, look at what's around you, because this square is a survivor's gallery. The little round temple by the river — the one that looks like a Greek postcard — is the Temple of Hercules Victor, one of the oldest marble buildings in the city, standing more or less intact since the Republic. Its rectangular neighbour, the Temple of Portunus, has done nearly as well. This was the Forum Boarium, Rome's riverside cattle market, and its working buildings simply never stopped standing.

[beat]

Here's the thing worth carrying away. Rome is a city where a piece of ancient plumbing can become a global celebrity, while two of the best-preserved temples of the ancient world stand quietly across the road, unphotographed, keeping their own counsel. Test the legend if you like — the line moves fast, and the church behind it, cool and dim and eight hundred years old, is the reward almost nobody stays for.
