Romans have never quite forgiven this building. Since the day it opened they've called it the wedding cake, the typewriter, the set of false teeth — and the insults are part of its story, because the veet tor yah noh was designed to be impossible to ignore. It commemorates Victor Emmanuel the Second, the first king of a united Italy, and it rose between eighteen eighty-five and nineteen twenty-seven in marble so aggressively white that it glows against a city built of warm, honey-coloured stone. That was a choice. The marble is bot tee chee noh, brought down from the north near bresh ah — northern stone for a northern king, planted in the middle of ancient Rome.

You should be at the edge of pee aht sah veh neh tsee ah, facing the monument — and you'll have noticed the square itself is a construction site. Rome is digging a metro station here, and it's worth knowing what's behind the hoarding: retaining walls sunk eighty-five metres down, the deepest in Europe, for a station that will double as an underground museum displaying whatever the excavation turns up. It has already turned up plenty — including a lecture hall built by the emperor Hadrian that nobody knew was there. In Rome you cannot build the future without excavating the past, and this square is where the city is doing both at once.

Now look up the steps to the centre of the monument. The bronze horseman is Victor Emmanuel himself, at a scale that refuses to be read from down here: the statue is so large that in nineteen eleven, before it was hoisted into place, a formal dinner was served to a party of about twenty inside the belly of the horse. There are photographs.

Below him, flanked by two sentries who are always present, burns the flame of the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier. An unidentified soldier of the First World War lies there; his coffin was chosen in nineteen twenty-one by mah ree ah bair gah mahs, a mother whose own son never came home. Whatever Romans say about the building, nobody jokes about this part of it.

There's a lift around the side that rises to the roof terrace, where the view runs from the Colosseum to Saint Peter's. Take it if you have the time — the building everyone loves to insult happens to be the one spot in central Rome from which you can't see it.
