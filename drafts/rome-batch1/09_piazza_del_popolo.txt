For centuries, this was the front door.

Travellers coming down from the north — pilgrims, painters, poets, everyone on the road from Florence and beyond — entered Rome through the gate on the far side of this square, the Porta del Popolo, and this piazza was the first thing the city showed them. Everything you're looking at was arranged with that in mind. Rome has always understood first impressions, and this square is the city dressing for the door.

You should be near the obelisk at the centre — and start with it, because it has the longest résumé of any object in the square. It was carved for Ramses the Second in Egypt more than three thousand years ago, hauled to Rome by the emperor Augustus after Egypt fell, and it was the first obelisk ever brought here. Rome developed a taste for them: by most counts the city now has more ancient obelisks standing than anywhere else on earth — more than remain upright in all of Egypt. This one started the collection, and a pope moved it here in 1589 to be the pin at the centre of the arriving traveller's view.

Now look south, at the two churches framing the three streets that fan into the city. They read as perfect twins — matching domes, matching porticoes — and they are not. The plots are different sizes, so the architect gave one church a round dome and the other an oval one, tuned so that from where you're standing the two look identical. It's a stage set: symmetry as illusion, built for exactly one viewpoint, and you're on it.

And in the corner by the gate, easy to miss, sits Santa Maria del Popolo — a modest front hiding one of the best small art collections in Rome, including two Caravaggios you can see for the price of walking in. The church's origin story says it was built to settle the ghost of Nero, who was buried on this spot and haunted a walnut tree here; that's a legend, faithfully retold for nine centuries.

Before you leave, walk to the gate itself and stand in the opening, facing into the square. This is the view that ended a thousand journeys — the moment the road stopped being the road, and became Rome.
