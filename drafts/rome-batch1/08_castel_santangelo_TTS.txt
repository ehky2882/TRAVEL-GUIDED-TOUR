The bridge under your feet has been carrying people toward that round fortress for nearly nineteen hundred years — and for most of that time, both of them have been busy being something else.

Stand at the middle of Ponte Sant'Angelo (pon teh sahnt ahn jeh loh), facing the great drum-shaped building across the river. Along both balustrades, ten marble angels line your path, each holding something aloft. The river below is the Tiber; the three arches at the centre of the bridge are the Roman originals, laid down in the one thirties AD.

The building first, because its résumé is the whole point. It began as the emperor Hadrian's tomb — a mausoleum for himself and his family. Then Rome, being Rome, refused to let a good building retire: it became a fortress, then a papal stronghold, then a prison, and today a museum. There's even a raised stone corridor — the Passetto (pahs set toh) — running from the castle to the Vatican, built so that popes in trouble could flee along the top of a wall. More than one did. This city doesn't just reuse its old stones; here it reassigned an entire imperial tomb.

Now the angels. In the late sixteen sixties, Bernini (bair nee nee) — by then an old man — designed all ten, each carrying an instrument of the Passion, turning the bridge into a procession. He carved two of them himself, and the pope judged those two too beautiful to leave out in the weather. So copies took their place on the balustrade, and the originals ended up in a small church near the Spanish Steps, where they still stand today — masterpieces that never met the rain.

The name comes from a story. In five ninety, with plague moving through the city, Pope Gregory is said to have seen the archangel Michael standing on top of the mausoleum, sheathing his sword — a sign the dying was over. It's legend, not record, but the bronze angel crowning the castle is still sheathing that sword today.

One more thing. On the far side of the castle, past the gardens, you may spot hoarding and cranes: Rome is digging a metro station down there, and like the others along that line, it will double as a museum for whatever the digging turns up. A tomb that became a fortress, a bridge that became a gallery — and underneath it all, the city is still adding layers.
