You crossed an international border a few steps back, and nothing marked it — no gate, no checkpoint, just a shallow line of white stone in the paving where Italy ends and Vatican City begins. This is Saint Peter's Square, the front porch of the smallest country on earth.

Stand anywhere along the centre of the square, facing the basilica, with the obelisk between you and the dome. On either side of you, two enormous curved colonnades sweep out from the church like a pair of open arms.

Start with those arms, because the embrace is not a guidebook metaphor — it's the design brief. Gian Lorenzo Bernini (jahn loh rent soh bair nee nee) laid out the square in the sixteen fifties and sixteen sixties and described the colonnades as exactly that: arms, reaching out to gather people in. Four rows of columns deep, nearly three hundred columns in all, with a hundred and forty stone saints keeping watch from the top.

Now find the trick hidden in the paving. Between the obelisk and each fountain sits a plain round stone, easy to miss. Stand on one, look at the nearest colonnade, and the four rows of columns snap into a single file — every shaft hiding perfectly behind the one in front. Bernini built the illusion in and never signposted it.

The obelisk itself is the oldest thing here by a distance. Egyptian granite, shipped to Rome under Caligula (kah lig yoo lah), it stood for centuries in the circus where, by tradition, Peter was executed — a reverent piece of ground long before any church rose on it. In fifteen eighty-six it was hauled to the centre of the square with an army of men, horses and winches, and of all the ancient obelisks in this city, this one — by most accounts — is the only one that never fell.

Now turn around. The straight boulevard running from the square to the river is Via della Conciliazione (vee ah dehl lah kohn chee lee ah tsyoh neh), cut through a medieval quarter under Mussolini's regime in the nineteen thirties. Before it existed, pilgrims threaded narrow streets and burst without warning into all this light — the surprise Bernini was counting on. The regime built the grand approach; the city absorbed it; the surprise never quite came back.

Before you move on, look down and find that border line again. You're standing inside the arms of one country, a step away from another — and the architecture was built so you'd never feel the seam.
