Start with the holes.

Look at the great curved wall in front of you — the tall side, where the Colosseum still stands four storeys high. The stone is pocked with thousands of small square holes, row after row, as if the whole building had been sprayed with something. Those holes are where the iron used to be. The Romans raised this wall without mortar, pinning the travertine blocks together with iron clamps — around three hundred tonnes of them — and in the Middle Ages the city mined every clamp it could reach for the metal. What looks like damage is actually a ledger: the record of a thousand years of Rome feeding on itself.

You should be standing on the north side, between the amphitheatre and the end of Via dei Fori Imperiali, looking at the outer ring where it survives at full height — nearly fifty metres. Walk a quarter of the way around and the building drops abruptly: an earthquake in 1349 brought down the southern half of the outer wall, which is why the Colosseum looks whole from one angle and broken from the other.

It opened in the year 80, built by the emperor Vespasian and his son Titus on the drained lake of Nero's private palace — a shrewd piece of politics, turning a tyrant's pleasure garden into an arena for the public. Some fifty thousand people could get in and out through eighty numbered arches, with a ticketing system a modern stadium would recognise. The name isn't even Roman: officially this was the Flavian Amphitheatre, and "Colosseum" came later, borrowed from a colossal bronze statue of Nero that once stood nearby and has vanished so completely that only the nickname survives.

And the missing stone didn't vanish — it moved. For centuries the Colosseum served as fortress, chapel, workshop, and above all quarry: its travertine turns up in palaces across the city, and some of it is in St. Peter's. That's the thing to hold onto here, because you'll see it all over Rome — this city doesn't exactly preserve its past. It reuses it, continuously, until the past and the present become the same building.

[beat]

Stay on the tall side a moment. Two thousand years of earthquakes, popes, and scavengers, and the wall is still doing precisely what it was engineered to do — standing on its own.
