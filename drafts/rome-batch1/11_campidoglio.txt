In 1536, Rome had an emperor coming to visit and nowhere fit to receive him. Charles the Fifth was on his way, the most sacred hill of the ancient world was a muddy jumble of medieval brick, and Pope Paul the Third — mortified — handed the problem to Michelangelo. This square is the result. It took so long to build that Michelangelo saw almost none of it finished, but the city kept following his drawings for centuries after his death, and what you're standing in is essentially his mind, laid out in stone.

You should arrive the way he intended: up the Cordonata, the long shallow ramp from the street below, pitched gently enough to be climbed on horseback, with the giant statues of Castor and Pollux waiting at the top. Ahead of you, three palace facades frame the square around an oval of patterned paving, with a bronze horseman at the centre.

The horseman is Marcus Aurelius, the philosopher-emperor, and this is the only bronze equestrian statue of a pre-Christian emperor to survive from antiquity. It survived by mistake: in the Middle Ages, when pagan bronzes were melted down by the score, everyone believed this one showed Constantine, the first Christian emperor, and so it was spared. What stands out here now is a replica from 1981 — the original lives protected inside the Capitoline Museums, in the palace to your right, whose founding collection was a gift to the Roman people in 1471, which by most reckonings makes it the oldest public museum in the world.

Now the pavement. The twelve-pointed star was Michelangelo's design, and for four centuries it existed only on paper — the popes never laid it, perhaps uneasy about its whiff of pagan geometry. Rome finally paved it in 1940, faithful to the drawing.

And here is the quiet radicalism of the square: it faces the wrong way. For two thousand years this hill looked down on the Forum, the centre of the ancient world. Michelangelo turned the whole composition around, toward the living city and St. Peter's beyond it. The Renaissance didn't restore Rome's centre of gravity — it moved it, and this square is the pivot.
