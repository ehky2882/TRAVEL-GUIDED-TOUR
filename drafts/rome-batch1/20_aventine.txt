After the crowds down the hill, the quiet up here feels almost suspicious. The Aventine is Rome with the volume turned down — gardens, monasteries, embassies, birdsong — and it holds two of the most carefully engineered views in the city. Nothing you're about to see is an accident.

Begin under the orange trees. The walled garden at the top of the hill is the Giardino degli Aranci — the Orange Garden — laid out in the 1930s inside the surviving ramparts of a medieval family fortress, deliberately planned as the city's third great belvedere, alongside the Pincio and the Gianicolo. Walk the central avenue toward the terrace and keep your eye on the dome of St. Peter's floating dead ahead. Watch it as you go: the dome appears to shrink as you approach — a trick of the framing pines that works every single time, on everyone.

The terrace itself gives you the river, the rooftops, and the whole city arranged for inspection. And just outside the garden gate stands Santa Sabina, a basilica from the 400s that still looks and feels like the 400s — plain, luminous, and largely untouched. Its carved wooden doors are original: sixteen centuries old and still on their hinges.

Now walk a few minutes along the crest to a small piazza decorated with obelisks and coats of arms, and find the tall green door. This is the priory of the Knights of Malta, and set into the door is the most famous keyhole in the world. Lean in. Down a tunnel of clipped hedges, perfectly centred, sits the dome of St. Peter's — and the alignment is deliberate, designed in the 1760s by Piranesi when he rebuilt this corner of the hill. One more thing to know while your eye is at the brass: the garden belongs to the Knights, a sovereign order; the hill is Italy; the dome is Vatican City. Three countries, one keyhole.

Legend says Remus stood on this hill watching for omens while his brother watched from the Palatine — and Remus, famously, lost. His hill got the better deal. The Palatine got the emperors. The Aventine got the peace.
