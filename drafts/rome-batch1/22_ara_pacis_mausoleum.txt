Augustus wrote his own obituary — thirty-five chapters of everything he wanted remembered, in his own words — and this square holds the two monuments he built to make sure the remembering happened.

You should be in Piazza Augusto Imperatore, freshly relaid, between a long glass-and-travertine museum on the river side and a vast circle of brick and cypress in the middle of the square. The glass box holds the Ara Pacis, the Altar of Augustan Peace. The circle is the Mausoleum of Augustus, the tomb he built for himself and his family.

Now. The altar first. Consecrated in 9 BC to celebrate the peace Augustus brought home from his campaigns, it carries some of the finest carving Rome ever produced — a marble procession of the imperial family, children tugging at togas included. Then the Tiber buried it under four metres of silt. Fragments kept surfacing beneath a palazzo from the 1500s onwards, and in 1937 engineers froze seventy cubic metres of waterlogged ground solid just to cut the last pieces free.

It was reassembled here in 1938, on the two-thousandth anniversary of Augustus's birth — the centrepiece of the fascist government's remaking of this entire square, which levelled whole blocks to stage the ancient empire as backdrop for a modern one. That layer is still legible in the stern buildings around you, and in the obituary itself — the Res Gestae, recopied in that era onto a wall that survives beside the museum. The glass pavilion over the altar came in 2006, the first major new building in Rome's historic centre since the fascist years, and Romans have argued about it ever since; some still want it gone.

The mausoleum opposite is older than all of it — 28 BC, roughly ninety metres across, by most reckonings the largest circular tomb ever built. It held the ashes of Augustus and his dynasty, then spent the centuries as fortress, garden, bullring and concert hall, and is now deep in the final act of a restoration years in the making. You can't go inside yet. Two obelisks once flanked its door; one of them stands today on the Quirinal Hill.

[beat]

Augustus understood before anyone that power is partly memory management. Two thousand years of popes, dictators, architects and restorers have been re-curating him ever since — and the emperor is still under restoration, which he might have taken as a compliment. It means Rome is still deciding how to remember him.
