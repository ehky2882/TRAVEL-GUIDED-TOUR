What you're looking at is the end of a pipe.

A glorious end, admittedly — but the Trevi Fountain is, before anything else, the theatrical mouth of an aqueduct. The Aqua Virgo was built under the emperor Augustus, more than two thousand years ago, to carry spring water into the centre of Rome, and a descendant of that same channel still feeds the fountain in front of you. Romans built triumphal arches for their generals; this is something rarer — a triumphal arch for their plumbing.

You should be in the small piazza facing the fountain, which fills one entire side of the square and climbs the full face of the palace behind it. That's the trick of the Trevi: it isn't a fountain in front of a building, it's a building dissolving into water. The design is by Nicola Salvi, who won the competition in the 1730s and spent the rest of his life on it; he died before the water was turned on, and the fountain was finished in 1762.

The giant in the central arch is not Neptune, though nearly everyone assumes so — he's Oceanus, the older, stranger god of all the world's water. His chariot is drawn by two sea horses, one calm and one thrashing, which is the whole sea described in two animals. And the rockwork spilling toward you isn't a pedestal; it's carved to look as though the palace is breaking open and the water is escaping.

About the coins. The story goes that one coin thrown over your left shoulder brings you back to Rome — a legend, but a productive one, because the city collects the money for charity, and the fountain quietly raises well over a million euros a year for people who need it. These days access to the water's edge is metered, with a small ticket in daytime hours; the view from the piazza is free, always, and after the barriers open at night you can walk down for nothing.

If you can, come back late, after the crowds thin. The floodlights turn the travertine gold, the water is the loudest thing in the square, and the end of a Roman pipe becomes, briefly, the most beautiful place in the city.
