You're standing in a stadium.

Not on the site of one — in one. The long oval of Piazza Navona is the exact footprint of the Stadium of Domitian, built in the year 86 for Greek-style athletics, and the buildings around you stand directly on the foundations of its grandstands. Thirty thousand Romans once sat where the restaurants are. Even the name remembers it: the games were called agones, which fifteen centuries of Roman mouths slowly wore down into "Navona." The stones went the way all Roman stones go, but the empty space — the shape of the arena itself — has survived for nearly two thousand years. In Rome, even the voids are ancient.

You should be near the centre of the square, at the Fountain of the Four Rivers. This is Gian Lorenzo Bernini at full volume, from 1651: four colossal marble figures for the great rivers of four continents — the Danube, the Ganges, the Nile, the Plate — arranged around a hollowed-out crag of rock with an Egyptian-style obelisk balanced on top. Look at how little stone actually holds that obelisk up; the void underneath was Bernini showing off, and it has held for close to four centuries.

You may hear that the Nile figure covers his head in horror at the church behind him, Sant'Agnese, built by Bernini's great rival Borromini — and that one of the other figures raises a hand to shield himself in case the facade collapses. It's a wonderful story, and it's a legend: the fountain was finished before Borromini's facade went up. The rivalry was real; the insult is folklore. The Nile hides his face because, at the time, nobody knew where the Nile began.

One more picture to hold in your mind. For about two hundred years, on August weekends, the city plugged the fountain drains and flooded the whole centre of the square, turning it into a shallow lake. Carriages drove through the water; the nobility circled in the spray; the poor waded. A stadium, then a lake, now a piazza — the same oval, endlessly recast.

[beat]

Walk the perimeter once before you leave, following the curve of the buildings at the northern end. That bend in the architecture is the bend of the grandstand behind it. You're doing a lap.
