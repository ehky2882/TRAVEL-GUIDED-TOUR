The island you're about to walk onto is shaped like a ship — and that's not the river's doing. Ancient Rome faced its banks with travertine to sharpen the prow, and raised an obelisk in the middle for a mast. This city once turned an entire island into a vessel, and then gave it a cargo it has carried ever since.

Cross Ponte Fabricio to get there — the footbridge from the east bank, the one with the worn stone faces on its parapet. Pause halfway. This is the oldest bridge in Rome still standing in its original form, built in 62 BC, and the man who commissioned it, Lucius Fabricius, had his name carved into the arches — more than once — the Roman equivalent of signing your work. Every step you take is on his two-thousand-year-old guarantee.

Now the cargo. The story goes that during a plague, Rome sent a ship to Greece to fetch help from the god of healing, and a sacred snake slithered ashore exactly here — so the temple went up on the spot the snake chose. That's legend, told the way Romans liked to tell it. What's not legend is what followed: a temple of healing, then a medieval hospice, then a hospital founded in the 1580s that is still admitting patients today, its wards filling half the island. In a city that reassigns everything — tombs into fortresses, stadiums into piazzas — this little ship has held the same job for more than two thousand years.

And once, that job took courage. In 1943, when German forces raided the Jewish community just across the water, doctors here invented a diagnosis — a fearsome, fictional, highly contagious disease — and filled their wards with "patients" the occupiers were too frightened to inspect. People survived because this island kept doing what it has always done: taking people in.

Before you leave, take the steps down to the embankment and walk the island's rim at water level. From down there you can see the old travertine at the downstream tip — the last of the ship's prow, still cutting the Tiber into two streams, the way it has since the Republic.
