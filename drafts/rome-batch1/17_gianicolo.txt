Once a day, at noon exactly, this hill fires a cannon at the city below. It's been doing it since the 1840s — a blank shot, ordered up so that every church in Rome could set its bells to the same minute. The bells have long since gone electric. The cannon never stopped.

You're on the terrace at Piazzale Garibaldi, the top of the Gianicolo, with Rome spread out below you like a map that got up and became a city. Take the view first. The great pale dome floating to your left is St. Peter's. Straight ahead, the shallow saucer of domes and rooftops is the historic centre — you can pick out the flat grey disc of the Pantheon's dome, the white bulk of the Vittoriano, and behind them, the hills where the whole thing started.

From up here Rome looks eternal — finished, settled, permanent. So it's worth knowing that this hill is where the newest chapter was nearly strangled at birth. In 1849, a young republic declared in Rome made its stand on the Gianicolo against a French army sent to restore the pope, and the defence, led by Giuseppe Garibaldi, held out here for weeks before falling. The equestrian statue behind you is his; the stone busts lining the promenade are the men who fought beside him. Italy as a country was, in part, argued into existence on this ground.

Now walk a few minutes down the road toward the city and you'll meet the Fontanone — the Big Fountain — a wall of white marble arches with water thundering through it. Rome has a habit of raising triumphal arches to its own plumbing, and this is the grandest of them all: the finish line of an ancient aqueduct restored in the early 1600s, dressed in marble taken from the Roman Forum and granite columns salvaged from the original St. Peter's. Read the proud Latin inscription with sympathy — it credits the wrong aqueduct, and nobody ever corrected it.

If you can, be back up here for the last hour of light, when the domes go gold and the swifts come out. Or come at noon, and wait for it. The whole city still flinches, together, on schedule.
