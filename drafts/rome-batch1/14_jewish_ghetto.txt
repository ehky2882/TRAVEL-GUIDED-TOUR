The street ahead of you smells of fried artichokes. Hold that thought — it matters more than it seems.

You should be on Via del Portico d'Ottavia, the main street of Rome's Jewish quarter, facing the ancient ruin that gives the street its name: a great broken pediment on grey columns, patched with brick, standing where the restaurant terraces end.

Walk toward the columns. Augustus built this portico in the twenties BC and dedicated it to his sister Octavia — the grand entrance to a precinct that once held two temples and a pair of libraries. What stands today is mostly a repair from 203 AD, after a fire; even emperors patched in brick. Then, for the better part of a thousand years, this imperial gateway served as the city's fish market. The little church built into the ruin is still called Sant'Angelo in Pescheria — Saint Angelo at the fish market — and a stone once set the rule here that the head of any fish longer than the slab itself belonged to the city's magistrates.

In 1555, a papal decree walled in this handful of streets and ordered every Jew in Rome to live inside — gates locked at night, trades restricted, on low ground the river flooded at will. The community was even made to pay for the wall that enclosed it. That lasted more than three centuries, until Italian unification brought emancipation; the walls came down in 1888, and in 1904 the Great Synagogue rose by the river. Look for its dome above the rooftops — the one square dome on a skyline of round ones, and that was precisely the point.

As you walk here, look down now and then. Small brass cobbles sit in front of doorways, each carrying a name — the stumbling stones. They mark the homes of people taken away at dawn on 16 October 1943 and deported; almost none returned. The neighbourhood keeps their names at its feet.

[beat]

By most reckonings this is the oldest Jewish community in Europe — present in Rome since before the emperors. And that is the real monument on this street. Not the portico: the continuity. The community outlasted the empire that built the gate, the papacy that built the wall, and the occupiers who came at dawn.

So order the artichokes — carciofi alla giudia, Jewish-style, invented inside these walls when constraint had to become cuisine. The street is loud, crowded and alive, and that is exactly the point of it.
