If Trastevere (trahs teh veh reh) has a living room, you're standing in it. The piazza, the fountain, the church with the golden face — this is where the neighbourhood has gathered for centuries, and at dusk, when the lights come up and the mosaics catch fire, you can see why nobody ever wanted to meet anywhere else.

Stand by the fountain in the middle of the square, facing the church. Those steps you're probably sharing with a dozen other people ring a basin that was already on a map of Rome in fourteen seventy-two — by most reckonings one of the oldest fountains in the city, patched and polished by centuries of architects and still doing its original job as the neighbourhood's front porch.

Look at the facade before you go in. Across the top runs a band of mosaic: Mary at the centre, flanked by ten women carrying lamps, most still lit after eight hundred years. This church claims — in an inscription carved inside — to be the first in Rome dedicated to Mary. Another basilica across town disputes the title, and the argument has been running for centuries. What's beyond dispute is that Christians were gathering here before it was even legal to be one.

Now step inside, and look at the columns holding up the nave. All twenty-two of them are older than the church — hauled here in the eleven forties from the ruins of the Baths of Caracalla (kah rah kahl lah). An emperor's bathhouse, dismantled to hold up a church: this city doesn't preserve its past so much as put it back to work.

Then walk up toward the glow. The apse mosaic, from those same eleven forties, shows Christ and Mary enthroned side by side — his arm resting around her shoulders, an image of plain human tenderness that has been glowing up there for nearly nine hundred years. Below it, a strip of smaller scenes from her life, laid in gold in twelve ninety-one by Pietro Cavallini (pyeh troh kah vahl lee nee), a Roman pushing mosaic toward something new: real beds, real drapery, real space — painting's future, arriving in glass.

When you come back out, the square will still be doing what it does — kids, guitars, spritzes, pilgrims. The gold face above them has watched versions of this exact evening since the Middle Ages. Find a step. Join the room.
