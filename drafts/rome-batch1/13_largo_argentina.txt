Look down. The sunken square below the railing sits several metres beneath the one you're standing on, and that gap is the whole story — twenty centuries of Rome stacked layer on layer, until the modern street simply rose above its own past.

This is Largo di Torre Argentina. You should be at the railing of a busy square — buses, trams, taxis circling — looking down into a field of ruins: column stumps, temple platforms, umbrella pines at the corners. Four temples stand side by side down there, and the oldest goes back well over two thousand years, to a Rome that was still a republic with something to prove.

Find the round one first, the ring of columns near the middle. Archaeologists never fully settled which gods most of these temples served, so they go by letters instead of names: A, B, C and D. And behind them, at the far edge of the excavation, ran the great hall of Pompey's theatre complex — the Curia of Pompey, where the Senate was meeting on the Ides of March, 44 BC, when Julius Caesar was assassinated. The most consequential political killing in Roman history happened just there, behind those temple platforms, in what is now the middle of a traffic interchange.

The name, by the way, has nothing to do with Argentina. It comes from the tower on the corner — the Torre — built for a papal courtier from Strasbourg, a city whose Latin name was Argentoratum. The silver city, hiding in plain sight on the bus map.

All of this only surfaced in the 1920s, when whole blocks were demolished and the temples emerged blinking into the modern city. For nearly a century afterwards you could only peer down from up here, sharing the view with the residents who moved in when the builders moved out — a colony of cats, hundreds of them over the years, with a sanctuary of their own among the ruins. Then, in 2023, the city built a walkway down into the excavation, and for a small ticket you can descend and stand at the level Caesar knew.

[beat]

That is Rome's oldest habit, and this square is the clearest place to watch it work: the past here doesn't sit behind glass. It surfaces — into a tram stop, a cat colony, a lunchtime shortcut — and the city folds it in and carries on.
