On a clear evening, half of Rome seems to drift up here without ever agreeing to — and then just stands still, facing west.

This is the Pincio Terrace, on the edge of the Villa Borghese gardens. You should be on a broad paved overlook with a stone balustrade, umbrella pines behind you and the city falling away in front. Directly below sits the great oval of Piazza del Popolo — the twin-domed churches, the obelisk at the centre, maybe the spot where you stood earlier — and beyond it, on the horizon, the dome of St Peter's, lined up with the terrace almost as if someone planned the view. Someone did.

Walk to the balustrade. Two thousand years ago this hillside held the terraced gardens of Lucullus — a Roman general so famous for his banquets that his name still means luxurious living. For centuries the view from up here belonged to whoever owned the hill. Then, in the early 1800s, the terrace and the square below were laid out together as one composition — ramps, oval, overlook — and the Pincio opened as Rome's first public garden, at a time when nearly every park in the city was still some aristocrat's back lawn. The best view in Rome, handed to everyone.

The promenade behind you kept adding furniture to the idea. Stone busts line the paths — a couple of hundred of them, an open-air hall of fame of Italian poets, scientists and patriots, gathering since the mid-1800s. And a few minutes' walk into the gardens stands one of the city's oddest treasures: a water clock, built by a Dominican friar in 1867, ticking away on its own little island — a grandfather clock run entirely on falling water, and still keeping time.

Rome planned its three great balconies deliberately — this one, the Gianicolo across the river, the Orange Garden on the Aventine — and this is the original of the trio.

Lucullus threw the most exclusive dinner parties in Roman history on this slope. Now anyone with an evening to spare gets his view for nothing — which might be the quietest, most complete victory the public ever won over a rich man. Stay for the light. It's the whole point.
