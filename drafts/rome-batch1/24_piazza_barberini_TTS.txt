Look for the bees. Once you spot the first three, you'll start seeing them everywhere — and that's exactly what the family who put them there intended.

This is pee aht sah bar beh ree nee, a busy, traffic-ringed square at the foot of the kwee ree nahl Hill, and at its centre a muscular figure of weathered stone kneels on an open scallop shell, head tilted back, blowing a jet of water out of a conch. That's the Triton Fountain — bair nee nee, sixteen forty-three — carved not in marble but in travertine, the rough, pitted stone Rome is built from, so the sea god looks like he grew out of the city rather than being placed in it. Below him, four dolphins hold the shell aloft, and between their tails sits the papal crest with three bees. The water he's blowing skyward, incidentally, arrived by aqueduct — Rome never stopped throwing parades for its plumbing.

Look at the Triton first, then find the small fountain at the corner of vee ah veh neh toh: a shell opening like a book, with bees crawling down to drink. The Fountain of the Bees, bair nee nee again, a year later.

The bees were the emblem of the bar beh ree nee, the family of Pope Urban the Eighth, and this whole corner of Rome was effectively their front garden. Their palace stands just up the slope — and inside it, the great rivalry of the Roman Baroque shares a single building. When the original architect died in sixteen twenty-nine, the job went to bair nee nee, then a young sculptor-prodigy, over the older man's assistant — a prickly, brilliant draughtsman named bor roh mee nee, who stayed on anyway. Each left a staircase. bair nee nee's is grand, square and made to impress; bor roh mee nee's, across the courtyard, is an oval spiral that seems to turn itself upward like a shell — with tiny bees carved into its columns. Same palace, same patron, two irreconcilable souls. Keep that pair in mind; this story finishes one hill over.

The family took as freely as it gave. When bronze was stripped from the Pantheon's porch in their era, Romans pinned it on them with a shrug that became a proverb: what the barbarians didn't do, the bar beh ree nee did.

Power fades, but branding is forever. The popes' names wear off the guidebooks — the bees are still drinking at the fountain.
