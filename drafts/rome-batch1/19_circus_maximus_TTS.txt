It's race day, sometime in the second century. A hundred and fifty thousand people — some say a quarter of a million — are packed into the valley you're looking at, screaming for one of four colour-coded teams, while twelve chariots come off the line at the far end in a wall of dust and noise. This was the largest venue for live entertainment ever built by anyone, anywhere. And today it's a field.

Stand at the rail above the long grassy basin, anywhere along Via del Circo Massimo (vee ah del cheer koh mahs see moh), with the umbrella pines of the Palatine Hill rising on the far side. The shape below you is the whole show: a flattened U, six hundred metres long, its curved end to your right.

Now put the stadium back. The grass banks you're looking at were tiers of stone and timber seating, wrapping the whole valley. Down the middle ran a long spine crowded with monuments — lap counters shaped like eggs and dolphins, shrines, and a pair of Egyptian obelisks. Here's the connection worth having: one of those obelisks is the one now standing in Piazza del Popolo (pee aht sah del poh poh loh) — the first obelisk ever brought to Rome. Before it presided over a piazza, it spent fifteen centuries watching chariots take the turn at full speed, right about where the joggers are now.

The emptiness, oddly, is the exhibit. Almost every other ancient monument in Rome survives as a ruin you look at; the Circus survives as a space you can use. Romans run laps in it, walk dogs across it, fill it for concerts and championship celebrations — a quarter of a million people still turn up on the big nights, which is exactly what the valley was built for. The seating went into other buildings centuries ago, but the shape, the scale, the purpose — those never left.

Before you go, walk down onto the grass and along the centre line, where the spine ran. Palatine on one side, Aventine (ah ven teen) on the other, the roar long gone. It's the rarest kind of ancient site: one that's still doing its job, just more quietly.
