Stop when you reach the stretch of the concourse marked Promenades Cathédrale, and consider your position: you are standing in a shopping arcade, underground, directly beneath a Gothic cathedral. Above your head — through the ceiling, through bedrock-anchored concrete columns, past the crypt-level foundations — rise the pointed arches and gargoyles of Christ Church, seat of Montreal's Anglican diocese since the 1850s.

The church and the ground have been quarrelling from the start. The soft soil under the cathedral never could carry the weight of its stone steeple, and by the 1920s the tower had tilted more than a metre off true. The steeple came down in 1927 — some sixteen hundred tonnes of it — and for over a decade the cathedral stood decapitated, until an anonymous donor paid for a replacement in 1940 with a very Montreal solution: a spire of aluminum, cast and weathered to imitate stone. Look up at it when you surface. Almost no one on Sainte-Catherine Street ever guesses.

The ground problem was only truly solved in the 1980s, when the church agreed to let developers excavate this mall beneath it — the entire cathedral held aloft on stilts during the dig, then settled onto new columns sunk to bedrock. The deal fixed the foundations and left the congregation with rent flowing up from the shops below. A church that keeps the lights on by leasing out the earth beneath itself: Bourget, up the street, would have been scandalized, and possibly a little impressed.

[beat]

Take the escalators up and step out — into the cathedral if it's open, or onto Sainte-Catherine beside it. And notice what just happened: you crossed downtown twice today, once through the weather and once beneath it. That's the whole proposition of this city in a single walk. Montreal never beat winter. It just quietly built a second city where winter isn't invited.
