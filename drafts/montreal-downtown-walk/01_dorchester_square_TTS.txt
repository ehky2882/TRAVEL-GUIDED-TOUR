This square is downtown's summer living room — office workers on the grass, food trucks at the curb — but look down at the pathways as you walk, and you'll find small crosses set into the pavement. They're there because before this was a park, it was a cemetery. Montreal buried its dead here through the first half of the eighteen hundreds, including thousands lost to the cholera years, and when the growing city finally closed the grounds in eighteen fifty-four, many were moved to the new cemetery on the mountain — but not all. Some still rest below, and the crosses in the paving are the city's quiet way of saying it hasn't forgotten. A park was built over them partly out of respect: gardens disturb the ground less than buildings do.

Now raise your eyes to the grey granite colossus filling the whole east side of the square. That's the Sun Life Building, raised in stages between the nineteen-tens and the early nineteen thirties, and when it was finished it was the largest building in the entire British Empire by floor space — an insurance company's way of announcing that Montreal was where the Empire kept its money. Which, for a while, was literally true: during the Second World War, Britain secretly shipped a fortune in gold and securities across the Atlantic and locked it in a vault three levels beneath this building. The famous version of the story says the Crown Jewels were down there too. They weren't — that part is legend, and by most accounts a deliberately encouraged one, because a good rumour guards a vault better than a bad lock.

Walk toward the Sun Life Building and then bear right, to the south end of the square, where a very Roman dome rises above the office blocks. A cathedral is about to pick a fight with an entire neighbourhood.
