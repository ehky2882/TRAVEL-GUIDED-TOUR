If the silhouette in front of you looks familiar, it should: this is Saint Peter's Basilica in Rome, scaled down and set among Canadian office towers. And the location is not an accident — it's the whole point. When Bishop eeg nass boor zhay set out to build a new Catholic cathedral in the eighteen seventies, he planted it deliberately here, in what was then the Protestant, English-speaking end of downtown, and he made it a copy of Rome itself so nobody could mistake the message: the Catholic Church intended to stand in the very centre of the new city, in the grandest costume it owned.

Getting it built took stubbornness of a high order. The first architect boor zhay sent to study Saint Peter's came home and told him flatly that it couldn't be reproduced at a smaller scale. boor zhay's response was to send someone else — a chaplain, quietly dispatched to Rome to measure the original and build a scale model — and the cathedral went up anyway, consecrated in eighteen ninety-four.

Now look along the roofline. Saint Peter's carries statues of the twelve apostles up there. This cathedral carries thirteen figures, and they aren't apostles at all — they're the patron saints of the thirteen Montreal parishes that paid for the building. The donors put their own saints on the roof, which might be the most honest piece of fundraising in the history of architecture. Step inside if it's open; it's free, and the canopy over the altar is a story in itself.
