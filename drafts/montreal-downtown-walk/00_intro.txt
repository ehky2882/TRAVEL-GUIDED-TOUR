Every city walk you'll ever take assumes decent weather. This one doesn't. What follows was designed to work on the coldest day of a Montreal February — a city that gets metres of snow a year and decided, decades ago, that winter was a design problem it could solve. If you're walking it in July, wonderful; you'll simply see the machinery with the weather switched off.

The route is short and flat — about a kilometre and a half through the downtown core — and it ends somewhere unexpected: underground, beneath a cathedral, in the middle of the largest experiment in indoor city-building on the continent. Along the way: a square built over a graveyard, a fortress that guarded an empire's gold, a copy of Saint Peter's planted in enemy territory, and a tower that started it all by digging a hole.

[beat]

Start at Dorchester Square — Peel or Bonaventure métro will land you close — and find a bench somewhere near the middle, among the trees and the monuments. Look around, and when you've got your bearings, we'll talk about what's underneath you.
