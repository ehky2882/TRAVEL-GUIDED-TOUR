The cruciform tower above you went up in 1962, and it changed this city twice — once on the skyline, and once below your shoes. Its shape isn't just styling: four wings in a cross meant light on every floor and a silhouette nothing else on the continent had. And up top, look for the beacon after dark — four white beams that have swept the sky over Montreal every night since the tower opened, the closest thing a landlocked downtown has to a lighthouse.

But the real revolution was the hole it was built in. This block spent decades as an open railway trench gouged through the middle of downtown — locals just called it the pit — and when the developers finally built over it, they had leftover space below street level and a very Montreal thought: why waste it? So under the new tower they laid out a full concourse of shops and passages, connected it to the train station, and in doing so planted the seed of what locals now call the underground city — an indoor network that has been growing for sixty years and runs, today, for tens of kilometres beneath downtown, stitching together métro stations, food halls, hotels, universities and about anything else a person needs between November and April. Half a million people move through it on a winter day without ever reaching for a coat.

Time to join them. Find the escalators down into the Place Ville Marie concourse, and follow the signs north toward the Eaton Centre and Promenades Cathédrale — the names are posted overhead at every junction. It's a ten-minute indoor stroll, and pay attention as you go, because the finish line of this walk is directly above your head.
