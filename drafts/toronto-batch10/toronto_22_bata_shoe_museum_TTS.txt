On the corner of Bloor Street West and St. George Street, look up at the low limestone building that appears to be a box with its lid set on slightly crooked. That's not an accident. The bahta Shoe Museum is a building shaped like a shoebox, and the reason why tells you what the place is really about.

When the architect, Raymond moree yama, first saw the collection back in the late nineteen seventies, what struck him wasn't the shoes. It was the boxes they were stored in, protecting them from light and dust. So he built the museum as a box: pale French limestone chosen because it has the warm tone of raw leather, a copper lid that will slowly turn green, and a glass wedge for a door. The city's zoning capped the height, so he made the constraint the whole idea, a low, lidded box on a busy corner.

Inside are something like fifteen thousand objects spanning four thousand five hundred years, only a fraction shown at any one time. But the spine of the place is a single observation, made by its founder, sonya bahta, who started collecting in the nineteen forties while travelling the world with her husband's shoe company. Everywhere she went, she noticed the same thing: people's feet are identical the world over, and what they choose to put on them is different in every single culture.

That's the quiet argument this building makes. The shoe is the most overlooked thing you own and one of the most honest records you leave behind. It knows your climate, your work, your status, your vanity. There's a gentle irony in it, too: she began saving traditional footwear partly because her own family's factories were busy replacing it.

So before you go in, look down at whatever you've got on your feet. Somewhere in that box behind the crooked lid, there's a pair that tells a story a lot like yours.
