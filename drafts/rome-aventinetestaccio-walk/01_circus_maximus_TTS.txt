For centuries, this was where Rome came to scream.

It looks like a long grassy valley now — more than half a kilometre of it, with a raised ridge running down the middle and slopes rising on either side. Read it as a racetrack, because that's what it was: the ridge marks the spee nah, the central barrier the chariots thundered around, and the slopes held tier upon tier of seating. On race days, a crowd far larger than the Colosseum ever held packed into this valley to watch teams of four horses take the turns at full gallop.

And they didn't just watch — they belonged. Every racegoer followed one of four factions, known by their colours: the Blues, the Greens, the Reds and the Whites. People were born into a colour, married within it, rioted for it. Emperors were known to favour one team and to sulk when it lost. Star drivers, many of them born enslaved, earned fortunes that scandalized Rome's poets. If modern sport has an ancestor — the merchandise, the transfers, the heartbreak — it was invented in this valley.

The marble and the seating went the way most Roman marble went, into other buildings, and the valley returned to what it had always been underneath: a space in the middle of a city. Depending on the season, you may find a stage and banks of temporary seating filling one end — the valley hosts opera and concerts on summer nights, so don't be surprised to share it with a crew building something.

Now for the quiet after the roar. With the valley on your left, find the road that climbs the hill behind you — the ah ven teen — and take your time on the way up. In about ten minutes you'll come to a walled garden full of orange trees. That's our next stop: the jar dee noh del yee ah rahn chee.
