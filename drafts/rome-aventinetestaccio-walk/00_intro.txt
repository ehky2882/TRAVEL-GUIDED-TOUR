Every other walk in this city shows you what emperors and popes built for show. This one shows you what Rome built for itself.

A racetrack for its days off. A hilltop for its quiet. And at the end, a neighbourhood that fed the whole operation — with a hill made entirely by working hands. No triumphal arches on this route, no domes until one appears through a keyhole. Just the ordinary city, which in Rome is never ordinary for long.

You should be standing near the Circo Massimo metro station, at the southeastern end of a long green valley stretching away in front of you. That valley is the Circus Maximus, and it's our first stop. The full route runs about two and a half kilometres: flat along the valley, then one steady climb of about ten minutes up the Aventine Hill, and gently downhill the rest of the way into Testaccio. Wear comfortable shoes and bring an appetite — the finish line has some of the best food in Rome.

Walk along the edge of the valley until you have a clear view down its whole length, and stop there. The Circus Maximus first.
