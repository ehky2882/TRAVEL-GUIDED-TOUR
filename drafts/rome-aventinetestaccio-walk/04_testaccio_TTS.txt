The hill rising at the edge of this neighbourhood was made entirely by hand — and nobody who made it ever thought of it as a monument.

Make your way toward it through the grid of streets, or start at the covered market if you're hungry; either works, and the neighbourhood is small enough that you can't really go wrong. tess taht choh sits on the flats where ancient Rome kept its river port — the em poh ree um — and behind the docks stood warehouse after warehouse, holding the grain and wine and olive oil that fed a city of a million people. The oil arrived in big clay jars, and here's the catch: an oil jar can't be reused. The residue turns rancid. So for around three centuries, the dockworkers broke the empties and stacked the pieces — neatly, in terraces, dusted with lime — until the pile of broken jars was a hill more than thirty metres high. mon teh tess taht choh: the paperwork of an empire, filed in clay.

The neighbourhood around it has been Rome's working stomach ever since it was built for labourers' families in the late nineteenth century, and it never really stopped cooking. In the covered market — mornings are its best hours — you can eat as well as anywhere in the city while standing up. And beneath the market floor, excavations have uncovered one of those ancient warehouses itself, broken jars still in place, the whole story of this quarter sitting a few metres under the fruit stalls.

So here's where this walk has been heading. Uphill, emperors and popes built for eternity and mostly got it. Down here, working Rome just got on with the job — racing, unloading, breaking empties — and its leavings piled into the only hill in Rome that ordinary people made. It might be the most honest monument in the city.

Stay for lunch. Order whatever the person next to you is having. This neighbourhood has been feeding Rome for two thousand years, and it's very good at it.
