At the far corner of this quiet piazza, a small crowd gathers at a green door, taking turns to press one eye against it.

Join them. It's free, it moves quickly, and what's on the other side is worth the wait: through the keyhole, a long tunnel of clipped hedges runs away from you, and floating at the end of it, perfectly framed, is the dome of St. Peter's — kilometres away and seemingly close enough to touch.

The door belongs to the priory of the Knights of Malta, an order of knights founded during the Crusades and still going, and the square you're standing in was designed for them in the eighteenth century by Piranesi — an architect better known for drawing buildings than building them. He decorated these walls with trophies and emblems and built the whole piazza around an old idea of the Aventine as a sacred ship, ready to sail: the hedges beyond the door read as rigging, the hilltop as a deck.

Whether anyone planned the view through the keyhole is another matter. The tidy story says it was composed deliberately, hedge by hedge; the honest answer is that nobody knows who first lined it up, or when. Take the tidy story as a story. The dome doesn't mind either way.

When you've had your turn, take the street that drops downhill from the piazza and keep descending toward the river flats. Ten minutes or so brings you down into a grid of ordinary streets that smells faintly of coffee and frying. That's Testaccio — the last stop, and the reason you saved your appetite.
