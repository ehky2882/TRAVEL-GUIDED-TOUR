Breathe in as you come through the gate. Depending on the month, the air in here carries bitter orange — blossom in spring, fruit in winter — and either way it's the ah ven teen's calling card.

You're in the jar dee noh del yee ah rahn chee, the Garden of the Oranges, a walled square of green laid out inside the remains of a medieval fortress — the walls around you belonged to the sah vehl lee family, who held this hilltop when holding hilltops was how power worked. The orange trees have their own story: the first of them, so the story goes, was planted by Saint Dominic himself, from a cutting he carried from Spain eight centuries ago. A descendant of that tree is still tended in the monastery next door. Take the legend as a legend — but the monks take it seriously.

Walk to the terrace at the far end when you're ready. The whole city opens up below you in one sweep, from the river bending at your feet to the domes stacked up on the horizon.

Here's the thing worth knowing about this hill while you look. The ah ven teen was, for most of ancient history, the outsiders' hill. It stood beyond the poh meh ree um — Rome's oldest sacred boundary — so it collected everyone and everything the official city kept at arm's length: traders, foreign cults, and the plebeians, the common citizens, who by tradition staged their great political walkouts up here when the powerful pushed too far. The hill of the people with the best view in Rome. It took the modern city a few thousand years to catch on, and now the outsiders' hill is the quietest, most coveted address in the centre.

When you've had your fill of the view, come back through the trees and out the gate, and turn right along the quiet street — vee ah dee sahn tah sah bee nah. Three minutes of flat walking brings you to a small piazza with decorated walls, and a green door with a crowd in front of it. That's the Keyhole.
