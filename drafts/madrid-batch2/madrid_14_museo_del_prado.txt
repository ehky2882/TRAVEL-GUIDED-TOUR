There are bigger museums than this one, and there are museums that set out to tell you the whole story of art, start to finish. The Prado is neither — and that's the thing to understand before you go in. What you're about to walk through isn't a survey. It's a collection. For three hundred years, Spanish kings bought the paintings they wanted to live with, and what hangs here is, more or less, what they chose.

Stand for a moment on the Paseo del Prado side, with the long, low, sand-coloured building stretching away beside you. The architect, Juan de Villanueva, designed it in the 1780s as a home for natural history — a place for minerals and bones. It opened instead, in 1819, as a gallery for the royal pictures. The bones never moved in; the kings did.

Start with Velázquez, because the whole museum leans towards one room. Las Meninas is the painting everyone comes for, and it earns the crowd: the small princess and her maids, the dog, the painter himself with his brush, all caught mid-moment — and every one of them looking out at you. For centuries people have asked what he's actually painting in there. Then you notice the mirror on the far wall, with the king and queen reflected in it, and you realise you're standing roughly where they stood. The picture looks back at you because you've taken the monarch's place.

Then Goya, who is really two painters under one roof. Upstairs, the court portraitist, all silk and powder and royal flattery. Downstairs, the black paintings — the images he put straight onto the walls of his own house when he was old and deaf, never meant for anyone's eyes. They were lifted off the plaster and carried here. You're looking at a private darkness that was never for sale.

And among the Flemish rooms, Bosch's Garden of Earthly Delights, three panels of paradise and chaos that Philip the Second loved enough to keep close.

[beat]

That's the secret of the Prado. It runs deep in Spanish, Flemish and Venetian painting not by some grand plan, but because that's where the kings were passionate — and it goes quiet where they weren't. You're not walking through a textbook. You're walking through what a few powerful people genuinely loved, still hanging where they wanted it. Find the ones that stop you, and stay with them. That's exactly how it was always meant to be used.
