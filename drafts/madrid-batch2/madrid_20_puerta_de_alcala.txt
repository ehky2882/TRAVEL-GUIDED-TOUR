Most triumphal arches mark a victory — a battle won, an army home. This one marks nothing of the sort. The Puerta de Alcalá was built to be a door. A very grand door, for a city that didn't yet look the part.

You're standing in the Plaza de la Independencia, on a roundabout, with the arch stranded in the middle of it like an island in a river of cars. Walk around it if you safely can, because here's the first surprise: the two faces don't match. The architect, Francesco Sabatini, gave each side different decoration, as if he couldn't choose — and the story goes that Charles the Third, shown the competing designs, simply approved them both. So one face greets you coming in, the other sees you out, and they're not the same.

It went up in 1778, in granite, five openings across — three tall arches in the middle, a square doorway at each end. And it was, for its moment, a statement: Charles the Third had decided Madrid should stop looking like a dusty royal village and start looking like Paris or Rome. He paved streets, lit lamps, planted promenades, built fountains — the same campaign that gave you Cibeles down the road. The locals called him the best mayor Madrid ever had. This gate was his calling card.

Here's the second surprise, though. A gate is supposed to be in a wall, and there's no wall. When this was built, it stood at the eastern edge of the city, where travellers arriving from the town of Alcalá were stopped and taxed. Then Madrid kept growing, swallowed the boundary, and left the gate behind — a door to nowhere, a threshold with nothing on either side of it but the city itself.

[beat]

And somehow that's why it's loved. It's a monument to a king's ambition that the city promptly outran, a grand entrance that became a fond, useless centrepiece. Stand here as the traffic wheels around it, and you're looking at the exact spot where Madrid once ended — and at how completely it forgot to stop there.
