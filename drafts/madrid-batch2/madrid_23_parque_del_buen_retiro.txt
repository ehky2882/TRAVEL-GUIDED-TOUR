The word Retiro means retreat — and that's exactly what this was. Not a park. A king's private escape, walled off from the city, that ordinary people couldn't enter for the better part of two hundred years. The radical thing about where you're standing is how completely that changed.

Make your way to the Estanque, the big rectangular lake near the park's northern end — you'll know it by the half-circle of stone columns rising along one side, and the king on horseback at the top of them. That's the monument to Alfonso the Twelfth: a grand colonnade, a bronze rider, the whole vocabulary of royal seriousness. And directly below it, on the water it overlooks, people in hired rowing boats are going around in slow, cheerful circles, getting nowhere. That contrast is the whole park in one view.

Because this lake wasn't built for pleasure-boating. In the 1630s, when Philip the Fourth laid out these grounds beside a palace that's mostly gone now, the Estanque was a stage for mock naval battles — staged sea-fights, with real boats, to entertain a bored court. For two centuries this was the monarch's playground, off-limits, private. Then, in 1868, it was opened to the public, and the retreat stopped being a retreat. It became the city's living room.

And it still is. On a Sunday the Retiro fills with joggers, families, fortune-tellers, a drum circle that finds its own corner, dogs off their leads — the ordinary uses of a great green space, happening on ground a king once kept for himself. In 2021, the whole tree-lined avenue that runs from here down past the great museums was named a UNESCO World Heritage site — the Landscape of Light, Madrid's first. A royal indulgence, turned into shared everyday luxury, and then recognised as one of the finest of its kind anywhere.

So sit by the water a while. Watch the boats go nowhere under the gaze of a bronze king who never imagined you'd be allowed in. That's the best of Madrid, really — grandeur that stopped guarding itself, and simply let everyone in.
