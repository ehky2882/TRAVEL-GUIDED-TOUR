If the Prado is what Spanish kings loved, this is everything they left out. The Thyssen-Bornemisza is the third point of what Madrid calls the golden triangle of art — three great collections within a few minutes' walk — and its job, more or less, is to fill in the centuries and the schools the royal collection skipped.

You're in the Palacio de Villahermosa, an early nineteenth-century mansion that the architect Rafael Moneo gutted and rebuilt inside to hold the pictures. Start at the top and work down: the museum is hung chronologically, so you can walk the whole history of Western painting from the top floor to the ground, in order, in about an hour. That's the gift of the place — not one masterpiece, but the through-line.

And it's a through-line the Prado simply doesn't have. Up on the top floor are the early Italians and the Flemish primitives — gold-ground altarpieces, the careful beginnings — the stuff the Spanish crown was never especially interested in. Keep going and you reach the Impressionists and on into the twentieth century: a Degas dancer, a Van Gogh, the moderns. The Spanish kings stopped collecting long before any of this existed, which is exactly why it matters that it's here.

Because none of this was planned. The collection was built across two generations by a German-Hungarian baron and his family, assembling privately, on their own taste, with no thought of completing anyone else's gallery. Spain rented it, then bought it outright in 1993, and only then did the three collections line up into a single sweep — the crown's painting in the Prado, the modern century in the Reina Sofía, and here, the long middle that joins them.

[beat]

So treat it as the connective tissue. The Prado tells you what power wanted on its walls; the Thyssen tells you everything that was happening in the rooms power wasn't looking at. Between the two, almost by accident, you get the whole story. Walk it top to bottom and let the centuries arrive in order — it's one of the few museums in the world generous enough to let you do that.
