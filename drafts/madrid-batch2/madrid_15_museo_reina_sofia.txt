Most paintings hang where they were made, or near it. The one you've come to see has never worked like that. Guernica was painted in Paris, by a Spaniard who refused to set foot in his own country while a dictator ran it — and for more than forty years, it lived in exile, just like him.

You're in the Reina Sofía, in the former San Carlos Hospital — a plain eighteenth-century building given a pair of glass capsules bolted to the front that lift you up the outside, floor by floor. Jean Nouvel added the red-trimmed extension out the back in 2005. But the heart of the place is one room, and one canvas, and it's worth knowing how it got here before you stand in front of it.

In April 1937, during the Spanish Civil War, the Basque town of Gernika was bombed from the air and largely destroyed. Picasso, living in Paris, read the reports and answered with this: an enormous painting, nearly eight metres wide, in nothing but grey, black and white — a horse, a bull, a screaming figure, a single bare bulb burning over all of it. No colour. No blood, exactly. Just shock, made permanent.

Then he did something deliberate. He left the painting in New York, at the Museum of Modern Art, and asked that it stay out of Spain until the country was a republic again — until Spaniards were free. He didn't live to see it. The painting outlasted both the painter and the dictator, and it finally crossed the border in 1981, six years after Franco died, to a country that had just become a democracy. For a while it hung behind bulletproof glass.

[beat]

It's still contested, even now. As the ninetieth anniversary of the bombing approaches, the Basque region has asked to borrow it for a show in Bilbao; the museum is reluctant, arguing the canvas is too fragile to travel. Both sides have a real case, and the argument isn't really about logistics — it's about who a wound belongs to.

Stand close, then step back. Whatever you make of the politics, the thing in front of you was made by someone who decided a painting could be a form of evidence — and who refused to let it come home until home was worth coming back to.
