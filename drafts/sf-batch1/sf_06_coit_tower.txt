Let's clear something up right away, because you've probably already heard it. People love to tell you that Coit Tower was built to look like a fire-hose nozzle, because the woman who paid for it was obsessed with firefighters. It's a great story. The architect said it wasn't true.

You're standing on top of Telegraph Hill, at the base of a smooth, fluted concrete column a little over two hundred feet tall. The money came from Lillie Hitchcock Coit, a genuine San Francisco character who, as a girl, did chase fire engines and became a mascot of one of the volunteer companies. When she died in 1929 she left a third of her fortune to beautify the city, and this tower, finished in 1933, was the result. But Arthur Brown Junior, the architect, insisted the shape was just a clean Art Deco column. The fire-hose thing is a story the city told itself because it was better than the truth.

The real reward isn't the shape anyway. It's inside, around the base. When you step in, look at the walls: they're covered, floor to ceiling, in murals painted in 1934 by a small army of local artists, paid by a Depression-era federal program to put people back to work. And they didn't paint postcards. They painted California as it actually was in the thirties — laborers in the fields, workers on the docks, a man reading a newspaper full of left-wing headlines, a library shelf with the titles showing.

It was too much for some people. The imagery was considered so politically radical that the opening was delayed while the city argued about whether to paint parts of it over. In the end, they left it. It's one of the most honest pieces of public art in the country, hiding inside a tower famous for a shape its own architect denied.

*[beat]*

So enjoy the view — and on a clear day, from up top, it is one of the great ones, the whole bay laid out below you. But don't leave without going inside. The tower out here is the legend. The murals in there are the truth, and they almost didn't survive the telling.
