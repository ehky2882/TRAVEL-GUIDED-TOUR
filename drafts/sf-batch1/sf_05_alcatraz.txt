Look out across the water to that low island with the tower and the long pale building running along its top. That's Alcatraz — "The Rock" — and it has been, in turn, a fortress, a prison, a protest, and a place of pilgrimage. It's worth knowing all four before you go.

Start with the obvious one. From 1934 to 1963, Alcatraz was the federal government's maximum-security prison, the place it sent the men other prisons couldn't hold. Al Capone did time here. So did the man the movies called the Birdman, though in fact he kept no birds on this island. The cold water and the current were supposed to make escape impossible, and officially, no one ever made it off and was accounted for again. What finally closed the prison wasn't a breakout — it was money. Salt air had eaten the buildings, and the island cost nearly three times what a mainland prison cost to run. So in 1963 they shut it down and left it empty.

And then, in 1969, something remarkable happened. A group of Native American activists, calling themselves Indians of All Tribes, landed on the island and claimed it — pointing to a treaty provision that allowed Native people to reclaim surplus federal land. They stayed for nineteen months. The occupation drew national attention to federal policy toward Native Americans, and it's remembered as a turning point in that movement. Every year, before dawn on Thanksgiving, people still gather on the island for an Indigenous sunrise ceremony.

Today it's a national park, and the cellhouse audio tour is one of the most affecting things you can do in the city. There's also a live debate about its future: the current federal administration has floated reopening Alcatraz as a working prison. As of now that's a budget proposal, not a plan — the island is still a park, and the people who study it mostly call a reopening a long shot.

That's a lot of history for one small rock. Held the country's most feared men, then handed a movement its loudest stage, and is now the subject of an argument about what it should be next.

When you're ready to go out, the ferries leave from Pier 33 — and you'll want to book ahead. They sell out.
