The best thing on this pier is the one thing nobody planned, nobody built, and nobody owns. Follow the noise — that low, honking, barking racket coming from the west side — and look down at the floating docks in the marina.

Sea lions. Sometimes a few dozen, sometimes hundreds, hauled out on the docks, piled on top of each other, barking, shoving, sunbathing, and occasionally launching into the water for no clear reason. They are wild animals. No one feeds them. No one keeps them here. They just showed up.

The story is almost too neat. In 1989, right after the Loma Prieta earthquake, a handful of California sea lions started climbing onto the docks here. Nobody knew why — possibly the herring were running, possibly the marina offered shelter from predators and rough water. The boat owners were not thrilled; the sea lions had taken over slips people were paying for. The Marine Mammal Center was consulted, and the recommendation came back: leave them alone. They have as much right to be there as anyone.

So the marina gave up the docks. The boats moved out, the sea lions moved in, and a working marina quietly became one of the most popular free attractions in the city. The numbers swell in winter, when the herring come, and thin out in summer when a lot of the males head south to breed — so don't be surprised if the count looks different than you expected. On a big winter day there can be hundreds of them out there.

*[beat]*

There's something worth sitting with here. You're on a pier engineered down to the last souvenir to separate tourists from their money — the shops, the carousel, the fudge — and the thing everyone actually leans on the railing to watch is a crowd of animals who wandered in uninvited and refused to leave. The pier spent a fortune trying to be an attraction. The sea lions did it for free, by accident, by squatting.

Stay and watch them for a while. They'll knock each other off the docks, climb right back up, and start the argument over. It never gets old.
