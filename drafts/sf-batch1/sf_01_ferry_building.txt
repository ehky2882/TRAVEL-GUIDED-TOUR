For about forty years, you couldn't really see this building. A double-decker freeway ran right along the water in front of it, throwing the whole waterfront into shadow, and the city more or less forgot the Ferry Building was here.

Stand on the plaza at the foot of Market Street and look up at the clock tower. It's a little over two hundred and forty feet tall, and the design is borrowed: the architect modeled it on the Giralda, the medieval bell tower attached to the cathedral in Seville. When it opened in 1898, this was one of the busiest transit hubs on earth — second only to a station in London — because before the bridges, the only way across the bay was by boat, and everyone funneled through here. Fifty thousand people a day, two big ferry crossings, the front door to San Francisco.

Then the bridges came. The Bay Bridge in 1936, the Golden Gate in 1937, and almost overnight the ferries emptied out. The building faded. And in the 1950s the city did the thing cities did back then: it ran an elevated freeway straight across the foot of Market Street, sealing the building off from downtown behind a wall of concrete and traffic noise.

Here's the turn. In 1989, the Loma Prieta earthquake shook that freeway hard enough to crack it. It was condemned, and after a long fight, torn down. The earthquake did in a few seconds what years of complaints couldn't — it gave the waterfront back.

What you're standing in front of now is the second act. The hall behind those arches was restored in 2003 and turned into a food market: oyster bars, bread, cheese, coffee, and on market days the plaza fills with farm stalls. The clock still keeps time. The ferries, a few of them, still run.

*[beat]*

It's a strange thing to think about, standing here in the sun with the bay wide open in front of you — that this view, the obvious one, the one that looks like it's always been here, only came back because something fell down. The city turned its back on the water for half a century, and then an earthquake turned it around.
