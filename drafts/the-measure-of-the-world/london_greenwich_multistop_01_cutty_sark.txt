The Cutty Sark. She's up on stilts in a dry dock, lifted clear of the ground, so the first thing to do is walk underneath her — under the actual hull, which almost no one ever gets to see on a ship. Look up at the shape of it: long, lean, and sharp as a blade. That shape was the whole point.

She was built in 1869 as a tea clipper — one of the last and fastest of a breed designed to do a single thing: race the new season's tea from China to London quicker than anyone else, because the first cargo home fetched the best price. Speed was money. The Cutty Sark could run over three hundred miles in a day, which for a sailing ship was blistering.

Now, the gift — her name. Go round to the front and find the figurehead: a woman, bare-armed, reaching forward. She's Nannie, a witch from a Robert Burns poem, caught grabbing the tail of a fleeing horse. In the poem she's wearing only a short little shirt — a "cutty sark" in old Scots. So this hard-driving cargo ship is named, cheekily, after a witch's nightie. Sailors had a sense of humour.

Here's the quiet irony of her, though. She was one of the finest sailing ships ever built — and she was already obsolete the day she launched. The Suez Canal opened that same year, steamers could take the short cut east, and within a generation sail was finished for serious cargo. She's a masterpiece of a technology that was already dying. The most beautiful possible answer to a question the world had just stopped asking.

She caught fire during restoration in 2007, badly, and what you're looking at is the careful rebuilding that followed. From here, turn and walk along the river towards the grand domed buildings just ahead — the Old Royal Naval College.
