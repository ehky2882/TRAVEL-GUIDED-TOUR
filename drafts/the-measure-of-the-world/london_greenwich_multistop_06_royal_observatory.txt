The Royal Observatory. This is it — the top of the hill, the end of the walk, and the centre of the world's map. Stand in the courtyard, find the metal strip set into the ground with a queue of people photographing their feet, and you've found the Prime Meridian: zero degrees longitude. One foot either side, and you're standing in both halves of the planet at once.

Remember the problem from the bottom of the hill — ships getting fatally lost because they couldn't tell how far east or west they were. King Charles the Second built this observatory in 1675 to solve it: map the stars precisely enough, the thinking went, and sailors could find their longitude from the sky. In the end the cleaner answer was a clock. A clockmaker named John Harrison spent his life building timepieces accurate enough to keep perfect home-time on a pitching, salt-soaked ship — and his strange, beautiful machines are inside, still ticking. Solve the time, and you solve the position. Greenwich became the place the world set its clock by.

Look up at the building behind you and you'll see a red ball on a pole. At one o'clock every day, it drops — and it has, since 1833, so ships down on the river could set their chronometers by it at the exact instant. The world's first public time signal, and it still falls daily.

Here's the thing to stand here and feel, though. There's nothing under your feet. No natural line runs through this hill. In 1884 the nations of the world simply met, voted, and agreed to measure all time and all longitude from this one spot — an arbitrary line on an English hilltop that became the truth because everyone chose to believe it together. Every time zone on earth is counted from here. Greenwich Mean Time. The zero of the whole map.

[beat]

So that's the walk. You started at the river with a ship that got lost less often than the others, and you've climbed to the line the entire world agreed to measure itself from. Stand on it a moment, one foot in each hemisphere, and look back down the hill at everything that line paid for. Not a bad place to end.
