The Old Royal Naval College. Two huge stone blocks, each capped with a dome, facing each other across a long open lawn that runs down to the river. It's the grandest baroque set-piece in Britain, and the man behind it was Christopher Wren — the same architect who rebuilt St Paul's and half the City after the Great Fire.

But here's the thing it was built for, and it's not what you'd guess. This wasn't a palace or a parliament. It was a charity — a home for old, injured and worn-out sailors of the Royal Navy. The country that sent men to sea built them this to retire into. Pensioners shuffled around these royal courtyards in their three-cornered hats. Grandeur, for the discarded.

Step into the Painted Hall — it's just off the courtyard, and you should, even for five minutes. This was their dining room, and the ceiling and walls are covered, every inch, in a vast swirling scene that took the artist James Thornhill nineteen years to paint. People call it Britain's Sistine Chapel, and for once the comparison isn't silly: old sailors ate their dinners under one of the great painted ceilings of Europe. When Nelson was killed at Trafalgar — the man on the column you may have stood beneath in Trafalgar Square — his body was brought here, to lie in state under that ceiling.

Now the gift, and you can see it from the lawn. The two blocks don't join. There's a wide gap straight up the middle, and through it you can see a smaller white building beyond, with the hill rising behind. That gap is deliberate. Queen Mary insisted the river view from that white house must not be blocked, so Wren split his whole building in two and built the view a corridor. The grandest pile in Greenwich was made to bow to a sightline.

Walk up the lawn now, through the gap, towards that white house. That's where we're going.
