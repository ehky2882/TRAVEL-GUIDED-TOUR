The Queen's House. A plain white cube, calm and symmetrical, sitting quietly between the wings of the maritime museum. It looks ordinary to a modern eye — which is exactly why it's one of the most important buildings in the country, and you have to do a little time-travel to see why.

When this went up, around 1616, everything in England was still built the Tudor way — red brick, gables, towers, lovely but a bit higgledy. Then the architect Inigo Jones came back from Italy, having studied the clean, mathematical buildings of the Renaissance, and dropped this white classical box into the middle of red-brick England. It was startling — a piece of the future landed in a field. It's reckoned the first truly classical building in the country.

And if the name Inigo Jones rings a bell, it should: he's the man who built the Banqueting House on Whitehall, the white hall with the Rubens ceiling. These two buildings are the ones that brought Italy to Britain — and nearly every grand white-columned building you've ever seen in this country traces back to them.

The gift is inside, and it's free to enter. Find the Tulip Stairs — a spiral that curls up through the house with nothing beneath holding it, each step seeming to float on the one below. It was the first self-supporting spiral staircase in Britain, and it still looks like a magic trick.

It was built for queens — begun for one, finished for another — which is where the name comes from. Inside now hangs the famous Armada Portrait of Elizabeth the First, her hand resting on a globe: England, looking out at the sea, deciding the sea was hers.

Step across to the long building beside it — the National Maritime Museum.
