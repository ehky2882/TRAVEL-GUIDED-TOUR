Pull out your phone, open a map, and look at the numbers — your longitude. Zero degrees runs through here. Not through Paris, not through Rome, not through Washington. Through this hill in south-east London. The entire world measures east and west from the spot we're going to finish on today.

You're in grennitch, on the south bank of the temz, downriver from the centre — far enough out that it still feels like its own town. You've probably come in by boat, or on the little driverless train. Start down by the river, where a great sailing ship sits in dry dock; you can't miss the masts.

Here's the thread. For centuries, the deadliest problem in the world wasn't war or plague — it was getting lost at sea. A ship could find how far north or south it was easily enough, but east or west — its longitude — was guesswork, and the guessing killed people by the thousand. The answer, it turned out, was time: know precisely what o'clock it is back home, compare it to the sun where you are, and you can work out where you are on the globe. So a king built an observatory on this hill to crack the problem. They cracked it. Britain got the oceans. And as a side effect, the world ended up agreeing to measure both time and place from right here.

So this walk runs uphill, in every sense. We start at the water with the age of sail, move through the grand naval buildings the empire built with its sea-power, and climb through the park to the observatory at the top — and the line itself, where you can stand with one foot in each half of the planet.

It's an easy walk: flat along the river, then one good push up through the park. About a mile and a half, all in one piece. Start at the ship. That's the cutty sark.
