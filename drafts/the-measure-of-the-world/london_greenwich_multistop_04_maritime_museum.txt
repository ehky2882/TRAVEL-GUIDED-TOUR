The National Maritime Museum — the largest museum in the world devoted to the sea, and entry is free. You could spend hours; I'm going to point you at one object.

Everything Greenwich is about — the ships, the navigation, the empire built on sea-power — runs through this collection: charts, instruments, figureheads, the lot. But find the gallery with Nelson in it, and look for a small, plain naval coat in a glass case.

It's the coat Horatio Nelson was wearing at the Battle of Trafalgar, in 1805, when a French sniper's musket ball hit him. Look closely at the left shoulder and you'll see it — the actual bullet hole, the one that killed the most famous sailor in British history. We've already met Nelson twice today: on his column, if you came in from Trafalgar Square, and lying in state in the Painted Hall back down the hill. Here he is a third time, reduced to a single coat with a hole in it.

That's the gift of this whole museum, really. All the grandeur outside — the domes, the painted ceilings, the talk of empire and oceans — and then this: a piece of wool with a tear in the shoulder. The glory and the cost, two rooms apart.

When you've seen it, come back out and look up the hill. At the top, with a statue beside it and almost certainly a crowd, is the Royal Observatory. That's our climb, and our ending. Head into the park and start up.
