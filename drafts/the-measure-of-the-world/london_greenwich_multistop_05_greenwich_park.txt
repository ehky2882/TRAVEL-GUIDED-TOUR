Take your time on this hill — it's the only real climb of the day, and there's a bench or two if you want them. You're walking up through Greenwich Park, the oldest enclosed royal park in London, fenced off as a royal hunting ground nearly six hundred years ago. There were deer here for centuries.

Near the top you'll pass a statue of a soldier looking out over the view — that's General Wolfe, who won Quebec for Britain and is buried down in the town below. He's been given the best seat in Greenwich.

And here it is — the view. Stop at the top and turn back the way you came. Below you, the whole story of the walk is laid out at once: the two domes of the Naval College; the little white Queen's House framed dead-centre in the gap between them — that preserved sightline, seen now from the other end; the silver bend of the Thames; and rising straight behind it all, the glass towers of Canary Wharf. Four hundred years of the city in a single frame — a royal house, a naval palace, a working river, and the money that came after.

Get your breath. Then turn around. Behind you is the reason the whole world knows the name Greenwich — the Royal Observatory.
