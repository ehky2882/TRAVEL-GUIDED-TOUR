There's a wooden door on the Spui that most people walk straight past. On the other side of it, the city goes quiet.

Find the arched doorway near the corner of the square — during the day it's open. Step through the dim vaulted passage and you come out into an enclosed courtyard: a green lawn, two churches, and a ring of tall gabled houses facing inward, with the noise of the city switched off behind you. Notice, too, that you've stepped down. The Begijnhof sits about a metre below the streets outside, because this is the medieval ground level — the rest of Amsterdam rose around it, and the courtyard simply stayed put.

One thing before anything else: people live here, and the rule of the place is quiet. Speak softly, keep to the paths, and don't photograph the residents or their windows. The courtyard stays open to visitors because visitors hold up their end of that bargain — which, as you're about to hear, is a very old arrangement.

The Begijnhof was founded in the fourteenth century for the Beguines: devout Catholic women who lived, dressed and worked like nuns but took no permanent vows, teaching children and nursing the sick. When the city turned Protestant in 1578 and banned Catholic worship, the Beguines were allowed to stay — provided they kept their faith invisible. Their chapel still hides behind ordinary house-fronts across from the lawn; from outside you'd never know it was there. Tolerance, Amsterdam-style: you may keep your world, if you keep it quiet.

The women held up their end with some style. One of them, Cornelia Arens, refused on her deathbed in 1654 to be buried in the chapel the Protestants had taken over — so she was buried in the gutter of the courtyard instead, where her grave still lies, usually with flowers on it.

Before you leave, find house number thirty-four, the one with the black wooden front. Timber houses were banned in the city after the great fires of the early 1500s, and this is one of only two wooden house-fronts left in the whole centre. For decades it was celebrated as the oldest house in Amsterdam — until researchers dated the wood to 1528, and an even older house turned up hiding behind a dull nineteenth-century front near the red-light district. The courtyard doesn't seem to mind. It was never in this for the records.

The houses are still let only to single women — the last living thread of the Beguines' rule. Seven hundred years of keeping to the terms, in the quietest square in the city.
