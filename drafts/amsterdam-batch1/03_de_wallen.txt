The oldest neighbourhood in Amsterdam is also the most argued about — and has been for roughly seven hundred years.

You're in De Wallen, the medieval heart of the city: two long canals, Oudezijds Voorburgwal and Oudezijds Achterburgwal, laced together by narrow lanes, with the tower of the Oude Kerk rising over everything. After dark, some of the windows glow red.

First, the rules — because this neighbourhood has real ones. Never photograph or film the sex workers or their windows; that is the one absolute here, and it's taken seriously. Keep moving in the narrow lanes near the windows rather than gathering to stare. And keep your voice down, because behind and above all of this, ordinary people live here. This is a working neighbourhood, not a theme park — drinking on the street and smoking cannabis outdoors are both banned in the old centre, and the signs mean it.

Now the history, because the red lights are only the newest chapter. This was the port quarter of a port city. Sailors came ashore here for centuries with months of pay and months of appetite, and the streets adapted — taverns, lodging houses, and the world's oldest trade. What makes Amsterdam different isn't that this happened; it happened in every port on earth. It's what the city decided to do about it.

The Dutch answer, refined over centuries and made law in 2000, was regulation. Sex work here is legal, licensed and taxed. The women in the windows are independent entrepreneurs who rent their workspaces by the shift, with rights, representation and the police on their side. You can argue — plenty do, including the Dutch themselves — about whether this is enlightened or naive, protection or just another market. The city has been holding that argument in public for decades, adjusting the rules as it goes.

[beat]

And that, really, is the thing to understand about the view in front of you. Amsterdam never decided sin was good. It decided that pretending sin away was worse — that what you can see, you can regulate, and what you drive underground, you can't. Tolerance here has never been a shrug. It's a ledger.

Look up as you walk. Above the red glow are some of the oldest house-fronts in the city — seven centuries of Amsterdam, still keeping an eye on the street.
