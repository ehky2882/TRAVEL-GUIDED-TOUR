A hundred and fifty years ago, everything you can see from this lawn was pasture at the edge of the city. Then, in the space of about twenty years, Amsterdam built its entire cultural life on it.

You should be standing on Museumplein, the long green square behind the Rijksmuseum. Turn around slowly: the Rijksmuseum's towers at one end, the Van Gogh Museum and the white flank of the Stedelijk along one side, and at the far end a classical facade with a golden lyre on its roofline — the Concertgebouw, the concert building.

Look at the dates and something jumps out. The Rijksmuseum opened in 1885, the Concertgebouw in 1888, the Stedelijk in 1895 — a national gallery, a concert hall and a modern art museum, raised in a single decade, and almost none of it by royal decree. These were committees: citizens selling shares, writing furious letters to newspapers about the state of the city's music, buying up cow fields. The Dutch will tell you their golden age was the seventeenth century, but stand here and you can make the case for a second one — quieter, later, and built by subscription.

The Concertgebouw is the best story on the square. Six citizens founded it in 1881 and hired Dolf van Gendt, an architect who had never designed a concert hall and whose own family reportedly considered him unmusical. He studied the celebrated Gewandhaus in Leipzig, borrowed its shape, then had to shorten and widen his hall because the plot was too small. Out of that improvisation came what is now ranked, by the people who measure such things, among the two or three finest-sounding concert halls in the world. Nobody entirely understands why — the hall's own director has cheerfully called it luck.

Which explains the detail worth keeping: inside the main hall, some of the dust is deliberately left where it lies, because the sound is perfect and nobody dares change a thing — not even by cleaning.

[beat]

The Leipzig hall it was modelled on was destroyed in the Second World War. The copy outlived the original, and became the masterpiece. Find a spot on the grass between the temples and enjoy the strange arithmetic of that — a pasture that ended up holding more culture per square metre than almost anywhere in Europe.
