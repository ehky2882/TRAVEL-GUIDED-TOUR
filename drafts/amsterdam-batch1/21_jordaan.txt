The prettiest neighbourhood in Amsterdam was built as cheap housing, and everything you'll love about it started out as a way of getting by.

You're in the Jordaan — the tight weave of narrow streets and small canals west of the Prinsengracht, in the shadow of the Westertoren. There's no single monument here; the neighbourhood is the monument. Streets named after flowers and trees, small brown cafés, leaning house-fronts, flowerpots on every stoop. Even the name is a bit of folklore: possibly from the French jardin, garden — a nice story, told for centuries, proven never.

Here's what this place actually was. When the great canal ring was laid out in the sixteen-hundreds for the merchant class, the Jordaan was built alongside it for everyone else — the craftsmen, dyers, brewers, tanners and refugees who did the actual work. It was crowded, damp and poor, and it stayed that way for three hundred years. What you're walking through is working-class survival, weathered into charm.

You can read that history in two details. First, look for the gevelstenen — the carved and painted stone tablets set into house-fronts: a fish, a hand, a ship, a pair of scissors. Before house numbers existed, these were addresses — the sign of the fishmonger, the glover, the tailor. There are hundreds surviving in these streets, a picture-book directory of who did what, where. Second, the hofjes: behind unmarked doors sit small almshouse courtyards, built by wealthy patrons to house the elderly poor — pockets of astonishing quiet a metre from the street. Where a door stands open, you may usually look in; do it softly, because people live there still.

By the twentieth century the Jordaan was so run-down the city planned to demolish large parts of it. The residents fought back, the plans died, and from the 1970s the quarter was restored instead — which is why it exists for you to walk through at all.

One tradition survived the whole ride: singing. The Jordaan produced generations of sentimental crooners, and in a handful of cafés here the singalong nights carry on — a whole bar, arm in arm, belting out old tearjerkers about love, mothers and the Westertoren. Poverty left; the songs stayed.

So wander without a plan. The Jordaan doesn't have sights. It has streets that people refused to give up — which, in the end, is the better thing to see.
