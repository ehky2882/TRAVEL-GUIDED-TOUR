Here's an honest question to carry down this row of stalls: when does a market stop being a market and start being a memory of one?

You're on the Singel, the innermost canal, on the stretch between Muntplein and Koningsplein. The row of glasshouse shops on the canal side is the Bloemenmarkt, the famous floating flower market — and both halves of that name deserve a closer look.

Floating, first. The stalls sit on barges, and that part is real history: the market began in 1862, when growers sailed their stock up the water from the fields and sold it straight off the deck. What you see is the direct descendant of those boats — though the barges stopped going anywhere long ago, and now rest on piles, moored into permanence. Walk to a gap between the stalls and look at the water side; from the canal, the market still reads as a line of moored vessels.

Flowers, second — and here's where honesty is owed. Most of what's sold today is the idea of Dutch flowers rather than the fresh article: bulbs by the sackful, tulips in wood, seeds in printed packets, and the full national gift shop alongside. A few stalls still do cut flowers, and locals do still slip in for a bunch. If you're tempted by bulbs, look for the export-certified packets and accept that results vary — the growers' own garden centres, out of town, keep the higher reputation.

And yet. This little strip is the last visible trace, in the city centre, of the trade that once drove the country half mad — the seventeenth-century tulip fever, when fortunes were staked on bulbs, a story so good it has grown in the telling. The Netherlands is still the world's flower trader; the real business long since moved to vast auction halls near the airport. What stayed behind is this: a postcard of a market that used to be a market.

[beat]

Postcards have their uses, though. Come in tulip season, early, before the crowds, when the buckets are full and the light is low across the water — and the postcard, briefly, is the real thing again.
