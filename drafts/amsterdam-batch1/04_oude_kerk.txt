The oldest building in Amsterdam stands in the middle of the red-light district, and neither of them is embarrassed about it.

You should be facing the Oude Kerk — the Old Church — a great Gothic pile of brick and stone with a wooden-capped tower, on its own small square where the lanes of De Wallen open out. Red-lit windows face the church walls directly. This arrangement is roughly seven hundred years old and entirely deliberate: the church served the port, and the port has always been all of human life at once.

Step inside if you can, because the interior is the point. The church was consecrated in 1306, and the first thing that strikes anyone entering is the emptiness — high bare walls, a forest of columns, and light. It wasn't always like this. In 1566 a wave of Protestant iconoclasm swept the Low Countries and stripped out the altars, statues and paintings; what remains is the architecture itself, scrubbed to the bone. Above you spreads a vast wooden vault, one of the largest medieval wooden ceilings in Europe, built by the same kind of craftsmen who built the city's ships — which is why standing under it feels a little like standing inside an upturned hull.

Then look down, because you're walking on the dead. The floor is made almost entirely of gravestones — thousands of Amsterdammers lie buried beneath this church, rich and poor together. One stone matters more than the rest: Saskia van Uylenburgh, Rembrandt's wife, buried here in 1642, the same year he finished the Night Watch. Twenty years later, broke, he had to sell her grave. It tells you most of what you need to know about how his fortunes went.

[beat]

Today the Oude Kerk calls itself the oldest building and the youngest art institution in the city — it commissions contemporary artists to fill that great emptiness with new work, so you may well find Amsterdam's oldest space holding its newest ideas. Which is a very Amsterdam solution: don't freeze the past, put it to work.

Back outside on the square, cast in bronze among the cobbles, are a few quiet tributes to the neighbourhood's other working history. The church has seen all of it — and it's still open, every day, right in the middle of everything.
