This street bends because the sea told it to.

You're on the zay dike, the narrow street that curves from the top of the old centre, near the great domed church by the station, down towards the nyoo markt. The name means sea dike, and it's meant literally: the street runs along the top of the mediaeval wall of earth that kept the water out of the young city. Amsterdam exists because of a dam and a handful of dikes, and this is one of them, still holding its curve seven centuries on, with a street laid over it like a carpet.

Start at the top end, at zay dike one, the black-and-white timber building on the corner. This is in ut ahp yun, a café in a wooden house from the mid-fifteen hundreds — one of only two wooden facades left in the whole city centre. You may have already seen the other, tucked inside the buh gine hof. The name means In the Monkeys, and the story — told for centuries, so take it as a story — is that sailors short of cash paid for their lodging with monkeys brought back from the East, until the house was full of them. The Dutch still say someone in a fix has been lodged at the monkey's.

The sailors are the key to this street. The zay dike ran between the harbour and the town, which made it the first street in Amsterdam that most of the world ever saw — the city's arrival hall. Every wave of newcomers washed up here first. In the early nineteen hundreds it was seamen from southern China, stokers on the steamship lines, who settled at the harbour's edge and founded one of the oldest Chinatowns in continental Europe. It's still here, and thriving — look for the huh hwah Temple partway down, a full Chinese Buddhist temple in glazed tile and gold, one of the largest in Europe, sitting between the brown cafés as if it had always been there.

The street has had rough decades — the late twentieth century was unkind to it — and it was pulled back largely by its own residents, the Chinese community prominent among them. Walk it end to end; it takes ten minutes. Count the languages you hear on the way. On this particular street, that's not new. That's the oldest sound it has.
