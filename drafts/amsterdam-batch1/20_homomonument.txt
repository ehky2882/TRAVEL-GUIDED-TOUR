You could walk straight across this monument without ever knowing it was there. That is not a failure of the design — it is the design.

You should be standing on Westermarkt, the small square at the foot of the Westerkerk tower, where the Keizersgracht runs past. Look down. Three triangles of pink granite are set into the ground around you — one lying flat in the ground, one raised like a low podium, and one stepping down to the water of the canal — joined by thin granite lines that run through the square to form one enormous triangle, thirty-six metres to a side.

This is the Homomonument, unveiled in 1987 — the first monument anywhere in the world to the gay men and women persecuted for who they were. The pink triangle is not decoration: it was the badge gay men were forced to wear in the Nazi camps, and the generation that built this place took that mark and set it in granite, in the middle of the city, where it could not be hidden again.

Each triangle points somewhere. The one at street level points toward the Anne Frank House, a few hundred metres up the canal, and carries a line by Jacob Israël de Haan — a Jewish poet, and a gay man, writing a century ago of a boundless longing for friendship. The one at the water points to the National Monument on Dam Square. The raised one points toward the old headquarters of the COC, one of the oldest gay-rights organisations in the world. Past, present, future.

Amsterdam tells a story about tolerance, and by now you have heard the conditions attached — the Begijnhof's quiet rules, the church hidden in an attic. This monument marks the people the old arrangement never protected at all — and it does it not with a statue on a plinth but with the ground itself, woven into the square so completely that its designer, Karin Daan, said her favourite thing was watching taxi drivers park in the middle of it, unaware.

[beat]

People have brought flowers here nearly every day since it opened. Take the steps down toward the water, and give the place a moment. It was built to be part of the city — and it is asking you, quietly, to notice.
