If you want to know what Amsterdam actually eats, it's on this street.

You're in De Pijp, the tight-packed quarter south of the canal ring, standing on or near the Albert Cuypstraat — where, six days a week, the Albert Cuyp Market runs the length of the street: a corridor of stalls, awnings and shouted prices, one of the largest street markets in Europe. Fish, cheese, fabric, flowers, phone cases, olives, herring — and somewhere along the row, the smell of fresh stroopwafels being pressed, warm, which is the single best two-euro decision available in this city.

First, the neighbourhood. De Pijp was thrown up fast in the late eighteen hundreds — speculative housing for the workers of a booming city, long narrow streets of small apartments, cheek by jowl. Even the name is disputed folklore: "the Pipe," possibly for the long thin streets like pipe stems — a story, told with confidence, proven never. It was crowded and poor, and like the Jordaan before it, that turned out to be the making of it: students, artists and, above all, newcomers moved in, because it was where you could afford to arrive.

That's the thing to read in the market. The stalls follow the neighbourhood's arrivals like tree rings — Dutch herring and cheese, then Surinamese roti, Turkish flatbread, Moroccan olives, Indonesian spices, Vietnamese rolls — each wave of migration adding a row to the menu and staying. The market has been trading here since 1905, named for a Golden Age painter, and it has never once been quaint. It's not performing Dutchness for visitors; it's feeding a neighbourhood, and you're welcome to join.

[beat]

And that's the honest correction De Pijp offers to the postcard. The Amsterdam of the canal ring is the city's seventeenth century, preserved. This is the city's present tense — multicultural, noisy, a bit scruffy at the edges, eating well. Both are real. Only one of them will sell you dinner.

So do this properly: walk the market end to end once without buying, then go back for what you couldn't stop thinking about. Have the herring or have the roti — ideally both — and eat standing up, out of paper, like everyone around you. That's the tour.
