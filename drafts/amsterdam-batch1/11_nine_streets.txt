Every grand plan needs seams, and these are Amsterdam's.

You're in the Nine Streets — De Negen Straatjes — the little grid of cross-streets that stitches the canal ring together, three rows of three, running crosswise over the Herengracht, Keizersgracht and Prinsengracht. Each street is barely a hundred metres long. Pick any of them — Hartenstraat, Wolvenstraat, Berenstraat — and you're in the right place: small shopfronts, cafés, people browsing, and a bridge over water at each end.

Here's the thing to understand about where you're standing. The seventeenth-century canal plan was drawn for merchants' mansions — long, grand, water-facing. But a city of facades isn't a city; somebody has to sell bread, mend shoes, pour coffee. The cross-streets were the plan's working parts from the very start, the connective tissue where the shops went — and four hundred years on, they still are. The mansions became offices and museums; the little streets never stopped trading.

Now read the street names, because they're a fossil of the first trade here. Berenstraat — Bear Street. Wolvenstraat — Wolf Street. Reestraat — Deer Street. Huidenstraat — Hide Street. This corner of the new district was given over to the leather and fur trades: tanners, skinners, pelt-dealers, the smelly essential work that the gentlemen on the Gentlemen's Canal wanted close by but not out front. The wolves and bears on the street signs were once hanging in the windows.

[beat]

And that's the quietly satisfying thing about what's here now. The boutiques, the vintage dealers, the cheese shops, the little galleries — it's the same economy the streets were built for: small premises, skilled trades, high footfall, everything at walking pace. The product changed; the pattern held. Cities keep their habits longer than they keep their buildings.

So don't try to see the Nine Streets efficiently — they don't reward a checklist. Pick a street, follow it over a bridge, turn where something looks good. Nine short streets, three canals, and no wrong way through. The grand plan is all around you; this is the part of it built for wandering.
