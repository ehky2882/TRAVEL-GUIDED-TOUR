Somewhere behind you, a bell rings — and it's probably a bicycle, riding straight through the middle of the national museum.

You should be on Museumplein, facing the Rijksmuseum: a vast brick palace of towers, gables and stained glass, looking less like a museum than a cathedral crossed with a railway station. That's no accident — Pierre Cuypers, who finished it in 1885, designed both kinds of building, and his Centraal Station across town is this building's twin. The story goes that the Protestant king took one look at all the neo-Gothic flourish and refused to set foot in "that monastery." The architect got the last word: the monastery became the front parlour of the nation.

Now the detail you can see from right here: the passage running through the centre of the building, at street level, is a public bicycle path. Cyclists have ridden straight through the middle of the Rijksmuseum for over a century, and when a renovation threatened the route, the cyclists won. Stand near the passage a moment and listen — bells, tyres and echoes under the painted vaults. No other national museum on earth doubles as a commuter shortcut.

Inside, the building funnels everyone to a single room: the Gallery of Honour, ending at Rembrandt's Night Watch of 1642 — the militia portrait that refused to hold still, a group photo turned into a thriller, mid-stride, mid-shout, dog barking. Around it hang Vermeer, Hals and the rest of the seventeenth century's astonishing output. Of the museum's roughly one million objects, only around eight thousand are out at any time; what you see is the distilled top shelf.

[beat]

And this is what makes the place quietly remarkable now: the museum has stopped pretending the Golden Age was only golden. Its own researchers have traced the slavery and colonial trade behind many of the fortunes on these walls, and the labels increasingly say so — the silver, the portraits, the sugar money named for what they are. The national museum, auditing the nation. It makes the splendour more honest, not smaller.

Give it a morning if you can. And when you leave, use the bike passage on foot — walking through the building you've just seen the inside of is the best free exhibit in the city.
