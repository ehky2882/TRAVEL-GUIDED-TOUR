The little castle in the middle of this square has had more careers than any building in Amsterdam.

You're on the Nieuwmarkt, the broad market square at the eastern edge of the old centre, and the turreted brick fortress in the middle of it is De Waag. Walk around it once before anything else — it reads differently from every side, which turns out to be the whole story.

It was born in 1488 as Saint Anthony's Gate, a fortified gateway in the mediaeval city wall — the Zeedijk, the old sea dike, runs straight into this square, and this was where you entered the city at the end of it. Then Amsterdam grew right past its own wall, and the gate lost its job within a century. In 1617 the city rehired it as a weigh house — de waag — where goods coming off the ships were weighed and taxed. The guilds moved into the towers above: blacksmiths, masons, painters, surgeons, each with its own entrance. Circle the building and you can still find their separate doorways, guild emblems carved over each one. Above the surgeons' door, two words in stone: Theatrum Anatomicum.

That door is the reason this building hangs in one of the world's great paintings. The surgeons' guild held its anatomy lessons here — one public dissection a year, the body of an executed criminal, tickets sold to anyone curious. In 1632 the guild commissioned a group portrait of exactly such a lesson from a twenty-five-year-old newcomer just arrived from Leiden. The painting was The Anatomy Lesson of Doctor Tulp, the newcomer was Rembrandt, and it made his name overnight. It hung in this building for generations. He keeps turning up, this painter — a grave in the Oude Kerk, a mystery in the Westerkerk, and his first triumph here, above a weigh house.

Gate, weigh house, guildhall, anatomy theatre, fire station, museum — and today, a café-restaurant. This is the oldest non-religious building in the city, and it has never once been allowed to retire. That's the Amsterdam way with old buildings: no embalming. Keep them working.

The square in front of you runs a market most days, as it has for four centuries. Buy something small, find a spot on a terrace, and consider that you're having coffee in the shadow of a fifteenth-century city gate that simply refused to become a ruin.
