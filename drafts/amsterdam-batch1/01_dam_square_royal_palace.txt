Every city has a founding myth. Amsterdam's is a piece of civil engineering.

You should be standing in Dam Square, the wide plaza at the heart of the city, with the grey stone bulk of the Royal Palace along its western side and a tall white pillar — the National Monument — facing it across the square. Trams cross between them. So does most of Amsterdam, eventually.

Start with the ground under you. Around 1270, the river Amstel was dammed roughly where you're standing — a dam on the Amstel, Amstelledamme, Amsterdam. The name of the city is a description of this square. Everything else — the canals, the gables, the harbour, the trading empire — grew outward from a wall of mud and timber holding back a river.

Now the palace. It wasn't built as one. When it went up between 1648 and 1665, this was the town hall — the town hall of a merchant republic at the very peak of its wealth, and it shows. Jacob van Campen's building was the largest public building in Europe when it was finished, and it stands on thirteen thousand six hundred and fifty-nine wooden piles driven into the wet ground — a number Amsterdam schoolchildren still learn by heart. Inside, the Citizens' Hall has maps of the eastern and western hemispheres inlaid into the marble floor, so citizens could literally walk across the world their ships were trading in. And up on the roof, holding the sky on his shoulders, stands a bronze Atlas.

[beat]

Here's the twist. A building this grand was meant as a boast that Amsterdam needed no king — a city of merchants governing itself, thank you very much. Then in 1806 Napoleon's brother Louis was installed as King of Holland, looked around for a residence, and took the finest building in the country. The town hall has been a royal palace ever since. It still earns its keep: the King uses it for state occasions, and on most other days it's open to visitors, so you can walk that marble world map yourself.

The white pillar opposite is the National Monument, raised in 1956 to the country's dead of the Second World War; the King lays a wreath there every May.

A dam, a town hall, a palace, a memorial. One square carrying most of the city's story — and the whole of its name.
