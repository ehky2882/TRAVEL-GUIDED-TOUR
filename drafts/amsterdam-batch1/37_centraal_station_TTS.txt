If you've stood in front of the rikes mew zay um, this building should give you day zhah voo — same red brick, same gothic towers, same air of a cathedral that never quite found its congregation. Same architect, too. We'll get there.

First, find your spot. Stand on the station square, the stah shons pline, facing the long red-brick front of the station, with the dam rak and the city centre behind you. You want the whole facade in view: a central block like a palace gatehouse, and a tower at each end, each with a large dial.

Start with those dials. The one on the right, as you face the building, is a clock. The one on the left is not — it's a wind vane dressed up as a clock, its single hand tracking the wind across a compass rose. A weather instrument on the front door of a railway station: the reflex of a city that lived by sailing weather for centuries, kept on out of something like muscle memory.

Now the day zhah voo. Pierre kow pers designed this station and the rikes mew zay um both — two red-brick cathedrals holding the old city between them, one for art at the southern edge, one for arrivals at the northern. Amsterdam's Protestants thought both looked suspiciously Catholic — kow pers was — and nicknamed the station the French Convent and the museum the Bishop's Castle. The names didn't stick. The buildings did.

Here's the part you can't see. There was no land here. The station stands on three artificial islands raised out in the eye — the broad water behind the station — on eight thousand six hundred and eighty-seven wooden piles driven into the harbour floor. The city that began with a dam in a river built its front door on ground it invented. But there was a price: the station sealed off the old open harbour, and a city that had faced the sea for five centuries suddenly faced a railway station instead. Amsterdam turned its back on the water here, at the exact spot where it had always looked out.

The water didn't go anywhere, though. Walk through the station to the far side and the eye is still there, wide and working, with flat blue-and-white ferries crossing it every few minutes — free, no ticket, no barrier. Ride one over and back if you have twenty minutes to spare. It's the closest thing the city offers to time travel: Amsterdam seen from the water, the way everyone arrived for five hundred years.
