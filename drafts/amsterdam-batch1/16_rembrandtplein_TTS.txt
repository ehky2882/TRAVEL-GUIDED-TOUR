The man on the pedestal in the middle of this square died broke, a fifteen-minute walk from here, and was buried in a rented grave nobody can find. Then the city put him on its first pedestal. Timing is everything, even for genius — especially for genius.

You're on rem brant pline, the leafy square ringed by grand cafés, hotels and neon, a few streets east of the flower market. The statue in the centre, the gentleman in the painter's cloak gazing out over the terraces, is rem brant — cast in iron in eighteen fifty-two, and the oldest surviving statue in the city. Let that register: when Amsterdam finally decided to put somebody on a public pedestal, it chose neither a king nor an admiral. It chose a painter — one it had let die bankrupt two centuries earlier.

The square under him has had its own careers. Like so many open spaces on the old ring, it began at the city's edge: a gate stood near here, and where the wall met the countryside, the farmers gathered. For two centuries this was the boh ter markt — the butter market — where dairy came in from the villages and, every autumn, a fair rolled in with dance bands and circus tents. In eighteen seventy-six, with the painter's statue moved to the centre, the butter market got a new name and never looked back. The fair, in a sense, never left either: the square slid from farmers' stalls into cafés, music halls and nightlife, and it has been the city's good-time square ever since. Around you now are some of the grandest café interiors in Amsterdam — pick one with a terrace and you're participating in the square's second century of the same idea.

So the pivot is the pairing. Below: butter, beer, dance bands — the perishable pleasures. Above, on the pedestal: the man whose work outlasted everything, watching over a square that has always specialised in the moment.

He keeps turning up, this painter — a wife's grave in the ow duh kairk, his own unfound somewhere in the ves ter kairk, his first triumph above a weigh house. Here he gets the ending the others withheld: the whole square, wearing his name in lights.
