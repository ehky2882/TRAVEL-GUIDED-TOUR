The most romantic view in Amsterdam was drawn with a ruler.

Stand on the hair en grahkt where it bends, roughly between lide suh straht and vye zel straht — the stretch called the Golden Bend. Water in front of you, a curve of tall brick and sandstone houses on either side, trees leaning over the canal, bicycles going past behind you. It looks like something that simply grew here over centuries. It didn't.

In sixteen thirteen the city, bursting with money and migrants, decided to expand — and instead of letting the growth sprawl, it drew a plan: three broad canals in concentric arcs around the medieval centre, dug in phases over roughly fifty years, with rules governing plot sizes, building heights and materials. hair en grahkt, the Gentlemen's Canal, for the richest; kye zers grahkt, the Emperor's; prin sen grahkt, the Prince's, with warehouses and working buildings further out. It was, in effect, the world's first planned boom suburb — a seventeenth-century property development so good that four hundred years later it's a World Heritage Site, and people cross oceans to walk through the diagram.

Now learn to read it, because the houses are a ledger written in brick. Look at the tops — the gables. A step gable, like a little staircase, usually means the oldest houses; the neck and bell shapes came later; a plain straight cornice, later still. You can date a canal house from across the water. Near the top of almost every one, a wooden beam with a hook juts out: the hoisting hook, for winching goods — and to this day furniture, since the staircases inside are famously narrow and steep — up the outside of the house. Many facades lean forward on purpose, so the load wouldn't scrape the front on its way up. And here on the Golden Bend, the plots are double width, because this is where the regents and bankers built. You'll hear that houses were narrow because tax was charged on frontage — a good story, and probably only partly true; scarce, expensive canal-front land did most of the work.

That's the quiet genius of this place. Other cities of that era showed off with palaces and cathedrals. Amsterdam's monument is a plan — merchants' houses, in rows, on water, built to make money and beauty at the same time and refusing to choose between them.

If you want one last trick, walk to where ruh goo leers grahkt crosses the hair en grahkt and look down the smaller canal: seven bridges, lined up in a row, arch after arch. The diagram, showing off just a little.
