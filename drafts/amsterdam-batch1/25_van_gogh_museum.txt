The most visited painter in this city sold almost nothing while he lived. The building in front of you is what happened to the leftovers.

You're on Museumplein, facing the Van Gogh Museum — the modern glass-and-brick complex a short walk from the Rijksmuseum. One practical note before anything: entry is by timed online ticket, and in high season the good slots go early, so book ahead. It is the most popular single-artist museum in the world, which, given how the artist's life went, is its own kind of story.

Here's that story, briefly. Vincent van Gogh painted for only about ten years, mostly in poverty, supported the whole way by his brother Theo, an art dealer who believed when nobody else did. When Vincent died in 1890, virtually unknown, hundreds of paintings and drawings simply piled up — unsold, unwanted. Theo died just six months later. And that should have been the end of it, except for Theo's widow, Jo van Gogh-Bonger, who inherited the stacks of canvases and a mountain of letters and decided, against all sensible advice, to make the world pay attention. She organised exhibitions, published the letters, sold strategically, kept the core together. The reputation you take for granted — she built it.

The family held on to the rest: over two hundred paintings, five hundred drawings, and more than nine hundred letters. In 1962 Vincent's nephew handed the entire collection to a foundation, the state built this museum for it, and the leftovers nobody wanted became the treasure people cross the world to see. Inside are the Sunflowers, the Potato Eaters, Almond Blossom, the bedroom at Arles — and the letters, which may move you more than the paintings, because in them you hear him thinking.

[beat]

That's the reframe worth carrying inside. This isn't a museum about a mad genius — that myth flattens him. The letters show a deliberate, well-read, ferociously hard-working artist who taught himself by sheer volume, knew exactly what he was attempting, and kept going without an audience. The colour on these walls wasn't a symptom. It was a decision, made daily, by someone nobody was watching.

Go and see what a decade of unwitnessed work looks like. Then be glad somebody's sister-in-law refused to let it disappear.
