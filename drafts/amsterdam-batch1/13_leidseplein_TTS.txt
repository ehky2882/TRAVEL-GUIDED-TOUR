Every city has a spot where it lets its hair down. Amsterdam's has been doing the job for three and a half centuries.

You're on lide suh pline, the busy square at the south-western corner of the canal ring, where the trams sing around the curve and the terraces run shoulder to shoulder. Take the centre of the square and turn slowly, because the buildings around you are the story.

First, why there's a square here at all. This was the edge of the city — the lide suh poort, the gate where the road to lye den left town, stood right here. Farmers and travellers parked their wagons outside it, the way you'd park a car at the city limits today, and where people wait, entertainment follows. The gate is long gone, the wall with it, but the habit stayed. Amsterdam has a pattern you can start collecting from this square: wherever a city gate once stood, you'll now find an open square with a crowd on it. The wall is gone, but you can still walk its ghost.

Now the buildings. The grand one with the arched loggia is the municipal theatre, in its red-brick pomp — the city's stage for well over a century. Across the way, the turreted building with the clock is the American Hotel, whose café behind those windows is an art noo voh period piece so complete it's protected as a monument; step in for a coffee and the twentieth century hasn't started yet. And just off the square, a former church called pah rah dee so became one of Europe's storied pop venues — a congregation traded for concerts, which by now you'll recognise as a very Amsterdam career move.

That's the pivot, really. From travelling players at the wagon-park to opera, from opera to rock in a church, the square has never once changed its job — only its costume. The city's threshold became the city's stage, and stayed it.

Come back after dark if you can, when the terraces glow and the street performers claim the centre. Daylight shows you the architecture. Evening shows you the point.
