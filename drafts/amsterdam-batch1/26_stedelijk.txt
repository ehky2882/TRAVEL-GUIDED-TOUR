One museum on this square is nicknamed for a bathtub — and the odd thing is how much the name explains.

You should be standing on Museumplein, the long lawn behind the Rijksmuseum, facing the Stedelijk Museum. You cannot miss it: a nineteenth-century brick building with an enormous, smooth, brilliant-white form wrapped around its flank, seamless as enamel, floating over a glass entrance hall. Amsterdammers took one look at it and called it the bathtub, and the name stuck so completely that the museum uses it too.

Start with the brick. The original building opened in 1895, founded not by a king or a ministry but by a group of private citizens who wanted their city to have a home for new art — and it grew into one of the great modern collections in Europe: Mondrian, Chagall, Matisse, Warhol, and one of the largest holdings of Kazimir Malevich's work outside Russia, alongside a design collection that treats chairs and posters as seriously as paintings.

Now the detail that ties the whole place together. In 1938, a curator named Willem Sandberg did something genuinely radical: he painted the museum's ornate interiors white. Plain, empty, gallery white. It sounds like nothing — but this was one of the first white-cube galleries anywhere in the world, the idea that art needs silence around it, and nearly every modern gallery you have ever walked through is quietly copying this building.

So look again at the bathtub, added in 2012. It is not white by accident — its architects said the form was a tribute to Sandberg, the museum wearing its own big idea on the outside. And the new wing turned the entrance around to face the square, so that a museum which once kept its back to the lawn now opens straight onto it.

[beat]

Inside, it is usually the least crowded of the three big museums on this square, which is its own kind of gift. But even if you never go in, you now know the joke hiding in plain sight: a building famous for its white walls, wrapped in the whitest wall in Amsterdam.
