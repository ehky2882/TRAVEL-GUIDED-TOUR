Two buildings face each other across this street. Between them runs one of the hardest stories this city has to tell — and one of its bravest.

You should be on plan tah zhuh mid den lahn, a wide, leafy avenue. On one side stands a pale nineteenth-century facade with a colonnaded front: the hol lant suh skow burg. Directly opposite, a sober brick building that was once a teachers' college — now the National Holocaust Museum.

Start with the theatre. From the eighteen nineties this was a popular playhouse, a place this neighbourhood came for comedies and operettas. In nineteen forty-two the occupying authorities turned it into something else: an assembly point, where the city's Jews were held before deportation. Families waited inside, sometimes for days, under guard, before being taken away. Today the building is kept as a memorial — a quiet courtyard, an eternal flame, and a wall carrying family names. It is free to enter, and it asks nothing of you except quiet.

Now the other side of the street. Next to the theatre's holding annex was a crèche, where the youngest children were kept — and beside that, the teachers' college. The people who worked there, together with students and members of the resistance, began smuggling children out: carried quietly through a side corridor, passed over the garden hedge, taken a few at a time to hiding places across the country. Some six hundred children were saved this way. The corridor they left through still exists, inside the museum that opened in the college building in twenty twenty-four — the first museum to tell the full story of the persecution of the Jews of the Netherlands.

The width of this street is a few metres. It held the worst that people did, and, at exactly the same time, the best. There is no way to stand here comfortably, and you are not meant to. But stand here anyway, for a moment, between the two buildings — because holding both sides of this street in mind, together, is the whole reason to come here at all.
