The best boat trip in Amsterdam is free, runs all night, and most visitors never find it.

You should be on the north bank of the IJ, in front of EYE — the sharp-angled white building that looks, depending on where you stand, like a ship about to launch or the eye it is named for. Beside it rises the A'DAM Tower, and across the water sits the long roofline of Centraal Station. If you're still on the other side, the ferries leave from the quay directly behind the station — the one marked Buiksloterweg takes a few minutes — and you can simply walk on.

Start with the crossing itself, because it is the point. Amsterdam never built a bridge over the IJ here. Instead, the city runs ferries — free, day and night, every few minutes, for anyone on foot or on a bike. That is not an oversight; it is policy. Crossing the water is treated as a public right, like the street itself. At Centraal Station you heard how this city built its front door on invented ground and then turned its back on the harbour that made it. The ferries are where Amsterdam turns back around.

Now the buildings. EYE is the national film museum, opened on this bank in 2012 — a cinema, an exhibition hall and one of Europe's important film archives, folded into a building that photographs differently from every angle. The tower beside it has a better secret: it is a recycled office block from the 1970s, built for Shell, whose laboratories once covered this shore. Today it holds a hotel, clubs, a revolving restaurant, and a rooftop lookout with a swing — said to be the highest in Europe — that tips you out over the edge, a hundred metres above the water.

For most of a century, the North was where Amsterdam kept its industry and its noise, and the city looked away. Now its most confident new architecture stands on this bank, facing back across the river.

[beat]

If you time it right, ride the ferry home at dusk, when the lights come on across the water. It is the finest free view in the city — from the deck of a boat the city gives away.
