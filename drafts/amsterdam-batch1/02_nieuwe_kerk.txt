The New Church has been new for six centuries. The name stuck when it was consecrated in 1409 as a second parish church for a town outgrowing its first, and Amsterdam, ever practical, never saw a reason to update it.

You're on Dam Square. Face the Royal Palace, and the church is the tall Gothic building on its right, standing so close the two are nearly joined at the hip. The entrance is on the square side.

Here's what to understand before you go in: this is no longer a church in the working sense. No congregation, no Sunday service. What it holds instead is the nation. Every Dutch monarch since 1814 has been inaugurated at its pulpit — the current king took his oath here, and was married here before that — and between royal occasions the nave fills with large exhibitions, art and photography under mediaeval vaults. A church that swapped its congregation for a country.

Two things to find inside. First, the grave of Joost van den Vondel, the seventeenth-century poet the Dutch rank the way the English rank Shakespeare. If you've been to Vondelpark, you've already walked through his name — the city gave its finest park to a writer who died with almost nothing.

Second, and grander: the tomb of Michiel de Ruyter, the Republic's greatest admiral, carved in white marble in the choir — on the exact spot where the high altar stood before the Reformation. Read that placement plainly. In seventeenth-century Amsterdam, the space once reserved for God went to a sailor, because the sea, and what came home across it, was what this city truly worshipped.

Beneath your feet the floor is paved with roughly ten thousand graves — burials inside stopped only in the eighteen sixties — so almost every stone you cross is somebody.

[beat]

One last pairing before you move on. The palace next door began life as the town hall; the church beside it gave its altar's place to an admiral. On this square the sacred and the civic traded jobs very early, and nobody has ever traded them back. Step inside if there's an exhibition on — there usually is — and look up past whatever's hanging. The vaults have outlasted every use anyone has found for them.
