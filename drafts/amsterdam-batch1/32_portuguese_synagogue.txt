The great hall of this building has never been wired for electricity. That is not neglect. It is a choice, renewed for three and a half centuries.

You should be standing where the busy road opens out, facing a massive block of dark brick with tall arched windows — the Portuguese Synagogue, finished in 1675. Beside it, the smaller square with a bronze statue of a heavyset man, his sleeves rolled: the Dockworker. Both belong to the same story.

The people who built this synagogue were Sephardic Jews whose families had fled Spain and Portugal, where the Inquisition made their faith a capital offence. Amsterdam let them in — tolerance, with the conditions you have heard about elsewhere in this city — and within a few generations they were prosperous enough to raise what was said to be the largest synagogue in the world at the time, its design echoing descriptions of the Temple of Solomon.

Inside, remarkably little has changed. The hall is lit by candles, hundreds of them in brass chandeliers, and the wooden floor is strewn with fine sand — by tradition, to hush footsteps and drink up the damp. Next door is Ets Haim, often called the oldest functioning Jewish library in the world, its collection still consulted by scholars. When services and concerts happen here after dark, they happen by candlelight, as they did in 1675.

Now the statue. In February 1941, after the first round-ups of Jewish men from these streets, the workers of Amsterdam — dockers, tram drivers, factory hands — walked off the job in protest. The February Strike was broken within days, but it remains one of the very few mass protests against the persecution of Jews anywhere in occupied Europe, and the Dockworker stands where it began. Every year, on the anniversary, people file quietly past him with flowers.

The community that built this place survived the Inquisition, only for its descendants to be nearly destroyed here, three centuries later. The building came through. The candles are still lit. Some kinds of persistence do not need a caption — but they do ask you to stand here a moment longer than you'd planned.
