This is the most visited house in the Netherlands, and from the outside there is almost nothing to see. That is part of the story.

You should be on the prin sen grahkt, close to the tall tower of the ves ter kairk, facing number two sixty-three — a plain brick canal house in a row of plain brick canal houses. The museum entrance is the modern building around the corner. One practical thing, said plainly: entry is by online timed ticket only, released weeks in advance, and they sell out. If you're standing here without one, this may be as close as you get today — and standing here still counts for something.

The house belonged to the business of Otto Frank, a Jewish businessman who had already fled Germany once. In July nineteen forty-two, as deportations from Amsterdam began, he moved his family into the empty rooms at the back — the annex — hidden behind a hinged bookcase on the landing. Eight people lived there for more than two years: the Franks, the fan pels family, and a dentist named fritz feffer, kept alive by a handful of office staff downstairs who brought food, news and books at enormous risk to themselves. The youngest of the eight was Otto's daughter Anne, thirteen when she went in, and she spent those two years writing.

She was not keeping a diary idly. She wanted to be a writer, and after hearing a radio broadcast calling for wartime diaries to be preserved, she began revising her own for publication — editing, rewriting, giving people pseudonyms. She was doing an author's work, in hiding, as a teenager.

In August nineteen forty-four the annex was discovered — how, no one has ever established for certain — and all eight were deported. Anne and her sister mar go died in bair gen bel sen in early nineteen forty-five, months before the war ended. Otto was the only one of the eight to survive. He came back, was handed his daughter's papers, and did the one thing left to do: he finished her project. The diary has since been read in some seventy languages.

Inside, the annex rooms are empty. Otto wanted them left that way, and the museum has honoured it — no furniture, no reconstruction. You walk through rooms where the absence is the exhibit. It is the rarest kind of museum: one with nothing to show you, and everything to say.

The ves ter kairk bells, which Anne could hear from the annex and wrote about, still ring every fifteen minutes. When they sound while you're standing here, you are hearing something she heard.
