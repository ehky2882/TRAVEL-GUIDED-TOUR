The Jordaan keeps its memory in one triangular square — and on most mornings, the memory smells like apple pie.

You should be standing on Noordermarkt, the market square wrapped around the Noorderkerk, the plain brick church at its centre, with the Prinsengracht at the square's open end. Two minutes north, across the top of the neighbourhood, runs the Brouwersgracht. We will get there.

Start with the church. Finished in 1623 and designed by Hendrick de Keyser — the same architect behind the Westerkerk, whose tower you can see down the canal. But where the Westerkerk was built grand, for the merchants of the new canal ring, the Noorderkerk was built plain and square, for the working people of the Jordaan — same architect, two churches, and the class line of the seventeenth-century city drawn in brick between them.

The square has been the neighbourhood's gathering point ever since, and not always gently. A memorial here recalls the riots of 1934, when the Jordaan's unemployed rose up against cuts to their support. And in February 1941, the organisers of the February Strike — the protest against the Nazi deportation of the city's Jews — held their first public meetings on this square. This ground has done serious work.

These days it does gentler work. Saturdays bring one of the city's best-loved farmers' markets, all cheese and bread and flowers, while Mondays bring a flea market, with a textile market spilling down Westerstraat behind the church. And the cafés here and along the canal conduct a decades-long argument about who bakes the best apple pie in Amsterdam — a dispute you are allowed to research personally.

Now walk to the Brouwersgracht, the brewers' canal. Here is the detail worth keeping: the brewers gave this water its name, but it was too foul to brew with — so they shipped clean water in by barge, and made their beer with imported water in a city built on the stuff. The warehouses that line the canal — shutters, spout gables, hoisting beams — once held spices, coffee and whale oil, and are now some of the most coveted homes in the city. Readers of a local paper once voted this the most beautiful street in Amsterdam.

That is the Jordaan's whole arc, told in five minutes of ground: built poor, kept fighting, and ended up on the postcards. The pie is a bonus.
