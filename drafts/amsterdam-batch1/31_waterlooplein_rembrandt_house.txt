Every museum is built from what survives. This one was built from a list of everything a man lost.

You should be on Jodenbreestraat, in front of a tall brick house with red shutters and a stepped stone entrance — the Rembrandt House. Behind you, a short walk away, the stalls of the Waterlooplein market fill the square. Both belong in this stop, because both are about what remains.

Rembrandt bought this house in 1639, at the height of his fame, for a sum he never quite managed to pay off. For nearly twenty years he lived, taught and worked here, running a studio on the upper floors and filling the rooms with the collection of a man who could not stop buying things: paintings, prints, shells, weapons, plaster casts, curiosities from the ships in the harbour. In 1656 it all caught up with him. He was declared insolvent, and clerks walked through the house room by room, writing down everything he owned so it could be auctioned. The house was sold two years later, and he moved to a small rented place across town, where he died in 1669.

Here is the turn. That humiliating document — the inventory of his worst year — survived. And centuries later it became the blueprint: the museum around you was rebuilt, room by room, object by object, from the list of everything he lost. You have met Rembrandt all over this city — beside Saskia's grave in the Oude Kerk, beneath the tower of the Westerkerk where his own grave is lost, in front of the Night Watch. The city knows his house better than it knows his resting place, and it knows it because of the paperwork of his ruin.

Now turn to the square. Waterlooplein was the heart of the old Jewish quarter, and its flea market began in the eighteen nineties, when the city moved the neighbourhood's street traders here. The community that built it was almost entirely destroyed in the war — a story this quarter holds, and tells carefully, in its synagogues and its memorials.

The market goes on, most days of the week, stall after stall of other people's belongings finding new hands. In this square, that feels less like commerce and more like a habit of survival.
