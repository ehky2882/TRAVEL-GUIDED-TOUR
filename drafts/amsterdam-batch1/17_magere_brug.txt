A city that invented its own ground, drained its own sea and rebuilt its central station while the trains kept running allows itself exactly one piece of pure sentiment. You're looking at it.

You're on the bank of the Amstel — the river the city is named for — at the Magere Brug, the Skinny Bridge: white-painted timber, nine arches stepping across the water, a double drawbridge at the centre with its counterweight beams raised against the sky like a pair of old-fashioned scales. The best view is from the neighbouring bridges or the quay, where you can take in the whole span.

Here's what makes it remarkable: none of it needs to be like this. There has been a bridge on this spot since the sixteen nineties, and it has been rebuilt again and again — most recently in the twentieth century — and every single time, the city chose to build it back in wood, in white, in the old form, when steel and concrete stood ready. It still works for a living, too: the deck still splits and rises to let boats through, as it has for three centuries. Watch a while and you may catch it — the traffic pausing, the twin leaves tilting up, the river asserting its old right of way.

The name is the mystery it wears best. Skinny Bridge — but why? The dull explanation says the original crossing was so narrow two people could barely pass. The better story, told for centuries so take it as a story, involves two sisters named Mager — which happens to mean skinny — living on opposite banks, who built themselves a bridge rather than walk the long way round to visit each other. Amsterdam has never chosen between the versions, possibly because a city this practical knows better than to fact-check its one good romance.

[beat]

Come back after dark. The whole bridge is strung with lights, arches doubled in the black water, and the river does the rest. The city keeps a thousand working bridges without ceremony. This is the one it keeps for love — and it knows it.
