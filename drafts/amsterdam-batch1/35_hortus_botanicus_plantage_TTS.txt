The quietest place in this quarter is a garden that changed what half the world drinks for breakfast.

You should be at the gates of the hor tus boh tah nee kus on plan tah zhuh mid den lahn, where glasshouses rise behind an old brick wall and the traffic noise drops away almost at once. Around you is the plan tah zhuh itself — the broad, tree-lined quarter of villas and gardens that became the city's first green suburb.

The garden is older than almost everything around it. The city founded it in sixteen thirty-eight, after years of plague, as the hor tus may di kus — a medicinal garden where doctors and apothecaries trained, and where the remedies of the age grew in beds you could walk between. A pharmacy with paths. It moved to this spot in sixteen eighty-two and has been growing ever since.

Then the ships changed everything. Amsterdam sat at the centre of a trading empire, and the East India Company's vessels brought back seeds and cuttings from three continents. This little garden became the greenhouse where the world's plants were received, studied and sent onward — and it is honest, these days, about what that meant. The knowledge and the wealth arrived on the same ships as the colonial trade, and the plantations that some of these plants went on to seed were worked, for a long time, by enslaved people.

Which brings us to the most consequential houseplant in Dutch history. A single coffee plant, raised in this collection, became — by the most-told version, via a cutting presented to the King of France — the ancestor of much of the coffee later grown across Central and South America. The odds are respectable that the coffee in your cup this morning is, distantly, descended from something that grew in these greenhouses.

The garden nearly died itself, in the nineteen eighties, when its university funding was cut — and it was ordinary Amsterdammers who saved it, the same way their great-great-grandparents built the concert hall and the museums: by chipping in.

Go in if you have an hour, or just stand at the wall and breathe. Either way, this is the plan tah zhuh doing what it was designed to do — giving a dense old city somewhere green to exhale.
