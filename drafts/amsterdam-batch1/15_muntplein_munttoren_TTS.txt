The tower in front of you is named for a job it held for about two years, three and a half centuries ago. Amsterdam never got around to updating the label — you may notice a theme developing in this city.

You're on munt pline, the busy junction where the am stel meets the sing ul, trams crossing from every direction. The brick tower with the elegant openwork crown is the munt tor en — the Mint Tower. Find a spot out of the tram lines and give it a proper look.

What you're seeing is a stump — a magnificent one. A mediaeval city gate stood here, the ruh goo leers poort, twin towers and a guardhouse, part of the same wall whose ghost you can chase across the city. In sixteen eighteen the gate burned, and only this one tower survived. Rather than pull it down, the city crowned the survivor: the graceful spire above the brick was added in sixteen twenty by hen drik duh kye zer, the architect who gave Amsterdam most of its best skyline, and its carillon still drops bell-music onto the traffic below.

The name came later, and by accident. In sixteen seventy-two — the year the Dutch still call the Disaster Year — the Republic was invaded, the roads south were cut, and silver could no longer safely reach the national mints. So for a brief emergency stretch, coins were struck right here, in the guardhouse of a burned gate. The crisis passed, the minting moved out, and the name simply never left. A two-year job, a permanent title.

One more thing, and it's the detail to keep: the square you're standing on is, officially, a bridge. Water runs beneath this whole junction, where canal meets river arm — the ground here, like so much of this city, is a polite fiction laid over water.

Before you move on, wait for the carillon if the hour is close. Bells from a tower that outlived its gate, its wall, and its job — playing on, over a square that is secretly a bridge. That's Amsterdam in one intersection.
