The green space in front of you is the city's living room — and it's slowly, politely, sinking.

You're at von del park, the long ribbon of lawns, ponds and winding paths just beyond the Museum Quarter. Pick any entrance; within a minute you'll have the full picture: joggers, herons, toddlers, picnic blankets, and bicycles moving through it all like a current. On the first warm day of the year, half of Amsterdam seems to be lying on this grass.

It looks like a slice of preserved countryside. It's the opposite — every metre of it is designed. In the eighteen sixties a group of wealthy citizens decided their booming city needed what London and Paris had, bought a stretch of soggy pasture outside the edge of town, and hired landscape architects to sculpt an English-style park: artificial ponds, artificial hills, sightlines composed like paintings. It opened in eighteen sixty-five, funded not by the crown or the city but by private subscription — a park the citizens bought for themselves, which is about as Amsterdam as origin stories get.

They named it after yohst fan den von del, the seventeenth-century playwright the Dutch rank as their Shakespeare, and his statue has presided near the centre since eighteen sixty-seven — a national poet watching over sunbathers, which he'd probably have written a scathing verse about.

Now the detail underfoot. This park is built on peat, like most of the city, and unlike the buildings it doesn't stand on piles driven to firm sand — so it sinks, gently and continuously, and parts of it lie a couple of metres below sea level. The ponds double as drainage; the ground is managed like the deck of a ship; trees here grow in soil that is quietly trying to become marsh again. The most relaxed place in Amsterdam is also one of its ongoing engineering projects.

Which is the thing to enjoy about it. The Dutch have an old saying: God made the world, but the Dutch made the Netherlands. This park is that idea off duty: invented ground, held in place by constant quiet effort, so that everyone on it can lie back and think about absolutely nothing.

Find a patch of grass and join them. The sinking is slow. You have time.
