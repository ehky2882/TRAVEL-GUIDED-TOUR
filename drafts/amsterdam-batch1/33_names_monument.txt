Walk along this wall slowly. Every brick carries a name.

You should be on Weesperstraat, beside a monument of low brick walls that turn and fold back on themselves like a maze, topped with panels of polished steel. This is the National Holocaust Names Monument, designed by Daniel Libeskind and opened in 2021.

Each brick is one person: a name, a date of birth, an age. There are more than a hundred thousand of them — the Jews, and the Roma and Sinti, taken from the Netherlands and murdered in the Holocaust. For most of these people, there is no grave anywhere in the world. This wall is the only place their names stand.

Look up at the steel. The mirrored panels are shaped so that, seen from above, they form four Hebrew letters — a phrase meaning "in memory of." From where you stand, they do something simpler: they reflect the sky, the street, the trees, and you, standing among the names. The monument was built to hold the living and the dead in the same frame.

What this place refuses to do is round anything off. A number with that many zeros slides past the mind; a single brick, with a single name and an age, does not. The design asks you to take them one at a time — which is, of course, the only way they were ever taken.

[beat]

Choose one brick. Read the name, and the age. Stand with it for a moment before you move on. That small act is the entire purpose of this place — and it is enough.
