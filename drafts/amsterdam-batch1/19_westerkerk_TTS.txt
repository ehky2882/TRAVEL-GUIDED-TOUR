A strictly Protestant church has no business wearing a crown. This one wears an emperor's.

You should be on the prin sen grahkt at the ves ter markt, looking up at the ves ter kairk and its tower — at eighty-five metres, still the tallest church tower in the city, visible from half of Amsterdam. Look at the very top: that blue and gold object above the clock faces is the imperial crown of maks i mill ee an of Austria. Amsterdam once lent the habs burgs money and backed them in a pilgrimage, and in return maks i mill ee an granted the city the right to bear his crown on its coat of arms. The city, being Amsterdam, has been displaying the receipt ever since — a crown at the top of the skyline, earned not by conquest but by lending.

The church below it, finished in sixteen thirty-one, is the plain kind — the Protestant kind. Whitewashed walls, clear glass, no saints. Which makes it all the stranger that this building has become the city's great collector of feeling.

Start with Rembrandt. He was buried here in sixteen sixty-nine — broke, out of fashion — in a rented pauper's grave with no marker, and the exact spot has never been found. The most famous painter the city ever produced lies somewhere under this floor, and nobody knows where. There's a memorial inside, standing in for a grave that can't be visited.

Then the bells. Anne Frank, hidden a few doors down the canal, could hear this tower's carillon from the annex, and wrote about how much its ringing meant to her — one of the few pieces of the outside world that still reached her. The bells you hear today are, for the most part, the same seventeenth-century bells she heard.

And then the neighbourhood. To the people of the yor dahn, whose streets begin just across the canal, this has never been "the ves ter kairk" — it's the ves ter tor en, the Old Wester, the Pearl of the yor dahn, the tower half the old singalong songs are addressed to. Queen bay ah triks was married here; the flag flies from the tower on the city's big days. A bare church, built to house no images at all, ended up holding more of Amsterdam's memory than any painting could.

Stand a moment and wait for the quarter hour. When the carillon starts, you'll understand what all the songs are about.
