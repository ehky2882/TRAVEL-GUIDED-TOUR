The yor dahn keeps its memory in one triangular square — and on most mornings, the memory smells like apple pie.

You should be standing on nor der markt, the market square wrapped around the nor der kairk, the plain brick church at its centre, with the prin sen grahkt at the square's open end. Two minutes north, across the top of the neighbourhood, runs the brow ers grahkt. We will get there.

Start with the church. Finished in sixteen twenty-three and designed by hen drik duh kye zer — the same architect behind the ves ter kairk, whose tower you can see down the canal. But where the ves ter kairk was built grand, for the merchants of the new canal ring, the nor der kairk was built plain and square, for the working people of the yor dahn — same architect, two churches, and the class line of the seventeenth-century city drawn in brick between them.

The square has been the neighbourhood's gathering point ever since, and not always gently. A memorial here recalls the riots of nineteen thirty-four, when the yor dahn's unemployed rose up against cuts to their support. And in February nineteen forty-one, the organisers of the February Strike — the protest against the Nazi deportation of the city's Jews — held their first public meetings on this square. This ground has done serious work.

These days it does gentler work. Saturdays bring one of the city's best-loved farmers' markets, all cheese and bread and flowers, while Mondays bring a flea market, with a textile market spilling down ves ter straht behind the church. And the cafés here and along the canal conduct a decades-long argument about who bakes the best apple pie in Amsterdam — a dispute you are allowed to research personally.

Now walk to the brow ers grahkt, the brewers' canal. Here is the detail worth keeping: the brewers gave this water its name, but it was too foul to brew with — so they shipped clean water in by barge, and made their beer with imported water in a city built on the stuff. The warehouses that line the canal — shutters, spout gables, hoisting beams — once held spices, coffee and whale oil, and are now some of the most coveted homes in the city. Readers of a local paper once voted this the most beautiful street in Amsterdam.

That is the yor dahn's whole arc, told in five minutes of ground: built poor, kept fighting, and ended up on the postcards. The pie is a bonus.
