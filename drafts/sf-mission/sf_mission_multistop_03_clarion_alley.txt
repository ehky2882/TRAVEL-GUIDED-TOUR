THE MISSION — Stop 3: Clarion Alley

Turn into Clarion Alley and the temperature of the art changes.

This is a single block, running between Valencia and Mission Streets, and nearly every inch of it — both walls, the fences, the garage doors, the backs of buildings — is painted. It's been this way since 1992, when a handful of local artists started the Clarion Alley Mural Project, and the rule from the beginning was simple: the work would be free to make, free to see, and open to anyone in the community who had something to say.

So look closely, because these murals are not here to be pretty. They're here to argue. You'll find pieces about evictions and rising rents, about police and prisons, about queer and trans rights, about the people being pushed out of this exact neighborhood as the money moves in. The art is loud, direct, sometimes raw — and that's the point. Clarion Alley is the Mission's op-ed page, published right on the brick.

It also doesn't sit still. Unlike a museum, where a painting might hang for a century, the work here gets painted over and replaced as new artists and new fights come along, which means whatever you're looking at right now is really a snapshot — a record of what this neighborhood is angry about, hopeful about, fighting for, this particular season. Come back in a year and the conversation will have moved on.

Step back out onto Valencia for a second and you'll feel the whole reason this alley exists. Out there: the new restaurants, the boutiques, the polished version of the Mission that money built. In here: the neighborhood that was here first, still holding its ground, one wall at a time.

[beat]

Take your time reading these. They reward it.

When you're done, we're headed to the alley that started it all. Make your way south and east, down toward Twenty-fourth Street, to a smaller, older lane called Balmy Alley — the place where the Mission's painted walls began, and where the story underneath them gets deeper.
