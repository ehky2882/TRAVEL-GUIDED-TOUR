THE MISSION — Stop 2: Dolores Park

You've reached the part of this walk where the whole neighborhood lies down in the sun.

This is Dolores Park, and the first thing to do is climb to the top corner — the southwest corner, up to your right as you came in, the high ground near Twentieth and Church. Turn around when you get there. The park drops away below you like a green grandstand, and past it, framed by palm trees, is the downtown skyline. It's one of the best free views in San Francisco, and on a sunny weekend half the city is up here sharing it, sprawled on blankets, watching the same thing you are.

It wasn't always this gentle. For decades this land held two Jewish cemeteries, until the city moved the graves out and turned the hill into a park in 1905. A year later, the earthquake hit. When the fire that followed drove tens of thousands of people from their homes, they came here — Dolores Park became one of the city's great refugee camps, a slope covered in tents and cooking fires and families who had lost everything overnight. The same view you're enjoying was, for a while, the last thing a lot of people had left.

That's the quiet history under the picnic blankets, and it tells you something about what this park is for. San Francisco doesn't have many backyards — it's a dense city of apartments and stairs and small rooms — so it shares one. Old families and brand-new arrivals, the comfortable and the barely-hanging-on, everybody ends up on this same hill, and nobody really owns the best spot. You just have to get there early.

[beat]

Take a minute up here. Let the city show off a little.

When you're ready, head back down and out the bottom of the park, and make your way a few blocks northeast toward Valencia Street and a narrow lane called Clarion Alley. The view you just had is the postcard San Francisco; Clarion Alley is the city talking back.
