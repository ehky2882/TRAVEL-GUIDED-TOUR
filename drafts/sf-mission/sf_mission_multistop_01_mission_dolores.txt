THE MISSION — Stop 1: Mission Dolores

You're standing in front of the oldest building in San Francisco — and it's easy to walk right past it, because it's small.

That's almost the point. Look at the little pale church, not the big ornate one beside it. The grand one is the basilica, and it's just over a hundred years old. The modest one — thick white walls, four squat columns, a red-tiled roof — is Mission San Francisco de Asís, finished in 1791. While nearly everything around it burned or fell in the earthquake and fire of 1906, this building simply stood there, the way it had stood for over a century, and the way it's stood ever since.

It survived because of how it was made. The walls are adobe — sun-dried mud brick — and they're roughly four feet thick. The roof beams are redwood, lashed together with rawhide by the Ohlone people, the Native Californians who were here long before the Spanish, and who built this place with their hands. Step inside if it's open, and look up: the ceiling is painted in patterns the Ohlone designed, using colors made from vegetable dyes. It's their work, on their land, in a building meant to replace the world they already had.

That's the harder truth underneath this pretty white chapel. The mission was the beginning of San Francisco, and it was the end of something, too. Out back there's a small cemetery — a camposanto — and thousands of Ohlone are buried in this ground, many of them lost to diseases that arrived with the missionaries. The garden is quiet and kept with care, and it asks you to remember them.

One more thing, and it's where the name comes from. The mission's formal name honors Saint Francis — San Francisco — and that's the name the whole city took. But there was a creek nearby the Spanish called the Laguna de los Dolores, the lagoon of sorrows, and that nickname stuck — first to the mission, then to the street, then to the park we're walking to next.

[beat]

Here's the detail to carry with you. The mission was founded in 1776 — the same summer that, on the far side of the continent, a group of colonists was declaring the United States independent. San Francisco and the country are almost exactly the same age, and they both began in that one remarkable year.

When you're ready, walk up Dolores Street, away from the church, three blocks to the green slope of Dolores Park. It's a gentle uphill, lined with palm trees, and the view at the top earns the climb.
