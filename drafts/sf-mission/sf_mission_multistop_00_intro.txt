THE MISSION — Walk Intro

Here's something most people get wrong about San Francisco: it didn't start at the water.

It started here, in the Mission — a flat, sunny valley a few miles inland that the fog usually forgets, which is exactly why the Spanish picked it in 1776, and why everyone from earthquake refugees to artists has wanted to be here ever since. This is the warmest, sunniest neighborhood in the city, and on a good afternoon it shows.

This walk is an easy one. It's mostly flat, it's short — a little over a mile, end to end — and it runs from the oldest building in San Francisco to some of the youngest art in it. We'll start at the mission that gave the city both its birthplace and its name, climb a gentle slope to a park with one of the best free views in town, then drop into two alleys where the neighborhood paints exactly what it thinks, right on the walls.

That's the thread to hold onto. This is the place where San Francisco began, and it's still beginning — still arguing, still changing, still announcing itself. The mission tells you where the city came from; the murals tell you who lives here now, and what they're not willing to lose.

A note before we go. The Mission is, today, the heart of San Francisco's Latino community — a culture that's been here for generations and has had to fight, more than once, to stay. You'll see that fight on the walls toward the end of this walk, so it's worth arriving ready to read it.

Our first stop is the mission itself. Make your way to the corner of Sixteenth and Dolores Streets, where a low, pale adobe church sits beside a much grander one. The small, plain one is the one we want. I'll meet you there.
