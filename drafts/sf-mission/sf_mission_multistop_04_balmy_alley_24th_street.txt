THE MISSION — Stop 4: Balmy Alley & 24th Street

This is where the Mission's painted walls began.

Balmy Alley is narrower and quieter than Clarion, a single block off Twenty-fourth Street, and the murals here go back further than almost anywhere in the city. Artists started painting these walls in the early 1970s, but the alley became what it is today in 1984, when a group of local muralists called PLACA covered it with work protesting the wars and the suffering then tearing through Central America — places many people in this neighborhood had come from, or still had family in. The murals weren't decoration, and they weren't tourism. They were a community on one side of the world refusing to look away from another.

Decades later, the walls are still working. Some honor that original Central American story; others celebrate Mexican and indigenous heritage, or mourn neighbors lost, or — like Clarion — push back against the displacement pressing in on the Mission right now. The tradition gets repainted here, carefully, by people who treat these walls as something closer to a community altar than a gallery.

Walk back out to Twenty-fourth Street and look around. This stretch has an official name now — the Calle Veinticuatro Latino Cultural District, recognized by the city in 2014 — a designation meant to protect the murals, the businesses, and the culture of a neighborhood that has spent decades being told, in one way or another, to leave. The taquerias, the bakeries, the produce stands, the music coming out of open doors: this is a living culture, not a preserved one.

[beat]

So think about where this walk started. A few blocks from here, two and a half centuries ago, the Spanish built a small adobe church, called the place San Francisco, and began the city — which was also the end of the Ohlone world that came before. And here, at the other end of the same neighborhood, another community paints its own survival onto the walls, year after year, so that no one can pretend it isn't here.

That's the Mission. It's where San Francisco was born, and it's still, every single day, insisting on who it is. You picked a good place to stand and pay attention. Stay a while — get something to eat, read a few more walls. The neighborhood's glad to have you, as long as you're really looking.
