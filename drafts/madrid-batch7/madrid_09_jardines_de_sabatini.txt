You're at the edge of the Jardines de Sabatini, the formal garden along the north side of the Royal Palace — and this stop is really about two gardens, because the palace has a green flank on this side and a very different one around the back, and the contrast between them is the whole point.

Start with where you are. Sabatini is all geometry: clipped hedges in neat beds, a long rectangular pool, marble statues at intervals, the palace looming behind. It feels older than it is. Despite the Italian name — Francesco Sabatini, Charles the Third's architect — Sabatini didn't design this garden; he designed the royal stables that used to stand on this spot. The stables were cleared and the garden laid out in their place in the 1930s, and it opened to the public only in 1978. The statues lined up here are an odd detail: several were originally carved for the roofline of the palace itself, and never made it up there.

Now picture the other side. Around the back of the palace, dropping toward the river, is the Campo del Moro — the Field of the Moor. It's everything Sabatini isn't: not geometric but romantic, a wooded landscape of winding paths, big old trees, two fine fountains and, wandering loose among them, peacocks. Its name reaches back to 1109, when a Muslim army camped on this slope to besiege the fortress that then stood where the palace is now.

So the crown gave itself two kinds of green: a formal parade-ground of a garden out front, to be seen from the palace windows, and a wild romantic park behind, to disappear into. One is for show; the other for escape.

Both are free, both are quiet by Madrid standards, and Sabatini in particular is one of the best places in the centre to watch the sun set behind the palace. Pick the mood you're in — order or wilderness — and the palace will give you either one.
