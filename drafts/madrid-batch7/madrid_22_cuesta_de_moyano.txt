You're on the Cuesta de Moyano — a short, sloping pedestrian street running between the edge of the Retiro and the Atocha end of the Paseo del Prado. One side of it is lined with a row of low wooden stalls, grey-blue, shuttered up like beach huts when they're closed and folded open into tiny bookshops when they're not. There are around thirty of them, and they've been here, doing exactly this, since 1925.

This is Madrid's permanent open-air book fair — the largest standing gathering of open-air bookshops in Europe, and the place to come for the books you can't find anywhere else. Out-of-print titles, first editions, old magazines and maps, the contents of someone's emptied library. Many of the stalls are run by the children and grandchildren of the people who ran them decades ago. The stall design itself was drawn up by a city architect, Luis Bellido, who also built Madrid's old slaughterhouse.

There's a phrase worth carrying away. Early on, the writer Ramón Gómez de la Serna nicknamed the place the Feria del boquerón — the anchovy fair — because a book here cost roughly what an anchovy cost. Cheap reading for anyone who wanted it, which was the entire idea.

The stalls have a quietly heroic history. They stayed open almost without a break through the Civil War. In the censorship of the years that followed, this slope became a discreet network for the books that weren't supposed to circulate — banned titles passing hand to hand between sellers who trusted their customers. A row of cheap second-hand stalls turned out to be one of the harder things in the city to fully control.

At the top of the slope stands a statue of the novelist Pío Baroja; at the bottom, the politician Claudio Moyano, whose education law gave the street its name. And as of early 2026 the fair is formally recognised as part of the region's living cultural heritage — a tidy thing to happen to a place that has spent a hundred years selling people books for the price of an anchovy.
