If you've found your way here, you've left the busy heart of the park behind. You're in the rosalayda — the rose garden — a neat ellipse tucked into the southern end of the reteero, ringed by clipped hedges, with two stone fountains and a series of iron arches the climbing roses are trained up and over. The statue of the Fallen Angel is a short walk away, if you want a landmark to fix your bearings by.

Time of year decides what you get. Come in May or June and the place is at full volume — more than four thousand rose bushes, something like five hundred varieties, scent you could find with your eyes shut, the arches heavy with bloom. Any other month it's a calm, geometric green space, almost empty, which has its own quiet appeal.

The garden is younger than it looks, and it has a good origin story. Around nineteen fifteen the city's head gardener, thaytheelyo rodreegeth, set out to give Madrid a rose garden to rival the famous ones in Paris. He travelled there himself to select the first roses — and chose the worst possible moment, because the First World War broke out while he was away and he had a hard time getting himself and his plants home. He laid the garden out on a spot with its own buried history: it had held a private greenhouse belonging to the markess day salamanka, and before that a pond that froze hard enough each winter for people to skate on it.

The roses he brought back were lost in the Civil War, which left little of the park untouched. The garden was replanted in nineteen forty-one — four thousand bushes again — and carefully restored in the decades since. So what you're looking at is a faithful remake of one man's idea, twice over.

It's an easy place to underrate: a rose garden in the corner of a famous park. But it's one of the most deliberate, least hurried things in the reteero — built, lost, and rebuilt, simply so the city would have somewhere lovely to stand and breathe. Take your time in it.
