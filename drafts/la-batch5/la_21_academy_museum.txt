The movie industry is a century old, and for most of that century it never built itself a museum. When it finally did, it chose a department store — and then bolted a sphere onto the back.

You should be at Wilshire Boulevard and Fairfax Avenue, looking at a golden cylinder rising at the corner — a curved wall of gold mosaic tile catching the sun — attached to a long, clean building of pale limestone. This is the Academy Museum of Motion Pictures, and the building came first: the May Company department store, built in 1939, the grandest piece of Streamline Moderne left in Los Angeles. Its architect, Albert C. Martin, also designed City Hall — the man got around. That gold corner holds more than three hundred fifty thousand tiles, and when restoration required replacements, they came from the original mosaic workshop in Venice, Italy.

Walk around to the north side, or just look past the building, and you'll see the addition: a giant concrete-and-glass sphere, lifted off the ground on piers, connected to the old store by glass bridges. Renzo Piano designed it to hold a thousand-seat theater, with a glass-domed terrace on top that looks straight out at the Hollywood Sign — the museum aiming its best view at the industry's oldest advertisement.

Inside, the collection runs deep — millions of objects going back to the beginning of the movies, from costumes and screenplays to props, including a certain full-sized mechanical shark suspended over the escalators, the only one of its kind to survive its famous 1975 film.

Here's what's worth chewing on. The industry that manufactures permanence for everyone else — that decides which faces and stories get preserved — waited nearly a hundred years to preserve itself, and when it did, it moved into a building designed for selling dresses and dinnerware. A department store from 1939, the same year often called the movies' greatest: the future as imagined then, holding the past as curated now. The gold corner was built to stop traffic on Wilshire. It still does — it just sells something different.
