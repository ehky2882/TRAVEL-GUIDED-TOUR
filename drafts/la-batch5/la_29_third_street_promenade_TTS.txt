THIRD STREET PROMENADE

Here's a piece of history hiding under your feet: this street is one of the oldest pedestrian malls in America — and one of the only ones that ever worked.

You should be standing on Third Street between wil sher and Broadway, three blocks closed to cars, storefronts on both sides, and — down the middle of the street — a row of enormous topiary dinosaurs spouting water from their mouths. We'll get to the dinosaurs.

Start with the experiment. In the nineteen sixties, American downtowns were losing everything to the suburbs, and roughly two hundred cities tried the same desperate fix: close a shopping street to cars and hope people came back. Santa Monica closed this one in nineteen sixty five, one of the first in the country. Almost every mall like it eventually failed — and for a while, this one did too. By the nineteen eighties it was struggling: dark storefronts, thin crowds, the same slow fade playing out on pedestrian malls everywhere.

Then Santa Monica did the strange thing. Instead of giving the street back to cars, it doubled down — a full redesign, reopened in nineteen eighty nine, built around restaurants, movie theaters, and street performers rather than shops alone, so the street had a reason to stay alive after dark. It worked spectacularly. For decades this was the proof, cited by planners all over the country, that a street built for feet could beat the shopping mall at its own game.

The dinosaurs arrived with that redesign — six of them, ivy-covered fountains by the French artists lay lah lahn — and they've outlasted nearly every store that was open when they were planted. Which is the honest part of the story: retail comes and goes hard on this street. Right now it's mostly going — you'll see empty windows between the open ones — and the city is responding the way it did last time, by betting on the street itself: looser rules, more restaurants, more events, even a zone where you can carry a drink outdoors as you walk, the first of its kind in Southern California.

So read this street as a running argument, sixty years long, about whether a car city can hold onto a place built for walking. The argument has been won, lost, and won again — and the dinosaurs, who have watched all of it, don't look worried.
