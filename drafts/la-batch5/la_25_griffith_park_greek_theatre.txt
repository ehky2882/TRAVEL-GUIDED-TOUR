GRIFFITH PARK & THE GREEK THEATRE

Los Angeles has a reputation for paving things, so it's worth pausing on what it didn't pave.

You should be near the Greek Theatre, on Vermont Canyon Road where Los Feliz runs out and the hills take over — the amphitheater's white colonnade on one side of you, and on the other, chaparral climbing into what looks, honestly, like wilderness. That's because it is.

Griffith Park covers more than four thousand acres — nearly five times the size of Central Park — and unlike Central Park, most of it was never landscaped. It's genuinely rugged: canyons, coyotes, trails where you can lose sight of the city while standing in the middle of it. It exists because a mining millionaire named Griffith J. Griffith gave the land away in 1896, declaring that public parks were a safety valve for great cities. His own story turned dark afterward — a crime, a prison term — and Los Angeles argued for years about whether to keep taking his money. It did, and his will paid for two things: the Observatory on the ridge above you, and the theater beside you.

The Greek opened in 1930, set into this canyon because the acoustics were already here — the hillside does work an architect would charge millions for. It seats nearly six thousand people, spent World War Two as army barracks, and now fills a season of concerts every year from spring to fall. Angelenos will tell you, with some heat, that a show here beats the Hollywood Bowl.

But the best story in this park walked in on four feet. In 2012, a trail camera caught something that shouldn't have been possible: a mountain lion, living in Griffith Park. He had crossed two of the busiest freeways in America to get here, and he stayed a decade — eight square miles, the smallest territory ever recorded for his species, with millions of people around the edges. They called him P-22, and a photograph of him prowling below the Hollywood Sign made him the most famous cat in the country. He died in 2022, and the city genuinely mourned him; his story helped get a wildlife crossing built over a freeway, so the next lion wouldn't need his luck.

That's the thing to carry up the trail: this isn't a park in a city. It's a piece of wild California the city grew around — and never quite tamed.
