Griffith Park covers more than four thousand acres — nearly five times the size of Central Park — and unlike Central Park, most of it was never landscaped. It's genuinely rugged: canyons, coyotes, trails where you can lose sight of the city while standing in the middle of it. It exists because a mining millionaire named Griffith J. Griffith gave the land away in eighteen ninety six, declaring that public parks were a safety valve for great cities. His own story turned dark afterward — a crime, a prison term — and Los Angeles argued for years about whether to keep taking his money. It did, and his will paid for two things: the Observatory on the ridge above you, and the theater beside you.

The Greek opened in nineteen thirty, set into this canyon because the acoustics were already here — the hillside does work an architect would charge millions for. But the best story in this park walked in on four feet. In twenty twelve, a trail camera caught something that shouldn't have been possible: a mountain lion, living in Griffith Park. He had crossed two of the busiest freeways in America to get here, and he stayed a decade — eight square miles, the smallest territory ever recorded for his species, with millions of people around the edges. That's the thing to carry up the trail: this isn't a park in a city. It's a piece of wild California the city grew around — and never quite tamed.
