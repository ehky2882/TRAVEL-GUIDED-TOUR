Walk up to the front doors of Grace Cathedral and look closely at them, because they're a secret hiding in plain sight. Those tall bronze doors, divided into ten gilded panels of biblical scenes — they are exact casts of one of the most famous works of the Italian Renaissance. The originals are in Florence, on the Baptistery, and Michelangelo is supposed to have called them the Gates of Paradise. The ones in front of you were cast from the same molds. You're standing at a set of Renaissance doors, on a hill in San Francisco.

Step back and take in the whole building. It looks like a French Gothic cathedral — pointed arches, soaring nave, a great rose window — and it more or less is, except for one thing: it's made of concrete. Construction dragged on for decades, beginning in nineteen twenty-eight and not finishing until nineteen sixty-four, and the budget never stretched to stone. So they faked it, beautifully. This is one of the largest cathedrals in the country, and it's essentially a Gothic dream poured out of concrete.

But the doors aren't even the best part. The best part is on the floor. Set into the stone, both inside the cathedral and out on the plaza, are two labyrinths — winding circular paths modeled on the medieval one at Chartres. They are not mazes; you can't get lost. There's one way in and the same way out, and the idea is simply to walk it slowly, as a kind of moving meditation. And here's what matters: anyone can. You don't have to be religious, you don't have to pay, you don't have to believe anything. You just walk.

Inside, there's one more thing to find — a small chapel near the entrance with an altarpiece by the artist Keith Haring, his very last finished work before he died, dedicated to those lost in the AIDS epidemic.

That's the quiet genius of this place. A twentieth-century building, made of humble concrete, that reached back five hundred years to borrow Renaissance doors and a medieval path — and then opened all of it to anyone who wanders up the hill. Go walk the labyrinth. That's the part you'll remember.
