You're at the top of the famous block — Lombard Street, the one with the eight tight switchbacks zigzagging down the hill through the flower beds. Cars inch down it nose to tail, brakes squealing, while people line both sidewalks taking the same photo everyone takes. It's called the crookedest street in the world. Two things about that.

First, it wasn't built to be charming. It was built to be survivable. This block of Lombard runs straight down Russian Hill at a natural grade of about twenty-seven percent — steep enough that a car coming down it in a straight line, especially in the early days of cars, was a genuine hazard, and a person walking it wasn't much better off. So in nineteen twenty-two the property owners came up with a fix: instead of going straight down, switchback across the slope. The curves cut the effective steepness almost in half, to a manageable sixteen percent or so. The famous zigzag is, at heart, a safety feature — an engineering compromise with a hill that didn't want a road on it.

Second — and locals enjoy this one — it isn't actually the crookedest street in San Francisco. That title arguably belongs to a stretch of Vermont Street, over on Potrero Hill, which has fewer curves but tighter, sharper ones. Lombard won the fame because it's prettier and better placed, draped in hydrangeas with the bay glittering behind it. Vermont Street is crookeder. Lombard is photogenic. Fame went to the photogenic one, which tells you something about how reputations work.

So take the picture — everyone does, and the view straight down through the curves and out to coyt Tower and the bay really is lovely. But as you watch the cars crawl through those eight bends, remember what you're actually looking at. Not a whimsy, not a folly. A practical answer to a stubborn hill, dressed up so well in flowers and brick that the whole world decided it was charming on purpose.
