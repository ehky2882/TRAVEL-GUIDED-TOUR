Here's a thing you can do in San Francisco that you can't do anywhere else on the planet: you can ride a National Historic Landmark. Not visit one. Ride it. The cable cars are the only ones of their kind left running in the world.

You're probably near the Powell Street turnaround, the spot at Powell and Market where the line ends and a small crowd is usually waiting. Watch what happens when a car arrives. It can't turn around on its own — these cars only run one direction — so the operators get out, push the whole thing by hand onto a wooden turntable, and physically rotate it to point back up the hill. A hundred-and-fifty-year-old machine, turned around by two people leaning on it. That alone is worth the wait.

Now, how the thing actually moves. There's no engine in the car. Underneath the street, running in a slot you can see between the rails, is a steel cable, and it's moving — constantly, in a loop, pulled by a giant powerhouse a few blocks away — at exactly nine and a half miles an hour, all day long. To go, the operator, called the gripman, pulls a lever that reaches down through that slot and clamps onto the moving cable, and the car is yanked into motion. To stop, he lets go and works the brakes. That's it. The car has no throttle. It's either gripping the cable and going nine and a half miles an hour, or it isn't.

The system dates to 1873. The story goes that the inventor, Andrew Hallidie, watched a team of horses lose their footing and get dragged down one of these wet, brutal hills, and decided there had to be a better way to climb them.

*[beat]*

So when you ride one — and you should — pay attention to the gripman. Listen for the clang of the bell and the deep rumble of the cable in the slot under your feet. He's not driving. He's doing something stranger and older than that: grabbing a moving rope under the street, by hand, and hanging on, exactly the way it's been done for a hundred and fifty years.
