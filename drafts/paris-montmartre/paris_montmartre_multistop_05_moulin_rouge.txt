You've come down off the hill, and here at the bottom, on a busy boulevard, is the red windmill — the Moulin Rouge. Its sails turn in red neon now, but stop and think about that windmill for a second, because it's a clue to everything you just walked through.

It's fake. It always was — a decorative red windmill bolted to a cabaret roof when the place opened in 1889, the same year the Eiffel Tower went up across the city. But it's a fake of something that was once completely real. This whole hill was once covered in working windmills, dozens of them, grinding grain and pressing grapes back when Montmartre was farmland. By the time the cabaret opened, nearly all of them were gone — so the Moulin Rouge bolted a pretend one onto its roof, a bit of rustic nostalgia for a countryside the city had just paved over. Two of the genuine old windmills still stand a few streets away, if you want to find the real thing later.

What opened underneath that fake windmill, though, was brand new. The Moulin Rouge took a rowdy, high-kicking dance and turned it into a spectacle — the cancan as the world now knows it, fast and loud and deliberately scandalous. And a short, sharp-eyed painter named Toulouse-Lautrec sat in the audience night after night and put it all on paper, in posters so good they became the image of Paris nightlife forever.

*[beat]*

So here's where the walk leaves you, and it's a fitting place. You started at the quiet top of the hill, in the real village under the souvenir, climbed past a white church built out of guilt, through a square performing its own past — and you've come down to a red windmill that was never a windmill, spinning over a dance floor, at the foot of the hill where the city finally caught up with the village. Montmartre, top to bottom: the real thing and the show of it, all on one slope. Stay for the lights coming on. They're worth it.
