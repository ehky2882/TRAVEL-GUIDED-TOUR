Most of Paris is flat. This corner isn't.

monmartr is a hill — a steep one, the highest ground in the city — and for most of its history it wasn't even part of Paris. It was a separate village up here above the smoke, with windmills turning on top and gypsum mines hollowing out the rock below, close enough to the city to see it but far enough to feel like another world. Paris only swallowed it in eighteen sixty. And the name carries something older and darker: the mount of the martyr, after a bishop who, the legend says, was beheaded on this hill and then stood up and walked off carrying his own head.

This walk is about what happened next — when, around the year nineteen hundred, the cheap rents and the village quiet drew the artists up here, and monmartr became, for a few decades, the most famous bohemia on earth. Painters, poets, dancers, drinkers. That moment is long over, but the hill has been performing it ever since, and the walk moves through both versions at once: the real village that still survives in the side streets, and the souvenir of itself that the crowds come to see.

A warning about the walk. It's short — you stay on and around one hill — but it is genuinely steep, with staircases instead of streets in places. Take it slowly. If your legs object, there's a little funicular that carries you up the worst of the climb to the church at the top.

We'll start partway up, in a square that's still the real monmartr, climb to the white church on the summit and the painters' square behind it, and come down the far side to a red windmill at the bottom. Begin at the plass dayz abess, at the green metroh entrance that looks like something grown rather than built.
