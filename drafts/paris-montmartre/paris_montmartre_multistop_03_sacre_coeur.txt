You've climbed it, and you're standing under Sacré-Cœur — the great white basilica with its cluster of round pale domes, the building you can see from half of Paris. Catch your breath on the steps and turn around first, because the view is the reward: the whole city spread out below you, flat and grey-gold to the horizon, with the hill you're on as the highest natural point in Paris. People have been coming up here to look down at the city for about as long as there's been a city to look at.

Now the church itself, and two things worth knowing. The first is the colour. It never seems to get dirty, and that's not luck — it's built of a stone that bleaches itself. When it rains, the stone reacts with the water and releases a white mineral, so the building effectively washes itself paler with every shower. While the rest of Paris darkened with soot over the past century, Sacré-Cœur kept turning whiter.

The second thing is why it's here, and it's heavier than the bright domes suggest. It was begun in 1875, in the aftermath of a disastrous war and a brutal civil uprising — the Commune — that had begun, and been crushed, partly on this very hill. The church was raised as a kind of national penance: an act of atonement for what France had done and suffered. To some, that made it a monument of healing and faith; to others, then and now, it looked like the winners building a giant church on the grave of the losers. Both readings have followed it ever since.

*[beat]*

So it's a more complicated building than the postcard lets on — beautiful, contested, and permanently, deliberately white.

When you're ready, walk around to the back of the basilica, into the small square just behind it. After the crowds on these steps it'll feel tighter and older, packed with easels and painters. That's the Place du Tertre.
