This is the Place du Tertre, and it's the strangest stop on the walk — because it manages to be the most authentic and the most fake place on the hill at the same time. This little square was the real heart of Montmartre when it was a village: the market square, the centre of everything. And around the year 1900, when the artists moved in, this was their neighbourhood square too.

The real thing happened just steps from here. In a ramshackle building nearby that everyone called the Bateau-Lavoir — the laundry-boat, because that's what it looked like — a young Spaniard named Picasso painted some of the pictures that broke art open, including, around 1907, the one that detonated modern painting. That genuinely happened, right here, on this hill.

But that was well over a century ago, and what fills the square now is the memory of it, sold by the metre. The painters you see crowded under umbrellas, offering to sketch your portrait in ten minutes, are working a kind of permanent costume drama — Montmartre playing the role of itself. It's easy to be sniffy about it. Try not to be; it's also the genuine descendant of something that was once entirely real.

*[beat]*

Want the proof that this was a true village and not a stage set? Just down the slope on the far side there's a working vineyard — an actual one, still making wine each year, a last green survivor of the orchards and fields that covered this hill before the city climbed up to meet it.

From here, the walk goes downhill — a long descent off the front of the hill toward the bright lights at the bottom. Head down, and aim for a red windmill. That's the Moulin Rouge.
