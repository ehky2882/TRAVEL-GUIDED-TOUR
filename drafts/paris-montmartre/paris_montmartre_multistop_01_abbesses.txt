You're at the Place des Abbesses, and the first thing to look at is the thing you probably just came up through: the Métro entrance. That curving green cast-iron canopy, with the word Métropolitain in those strange melting letters and a glass roof like a dragonfly's wing, is the real thing — designed by Hector Guimard around the turn of the twentieth century, when the Métro was new and Paris wanted its entrances to look like the future. Most of his canopies were ripped out over the years. This is one of only two of the fully glass-roofed ones left in the entire city, so you're looking at a genuine survivor.

There's a reason you arrived a little breathless, too. Abbesses is the deepest station in the Paris Métro — about thirty-six metres down, the depth of a ten-storey building, because the hill above it is solid rock. The spiral staircase is famous, and famously punishing; most people take the easier way up and regret nothing.

The square's name reaches further back. Abbesses means the abbesses — the women who ran a powerful abbey that once crowned this hill, centuries before any painter showed up. The convent is long gone, but the name stuck to the square.

Stand here a moment and notice what this place is not. No tour groups, no easels, no crush of people — just a café-lined square, someone buying bread, a carousel turning. This is the Montmartre that's still simply a neighbourhood, the village underneath the souvenir. Hold onto that feeling, because once we climb, it gets harder to find.

Just off the square is a small walled garden, and set into it is a wall covered in three words. Head there next — it's the Wall of Love.
