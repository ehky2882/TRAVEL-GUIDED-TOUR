You've come a fair way out to get here — north of downtown, off the Don Valley Parkway, past the office towers and the traffic, to a low pale building set on a rise with formal gardens around it. That distance is worth thinking about, because Toronto very nearly didn't get this at all.

The man behind it, the late Aga Khan — spiritual leader of the world's izmaeelee Muslims — first wanted to build his museum in London, on the Thames, across from Parliament. That plan collapsed in two thousand two. So he chose Canada instead, and said plainly why: he admired this country's pluralism. Which means there is a world-class museum of Islamic art out here, on the edge of North York, that exists essentially as a compliment.

Now look at the building, designed by the Japanese architect foomiheeko mahkee — working, as it happens, with the same Toronto firm behind the bahta Shoe Museum downtown. In a letter, the Aga Khan asked mahkee to build the whole thing around a single idea: light. So the structure is angled precisely off north, so that every outer wall catches the sun at some point across the day, and inside, a glass-roofed courtyard is etched with patterns that throw shifting geometric shadows over the floor as the light moves. The building is, in a real sense, a machine for watching daylight.

Inside it is more than a thousand years of art from across the Muslim world, from Spain all the way to China — ceramics, textiles, astronomical instruments, and among the manuscripts, one of the oldest surviving copies of avisenna's great medical encyclopedia, a book European doctors leaned on for centuries.

When you've finished inside, go out into the garden — four green quarters and still reflecting pools, a form meant, in this tradition, to picture paradise. It's very quiet out here, deliberately so. You had to leave the city to reach it, which is rather the whole point.
