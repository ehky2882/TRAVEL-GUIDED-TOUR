Stand on Cumberland Street or Yorkville Avenue, just north of Bloor, and look at what's left of the old village: a row of narrow Victorian houses, brightly painted, now holding boutiques and restaurants where the rent would make your eyes water. Hold that picture. Because these exact houses have staged two completely opposite versions of cool.

In the eighteen thirties this was its own village, founded by a brewer and a sheriff, annexed by Toronto in eighteen eighty-three, and left to quietly age. By the nineteen sixties the Victorian houses had gone cheap and a little shabby — which is exactly why the artists came. Coffee houses opened in the front rooms, all-ages and unlicensed, and for one remarkable decade this was Canada's Greenwich Village. Gordon Lightfoot, Joni Mitchell, Neil Young, Ian and Sylvia, buffy sant maree — a whole generation of the Canadian songbook was worked out in these basements, night after night, in front of a hundred people at a time. Neil Young later wrote a song looking back on the Riverboat, the coffee house that outlasted all the others.

It didn't last. As the crowds and the drugs grew, the city grew alarmed — bylaw crackdowns, curfews, a heavy police presence — and by the end of the nineteen sixties the scene had been cleared out. What moved in was the exact opposite of a coffee house. Bloor Street, along the south edge here, became the Mink Mile: Canada's most expensive stretch of shopping, the one street in the city with its own granite sidewalks, lined end to end with the global luxury names.

So the neighbourhood that got famous for being cheap and creative is now famous for being neither. But here's the detail I'd send you to see. Tucked into the little park on Cumberland, there's a boulder — six hundred and fifty tonnes of billion-year-old granite, quarried up north on the Canadian Shield, sawn into pieces, trucked down on twenty flatbeds, and reassembled here to look as though it grew out of the ground.

Think about that for a second. In the most expensive neighbourhood in the country, on land where cheap rent once made real art possible, someone decided the missing ingredient was a genuine piece of Canadian wilderness — so they bought one and installed it. Yorkville, in a single rock.
