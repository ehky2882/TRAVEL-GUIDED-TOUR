PALACIO REAL — THE ROYAL PALACE (TTS-safe)

On Christmas Eve in seventeen thirty-four, the old royal fortress that stood on this spot burned to the ground. What replaced it is the largest palace in Western Europe.

Look up at the front of it. The Royal Palace has more than three thousand rooms — three thousand four hundred and eighteen, if you want the exact figure — and spreads across a footprint you simply cannot take in from any single angle. When the old alkasar burned, King Philip the Fifth, who had grown up at vairsye, seized the chance to build something to rival it: a vast stone palace in the French taste. And because the last one had gone up in flames, this one was built almost entirely of stone and brick, with hardly any wood — a palace designed, above all, never to burn.

It took from seventeen thirty-eight to seventeen sixty-four to finish, and the first king to actually live in it was Charles the Third. Inside are things worth knowing about even from out here: a complete set of stradivairius instruments, a celebrated royal armoury, ceilings painted by tyepolo, walls hung with Velázquez, Goya and Caravaggio.

But here's the detail that reframes the whole building. No one lives here. The Spanish royal family moved out generations ago and now lives at the far more modest sarswela palace, on the edge of the city. This place is kept for ceremony alone — state dinners, the reception of ambassadors, the formal business of the crown.

So look at it for what it is: not a home, but a stage. A hundred-thousand-square-metre stage set, built to impress foreign visitors and project the power of a monarchy at the height of its empire — and still doing exactly that job today, for a king who sleeps somewhere else. The grandeur isn't decoration. The grandeur is the entire point.
