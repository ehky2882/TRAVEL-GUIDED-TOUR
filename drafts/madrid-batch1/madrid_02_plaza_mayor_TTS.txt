PLAZA MAYOR (TTS-safe)

This square was built to be a stage.

Look around: plassa mayor is closed on all four sides by the same three-storey building, uniform red facades with rows of balconies — two hundred and thirty-seven of them — and you can only get in through one of nine archways tucked into the corners and sides. That enclosure is the whole point. When it was completed in sixteen nineteen, under King Philip the Third, this was the largest open space in the city, and it was designed so that whatever happened in the middle could be watched from above.

And a great deal happened in the middle. For centuries this was Madrid's main square in every sense — an ordinary market on most days, but on special ones a vast outdoor theatre. There were festivals and tournaments, bullfights with the ring set up on the stones, the proclamation of kings. The royal family and the city's grandest households watched from the balconies, which were effectively the best boxes in town, while the crowd packed the floor below.

It also held darker spectacles. During the Spanish Inquisition, the square was used for the public trials and punishments of those the Church condemned — solemn, frightening ceremonies staged in front of the whole city. It's worth a quiet moment to remember that the same balconies built for celebration looked down on that too.

In the centre, on horseback, is Philip the Third himself, the king who finished the square. And on the north side, find the building with paintings across its front — that's the kasa day la panadereea, the old bakery house, the one side that breaks the uniform pattern with its frescoes.

The square is calm now — cafés under the arcades, someone selling stamps on a Sunday, people drifting through. But read the shape of it. A closed rectangle, balconies on every side, a single open floor: this isn't just a handsome square, it's a theatre, and for four hundred years Madrid was the audience.
