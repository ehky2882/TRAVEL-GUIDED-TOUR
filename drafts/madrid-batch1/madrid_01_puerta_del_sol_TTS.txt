PUERTA DEL SOL (TTS-safe)

Stand still for a second. You're at the centre of Spain — and not in a poetic way. Literally.

Look at the ground in front of the grand building with the clock tower, the rayahl kasa day korrayos, and you'll find a small stone plaque set into the paving: Kilometre Zero. Every major road in the country is measured from this exact spot, and Madrid's street numbers count outward from here too. The original marker wore smooth under a couple of centuries of footsteps and was replaced in two thousand nine, but the idea hasn't changed — this is the point from which Spain measures itself.

Now turn toward the start of kahyay day alkala and find the statue everyone is photographing: a bear reaching up into a strawberry tree. This is el oso ee el madronyo, the emblem of Madrid, and it sits on the city's coat of arms. There's an old story that Madrid was once called oorsaria, the land of bears, for the animals in the forests nearby. The bronze weighs around twenty tonnes, and until two thousand nine it stood on the other side of the square — half of Sol seems to have quietly relocated that year.

But the thing that makes this square matter to every person in the country is up on that clock tower. On the last night of the year, the bells ring midnight, and as they strike, people across Spain eat twelve grapes — one for each chime — for luck in the year ahead. It has been broadcast live from this clock since nineteen sixty-two, which means that for most Spaniards alive today, the new year has always begun, in some sense, right here.

That's the strange thing about Puerta del Sol. It isn't beautiful, exactly — it's a busy half-circle of a square, crossed by thousands of people an hour. But it's the country's gathering place. When Spain celebrates, mourns, or argues with itself, it tends to do it here.

So don't just pass through. Stand on Kilometre Zero for a moment, with the whole country arranged around you, and go and find the bear before you leave. This is less a thing to look at than a place that does a job — and has done it for a very long time.
