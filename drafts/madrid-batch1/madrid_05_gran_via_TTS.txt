GRAN VÍA (TTS-safe)

This street looks grand and old. It's grand — but it's younger than your great-grandparents, and it was built by destroying the city that stood here before.

For centuries, this was a knot of narrow medieval lanes. Then, early in the twentieth century, Madrid decided it wanted a great modern avenue slicing through the middle of it — and to get one, the city demolished more than a dozen old streets and hundreds of buildings, driving gran veea straight through the wreckage. Work began in nineteen ten and wasn't finished until nineteen twenty-nine, and people argued about it the whole way: it was bold, it was brutal, it was the future, it was vandalism. All of those were true at once.

What rose along it was Madrid's idea of the modern world. Look for the metropolis building, with its dome and the winged figure on top — one of the most photographed corners in the city. Further along stands the telefonika building, a tower that was, when it went up, one of the first true skyscrapers in Europe. And all down the avenue: cinemas, theatres, grand hotels, neon signs the size of buildings. People call it the Spanish Broadway, and on a Saturday night you'll see exactly why.

That's the thing to hold onto here. gran veea isn't a survival from old Madrid — it's the opposite. It's the city in the nineteen-tens and twenties announcing that it intended to be a modern metropolis, built in the very same decades the great downtowns of America were going up. The street that looks like history is actually Madrid's leap into the future, carved a century ago straight through the past.
