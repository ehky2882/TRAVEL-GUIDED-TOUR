MERCADO DE SAN MIGUEL

Step inside and let the building work on you first, before the food does.

Mercado de San Miguel is a box of iron and glass, built in 1916, and it's one of the last markets of its kind left in Madrid — a delicate metal frame holding up walls that are almost entirely windows, so the whole interior glows. For most of the twentieth century this was an ordinary neighbourhood market, the kind where you came to buy fish and vegetables and carried them home to cook. By the early two-thousands it had fallen on hard times and was nearly lost.

What saved it was a change of purpose. Rather than knock the beautiful old shell down, the market was restored and reopened as a place to eat rather than shop. Today the stalls sell small things to taste on the spot — slivers of cured ham, olives, croquettes, a dozen kinds of vermouth, oysters on ice — and the idea is to wander with a glass in one hand, grazing from counter to counter, standing up, elbow to elbow with everyone else.

It's worth being honest: this is firmly on the tourist trail now, busier and pricier than the everyday markets where locals actually do their shopping, and some Madrileños roll their eyes at it a little. But the building itself is the real thing, and the pleasure of eating well under that glowing iron roof is genuine.

[beat]

Think of San Miguel as a small lesson in how this city treats its past. The market outlived the job it was built for, and instead of tearing it down, Madrid kept the gorgeous bones and changed what happens inside. Take a slow lap before you commit to anything — and then have the ham.
