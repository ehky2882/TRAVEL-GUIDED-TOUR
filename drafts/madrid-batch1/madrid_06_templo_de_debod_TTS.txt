TEMPLO DE DEBOD (TTS-safe)

There is a two-thousand-year-old Egyptian temple in this park. That sentence is exactly as strange as it sounds, and it's worth pausing on.

This is the templo day debod, and it is the real thing — not a replica, not a copy. It was built more than two thousand years ago in noobia, in the far south of Egypt, as a shrine to the gods ammun and Isis, and for most of its life it stood beside the Nile. Then, in the nineteen-sixties, Egypt began building the great aswan High Dam, and the rising water threatened to drown a whole landscape of ancient monuments. An international rescue effort, led by you nesko, saved what it could — and to thank the countries that had helped, Egypt gave several of the smaller temples away. One went to a museum in New York, one to Turin, one to the Netherlands. Spain was given this one, in nineteen sixty-eight.

It was taken apart, shipped across the Mediterranean, and rebuilt here stone by stone, opening in nineteen seventy-two. You can walk right inside — entry is free, though only a few people are allowed in at a time — and find the original carvings still on the walls.

But the reason to come is the setting. The temple sits on a low hill with a reflecting pool in front of it, facing west, and it is one of the great places in Madrid to watch the sun go down. Time it right and you'll be standing in front of a Nubian shrine, three thousand kilometres from where it was carved, while the sky behind it turns to gold. Of all the things this city has kept or been given, this is the most improbable — and quietly, the most romantic.
