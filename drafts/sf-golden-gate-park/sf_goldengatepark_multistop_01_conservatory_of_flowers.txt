You're standing in front of the Conservatory of Flowers — and you're looking at the oldest building in Golden Gate Park, older than the park's museums, older than most of its trees. It's a Victorian greenhouse, all white-painted wood and glass, a long central dome with two wings, sitting up on its little rise like a wedding cake.

It opened in 1879, and it has the kind of origin story this park specializes in. The whole thing started as a prefabricated kit — milled wood and thousands of panes of glass — ordered by an eccentric millionaire named James Lick, who died before he ever put it together. It sat in crates until a group of wealthy San Franciscans bought it and gave it to the new park. The design was modeled on the great Palm House at Kew Gardens in London, which is why, if you've been to Kew, this might feel like a smaller cousin a long way from home.

Step inside if you have time — it's warm, wet, and dense with tropical plants, including high-altitude orchids and water lilies with pads big enough to worry you. But here's the thing to hold onto: this building has nearly been lost more than once. It survived the 1906 earthquake, but a windstorm in 1995 shattered nearly half its glass and almost finished it off. The city raised twenty-five million dollars and spent the better part of a decade rebuilding it pane by pane.

[beat]

So this delicate-looking glass box is actually one of the toughest survivors in the park — a Victorian greenhouse that refuses to break for good.

When you're ready, follow John F. Kennedy Drive west, about a ten-minute walk, toward the Music Concourse. The first museum you'll come to, with the strange twisting copper tower, is the de Young — meet me out front.
