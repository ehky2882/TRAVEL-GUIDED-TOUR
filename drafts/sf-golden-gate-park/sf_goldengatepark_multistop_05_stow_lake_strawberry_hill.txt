And here's where the walk comes to rest: Stow Lake — though if you look at a new sign, you'll see it now goes by Blue Heron Lake. We'll come back to that name in a moment. Stand anywhere on the path that rings the water and take in the shape of the place: a doughnut of lake wrapped all the way around a single wooded island.

That island is Strawberry Hill, and it's the highest point in the entire park. The whole scene looks utterly natural, like it's always been here — and it's completely man-made. Remember the dunes from the start of the walk? This lake was dug out of that sand in 1893, partly as a reservoir to water the rest of the park, partly as a place for people to drift around in rowboats on a Sunday. You can still rent a boat at the boathouse and do exactly that.

Look across to the island and you'll spot two gifts. One is Huntington Falls, a hundred-and-ten-foot waterfall tumbling down Strawberry Hill — also entirely artificial, funded by a railroad baron and engineered to look wild. The other is the Golden Gate Pavilion, a bright Chinese pagoda given to San Francisco by its sister city, Taipei, as a gesture of friendship.

[beat]

Now, that name. The lake was named for a nineteenth-century politician named William Stow, and when the city took a harder look at his record — he was openly, aggressively antisemitic — it decided, recently, to rename the water Blue Heron Lake. You'll hear both names for a while yet. It's a small, honest example of a city deciding what it wants its public places to stand for.

And that's the park, in miniature: a forest, a lake, a waterfall, all conjured out of bare sand and handed to everyone, and still being argued over and cared for more than a century on. Find a bench. Watch the boats. You've reached the calm center of the thing McLaren built.
