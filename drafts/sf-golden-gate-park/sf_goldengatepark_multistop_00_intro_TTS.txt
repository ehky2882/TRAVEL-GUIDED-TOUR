Here's something almost nobody believes when they first hear it: every tree around you was planted on purpose, on bare sand. Before this was a park, this was dunes — windswept, shifting, treeless dunes running out to the ocean, land everyone agreed was worthless. The whole green world you're about to walk through is artificial, in the best sense of the word. Someone willed it into being.

That someone was mostly a Scottish gardener named John muhklairun, who ran this park for over fifty years and is said to have planted something like two million trees. He took a few square miles of sand and grew a forest on it — and then he filled that forest with things for everyone. Not a private estate, not a rich man's garden: a public park, free, open, democratic, with museums and gardens and lakes that anybody could walk into.

This walk takes you through the cultural heart of that idea, the cluster around the Music Concourse — the sunken, tree-lined bowl that's been the park's living room since a World's Fair came through in eighteen ninety-four. Five stops: a Victorian glass greenhouse, two great museums facing each other across the Concourse, a Japanese garden with a complicated and beautiful history, and finally a lake that started life as a reservoir.

For your feet, this is one of the gentlest walks in the city — flat, paved, about a mile and a half end to end, with the stops bunched close together in the middle. Take your time; the park rewards it.

We'll start at the east end, at the oldest thing in the whole park. Head for the Conservatory of Flowers, the big white glass greenhouse on the rise off John F Kennedy Drive, and meet me on the lawn out front.
