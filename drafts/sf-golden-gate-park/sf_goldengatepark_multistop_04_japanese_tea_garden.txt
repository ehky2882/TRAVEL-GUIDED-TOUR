You're at the gate of the Japanese Tea Garden — five acres of winding paths, ponds, stone lanterns, and clipped pines, tucked right beside the Music Concourse. It's the oldest public Japanese garden in the United States, and like a lot of this part of the park, it's a leftover from that 1894 World's Fair, which set up its whole midway right here where you're standing.

Walk in and a few things will find you. There's a drum bridge, an arc so steep you climb it more than cross it, built to make a perfect circle with its own reflection in the water. There's a five-tiered pagoda, and a great bronze Buddha, and a tea house that still serves tea the way it has for generations. Take it slowly — the garden is designed to slow you down, that's the entire point.

But the garden's heart is a family. After the fair, a Japanese gardener named Makoto Hagiwara took over the site and spent the rest of his life turning a fairground exhibit into a real garden, importing plants, birds, and stone from Japan, raising his family in a home right here on the grounds. The Hagiwaras tended this place for nearly fifty years.

[beat]

And then, in 1942, they were forced out. Like a hundred and twenty thousand other Japanese Americans during the war, the Hagiwara family was sent to an internment camp, and they were never allowed to return to the garden they had built. For a time the city even stripped the name, calling it the Oriental Tea Garden, before it was set right. It's a quiet place with a hard history, and the garden marks it honestly, which is the right thing to do.

One small, sweeter footnote, and it connects to something the rest of this city likes to claim: the modern fortune cookie was very likely first served right here, by the Hagiwara family, before it ever became a Chinatown staple. The thing San Francisco files under "Chinese" was, once again, a Japanese invention — and this exact garden is where it started.

When you're ready, leave the way you came and head west a few minutes to Stow Lake — now also called Blue Heron Lake — where this walk comes to rest on the water.
