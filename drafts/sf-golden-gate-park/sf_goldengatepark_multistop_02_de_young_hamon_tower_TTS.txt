You're at the de Young Museum now, on the north side of the Music Concourse. You can't miss it — it's the building wrapped in copper, with that nine-story tower twisting up out of one end. It's the city's main art museum, and the building itself is the first thing worth reading.

Look closely at the skin. The whole exterior is copper, perforated and embossed with a dimpled pattern, and it was designed to do something most buildings try to avoid: corrode. The architects, the Swiss firm hertsog and de moyron, wanted the copper to oxidize over time, to streak and darken and slowly turn green, so the building would settle into the green of the park and half-disappear into it. It's a museum engineered to age, on purpose.

The de Young is here at all because the old building on this spot was wrecked in the nineteen eighty-nine earthquake and had to come down. This copper replacement opened in twenty oh five.

Now, the gift, and it's a generous one. That twisting tower has an observation floor at the top, with windows on all sides and one of the best free views in San Francisco — the park below, the city beyond, the ocean to the west. You don't need a museum ticket to ride up. A lot of people never realize that.

So before you move on, consider going up: a free, quiet, glass-walled room above the treetops, in a building designed to vanish into them.
