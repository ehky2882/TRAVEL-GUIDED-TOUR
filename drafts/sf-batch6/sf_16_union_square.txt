You're standing in Union Square, the city's front room — a single block of open granite ringed by department stores and hotels, the place San Francisco does its shopping, its protesting, its ice-skating at Christmas, and a fair amount of its people-watching. Most people pass through it on the way to a store. Give it five minutes and it pays you back.

Start with the name, because it isn't about labor unions or the shape of the streets. In the eighteen-sixties, on the eve of the Civil War, this was the spot where San Franciscans held mass rallies in support of the Union — for keeping the country together. The name stuck, a small political fossil sitting in the middle of the luxury district.

Now look at the tall column in the center, with the bronze figure on top. That's the Dewey Monument, raised in 1903 to mark Admiral Dewey's naval victory at Manila Bay. It came through the 1906 earthquake standing, while much of the city around it fell. But the better story is the figure herself — Victory, holding a wreath and a trident. The sculptor needed a model, and he chose a tall young working-class woman named Alma de Bretteville, who posed for the goddess for a few dollars.

[beat]

Here's where it turns into a fairy tale. That same Alma later married Adolph Spreckels, heir to a sugar fortune, became one of the richest women in the city, and spent decades giving the money away — she's the reason the Legion of Honor museum out at Lands End exists. So the goddess of Victory on this column isn't an abstraction. She's a real San Francisco woman, modeled before she was wealthy, watching over the shopping district she'd one day help shape.

That's the trick of Union Square. It looks like pure commerce — flagship stores, glossy windows, somebody always handing out samples. But the granite under your feet remembers Civil War rallies, the column remembers a war most people have forgotten, and the woman on top climbed from a few-dollar modeling job to one of the great fortunes in the city. Stay on a bench a while. The square is busier with history than it lets on.
