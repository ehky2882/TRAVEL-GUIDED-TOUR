You're on Waverly Place, a two-block lane running parallel to Grant, and the trick to this street is simple: don't look at the shops. Look up.

They call this the Street of Painted Balconies, and once you raise your eyes you'll see why. The upper floors are banded in color, deep reds, golds, greens, jade blues, tier on tier of carved and painted balconies climbing three and four stories over your head. It's the most beautiful street in Chinatown, and almost all of its beauty is above eye level, which is the whole point.

Here's why it's up there. When Chinatown rebuilt after the nineteen oh six fire, the neighborhood was hemmed in, boxed by hostile city politics, denied room to spread out. So it grew the only direction left: up. The ground floors went to shops and restaurants, the rent-payers. But the soul of the community, the family associations, the social clubs, and above all the temples, climbed to the top floors, where the air was cleaner and the spirits, by tradition, belong closer to the sky.

Find number one-twenty-five. On its top floor sits the Tin How Temple, founded in eighteen fifty-two, generally called the oldest Chinese temple in the United States. It's dedicated to Tin How, the goddess who protects sailors and travelers, which makes sense for a community that crossed an ocean to get here. To reach it you climb several narrow flights past the association offices, and you emerge into a small, red, incense-thick room hung with hundreds of gold lanterns, suspended right over the street you were just standing on.

And that's the thing worth carrying with you down the block. The Chinatown most visitors photograph is the ground-floor one, the lanterns and souvenirs along Grant. But the real one was built upward, deliberately, a vertical neighborhood stacked floor by floor because that was the only room it was allowed. The temples on the top floors aren't hidden by accident. They're up there on purpose, and the only way to find them is to stop, on a lane like this one, and look up.
