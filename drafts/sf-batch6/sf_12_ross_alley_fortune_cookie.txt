Step into Ross Alley and the noise of Grant Avenue drops away. It's narrow, a single block, brick on both sides — one of the oldest alleys in the city, and for a long stretch of its life one of the most notorious, lined with gambling dens and worse. Today it's quiet, photogenic, a favorite of film crews. But halfway down you'll find a small open doorway, and the smell coming out of it — warm, sweet, vanilla — is the reason most people come.

This is the Golden Gate Fortune Cookie Factory, and inside, women sit at small rotating griddles folding fortune cookies by hand, one at a time, the way it's been done here for decades. Watch for a minute. A flat, hot disc comes off the iron, a paper slip goes in, and three quick folds turn it into the shape you know. It's mesmerizing, and it's a good place to buy a warm bag straight off the griddle.

Now, the cookie itself — here's the part worth knowing. The fortune cookie is one of the most "Chinese" things in the American imagination, and it isn't Chinese at all. You won't find it in China; people there often find it baffling. Its real roots trace to Japan — a grilled cracker with a paper fortune tucked inside — brought to California by Japanese immigrants around the turn of the last century, including, by the most common account, the family that ran the Japanese Tea Garden across town in Golden Gate Park.

[beat]

San Francisco claims it invented the fortune cookie. So does Los Angeles, loudly, and the two cities have genuinely feuded over it — there was even a mock trial. The honest answer is that nobody can fully prove it, so treat the claim as exactly that: a claim, and a very San Francisco one.

Which is the real story of this little cookie. The thing the whole world reads as ancient Chinese wisdom is actually an immigrant invention — Japanese in origin, Chinese-American by adoption, born somewhere in California — that became a cliché before anyone thought to ask where it came from. Crack one open before you leave. The fortune won't be true. The history behind the cookie is the better one anyway.
