Stand back far enough to take in the whole shape, the white spire narrowing to a point above the Financial District. You know this building even if you've never been to San Francisco. It's on the postcards, the souvenir mugs, the logo of half the local businesses in town. The Transamerica Pyramid is the city's signature, its most recognizable silhouette. And when it was proposed, people despised it.

Go back to nineteen sixty-nine. San Francisco was a city of flat-topped towers and bay windows, and the architect William puhrairuh handed it a four-sided pyramid, eight hundred and fifty-three feet of it, tapering to a hollow spire. Critics called it an insult to the skyline. There were protests. The nicknames were unprintable. A building that's now beloved arrived as something close to a civic scandal.

It opened in nineteen seventy-two and held the title of the city's tallest for more than forty years. The shape, it turned out, was practical, the taper lets more light reach the streets below than a boxy tower would, and as it narrows the upper floors get smaller, ending in that pointed crown that's purely there to be looked at. There's nothing functional in the top spire at all. It exists to give the building its profile.

Now walk to the base, on the Washington Street side, into Redwood Park. Here's the surprise the skyline doesn't tell you: at the foot of this tower is a small grove of coast redwoods, transplanted from the Santa Cruz Mountains, growing in the middle of the Financial District. A twenty twenty-four renovation reopened the grove and the lobby to the public, so you can sit under real redwoods with the glass canyons of downtown rising all around you.

And that's the quiet lesson of this place. The most distinctive thing on the San Francisco skyline is the one the city fought hardest to keep out. People wanted it gone; now they can't imagine the view without it. Tastes change, and the buildings we're sure we hate have a way of becoming the ones we put on the postcard, a useful thing to remember the next time something new goes up and everyone tells you it ruins everything.
