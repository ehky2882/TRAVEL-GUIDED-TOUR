This elegant building, all clean classical lines, looks calm and a little plain beside the government piles around it. Hold that in mind, because something terrible happened just outside it.

First, what it is. The Banqueting House is the only significant survivor of that vast lost Palace of Whitehall — everything else burned, this remained. It was designed by innigo Jones and finished in sixteen twenty-two, and at the time it was revolutionary: the first proper classical building in England, calm and mathematical, when everyone around was still building in a tangle of Tudor brick. Go inside and look up — the ceiling is covered with nine huge paintings by roobenz, commissioned by King Charles the First, glorifying his father James and the sacred, God-given right of kings to rule.

Now the terrible part. On a freezing January day in sixteen forty-nine, after a civil war he had lost, Charles the First walked through that very room — beneath his own father's glorified ceiling — and out onto a scaffold built against the wall outside, where he was executed in front of a crowd. A reigning king, put to death by his own Parliament. It had never happened in England before.

Sit with the awful symmetry of it: the building raised to celebrate the divine right of kings became the stage on which the country declared that kings could be held to account like anyone else. Whether that was justice or murder, people argued then and argue still. Either way, it's one of the hinge-points of British history, and it happened on the pavement you're standing on.
