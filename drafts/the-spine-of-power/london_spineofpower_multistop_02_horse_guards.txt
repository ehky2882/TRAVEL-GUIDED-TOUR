About halfway down on your right you'll come to a handsome stone building with an archway through the middle — and, almost certainly, a mounted soldier sitting perfectly still on a gleaming horse, being photographed by absolutely everyone.

This is Horse Guards. The motionless riders are real working cavalry — the Household Cavalry, the monarch's mounted bodyguard — and standing sentry here is one of their duties, in full ceremonial dress, swords drawn, horses trained to ignore the chaos of tourists around them. Spare them a thought; it's harder than it looks.

But the building is more than a photo opportunity. That archway is, officially, the entrance to the royal palaces. Pass through it and you're technically crossing into the precinct of the sovereign — historically, only the monarch could drive a carriage through the central arch. Behind it opens a huge gravel parade ground, Horse Guards Parade, and that's where one of the great set-pieces of British ceremony happens each summer: Trooping the Colour, the monarch's official birthday parade, hundreds of soldiers wheeling in formation.

*[beat]*

What you're really looking at is power dressed up as theatre. The cavalry here haven't fought from horseback in a very long time; the swords and the plumes and the mirror-polished boots are a performance — but a deliberate one, the state making itself visible and magnificent in a way that words can't. Watch the changing of the guard if your timing's lucky. Then look across the road, because directly opposite stands the one building that survived the lost palace — and the place a king once walked to his death. Cross to the Banqueting House.
