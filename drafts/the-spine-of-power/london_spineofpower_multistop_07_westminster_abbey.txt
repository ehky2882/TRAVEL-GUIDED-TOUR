Here's the last stop, and the oldest power of all. Westminster Abbey doesn't govern anything and never has — and yet no British monarch is truly a monarch until they've been inside it.

This is the coronation church. For nearly a thousand years — since 1066, since William the Conqueror — every crowning of an English and then British sovereign has happened here, in front of that high altar. The coronation chair, a battered wooden throne built around seven hundred years ago, still sits inside, and every monarch in living memory has been crowned in it. This is where raw power becomes sacred power, where a person becomes a king or queen.

Look up at the west towers as you face the front — those two pale Gothic towers framing the entrance. Here's a quiet connection: they were designed by Nicholas Hawksmoor, the same strange genius whose churches haunt the rest of London. Even the coronation church carries his fingerprints.

Inside lie the dead who mattered: kings and queens, but also the Tomb of the Unknown Warrior, an unidentified soldier of the First World War buried among the monarchs, and Poets' Corner, where the great writers rest.

*[beat]*

So that's the spine, end to end. You started at a victory at sea and walked down through the army, a dead king, the Prime Minister's door, the war dead, and the people's square — and you finish here, at the church that turns power into something holy. Half a mile of street, and the whole anatomy of how this country is ruled, remembered, and crowned. Go inside if you can. Then stand a moment by the great west door and look back up Whitehall the way you came. You've just walked the spine of Britain.
