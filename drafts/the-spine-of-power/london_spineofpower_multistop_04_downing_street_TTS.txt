A little further down, on the right, you'll find a set of tall black iron gates with armed police behind them and a crowd peering through. Look past them, down the side street, and you'll see it: a plain brick terraced house with a glossy black door and a single number on it. Ten.

That's it. Number Ten Downing Street, the home and office of the British Prime Minister — arguably the most powerful address in the country — and it looks like a slightly grand family house on an ordinary Georgian street. That modesty is the whole character of the place. There's no palace, no dome, no grand portico; the leader of the government works behind a front door you could walk straight past, if it weren't for the gates and the guns.

It's been the Prime Minister's residence since seventeen thirty-five, when King George the Second gave the house to Robert wawlpole, the man we now call the first Prime Minister. And behind that single famous door, the building sprawls backwards into a warren of connected houses — far bigger inside than out, another very British piece of understatement.

You can't walk down the street any more — the gates went up in nineteen eighty-nine, for security — so this is as close as anyone gets. But that's almost fitting. Real power in Britain has always rather preferred not to show off; it sits behind a plain door on a side street and lets the palaces and monuments do the shouting. Take your look through the railings, then rejoin Whitehall, because just ahead, alone in the middle of the road, stands the most solemn object on the whole street. Walk down to the Cenotaph.
