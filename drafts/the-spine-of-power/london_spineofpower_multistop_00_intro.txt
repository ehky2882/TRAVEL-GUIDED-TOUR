You're about to walk down one short, wide street — and by the end of it you'll have passed through every kind of power this country has ever had.

The street is Whitehall, and it runs barely half a mile, from Trafalgar Square at the top to Parliament Square at the bottom. But this half-mile is the spine of Britain. The word "Whitehall" is shorthand for the entire government — when people say a decision was made "in Whitehall," they mean here, on this street, where the great ministries line up shoulder to shoulder.

It's named for a ghost. A vast royal palace, the Palace of Whitehall, once sprawled across all of this — the largest palace in Europe, fifteen hundred rooms, home to the kings and queens of England for a century and a half, until it burned to the ground in 1698. Almost nothing of it survives. But the street kept its name, and power never left.

*[beat]*

What makes this one worth doing on foot is that the power here isn't one thing — it's stacked, building by building. In the next fifteen minutes you'll pass the navy's great victory monument, the gate the cavalry still guards on horseback, the hall where a king was put to death by his own people, the most famous front door in the country, the nation's memorial to its war dead, the square where governments get shouted at, and finally the church where every monarch for a thousand years has been crowned. Royal power, military power, the power to govern, the power to mourn, the power to consecrate — all on one street.

Start at the top, in the open space at the north end. This is Trafalgar Square.
