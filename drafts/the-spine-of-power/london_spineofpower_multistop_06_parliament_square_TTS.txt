You've reached the bottom of Whitehall, and the street opens into a green square ringed with statues, the Houses of Parliament and the great clock tower rising on the far side. This is Parliament Square, and it's where British power gets argued about out loud.

Walk the edge of the green and read the statues, because they're a deliberate gathering. For a long time they were all statesmen — Churchill, glowering, leaning on his stick, nearest Parliament; prime ministers and generals around the rest. But look closer and the cast has been widening: there's Gandhi, who fought British rule; Nelson Mandela; Abraham Lincoln; and Millicent Fawcett, the campaigner for women's votes — the first woman ever honoured with a statue in this square, and a fairly recent arrival. The line-up keeps shifting as the country argues about whom it wants to remember.

That arguing isn't only in bronze. This square is the traditional home of protest in Britain — if there's a demonstration in London, it very often ends up here, in the shadow of the Parliament it's addressing. Some of the statues themselves have been fought over, defaced and defended, as the country wrestles with its own history.

So this is the noisy, unfinished end of the spine — the place where power doesn't sit behind a black door or stand silent in the road, but gets shouted at, voted on, marched against, and rethought. It's the most democratic spot on the whole walk, precisely because it's never settled. Take it in, then turn to the great church on the western side, because there's one more kind of power to see — the oldest of all. Cross to Westminster Abbey.
