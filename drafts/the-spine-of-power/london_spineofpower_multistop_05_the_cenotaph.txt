In the middle of the road ahead — traffic flowing around it on both sides — stands a tall, pale, austere stone monument with no statue, no cross, no obvious decoration. Stop here, because this is the most important object on Whitehall, and it asks for a moment of quiet.

This is the Cenotaph, the United Kingdom's national memorial to its war dead, designed by Edwin Lutyens and unveiled in 1920, after the First World War. The word itself means "empty tomb" — there is no one buried here. It stands for everyone: the hundreds of thousands who never came home, buried far away or never found at all. Every November the nation gathers around this spot, and the monarch lays the first wreath.

Look at how plain it is, and understand that the plainness is deliberate. Lutyens stripped away almost all religious symbolism on purpose, so that it could honour the dead of every faith and none — Christian, Jewish, Muslim, Hindu, all the men of a global empire who died together.

*[beat]*

And here's the hidden craft. It looks like a simple stack of straight blocks, but there isn't a straight line in it. Every surface is very subtly curved — the sides, extended upward, would meet at a point a thousand feet in the air, and the flat-looking horizontals are actually sections of an enormous sphere. You can't consciously see it, but you feel it: a stillness, a rightness, that a box of plain straight edges would never have. After the noise of everything else on this street, the Cenotaph is where power falls silent and counts the cost. Stand a moment. Then walk on towards the towers ahead — we're nearly at Parliament Square.
