The tower ahead of you is thirteen stories of stacked white curves with a needle rising off the roof, and since the day it opened in nineteen fifty six, people have said the same thing: it looks like a stack of records on a turntable spindle. Here's the fun part — the architects always insisted that was a coincidence. The design, from the office of wel tun bek it, was pitched as pure practicality: a circular floor plan gave every office the same light and spared anyone fighting over corner suites. Believe whichever version you like; Los Angeles has never let the truth spoil a good silhouette. It's often called the first circular office building in the world, and it became shorthand for the whole industry it housed more or less overnight.

Capitol Records moved in at the label's peak — Frank Sinatra, Nat King Cole, whose records so bankrolled the place that it's long been called the house that Nat built — and the real marvel is below the sidewalk: echo chambers sunk thirty feet underground, concrete bunkers designed with input from the guitarist and inventor Les Paul, producing a reverb so distinctive that artists still book time here just to use them.

Now look at the needle on top and wait for dark, or take it on faith: the beacon at its tip has been blinking the word Hollywood in Morse code since opening night, when Samuel Morse's granddaughter threw the switch.

And that's the walk. A sidewalk that sells stardom by nomination and invoice, a forecourt of handprints, a room that crowns winners, the palace where the premiere itself was invented — and here at the end of the assembly line, a tower that does nothing but signal the town's name to the sky, over and over, in a code almost nobody left can read. Fame, manufactured block by block, and shipped worldwide. Stay a minute and watch for the blink. It never stops.
