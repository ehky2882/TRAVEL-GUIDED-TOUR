The courtyard is the tell. Most theaters keep their lobby indoors; the Egyptian starts working on you from the sidewalk — a long forecourt lined with palms and heavy columns, hieroglyphs on the walls, a stage-set facade drawing you toward doors fit for a pharaoh's tomb. Sid Grauman built it in 1922, five years before his Chinese Theatre up the block, and it opened just weeks before archaeologists found King Tut's tomb — Egyptomania was already raging, and the discovery made Grauman's timing look, as usual, prophetic.

What happened here on opening night in October 1922 is the reason this walk exists. For the premiere of Robin Hood, starring Douglas Fairbanks, Grauman staged a movie's opening as a public spectacle for the first time — searchlights, crowds behind ropes, ushers in costume, guards pacing the rooftop calling out the minutes to showtime, and a red carpet to the doors, an idea he's said to have borrowed from the VIP carriage of a train. Every premiere since — every arrival, every velvet rope, every step-and-repeat — is a revival of one night at this address. The Chinese draws the crowds; this is the prototype.

The theater has needed rescuing twice: once in the nineties, after earthquake damage, when a nonprofit film organization bought it from the city for a single dollar and restored it; and again in the twenty-twenties, when a streaming company bought it and spent a fortune returning it to its 1922 grandeur. Read that however you like — the format accused of killing moviegoing now owns and maintains its temple, and classic films still run here on real film, some of it rare nitrate stock.
