The forecourt is free — walk in. Set into the concrete around you are the handprints, footprints, and signatures of roughly two hundred stars, from Mary Pickford and Douglas Fairbanks in 1927 to whoever knelt here most recently. This is the industry's oldest souvenir: proof, pressed into cement, that the gods were physically here.

The theater behind it opened in May 1927, the second great palace built by the showman Sid Grauman — an exotic fantasy of pagoda roof and temple bells, with artifacts genuinely imported from China. How the handprint tradition started is disputed, and the most popular version is a legend, so take it as one: the story goes that an actress stepped into wet cement by accident during construction, and Grauman — a man incapable of wasting a gimmick — saw the future on the spot. However it truly began, it worked. The forecourt made the Chinese the most famous movie theater in the world, and it's still a working cinema, with premieres most weeks and one of the largest IMAX screens in the business inside.

Take your time matching your hands to theirs — everyone does it, and that's exactly what it's for. Note who had small hands, who signed in character, who added a wisecrack.

And clock one small modern irony before you go: the name. It's officially the TCL Chinese Theatre now, because a Chinese electronics company bought the naming rights — the theater built as an American fantasy of China is finally, in a sense, actually Chinese.

Next door to the east you'll see a grand archway with a broad staircase climbing away from the boulevard — that's the entrance to the Dolby Theatre, and it's where the industry crowns itself. The stars on the ground, the handprints, and now the room where they hand out the Oscars: the factory tour continues.
