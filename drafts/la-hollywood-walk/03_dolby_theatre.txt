Climb the staircase off the boulevard — the entryway is public even when the theater is dark — and read the columns as you go. Each one carries a year and the name of a Best Picture winner, running back to the very first ceremony in 1929, and near the top you'll find columns left deliberately blank, waiting for winners that haven't been chosen yet. It's the most quietly confident piece of set dressing in Hollywood: a building announcing that it intends to matter for decades.

This is the Dolby Theatre, opened in 2001 and home of the Oscars since 2002 — a theater essentially purpose-built for one night a year, tucked, in the most Los Angeles arrangement possible, inside a shopping mall. On ceremony night the complex closes, the red carpet rolls out across roughly where you're standing, and around thirty-four hundred guests file into an auditorium engineered for television — the seating drawn tight to the stage so the cutaways feel intimate, the acoustics re-tuned by the sound company the building was renamed for in 2012.

And that's the detail worth keeping: the arrivals, the carpet, the camera angles are all staged so that you never see the mall. Hollywood's biggest night happens next to a food court, and the whole apparatus exists to make sure the world never notices.

Back down on the boulevard, continue east and cross to the south side of the street. A block or so along, look for a courtyard set back behind palm trees at number sixty-seven twelve — easy to walk right past, which would be a mistake. That's the Egyptian Theatre, 1922, and it's where everything you've seen so far on this walk was invented.
