Look down. Coral-pink terrazzo, brass rims, a name in every star — and they keep going like this, block after block, for more than a mile. Around twenty-eight hundred stars so far, in five categories: motion pictures, television, music, radio, and live performance, each marked with a small brass emblem beneath the name. Walk east and read as you go; the Walk of Fame is the only monument in the world you're supposed to step on.

Here's how a star actually happens, because almost nobody has it right. You can't buy one for yourself — but you can't exactly earn one on merit alone, either. Anyone can nominate a celebrity, the honoree has to agree, a committee selects around thirty a year from a few hundred nominations, and then somebody — a studio, a record label, a fan club — pays a sponsorship fee running to tens of thousands of dollars, which covers the star and helps maintain the Walk. The honoree is even required to show up to the unveiling. Fame here is nominated, vetted, scheduled, and invoiced.

And that's not a scandal — that's the point. The Walk was dreamed up in the late fifties by local businessmen to glamorize the boulevard, and the first permanent stars went down in 1960. It has never pretended to be anything but promotion. It's just promotion so committed, so literal — your name in brass, in the sidewalk, indefinitely — that it became genuinely beloved anyway, which may be the most Hollywood outcome imaginable.

[beat]

Keep walking east, reading names as you go, and after a few blocks you'll see the crowds pooling ahead outside a jade-green pagoda of a building. That's the Chinese Theatre — 1927 — where fame stopped being something you read on the ground and became something you could put your hands in.
