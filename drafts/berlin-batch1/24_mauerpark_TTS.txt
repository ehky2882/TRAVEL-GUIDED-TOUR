On Sundays, this park sings. We'll get to that.

You should be in mow er park, the long green slope between prents low er bairg and vay ding, with a graffiti-covered stretch of wall running along the ridge at the top, beside the stadium.

Walk up the slope to that wall. The park's name translates, bluntly, as Wall Park, and the ground you're crossing is the reason: this whole green space was the border strip. The concrete on the ridge is a surviving piece of the hinterland wall — the inner barrier, the one that faced East — and it now has the distinction of being the only stretch of the Berlin Wall the city invites you to paint. It's a legal graffiti canvas, repainted so constantly that whatever you're looking at is probably younger than your visit to this city. Elsewhere, the Wall survives as a double line of cobblestones underfoot; here it survives as the shape of the park itself. The emptiness is the monument.

Now, the singing. On Sundays this modest park becomes one of the great gathering places in Europe: a flea market of hundreds of stalls fills the western edge — old cameras, vinyl, Soviet surplus, questionable jackets — and from spring to autumn, weather willing, the little stone amphitheatre hosts an open-air karaoke where thousands of strangers cheer other strangers through their bravest three minutes. Nobody official organised this. A man turned up with a mobile karaoke rig, kept turning up, and a tradition assembled itself around him.

That's the thing to understand about Berlin: its genius is for not finishing things. Leave a border strip alone long enough and the city will fill it with songs and second-hand jackets. The park itself was won this way — citizens fought off development plans for decades until the full green was secured.

A short walk south, at bair now er shtrah suh, the state built the formal memorial to this border, and it is right that it exists. This is the other end of the same scar — the end the people got. One scar, two answers: solemn remembrance there, a crowd singing here. Berlin needs both, and honestly, so do you. If it's Sunday, go find the amphitheatre. You don't have to sing. Almost everyone doesn't, together.
