The most harmonious square in Berlin was built by refugees — and finished by a king with a taste for Roman skylines.

You should be at the centre of zhahn dar men markt, near the white marble statue of the poet free drish shil ler. Around you, a matched set: a concert house with a broad staircase to the west, and two nearly identical domed buildings facing each other across the square — the French Cathedral on your right as you face the concert house, the German Cathedral on your left. The stone underfoot is brand new, by the way — the whole square was relaid and reopened last year, new stones in the old pattern.

Take the French side first, because it explains everything. In sixteen eighty-five Prussia opened its doors to the hew guh nots — French Protestants fleeing persecution — and tens of thousands came north. Within a generation, as many as one in five Berliners spoke French. They brought their trades, their schools, and their church, which they built here on this square; the German congregation raised its own church facing it, like a polite reply.

Now the square's best secret: neither dome belongs to a church. A century after the churches went up, Frederick the Great had two grand domed towers built beside them — no altar, no congregation, no purpose beyond making the square look like a piazza in Rome. The "cathedrals" of zhahn dar men markt are stage scenery. Magnificent stage scenery — but scenery.

Between them stands the concert house, finished in eighteen twenty-one by karl free drish shin kel — the same hand behind half of what is graceful in this city. It rose on the ruins of a theatre that had burned down; he kept the surviving columns of the old portico and built the new hall around them.

So read the ensemble the way the city does: a refugee church, its German twin, two ornamental domes ordered purely for effect, and a concert hall raised out of a fire. Berlin's beauty, when it happens, tends to happen like this — imported, improvised, welcomed in, and then polished until it looks inevitable.

Stay by shil ler a moment. In the evening the domes go gold, and in summer the music drifts out of the hall onto the square. For a city famous for its scars, it's worth knowing it can do this too.
