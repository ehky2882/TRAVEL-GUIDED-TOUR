Five museums, one island, a hundred years of ambition — and, at the moment, a very honest amount of scaffolding.

You should be standing in the Lustgarten, the big rectangular lawn at the heart of Museum Island. Ahead of you, a long line of columns — that's the Altes Museum. To your right, the heavy green-domed bulk of the Berlin Cathedral. Behind you, across the road, the reconstructed palace. The Spree wraps around all of it; you're on an island, and the island belongs to art.

Begin with the colonnade. Eighteen columns, opened in 1830 — one of the first museums in Europe built for the public rather than for a court, designed by Karl Friedrich Schinkel, Prussia's house architect, whose fingerprints are all over this city. On the lawn in front of it sits a polished granite bowl nearly seven metres across, cut and shined from a single boulder left behind by a glacier. It was meant for the museum's rotunda and turned out too big for the door. Berliners adored it anyway — they called it a wonder of the world and left it outside, where it has sat ever since.

Behind the Altes Museum, four more museums rose over the following century, the last of them, the Pergamon, finished in 1930. The state kept changing around them — kingdom, empire, republic, dictatorship — and the museums kept coming. Today the whole ensemble is World Heritage. And here's the honest part: the island has been under repair, in one corner or another, for most of living memory. The Pergamon is closed for renovation until 2027, its famous altar out of sight, and the cranes move from building to building like gardeners.

The cathedral on your right is the loudest thing here — imperial swagger from 1905, the Hohenzollern family church, with the dynasty's crypt beneath it, freshly reopened after years of restoration.

Prussia declared this island a sanctuary for art and learning, and Berlin has treated it like a cathedral ever since — never quite finished, permanently tended, handed from one Germany to the next like a duty nobody questions.

Climb a few steps toward the colonnade and turn around: lawn, dome, palace, river. The island is doing what it was built for — giving the city somewhere to stand among beautiful things.
