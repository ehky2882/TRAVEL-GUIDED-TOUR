Cabaret — the musical, the film, the bowler hat — was set about three streets from where you're standing.

You should be on nol len dorf plats, facing the oo bahn station: a grand old building with a dome on top and elevated tracks curving in behind it. Four oo bahn lines meet here, which tells you this square was always a crossroads. The streets running off it to the south — mots shtrah suh, nol len dorf shtrah suh — are the heart of what Berliners call the ray gen boh gen keets. The rainbow neighbourhood. It has held that role, with one terrible interruption, for more than a century.

Find nol len dorf shtrah suh seventeen, a few minutes from the square, and look for the modest plaque beside the door. Christopher Isherwood lived here — a young English writer who arrived in nineteen twenty nine and rented a room from a landlady named may tah too row. His flatmate was a cabaret singer named Jean Ross. He turned the landlady into froy line shroh der, the flatmate into Sally Bowles, and the whole glittering, doomed city into the Berlin Stories — which became a play, then a musical, then the film that fixed vy mar Berlin in the world's imagination.

By the early nineteen thirties this keets held well over a hundred bars, clubs and cafés where gay and lesbian Berlin lived openly — an openness almost nowhere else on earth could match. The most famous, the el doh rah doh on mots shtrah suh, is now a supermarket that still wears the old sign above its door. Soon after taking power, the Nazis seized it, turned it into a party office, and set about closing this world down. Isherwood left in nineteen thirty three.

Now walk back to the station and find the triangle of pink stone set into its outer wall. It takes the badge that homosexual prisoners were forced to wear in the camps and turns it into remembrance — a dedication to the homosexual victims of National Socialism, killed and then, for decades, not even spoken of. It was among the first memorials of its kind anywhere, and there are usually flowers beneath it.

Here is what this square has to say: the life the nineteen thirties tried to erase came back — to the same streets, the same addresses, the same station. At night the dome above you is lit in rainbow colours. This isn't a museum of what was lost. It's the thing itself, still going. Stay into the evening if you can; the keets is at its best after dark.
