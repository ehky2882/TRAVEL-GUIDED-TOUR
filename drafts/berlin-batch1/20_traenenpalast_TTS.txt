Berliners named this little glass pavilion the Palace of Tears, and the name was not a joke.

You should be beside free drish shtrah suh station, on the river side, looking at a low pavilion of blue-tinted glass and steel — nineteen-sixties modern, and oddly elegant for what it was built to do.

First, understand the station behind it, because free drish shtrah suh was the strangest building in divided Berlin. The station stood entirely in the East, yet Western trains kept running through it, so under one roof the two halves of the city touched without ever meeting — platforms sealed off from each other, corridors walled and watched, a border crossing buried inside a working railway station. West Berliners could pass through daily and never set foot in the country they were technically inside.

This hall went up in nineteen sixty-two as the departure side of that crossing. Western visitors left through here after seeing family in the East, and the rare East German with an exit permit left through here for good — which is why East Berliners crowded this doorstep to say goodbye to a sister, a son, a grandmother, with no way of knowing when the state would allow a next visit. The building earned its name on this ground, one farewell at a time.

Go in when you have half an hour; it's free, and closed Mondays. The original passport-control booths still stand inside, and you can walk through one — step in, step through, step out. It takes a few seconds now, and the whole exhibition exists to explain why it once took hours, and nerve. The story is told through what people carried: suitcases, letters, smuggled photographs — the border as people lived it, not as the state drew it.

The Wall gets the photographs, but for most people the border was never concrete. It was paperwork — a form, a wait, a stamp, a uniformed man deciding — and this hall is where that machinery ran at its politest, which did not make it gentle. Heartbreak, administered under fluorescent light. When you leave, you'll use the same doors, and it will cost you nothing. That's the measure of the distance travelled.
