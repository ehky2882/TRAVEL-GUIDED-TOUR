You should be across the street from the noy uh zeen ah goh guh: a tall front of striped and patterned brick, two slender towers, and between them the great ribbed dome, gilded so thoroughly it seems lit from inside.

Start at street level. When this synagogue opened in eighteen sixty-six, it was the largest in Germany — more than three thousand seats — and it was built to be seen. Berlin's Jewish community put the newest engineering of the age, an iron frame, inside Moorish-style splendour on one of the city's finest streets, and the message was plain: we are of this city, and we are staying.

Then November nineteen thirty-eight, the night the synagogues burned. This one was set alight with the rest, but the district police officer, vil helm krewts felt, faced the arsonists down — he cited the building's protected status and had the fire brigade come and put the fire out. He is remembered by name because so few in his position did anything at all. What his stand saved, war later took: bombing gutted the hall in nineteen forty-three, and in nineteen fifty-eight East Berlin demolished the ruined sanctuary and left the front standing like a mask.

The rebuilding, between nineteen eighty-eight and nineteen ninety-five, made a careful choice. The front section and the dome were restored exactly; the great hall was not. Walk past the entrance and you can see it — behind the restored facade lies open ground, with the outline of the vanished sanctuary traced where it stood. Inside, a museum tells the story, closed Saturdays as a Jewish house should be, and a congregation prays here again. This is not only a monument; it is back in use.

Most restorations try to make loss invisible. This one keeps the loss in the plan: what stands is real, what was destroyed stays legibly absent, and the building tells you the truth from the street. Stay for the light if it's late in the day — the dome was rebuilt to be visible across the rooftops, and in the low sun it does exactly what it was always meant to do.
