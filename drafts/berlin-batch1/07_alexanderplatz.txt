You can see this tower from almost anywhere in Berlin. The one place you can't quite see it is here, at its feet — it's straight overhead.

You should be on Alexanderplatz, the wide, windy square east of the river: trams sliding through, the steel sphere of the television tower catching the light above you, and, these days, a skyline's worth of construction cranes. Find the World Clock — a metal rotunda on a column, ringed with the names of cities — and stand near it.

Start with the tower. The Fernsehturm went up between 1965 and 1969: three hundred and sixty-eight metres, still the tallest structure in Germany. The GDR built it to broadcast television, but mostly to be seen — from the West, specifically. When it was finished, almost nothing on earth stood taller. West Berlin got the message, and — the story goes — a joke in return: when sunlight hits the sphere, the reflection forms a bright cross. They called it the Pope's Revenge. It's a Cold War legend, polished by decades of retelling — but stand here on a sunny day and the cross really does appear.

Now the clock. The World Clock has told the time in more than a hundred cities since 1969, and it has been the meeting spot ever since — "under the World Clock" needs no further explanation in this city. People arranged first dates here under socialism, and they still do. That's the register this square deserves: not a monument to a regime, but the downtown where East Berliners went to the cinema, bought furniture, met their friends — ordinary lives, lived in full, whatever the system around them.

The square itself was rebuilt in the 1960s as a showcase of socialist modernity — vast, concrete, unapologetic. Berlin has been planning to tame it since reunification, and for thirty years almost nothing happened. Now the cranes have finally arrived, and new towers are climbing around the square — every one of them kept well below the television tower. Whatever Berlin becomes next, nothing is allowed to look down on this thing.

Look up one more time before you go — the sphere, the needle, the red and white. That's Berlin's compass. Wherever you wander in this city, it will find you over the rooftops and quietly point you back here.
