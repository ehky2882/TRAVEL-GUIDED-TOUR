Very few parliaments keep the graffiti of the army that conquered them. This one does — on purpose.

You should be on the wide lawn of plats dair reh poo bleek, looking at the rysh tahg's west front: a heavy grey nineteenth-century facade, four corner towers, columns at the centre, and a glass dome floating above the roofline like something from a different building entirely. Which, in a sense, it is.

Read the inscription over the columns first. daym doy chen fol kuh — "to the German people." The letters were cast in nineteen sixteen from two French cannons captured a century earlier, and the Kaiser resisted the dedication for years, because a building that belonged to the people rather implied the country didn't belong entirely to him. He lost that argument, then the war, then the throne.

The building's twentieth century reads like the country's. The fire in nineteen thirty-three that the Nazis used as their pretext for seizing emergency powers. The Soviet flag raised over the shattered roof in nineteen forty-five, in one of the most famous photographs of the war. Four decades standing half-used beside the Wall, a parliament building with no parliament. Then, in the summer of nineteen ninety-five, the artists kris toh and zhahn clohd wrapped the whole thing in silver fabric for two weeks, and five million people came to look — a farewell to the old building before the rebuild began.

Now the dome. Norman Foster's glass crown, finished in nineteen ninety-nine, with two spiral ramps climbing its inner walls. Anyone can go up — it's free, though the boon dess tahg asks you to register ahead — and from the top you look straight down through glass into the chamber where parliament sits. The arrangement is the argument: the public, literally above the politicians, watching them work. And inside, on walls the renovation deliberately left bare, the charcoal scrawls of Soviet soldiers from nineteen forty-five are preserved in the corridors of a working legislature.

Most countries rebuild their parliaments to look invincible. Germany rebuilt this one to be seen through — a building that keeps its scars where members of parliament pass them every day, in case anyone forgets what happens when this house fails.

Stay on the lawn a moment and watch the dome. On a clear evening it glows over the trees like a lantern left on.
