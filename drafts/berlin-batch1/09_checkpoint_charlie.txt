Almost nothing you can see here is original. Don't let that send you away — the fakery is part of the story.

You should be on Friedrichstrasse where it crosses Zimmerstrasse, looking at a small white guardhouse in the middle of the street, sandbags stacked around it and a flagpole above. Near it, a sign announces in four languages that you are leaving the American sector. The guardhouse is a replica — the original was lifted away by crane in 1990 and lives in a museum across town — and the sign is a copy too.

Find the corner of Zimmerstrasse first, and look at the ground. A doubled seam of cobblestones crosses the road and slips away between the buildings — the Wall's old route, still holding its line through the city whether anyone builds over it or not.

Now the name. The Allies crossed into East Germany and East Berlin at three points and named them from the radio alphabet: Alpha on the western border, Bravo at the edge of the city, and Charlie here — the third checkpoint, and the only one anyone remembers, because this was the crossing for diplomats, journalists and foreigners, which made it the crossing for cameras. In October 1961, American and Soviet tanks faced each other across this street with engines running and live rounds loaded, for the better part of a day and a night — as close as the Cold War ever came to turning hot in Berlin.

Look at what surrounds the guardhouse now: souvenir stands, fast food, and two big open lots with an exhibition strung along their edges. For thirty-five years the city couldn't decide what this corner should be — memorial or attraction — and so it became both, awkwardly. Some call that a scandal and some call it honest, and there's a case for each; the city has finally bought the empty lots and is choosing a design, so in a few years this corner may look very different.

[beat]

Here's the thing, though. Checkpoint Charlie was always theatre — chosen to be seen, photographed, filmed, the Cold War's front-of-house. The crowds with their cameras aren't spoiling the site. They are, in a strange way, its continuity.
