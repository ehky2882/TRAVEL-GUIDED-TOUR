This is the Berlin that history spared — barely.

You should be in the forecourt of shar lot ten boorg Palace, inside the wrought-iron gates, with the long baroque front stretching away on either side and the green dome rising in the centre, a gilded figure of Fortune turning in the wind on top.

Start with the horseman in the middle of the courtyard. That bronze — the Great Elector on horseback, by ahn dray ahs shlew ter — is one of the finest baroque equestrian statues anywhere, and it isn't where it belongs. It stood for two centuries on a bridge in the old city centre; during the war it was loaded onto a barge for safekeeping, and the barge sank. Raised from the water after the war, the Elector was set down here instead, a refugee statue in front of a survivor palace, and here he has stayed.

The palace itself began small: a summer house, finished in the sixteen nineties, for zoh fee shar lot tuh — a queen who preferred philosophers to courtiers and kept the great thinker libe nits in conversation for years. She died young, the palace took her name, and eventually a whole district of the city did too. And look back up at the dome: the gilded Fortune is a weathervane, the goddess of luck spinning wherever the wind sends her — which, for a palace in this city, is honest advertising. Every Prussian generation after her added a wing or a garden until it became what you see: the grandest run of royal building Berlin has left.

That last phrase is doing quiet work. Bombs gutted this palace in November nineteen forty-three, and afterwards there were serious voices for clearing the ruin away. Instead it was rebuilt, room by room, over decades — which is why you can stand here today inside a Berlin that otherwise barely exists: the merely royal city, elegant and unhurried, from before this town became the twentieth century's crash site.

That's what shar lot ten boorg is for — it's the control sample, the glimpse of the Berlin that might have been ordinary. Walk through to the gardens behind when you're done; they're free to wander, baroque parterre first, then loose English green down to the water. Take the long way. The palace was built for exactly that pace.
