The strip of green beside this street was, for twenty-eight years, the most dangerous ground in Berlin.

You should be at the southern end of Bernauer Strasse, near Nordbahnhof station, with the memorial grounds stretching ahead of you — a long ribbon of lawn, rust-coloured steel and scattered concrete running the length of the street. This was the border strip, and it has been kept roughly as it fell.

Walk to the rust-red walls first. They frame the one section, about seventy metres of it, where the whole system survives: the outer wall on the street side, the inner wall behind, and between them the cleared, once-floodlit corridor with its watchtower. People say "the Wall" as if it were a single thing, but it was two walls with a machine between them — raked sand, lamps, a patrol road, a tower — and this is the last place where you can stand and see the machine entire.

The border didn't arrive on this street; it erupted through it. The houses on this side stood in the East while their front doors opened onto a Western street, so in August 1961 the doors and lower windows were bricked up while families still lived upstairs, and in those first weeks people left through the upper windows, because a window was suddenly the only way west. Later the houses were emptied and demolished — their excavated foundations are still there in the grass.

Further along, an oval building of clay and timber marks the spot where a church stood inside the strip. The border couldn't tolerate it — it blocked the sightlines — so in 1985 the East German government dynamited it. The Chapel of Reconciliation went up on the same foundations after reunification, holding the old church's salvaged bells, and at the Window of Remembrance nearby, the people who died at this border are named and shown, one by one — the memorial's quiet insistence that this history has faces.

[beat]

Notice what this place refuses to do. There's no reconstruction and no staging — fragments and grass, left to speak in their own dimensions. The Wall was never an event; it was a condition, and the only way to grasp a condition is to stand inside its measurements for a while. Take your time here.
