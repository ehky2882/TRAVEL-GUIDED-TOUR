Berliners call this church the Hollow Tooth, and they mean it with love.

You should be on brite shide plats, at the top of the koor fewrs ten dahm, looking at a jagged, broken church spire from the eighteen nineties — and beside it, a low octagonal building of dark honeycombed concrete, with a matching tower currently wrapped in scaffolding.

Take the ruin first. The church was built to honour the first Kaiser vil helm, and its spire was once among the tallest things in the city. Bombing shattered it in November nineteen forty-three, and after the war the architect ay gon eye er mahn, hired to build its replacement, wanted the ruin cleared away entirely. Berliners rose up in protest — letters, newspapers, real anger — and the compromise you're looking at was the result: keep the broken tower, and build the new church around it. The city then named the whole family: the ruin is the Hollow Tooth, and eye er mahn's octagon and freestanding bell tower are the Powder Box and the Lipstick. The Lipstick is under wraps at the moment — after years behind scaffolding, its damaged concrete honeycombs are finally being repaired, cell by cell.

Now step inside the octagon, because the outside gives you no warning. The walls are made of thousands of small squares of stained glass, and the whole room glows a deep, oceanic blue — the glassmaker, gah bree el lwahr of shart, said the blue stood for peace. It is one of the calmest rooms in Berlin, thirty steps from one of its busiest corners. The base of the old tower holds a memorial hall too, its ceiling mosaics intact, gold still glittering over the fracture.

One quieter thing. In the steps at the square's edge runs a long, thin seam of gold — the memorial to the people killed here in the attack of December twenty sixteen. Their names are set where the seam begins.

Across town, the East rebuilt palaces and cleared ruins away. Out here, the West made the opposite bet — that a broken tower would say more than a whole one ever could. Sixty years on, with the traffic circling and the blue room glowing, it's hard to argue they were wrong.
