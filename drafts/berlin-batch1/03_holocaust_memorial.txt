One block south of the Brandenburg Gate, the city gave its most visible piece of ground to grief.

You should be at the edge of a field of grey concrete blocks — thousands of them, rectangular, unmarked, laid out in a strict grid covering an entire city block between Ebertstrasse and Wilhelmstrasse. This is the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe, Germany's national memorial to the six million Jewish men, women and children killed in the Holocaust. It opened in 2005, sixty years after the end of the war.

From where you stand, the blocks look modest — knee-height, waist-height. Walk in. The ground falls away beneath you as the stelae rise, until the city disappears and you're in narrow corridors of concrete well above your head, the light coming down in strips, the noise of Berlin gone. People appear and vanish at the ends of the rows. It is disorienting by design.

There are some two thousand seven hundred stelae, and not one of them carries a name, a date, or a word of explanation. The architect, Peter Eisenman, refused to say what the field means — not evasively, but deliberately. He built a place with no centre, no entrance, no set route and no message, and left the meaning to the person walking through it. The names are below: an information centre beneath the southeast corner of the field holds the records of the victims, and gives back what the stones above withhold — names, faces, families, and the ordinary lives that were taken.

[beat]

The location is the one thing that needs no interpreting. This ground lay beside the death strip of the Berlin Wall, in the ruined government quarter of the state that carried out the murder. When Germany reunified and this became some of the most valuable empty land in Europe, the parliament voted to give it to memory.

Take the walk through the field at your own pace, and let it be whatever it is to you. That was the architect's one request — and the memorial keeps making it, quietly, to everyone who comes.
