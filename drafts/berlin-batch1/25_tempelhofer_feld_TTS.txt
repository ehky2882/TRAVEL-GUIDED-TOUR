You are standing on a runway, and nobody is going to ask you to move.

You should be somewhere on tem pel hoh fer felt, having come through one of the gates — grass to the horizon, two concrete runways running away into the distance, and along the northern edge the immense grey arc of the old terminal building.

Walk out onto the old runway and get the scale first. The field covers around three hundred hectares — larger than the entire principality of Monaco — all of it inside the city, all of it open. Kites, gardens, skaters, and in the protected meadows at the centre, skylarks nesting where jets once rolled.

Now that terminal. It was built by the Nazi state in the late thirties, and it was built as a boast — among the largest buildings on earth at the time, a stage set for the world capital that never came. Its purpose was intimidation by scale, and the honest thing is to say so. But the field wrote itself a second chapter: in nineteen forty-eight, when the Soviets blockaded West Berlin, Allied aircraft landed here around the clock for nearly a year, keeping two million people alive — the Airlift, with its pilots dropping sweets on little parachutes to the children below. The same ground, built for domination, became the runway freedom landed on. Few places anywhere carry a reversal that complete.

The airport closed in two thousand eight, and the city did something remarkable: nothing. The field opened as a park, unchanged, and in twenty fourteen Berliners voted — by law, by referendum — to keep it unbuilt. The argument has since returned, as housing pressure bites: a citizens' panel said no again, an ideas competition has sketched futures with and without building on the edges, and the question may yet go back to a vote. One side sees a wasteful luxury; the other sees the purest democratic monument in Germany. The argument is honest on both sides.

This is the city's old habit of leaving things unfinished, elevated into policy — emptiness, chosen on purpose, defended at the ballot box. Walk to the middle of the field, where the horizon goes flat in every direction. That view is what everyone is arguing about.
