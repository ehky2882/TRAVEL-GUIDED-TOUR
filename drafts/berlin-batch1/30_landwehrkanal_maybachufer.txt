Berlin's most famous Kreuzberg market is not, strictly speaking, in Kreuzberg.

You should be standing on the Maybachufer, the tree-lined bank of the Landwehrkanal, with the water in front of you. The far bank, across the canal, is Kreuzberg; the ground under your feet is Neukölln. The canal is the border between them — and if it's a Tuesday or a Friday, the market stretching along the embankment behind you is the reason you came.

Start with the water. The Landwehrkanal was dug between 1845 and 1850, eleven kilometres of it, looping off the River Spree and rejoining it downstream, laid out by Peter Joseph Lenné — the royal gardener who also shaped the Tiergarten. It was built for barges. What survives is a promenade: a slow green seam through the south of the city, lined with old trees, protected as a monument, and fiercely defended. Hold on to that word — defended.

Now the market. Tuesdays and Fridays, well over a hundred stalls selling flatbread and olives, crates of herbs, fabric by the metre, Turkish coffee poured from long-handled pots. There has been a market on this bank since the 1880s, but the character it wears now arrived in the 1970s, when Turkish families settled the streets on both sides of the water and made this their weekly exchange. Berliners call it the Türkenmarkt, and nearly all of them will tell you it's in Kreuzberg. It is one of the city's most cherished geographical errors.

Then the trees — because the trees are the real story. In 2007, a stretch of this very quay wall slid into the canal, and the federal waterways authority proposed felling more than two hundred of the bank's old trees to make repairs. The neighbourhood refused. People formed associations, kept watch, and in some cases climbed up and occupied the branches — and the dispute grew into one of the largest public mediation processes Germany has ever run. It took years, and the trees won.

[beat]

In most cities, a collapsing canal wall gets an engineering report. Here it got a six-year public argument about who the water belongs to — and the answer, visible all around you, is the people sitting on its banks. Buy something small from a stall, find a spot at the water's edge, and join them. This is Berlin at its least monumental — which may just be Berlin at its best.
