The most important thing on this square is a pane of glass set into the ground, and most people walk past it twice before they find it.

You should be on Bebelplatz, the broad open square on the south side of Unter den Linden, Berlin's grand central boulevard. Face the boulevard and take the square in: the Staatsoper — the state opera house — on your right, a curved baroque library facade on your left, and behind you a domed church that looks like it wandered in from Rome. Frederick the Great laid all of this out in the 1740s as his Forum Fridericianum — opera, library, cathedral — a king's declaration that Prussia would be a country of ideas. The church, Saint Hedwig's, was modelled on the Pantheon and built for Berlin's Catholics as a gesture of tolerance in a Protestant kingdom; it reopened in 2024 after years of renovation, its interior stripped to white.

Now find the glass. Walk toward the middle of the square and look down for a square window set flush into the stones. Beneath it, softly lit, is a white room lined with empty bookshelves — enough shelving, roughly, for twenty thousand books. It's called The Empty Library, by the Israeli artist Micha Ullman.

On the night of the tenth of May, 1933, students and stormtroopers burned around twenty thousand books here — Heine, Marx, Freud, Kästner, Mann — the words of the country's own writers, in a square a king had built to honour learning. A bronze plaque nearby carries a line the poet Heinrich Heine had written more than a century earlier, about another burning in another age: that where books are burned, people will be burned in the end. In 1933, his own books were in the fire.

[beat]

That's Bebelplatz: the Enlightenment square where the Enlightenment was set alight, answered not with a statue but with an absence — empty shelves under glass, kept lit through the night. Across the boulevard, under the lime trees by Humboldt University, booksellers often set out their tables. The square burned books; the street sells them, every ordinary day.

Stay by the glass a moment before you move on. The shelves are empty, and the light stays on. Berlin's answers are like that — quiet, and underfoot.
