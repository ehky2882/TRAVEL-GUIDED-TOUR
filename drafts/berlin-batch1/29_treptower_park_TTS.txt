Germany is bound by treaty to keep this place forever.

You should be in trep toh er park, at the Soviet War Memorial — begin at the granite figure of a kneeling, grieving woman near the entrance, with the long formal axis opening beyond her.

Go in along that axis. This is the largest of the Soviet memorials in Germany, opened in nineteen forty-nine, and the first thing to understand is that it is not only a monument: it is a cemetery. Thousands of Red Army soldiers who died in the final battle for this city are buried beneath these lawns. Whatever else the place is doing, it is doing it over graves.

Walk between the two vast granite banners, lowered in the shape of mourning flags. The red stone, it is always said, was salvaged from the ruins of Hitler's chancellery — the story is repeated on every visit here, and whether every block truly made that journey, the intended meaning could not be plainer. Beyond them, sixteen stone sarcophagi line the sunken field, carved with battle scenes and with quotations from Stalin, left in place as a document of their moment.

At the far end, on the burial mound, stands the figure the whole site builds toward: a bronze soldier, twelve metres tall, a rescued child held in one arm, his lowered sword resting on a shattered swastika. The story attached to it — a real soldier carrying a German child out of the fighting in the war's last days — has been told many ways over the years, and has surely grown in the telling; the sculpture, either way, chooses protection as its final image.

Hold both truths as you walk. This place is grief and it is theatre — a genuine graveyard and a work of state propaganda, and it never lets you fully separate the two. In the treaties of reunification, Germany undertook to preserve and maintain the Soviet memorials on its soil, permanently, and it does, through every change in the political weather.

Whatever you make of the theatre, the grief underneath it is real — thousands of young men under the grass, very far from home. Walk the axis slowly, and give this place the silence you would give any graveyard.
