The golden woman on top of this column has moved house.

You should be at the groh ser shtairn, the roundabout in the middle of the teer gar ten, reached through the pedestrian tunnels under the traffic. Above you, on a column of red granite, stands a winged figure in gold — Victory, eight metres of her, though Berliners long ago renamed her golt el zuh: Golden Lizzy.

Look up at her first. The column went up in eighteen seventy-three to celebrate Prussia's victories over Denmark, Austria and France, and it wears its trophies openly — those gilded tubes set into the column's flutes are captured cannon barrels from the defeated armies. But she didn't start here. Until the late thirties, the whole monument stood in front of the rysh tahg; Hitler's planners moved it to this roundabout to decorate the grand east-west axis of the capital they intended to build. The move was made for the worst of reasons, and it may well be why she survived — her old neighbourhood was flattened in the war while she stood out here among the trees.

About those trees. The teer gar ten began as a royal hunting ground and became the city's great garden, but in the desperate winters after the war, freezing Berliners felled nearly all of it for firewood and planted vegetables between the stumps. Almost every tree around you was planted since — a forest younger than the peace it grows in.

The column, meanwhile, kept changing jobs. Nobody in this city celebrates or mourns Prussia's victories now, so Berlin gave her new ones: the roundabout has held raves and rallies and a million dancers at a time, the city's queer magazine named itself after her, and vim ven ders set his trench-coated angels on her shoulder to watch over the city. On most days you can climb her too — a spiral of nearly three hundred steps to the platform under her feet.

Monuments outlive their meanings; the question is what a city does with the leftovers. Berlin's answer is the kindest one available — it lets them become something else. When you're done, skip the tunnels back and just walk into the trees instead. That's what they're for now.
