Berlin keeps its best rooms behind its buildings.

You should be on Rosenthaler Strasse, just up from Hackescher Markt, at an arched entrance beneath a curved gable — the way into the Hackesche Höfe. From the street it reads as one handsome facade, but behind it eight courtyards link together like a chain, and you can walk the whole chain through the block and come out on Sophienstrasse on the far side.

Step through into the first courtyard. This is the showpiece: walls of glazed brick in blue, cream and gold, patterned right up to the rooflines — the work of August Endell, who took a working courtyard and dressed it like a ballroom turned inside out. When the complex opened in 1906, this was the point: courtyards had always been Berlin's back rooms, and here was one designed to be looked at.

Now the deeper story, because this is really a tour of a floor plan. Berlin's blocks run deep, and for a century the street front was for show while life went inward — side wings, rear houses, courtyard behind courtyard, homes stacked over workshops stacked over stables. The old building code asked only that each courtyard stay big enough for a horse-drawn fire pump to turn around, and that measurement — a bit over five metres each way — decided how much sky most Berliners got. The Höfe were built as the reform version: light, air, colour, a theatre, apartments and small factories sharing one address, everything the dark courts around them were not. After decades of grey neglect in the East, the whole complex was restored in the nineties, and it has been polished ever since.

Here's what to take with you: the street tells you what a city wants you to think, and the courtyard tells you how it actually lives. Keep walking — the crowds thin a little with every archway. And when you're done, look for the scruffier alley a couple of doors down the street, unrestored and layered in paste-ups and paint. One courtyard polished, one left raw, thirty steps apart — that's this neighbourhood, and honestly most of Berlin, in two doorways.
