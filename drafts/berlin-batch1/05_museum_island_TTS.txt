Five museums, one island, a hundred years of ambition — and, at the moment, a very honest amount of scaffolding.

You should be standing in the loost gar ten, the big rectangular lawn at the heart of Museum Island. Ahead of you, a long line of columns — that's the ahl tes museum. To your right, the heavy green-domed bulk of the Berlin Cathedral. Behind you, across the road, the reconstructed palace. The shpray wraps around all of it; you're on an island, and the island belongs to art.

Begin with the colonnade. Eighteen columns, opened in eighteen thirty — one of the first museums in Europe built for the public rather than for a court, designed by karl free drish shin kel, Prussia's house architect, whose fingerprints are all over this city. On the lawn in front of it sits a polished granite bowl nearly seven metres across, cut and shined from a single boulder left behind by a glacier. It was meant for the museum's rotunda and turned out too big for the door. Berliners adored it anyway — they called it a wonder of the world and left it outside, where it has sat ever since.

Behind the ahl tes museum, four more museums rose over the following century, the last of them, the pair gah mon, finished in nineteen thirty. The state kept changing around them — kingdom, empire, republic, dictatorship — and the museums kept coming. Today the whole ensemble is World Heritage. And here's the honest part: the island has been under repair, in one corner or another, for most of living memory. The pair gah mon is closed for renovation until twenty twenty-seven, its famous altar out of sight, and the cranes move from building to building like gardeners.

The cathedral on your right is the loudest thing here — imperial swagger from nineteen oh five, the hoh en tsol lern family church, with the dynasty's crypt beneath it, freshly reopened after years of restoration.

Prussia declared this island a sanctuary for art and learning, and Berlin has treated it like a cathedral ever since — never quite finished, permanently tended, handed from one Germany to the next like a duty nobody questions.

Climb a few steps toward the colonnade and turn around: lawn, dome, palace, river. The island is doing what it was built for — giving the city somewhere to stand among beautiful things.
