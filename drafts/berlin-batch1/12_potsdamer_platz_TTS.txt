Twice in the last hundred years, this was one of the busiest places in Europe — and in between, it was a field.

You've just come out at pots dahm er plats, and whichever way you turn there's glass: a brick-clad tower with a prow like a ship, a vast tented roof glowing to the north, and to the south-west a quarter of warm terracotta blocks. Before any of it, look down. Slabs of the original Wall stand near the station entrance, studded with tourists' chewing gum, and a doubled line of cobblestones cuts across the asphalt and runs on under the traffic, indifferent to everything built over it. You'll cross that line elsewhere in this city; it never stops marking where the Wall stood.

In the nineteen twenties this crossing carried trams, buses, cars and crowds in such volume that Berlin installed one of continental Europe's first traffic lights here, in nineteen twenty-four, up in a five-sided tower — a replica of it stands near the station. Grand hotels, famous cafés, one of the busiest intersections on the continent. Then the war flattened it, and what the bombs left, the border finished: the Wall ran straight through, and for nearly thirty years Europe's liveliest square lay as an empty plain between watchtowers, where rabbits grazed the death strip.

After reunification the city sold the emptiness to corporations, and through the nineties this became the biggest building site in Europe — an entire quarter invented from a blank page. Almost nothing here had a past to answer to, though two survivors made it through: one old wine house held its ground among the towers, and a gilded ballroom from the ruined hotel es plah nah duh was picked up whole and moved about seventy-five metres to fit the new plan — it sits behind glass under the tented roof today.

pots dahm er plats gets accused of feeling like anywhere, and the charge half sticks — this is a downtown without a childhood. But stand at the gum-flecked Wall slabs and look up, and you get the whole century in one eye-line: the traffic tower of the twenties, the concrete of the divide, the glass of the reinvention. A place erased twice doesn't get charm. It gets proof that the city kept going.
