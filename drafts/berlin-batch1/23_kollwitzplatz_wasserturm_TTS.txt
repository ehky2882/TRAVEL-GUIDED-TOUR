Nothing world-famous ever happened on this square — which, in Berlin, is its own kind of luxury.

You should be on kol vits plats, a leafy triangle in prents low er bairg, ringed by tall old apartment houses, with playgrounds under the plane trees and, on her plinth, a heavyset bronze woman sitting with her hands in her lap.

Find the statue first. That's kay tuh kol vits, who lived on this square for fifty years and drew what she saw from her window: the poor of a poverty district, mothers and children in the deep tenement blocks — the same front-house, back-house courtyards you met at the hah kesh uh hur fuh, only without the art tiles. If you've stood in the noy uh vah khuh, you've already met her grieving mother at the centre of the nation's memorial; here she is herself, and the neighbourhood's children climb into her bronze lap daily, polishing her knees bright. Of all the artists honoured with statues, she is the one who would have insisted on exactly this use.

One street over stands the vah ser toorm, the fat, round water tower on its little hill — the oldest in Berlin, from eighteen seventy-seven, with apartments curled inside it now. It carries one dark chapter, told plainly: in nineteen thirty-three, in the first months of the dictatorship, buildings on this site were used as an improvised prison where people were held and mistreated. A memorial stone remembers them.

Under the East, this whole district was the crumbling bohemian corner — coal stoves, unrenovated facades, artists and dissidents in cheap rooms, and churches nearby that sheltered the opposition in the autumn of nineteen eighty-nine. Then came the great polishing. Today prents low er bairg is the most renovated, most photographed, most expensive version of itself, and the argument runs on: some mourn the priced-out neighbours and the rougher, mixed city; others note that nobody truly misses coal smoke and shared toilets on the landing. Both sides are right about something, which is why the argument won't end.

One small square, five Berlins: the drawn poverty, the war, the grey decades, the revolution from the churches, and the young-family prosperity of now. On market days the square fills with stalls. Buy something small and sit a while — the fifth Berlin has its comforts.
