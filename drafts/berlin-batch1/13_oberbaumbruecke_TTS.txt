This bridge is wearing a costume, and it has been wearing it since eighteen ninety-six.

You should be on the oh ber bowm brew kuh, the double-decked brick bridge over the shpray between kroyts bairg and free drish shine — cars and walkers below, yellow trains rattling through the arcade above, and two fairy-tale towers rising mid-span.

Start with the towers. They guard nothing, and they never did. When the city built its new elevated railway at the end of the nineteenth century, it dressed the crossing as a medieval gate — battlements, pointed arches, north-German brick Gothic draped over modern engineering. The disguise was the point: a young, industrial Berlin borrowing the costume of an old one.

Then the twentieth century got hold of it. The middle of the bridge was blown up in the war's final weeks, and after that the river itself became the border — this crossing stood half-broken between two systems, reduced to a controlled walkway where West Berliners filed across into the East. When the city was stitched back together, the missing middle was rebuilt as a modern steel span by the Spanish architect sahn tee ah go kah lah trah vah, and notice what that means: the repair wears no costume. Old brick, new steel, the seam left honest. And the trains upstairs didn't cross again until nineteen ninety-five — six years after the Wall fell, the yellow line finally closed the gap.

Now look downstream, along the river. Three aluminium giants lean into each other in the water — the Molecule Men, some thirty metres tall and riddled with holes — standing where three districts meet midstream. And the two banks here, kroyts bairg and free drish shine, once glaring at each other across a border, were merged after reunification into one hyphenated borough. The locals mark this happy fact in their own way: most summers, residents of the two banks meet on this bridge and fight a cheerful mock battle with water and thrown vegetables. Reconciliation, Berlin-style.

Most bridges just carry traffic. This one carries the whole story of joining — built as theatre, broken by war, hardened into a border, and repaired with its seam showing. Stay for one train. When it crosses overhead, brick to steel to brick, that's the sound of a city that decided to be one place again.
