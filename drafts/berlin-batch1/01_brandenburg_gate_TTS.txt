The gate in front of you has been marched through, sealed off, fought over, and lit up in more colours than any monument in Europe — and through all of it, it never moved an inch. Berlin did the moving.

You should be standing on pah ree zer plats, the wide stone square on the gate's eastern side, with the gate ahead of you: two rows of six heavy columns, five passages between them, and a copper-green chariot on top driven by a winged goddess. The square around you is lined with embassies, a famous hotel, and banks in polite modern stone.

Start with the chariot. That's the kvah dree gah — four horses pulling the goddess of victory, hammered from copper in seventeen ninety-three. In eighteen oh six Napoleon defeated Prussia, rode through this gate, and shipped her to Paris as a trophy. Eight years later Prussia took her back, and she returned with a souvenir: the iron cross on her staff, designed by a young architect named karl free drish shin kel — a name this city is full of. She has faced east ever since, riding into Berlin, away from France.

Now the square. pah ree zer plats was Berlin's front parlour for two centuries — and almost everything you see on it is younger than the fall of the Wall. The war flattened the square, and the border kept it flat: for twenty-eight years this was a guarded emptiness, and the gate stood inside the restricted strip, unreachable from either direction. The hotel aht lohn, the embassies, the academy — all of it rebuilt from nothing in the nineteen nineties.

Walk toward the gate and look at the road just beyond it, where a double row of cobblestones crosses the asphalt. That line traces exactly where the Wall ran. You'll step over it in about ten paces. Keep the mark in mind — it threads through this whole city, and it will keep turning up underfoot.

The gate was built in seventeen ninety-one as a gate of peace, and since then it has framed victory parades, torchlight marches, an American president demanding that a wall come down, and the night in nineteen eighty-nine when the city climbed onto that wall to reach it. It isn't really a monument. It's a barometer — it means whatever Germany means at the moment you're looking at it.

Walk through it when you're ready, and take your time. For twenty-eight years, nobody could.
