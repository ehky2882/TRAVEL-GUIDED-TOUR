The oldest quarter in Berlin is younger than the Walkman.

You should be in the Nikolaiviertel, the little knot of cobbled lanes and gabled houses between the river and the red-brick city hall, with the twin spires of the Nikolaikirche rising over the rooftops.

Start at the church, because it really is old — the oldest building in the city, begun around 1230, back when Berlin was a trading post on a muddy river crossing. This quarter around it is where the city started. The church is a museum now rather than a working parish, which somehow suits it — the building has outlived its congregation's city several times over. But look closely at the gabled houses, and run a hand along a wall or two: much of what surrounds you is precast concrete. The war levelled the old quarter, and in the eighties East Germany rebuilt it for Berlin's seven hundred and fiftieth birthday — medieval gables moulded in the same prefabricated panels the state used for its tower blocks. A few pieces are genuine: one merchant's house came through the war, and the rococo Ephraim-Palais on the corner was rebuilt from its own original facade stones, which had been dismantled for a road-widening in the thirties and sat in storage through the entire catastrophe, waiting.

Now walk out toward the big red building. That's the Rotes Rathaus — the Red City Hall — Berlin's seat of government since the 1860s, and the name refers to the bricks, not the politics, though the city has enjoyed the double meaning through several governments where both applied. The cleared ground beyond it is next in line: the city is preparing to rebuild the lost quarter around the old Molkenmarkt, its earliest marketplace — the same trick attempted again, this time with arguments about how honest the copy should be.

[beat]

So is this place fake? Yes, cheerfully. But it's a copy with real things inside it — a genuine thirteenth-century church, true facade stones, the actual ground the city was born on — and that makes it a fair portrait of Berlin's memory in general: reconstructed, patched, argued over, and lived in anyway. Take a slow loop of the lanes. The concrete gables photograph beautifully, and there's something honest about that too.
