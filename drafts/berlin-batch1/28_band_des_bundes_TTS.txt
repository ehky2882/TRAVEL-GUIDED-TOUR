The chancellor of Germany works in a building Berliners call the washing machine.

You should be in the government quarter at the shpray boh gen, the great bend of the river north of the rysh tahg — on the lawns with the white, cube-shaped Chancellery ahead of you, its facade dominated by enormous circular openings. Hence the nickname. A capital city that laughs at its own seat of power is, on the whole, doing something right.

Start with the idea, because this whole quarter is one. When the government moved back from bon in the nineties, Germany got something democracies almost never get: the chance to design its own home from scratch. The winning plan, by ahk sel shool tes and shar lot tuh frahnk, was called the bahnt des boon des — the Ribbon of the Federation — a straight line of government buildings running east to west, crossing the shpray not once but twice. Both moves are deliberate sentences. The double river crossing staples together ground that division once separated. And the ribbon lies at a right angle across the ghost of the monumental north-south axis the Nazis had planned for their world capital — one intended monument, quietly cancelled by another.

The bahnt is still growing, honestly and visibly: a new wing for the Chancellery is rising across the river to the west, its shell already at full height, a fresh connecting bridge slid into place over the water this spring. The ribbon keeps writing itself.

Notice, too, what you're allowed to do here: walk. Lawns, riverbanks, paths straight through the middle of federal power — and next door, the glass dome where the public strolls above the heads of its own parliament. You met that idea at the rysh tahg: a state built to be looked into. This quarter is the same sentence at the scale of a district.

Most capitals inherit their architecture of power and make excuses for it. This one got a blank page in nineteen ninety and wrote down what it wanted to be: horizontal, transparent, stitched across its own old wounds. Come back along the riverbank at dusk if you can — the buildings light up, and the water carries it all twice.
