This boulevard is ninety metres wide, and every one of those metres was a political statement.

You should be at frahnk foor ter tor, where two domed towers face each other across the avenue. Look west down the boulevard and you can see all the way to the television tower, small and silver at the end of the axis. Everything between here and there was built as a single argument.

Take in the width first. In the fifties, the young East Germany raised this as shtah leen ah lay, its showcase street: two kilometres of seven- and eight-storey blocks it called workers' palaces — wedding-cake classicism faced in ceramic tile, much of it from the my sen works, with apartments behind the grand facades meant for ordinary families. The message ran in both directions: to the West, look what socialism builds; to its own people, this is who the street is for.

The two towers beside you came from the architect hair mahn hen zel mahn, and their domes deliberately echo the twin cathedral towers of zhahn dar men markt — the new state quoting old Berlin to claim its inheritance.

The street answered back twice. In June nineteen fifty-three, the boulevard's own construction workers put down their tools over raised work quotas, and the protest spread within a day into an uprising across the country, put down by Soviet tanks — the showcase street was the first place the state heard its workers say no. And in nineteen sixty-one, after Moscow renounced Stalin's cult, the street lost his name in a single clandestine night: signs swapped in the dark, the giant bronze statue taken away — de-Stalinised overnight by the same state that had cast him.

Today the whole ensemble is heritage-listed and argued over. Some see a dictatorship's stage set; others call it the most ambitious social housing Germany ever built. People wanted these apartments then, and they want them now — both camps are describing the same tiles.

That's the awkward gift of this street: it makes you hold two true things at once. Walk a block or two west before you go — the boulevard only makes sense at walking pace, which, whatever else you think of them, its builders understood.
