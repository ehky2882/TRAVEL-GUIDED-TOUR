Now look down at the floor. Running across the church near the front, on your right, is a thin brass strip set into the stone, and at one end of it stands a white marble obelisk. This is a nohmon — an astronomical instrument hidden inside a church. There's a small opening high in a window opposite; at midday, sunlight falls through it and lands somewhere along that brass line, and exactly where it lands depends on the time of year. Built in the seventeen hundreds, its real job was severely practical: to fix the date of the spring equinox, and from there the date of Easter, which drifts around the calendar every year. A sun-machine, installed to keep the church's own calendar honest.

You may have heard a different story. A wildly successful novel, a couple of decades ago — you'll know the one — recast this brass line as an ancient pagan meridian thick with secret meaning. It made such an impression that the parish eventually put up a notice, in plain language, explaining that the line is a piece of eighteenth-century astronomy and nothing occult whatsoever.
