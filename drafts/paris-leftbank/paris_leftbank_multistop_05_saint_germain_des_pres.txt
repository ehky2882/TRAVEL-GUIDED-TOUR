You're in a small, busy square now, with a stout, square stone tower rising over it — older and plainer than almost anything around it. That's the church of Saint-Germain-des-Prés, and it is the oldest church in Paris. There has been an abbey on this spot since the year 558; the tower you're looking at went up around the year 1000, which makes it one of the oldest standing things in the whole city. For most of its life this was a powerful monastery out beyond the old city walls — the name means Saint Germain of the Fields.

Now turn to the two corners across the way, because this is where the story gets quietly funny. On one side sits the Café de Flore; a few steps along, Les Deux Magots. Two perfectly ordinary-looking cafés — and in the middle of the twentieth century they became the most famous thinking-spots in Europe.

This was the headquarters of the existentialists. The philosopher Jean-Paul Sartre and the writer Simone de Beauvoir more or less worked here, at the tables, for hours, day after day, and Albert Camus and half of literary Paris drifted through. There's a practical reason alongside the romantic one: the cafés were heated and their rooms were not, so the warm café became the office, the salon and the stage all at once.

*[beat]*

Which is the through-line of this whole walk, sitting on a single corner. A thousand-year-old church, founded by monks who came here to think and pray in silence, quietly out-famoused by two coffee houses where a later set of thinkers did much the same job out loud. Same corner, same impulse, a millennium apart.

We're nearly at the river now. Walk north to the Seine, and a short way along the bank you'll find a huge old railway station with an enormous glass clock set into its wall. That's the Musée d'Orsay, and it's where we finish.
