You've come down off the hill into a maze of narrow, sloping streets — some of the oldest in Paris, laid out long before anyone thought to straighten them. You're near the river now, and if you've stopped where I think you have, you're in front of a green shopfront with books stacked in the windows and, more often than not, a small crowd gathered at the door. The sign reads Shakespeare and Company.

First, the streets themselves. This is the heart of the Latin Quarter, and the name is a fossil. For centuries the students of the university spoke and studied in Latin — the common tongue that let a scholar from anywhere in Europe argue with a scholar from anywhere else — and the quarter took its name from the language ringing through it. The students are still here; the Latin is long gone. The place is named for something nobody says anymore.

Which brings us to the bookshop, because it works exactly the same way. This shop — English-language, famous, photographed half to death — was opened in nineteen fifty-one by an American named George Whitman. But the name over the door isn't original to it. He borrowed it, in nineteen sixty-four, from another shop entirely.

The first Shakespeare and Company belonged to a woman named Sylvia Beach, who ran it a few streets from here in the years between the wars. It was the clubhouse of the expatriate writers — and in nineteen twenty-two, when no respectable publisher would touch it, Beach herself published James Joyce's novel Ulysses. Her shop closed during the Second World War and never reopened.

So the shop in front of you is, in a sense, a tribute act: a homage, under a borrowed name, to a place that no longer exists.

And that's the quarter's whole trick. It honours the past by impersonating it — a neighbourhood named for a dead language, anchored by a bookshop named for a vanished shop, both of them thoroughly alive on the strength of the thing they're remembering.

When you're ready, walk west a few minutes to the tall iron gates of a garden — the zhardan dew lewksombor. A homesick queen built it, and we'll go in next.
