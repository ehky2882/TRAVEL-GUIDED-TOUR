You're at the top of the hill now, in front of the ponteyon — that vast, grey, domed building with a long row of columns across the front, looking for all the world like a Roman temple that wandered into Paris and sat down.

Stand back far enough to take in the whole front and read the inscription across the top: it says, in French, that the grateful nation honours its great men. Hold on to that word — honours — because this building has spent its whole life arguing about who, exactly, deserves it.

It started as a church. A king, loo-ee the fifteenth, fell ill, swore that if he recovered he'd build a magnificent church to sant zhuhnvyev, the patron saint of Paris — and he recovered, and he built it. But the work took decades, and by the time it was done the Revolution had arrived, and a building meant for a saint suddenly looked like the perfect home for the nation's heroes instead. So they turned it into a temple to the great dead. Then a later government turned it back into a church. Then back to a temple. Back and forth it went, something like five times, depending on who held power and what they believed.

What finally settled the argument is downstairs. Below your feet, in the crypt, lie the people France chose to keep: the writers Voltaire and Rousseau and Victor Hugo, the novelist aymeel zola, and — placed here in nineteen ninety-five, the first woman admitted for her own achievements rather than her husband's — the scientist maree kewree.

And then there's the pendulum. Hanging from the centre of the dome is a long wire with a heavy weight swinging slowly at the end. In eighteen fifty-one a physicist named fookoh hung one here to prove something nobody could otherwise see: that the Earth turns. The pendulum keeps swinging in a single fixed direction while the floor — and the building, and the city — rotate slowly beneath it. People came to watch the planet move.

Which is the whole quarter in one room. A building that couldn't decide whether to worship God or genius, that finally chose its dead by merit, with a piece of pure human reasoning swinging where the altar would have been.

When you've looked your fill, head back out and down the hill, north toward the river, into the tangle of old streets below. We're walking into the Latin Quarter itself — and a famous bookshop — next.
