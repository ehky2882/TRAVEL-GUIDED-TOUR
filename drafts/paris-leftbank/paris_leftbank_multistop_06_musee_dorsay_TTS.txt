Last stop. Stand in the great main hall and look up: a long, vaulted ceiling of glass and pale iron arching the length of the building, daylight pouring down through it. It doesn't look like a museum, and that's because it wasn't built as one. This was a railway station.

It's called the gar dorsay, and it was put up in a hurry for the World's Fair of nineteen hundred — a grand, modern terminus right in the centre of the city, all that glass and iron showing off how confident Paris felt about the new century. The trouble was that trains kept getting longer, and by the late nineteen thirties the platforms here were simply too short for them. The station slid into odd, part-time uses, fell half-derelict, and came close to being torn down. Then, in nineteen eighty-six, it reopened as something else entirely: a museum.

And look at what they chose to fill it with. The collection here covers roughly the half-century before the First World War — the Impressionists and the painters who came just after: monay, renwar, sayzan, van goh and their whole restless generation. Which means the art on these walls was made at almost exactly the moment this building was going up. The station and the paintings are the same age.

Now find the great clock. High in the wall at the upper end of the hall is an enormous glass clock face, and you can stand right behind it and look out through the numbers at the city beyond — Paris framed inside a timepiece, which is about as on-the-nose as this place is willing to get.

And it's the right place to end. We started at the top of the hill, in a building that couldn't decide what it worshipped; we've come down through a quarter that names itself after a dead language, keeps a borrowed bookshop, and argues with novelists about its own floor. We finish inside a railway station that was nearly demolished and became, instead, a kind of mirror — the era looking back at itself, hung on the walls of its own train shed.

That's the Left Bank: the part of Paris that goes there to think, and that can't quite stop thinking about itself. Stay a while behind the clock. The view doesn't get old.
