Through the gates and onto the gravel, and the whole garden opens out in front of you — wide paths, clipped trees, a big octagonal pond, and beyond it a grand pale palace. Before anything else, find a chair. There are hundreds of them, dark green metal, scattered loose across the grounds, and the rule is wonderfully simple: drag one wherever you like and sit. We'll talk for a moment.

The palace at the far end was not built for the public. It went up in the early 1600s for Marie de Médicis — an Italian, from the great Florentine family, who had married a French king, been widowed, and found herself stranded in a grey northern city she never warmed to. So she built herself a piece of home: a palace modelled on the one she'd grown up around in Florence, heavy Tuscan stonework and all, an attempt to import a little Italy to the banks of the Seine. Today it houses the French Senate, which makes the garden you're sitting in, technically, the Senate's front lawn.

Off to one side, tucked under the trees, she added the thing she missed most — a long, shaded, dripping grotto of a fountain, Italian to its bones. It's called the Medici Fountain, it's the most un-Parisian corner of Paris, and it's the corner that feels most like the woman who ordered it.

*[beat]*

Here's the turn, though. A homesick queen's private consolation has become, four centuries on, the most generous public room in the city — a place where students cram for exams, retirees doze in the sun, children sail toy boats on the pond, and you arrange your own chair exactly where the light is best. The mind needs somewhere to rest as much as somewhere to work, and the quarter put one right in its middle.

When you've had your sit, leave by the western side and walk a couple of minutes to a very large church with two mismatched towers — Saint-Sulpice. There's a machine hidden in its floor.
