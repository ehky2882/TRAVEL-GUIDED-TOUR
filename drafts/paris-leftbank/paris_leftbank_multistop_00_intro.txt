For roughly eight hundred years, this corner of Paris has been where the city goes to think.

You're starting on the Left Bank — the south side of the river — in the part of town the maps call the fifth and sixth arrondissements and everyone else calls the Latin Quarter. The name isn't decoration. For centuries the students here studied, argued, and swore in Latin, the shared language of the medieval university, and the nickname stuck long after the language faded from daily use. The university in question is the Sorbonne, which has been teaching on these slopes since the Middle Ages, and the streets around it have been thick with students, booksellers and arguments ever since.

So this walk has a single subject: the life of the mind. Where the rest of Paris was built around power, money, or worship, this quarter grew up around ideas — around reading, teaching, writing, and the long habit of disagreeing in public.

The good news for your legs is that it's downhill. We begin at the highest point, the Panthéon, on the hill named for Sainte-Geneviève, and the route runs gently down from there — through the old university streets, past a famous bookshop, into a garden a queen built to cure her homesickness, by way of two old churches, and finally to the river, where the walk ends inside a museum that used to be a railway station. A little over two kilometres, easy going, mostly down.

One thread to carry the whole way: almost everything we'll stop at was built for one purpose and ended up serving another. The quarter has a habit of changing its mind — which, for a place dedicated to thinking, feels about right.

When you're ready, climb to the top of the hill and stand in front of the Panthéon — the enormous domed building that looks like it can't decide whether it's a church or a monument. As it happens, that's exactly the question, and it's where we start.
