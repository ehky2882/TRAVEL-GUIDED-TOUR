Here it is: the mooseo del prado, the heart of the golden triangle and one of the greatest collections of paintings anywhere. It opened in eighteen nineteen — and here's a surprise: the building wasn't designed as a museum at all. The architect hwan day veeyanwaiba drew it up as a hall of natural history, a home for fossils and specimens. Art only moved in later. So the Prado is, at bottom, a science building that became a temple of painting.

What truly sets it apart is what it is at heart: not a survey of world art, but the private collection of the Spanish crown, opened to the public. For three centuries the kings of Spain bought what they liked — and they had remarkable taste and very deep pockets. So you don't get a little of everything here; you get the best of what Spanish and court painters did, in extraordinary depth.

Three things to hunt for if you go in. velaskess's las meneenass, the painting other painters travel here just to stand in front of — a portrait that quietly puts you, the viewer, in the king's place. Goya's "black paintings," the nightmares he painted straight onto the walls of his own house in old age. And from far earlier, Bosch's Garden of Earthly Delights, a five hundred year old triptych so strange it still looks like it was finished yesterday.

That's the Prado: not the world's attic, but one family's eye, across three centuries. When you've had your fill — or made your peace with coming back — cross back over the boulevard to the lower, quieter building opposite: the teessen bornaymeessa.
