Stop in front of CaixaForum and give yourself a moment to work out what's odd about it, because something is. The heavy brick building appears to hover — its whole ground floor has been carved away, so the mass of it rests on shadow, with no visible base. That's deliberate. The Swiss architects Herzog and de Meuron — the same pair who turned a London power station into the Tate Modern — took an old electricity station that stood here, stripped it back to its brick shell, lifted it onto a hidden support, and crowned it with a jagged top of rusted steel.

Now turn to the wall on its left and look at the green. That entire flank of the neighbouring building is alive: a vertical garden, four storeys of living plants growing straight up the wall with no soil at all, fed through a kind of felt skin. It was designed by the botanist Patrick Blanc, it was the first of its kind in Spain when it opened, and it has been quietly growing there, changing colour with the seasons, ever since.

Entry to the square, and often the building, is free — which makes this the easy, generous note on the walk, the bit you can stumble into without a ticket.

It's also the boulevard making a point. The same street that began with Charles the Third's eighteenth-century fountains now floats a brick box on shadow and grows a garden up a wall. The art turned modern; so did the architecture.

One museum left, and it's the heaviest of them all. Keep going south to the far end of the boulevard, toward Atocha station, to the Reina Sofía.
