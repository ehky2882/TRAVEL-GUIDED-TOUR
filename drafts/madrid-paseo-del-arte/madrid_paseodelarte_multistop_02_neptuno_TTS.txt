This is Neptune — neptoono — Roman god of the sea, carved in white marble, riding a chariot drawn by horses with fishtails, trident in hand. He belongs to the same scheme as thibayles up the road: Charles the Third's architects dressed the whole boulevard with fountains like these in the seventeen eighties, a matched set of gods to make a promenade feel like a stage.

And here's the football story I promised. thibayles, behind you, is where rayal madrid celebrates. Neptune, in front of you, is where atletiko day Madrid celebrates — the city's other great club, traditionally the underdog. So the two fountains have become the two clubs' shrines, a few hundred metres apart on the same street. The detail people love is that it didn't start this way round: the allegiances were once reversed, the clubs effectively swapping fountains over the years, which is the kind of thing Madrid finds very funny and outsiders find baffling.

Mostly, though, Neptune is your signpost. You're standing at the hinge of the golden triangle now — within a two-minute walk of here are two of the three great museums, facing each other across the boulevard.

Look across and slightly uphill to the long, columned building on the far side. That's the Prado, the heart of the whole walk. Cross over to it.
