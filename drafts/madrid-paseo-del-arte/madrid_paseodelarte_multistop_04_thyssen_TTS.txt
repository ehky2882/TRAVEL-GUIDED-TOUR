This lower, pinkish building is the teessen bornaymeessa, and it's the museum that makes the triangle work. Here's why. The Prado, as you just saw, is the crown's taste — deep, but with gaps, because kings bought what kings liked. The rayna sofeea, where we'll finish, is mostly modern. In between sits everything else, and that everything is what this collection is.

It was assembled not by a country but by a family — two generations of teessen bornaymeessas, industrialists, who spent the twentieth century buying privately on a scale that normally only nations manage. The result fills in exactly what the Prado skips: early Italian gold-ground panels, Dutch interiors, the Impressionists, German Expressionism, even American painting and Pop art. Walk it from the top floor down and you walk straight through the history of Western painting, in order, almost without a gap.

The gift detail is how it got here. For decades this was one of the great private collections in the world, kept in Switzerland. In nineteen ninety-three the Spanish state bought it outright — a coup that completed Madrid's triangle in a single stroke, and is still talked about as one of the great museum deals of the century.

So: the crown's eye behind you, a family's eye here, and one more to go — the modern one. From the front of the teessen, carry on south down the boulevard a few hundred metres, to a curious brick building that seems to float just above the ground: kysha forum.
