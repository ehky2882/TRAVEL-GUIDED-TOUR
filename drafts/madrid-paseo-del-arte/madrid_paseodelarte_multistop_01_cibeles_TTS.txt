Stand back far enough to take in the whole fountain. At the centre, a serene woman rides a chariot pulled by two lions: she's sibbuhlee, a goddess the Romans tied to the earth and to cities, and she has been the unofficial symbol of Madrid since the seventeen eighties, when Charles the Third's architects set her here.

Now look past her at the building behind — the white one that looks like a wedding cake crossed with a cathedral. People assume it's a palace. It isn't. It was built, only a little over a century ago, as the city's main post office: a post office so grand that Madrid eventually decided it was wasted on stamps and moved the city government in instead. Today it's the town hall, and you can ride up to a viewing terrace inside for one of the best high views in the centre.

Here's a small thing worth knowing while you're standing here. This fountain is where one of the city's two great football clubs comes to celebrate its titles, the players climbing up to wrap the goddess in a scarf. Which club, and which fountain, we'll come to at the next stop — because the other half of that story is waiting just down the boulevard.
