You've reached the end of the boulevard and the end of the walk: the Reina Sofía, Madrid's museum of modern art. People come here for one painting, and you should too. Inside hangs Picasso's Guernica.

It's enormous — nearly eight metres of black, white, and grey: a screaming horse, a fallen fighter, a woman with her head thrown back, and a bare bulb like an eye burning over it all. Picasso painted it in 1937, in Paris, in a matter of weeks, after German and Italian warplanes — fighting for Franco — flattened the Basque town of Guernica in a single afternoon. It was one of the first times the world had seen a town erased from the air, and Picasso turned that horror into perhaps the most powerful anti-war painting ever made.

Then comes the part that makes standing here matter. Picasso refused to let Guernica come to Spain while Franco ruled: it would only come home, he said, when the country was free again. So for over forty years it lived in exile, mostly at the Museum of Modern Art in New York. Franco died in 1975; democracy returned; and in 1981 the painting finally crossed the Atlantic, arriving for good at the Reina Sofía in the early nineteen-nineties. The painting that came home.

And it still isn't finished arguing. Even now there's a quiet, unresolved tug-of-war over where it truly belongs — whether it should ever travel to the Basque town whose name it carries, where the bombing happened and where it has never once been shown. The museum says the canvas is too old and fragile to move. Others say that's a convenient reason to keep a national treasure in the capital. You don't have to settle it; you only have to know, standing in front of it, that this is a painting people still fight over because it still means something.

That's the whole boulevard, end to end: the crown's collection, a family's collection, a power station reborn, and finally one painting that crossed an ocean to come home and still won't sit quietly. Take your time in here. Of everything on this walk, this is the room to be unhurried in.
