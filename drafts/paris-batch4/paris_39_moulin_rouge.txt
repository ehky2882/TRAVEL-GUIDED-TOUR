You're in front of the Moulin Rouge, and the first thing your eye goes to is the red windmill turning on the roof. Here's the thing: it's a fake — a decorative one, built purely for show. But it's a fake with a point, because it's remembering something real.

Montmartre, this whole hill, was once covered in windmills — dozens of real working ones, grinding grain and pressing the grapes from the vineyards that used to blanket the slopes. As the city swallowed the village, the windmills came down one by one. So when this dance hall opened in 1889 — the same year the Eiffel Tower went up across the city — it put a bright red windmill on its roof as a wink to the rural Montmartre that was already disappearing. A monument to the working windmills, in the shape of a glamorous fake one.

And inside, it invented a legend. The Moulin Rouge was the birthplace of the modern cancan — that explosive, high-kicking, swirling-skirts dance that scandalised and thrilled Paris in equal measure. It was loud, athletic, a little outrageous, and utterly new, and it turned this place into the most famous cabaret in the world. The painter Toulouse-Lautrec, himself a fixture here, put its dancers and its dazzle into posters that are now as famous as the club itself.

More than a century on, it's still running, still putting on the spectacle, though the streets around it have slid from bohemian to frankly seedy in places. Look past that to the windmill.

Because that's the quiet story here: the most famous nightspot on earth wears a counterfeit windmill to honour the real ones it helped replace. Glamour built right on top of a vanished rural world — which is Montmartre in a single rooftop.
