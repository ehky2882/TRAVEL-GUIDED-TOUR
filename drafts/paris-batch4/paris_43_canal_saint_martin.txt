You're beside the Canal Saint-Martin, one of the most relaxed and fashionable corners of Paris today — young crowds picnicking on the stone banks, arched iron footbridges crossing the water, leafy trees leaning over the locks. It feels effortlessly cool. But it began as something completely unglamorous: a piece of plumbing.

The canal was dug in the early eighteen hundreds, under Napoleon, to solve a basic and urgent problem — Paris didn't have enough clean water, and disease spread easily through the crowded city. This waterway was built to carry fresh water in, and later to move goods: grain, building stone and timber, hauled through on barges. It was infrastructure, plain and useful, lined with warehouses and working docks. Not romantic in the slightest.

It nearly didn't survive. By the middle of the twentieth century the canal was seen as obsolete and in the way, and there were serious plans to pave the whole thing over and run a road along the top. Parts of it were in fact covered. It came surprisingly close to vanishing under traffic altogether — and then the plans stalled, the canal hung on, and slowly the neighbourhood around it became one of the most sought-after in the city.

So watch the water for a few minutes, and you might catch the best free show here: a boat passing through one of the locks. The gates close, the water level slowly rises or drops several metres to lift or lower the boat to the next stretch, and then the far gates swing open and it glides on. It's a two-hundred-year-old machine, still working exactly as it was designed to.

That's the charm of the place, once you know it: the coolest spot in Paris is a humble water pipe that was almost buried under a road, saved by delay and good luck, now living its best life. Find a spot on the bank and join in.
