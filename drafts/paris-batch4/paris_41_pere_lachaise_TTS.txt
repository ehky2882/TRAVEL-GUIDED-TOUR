You're at the gates of pair lashez, the most famous cemetery in the world — and one of the strangest success stories in Paris. It's laid out like a small city: cobbled lanes, street signs, tombs like little houses lining the avenues, all of it spilling across a green hillside. People come from everywhere to wander it. But when it opened, in eighteen oh four, almost nobody wanted to be buried here.

The problem was location. Back then this was well outside the city, and Parisians thought it too remote, too unfashionable, to be caught dead in — literally. The plots weren't selling. So the authorities did something brilliant and slightly absurd: they ran a marketing campaign using celebrities. They dug up the remains of several beloved famous figures — a revered playwright, a famous poet, and a pair of legendary medieval lovers whose tragic romance everyone knew — and reburied them here with great ceremony. The message was clear: if it's good enough for them, it's good enough for you.

It worked spectacularly. Suddenly a plot in pair lashez was the fashionable place to spend eternity, and it has been ever since. Over the years it filled with the famous. The composer Frédéric shoh-pan lies here; so does the American rock singer Jim Morrison, whose grave still draws quiet crowds; the Irish writer Oscar Wilde, whose tomb was kissed so often, in lipstick, that it had to be shielded behind glass; and aydeet pee-af, the beloved singer of the Paris streets. People arrive with maps to find them, the way you'd tour a city's landmarks.

So as you walk in, remember what you're seeing: not only a graveyard, but a place that became desirable through sheer clever salesmanship — a cemetery that sold itself with stars and never stopped. Take a lane at random and let yourself get a little lost. That's the only way to do it, and the quiet up here, above the city, is its own reward.
