You're at the entrance to the Catacombs, and what waits below is one of the most extraordinary and sobering places in Paris: the carefully arranged bones of some six million people, stretching through tunnels deep beneath the streets. To understand how they came to be here, you have to know two things about the ground under this city.

First, Paris is partly built from itself. For centuries the limestone that makes up so many of the city's buildings was quarried from directly beneath it, leaving a vast honeycomb of tunnels and empty galleries underground. The stone went up to make the city; the holes stayed below.

Second, by the late seventeen hundreds the city's old cemeteries were catastrophically full — overcrowded for generations, to the point that they had become a genuine danger to public health. The authorities needed somewhere to move the dead of centuries past. And there, conveniently, were the empty quarries. So over many years, working at night, the bones of millions were lifted from the overflowing graveyards and carried down into the old tunnels.

What makes it unforgettable is what was done with them. The bones weren't simply piled in heaps. They were stacked and arranged into neat, deliberate walls — long rows of skulls and limb bones set in patterns, lining the passages, turning a mass grave into something solemn and almost architectural. Above the doorway into the main ossuary, a carved inscription warns those who enter that this is the empire of the dead.

It's quiet down there, and cool, and it asks something of you. This isn't really a spectacle; it's a confrontation — the whole population of a city, anonymous and equal at last, quietly holding up the streets you walked in on. Go down respectfully. Six million people are a great many to stand among.
