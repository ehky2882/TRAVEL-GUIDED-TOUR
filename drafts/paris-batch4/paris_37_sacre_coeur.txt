You're at the foot of Sacré-Cœur, the great white domed church crowning the hill of Montmartre, the highest natural point in Paris. It looks ancient and timeless up there against the sky — and it's actually one of the youngest major monuments in the city. Building only began in the eighteen seventies, and it wasn't finished until just before the First World War.

And the reason it was built is heavier than its sugar-white domes suggest. In 1870 France suffered a catastrophic defeat in a war with Prussia, and in the bitter aftermath Paris tore itself apart in a brief, bloody civil uprising. Out of that double wound — a humiliating defeat abroad and Parisians turning on Parisians at home — came a movement to raise a great church as an act of national penance: an offering to ask forgiveness, to atone for what its backers saw as the nation's sins. Sacré-Cœur is, in its origins, a monument of guilt and mourning, built on the very hill where some of that internal conflict had begun.

That history still makes it quietly contested. To some it's a place of peace and pilgrimage; to others it has always been a victor's monument planted over a wound. Both readings live here together, and it's worth carrying both as you climb the steps.

Now, the thing everyone notices and few can explain: why is it so impossibly white, when every other old stone building in Paris is grey? It's a trick of the stone. The basilica is built from a special pale stone that reacts with rainwater and releases a white, chalky substance as it weathers. So instead of darkening with grime over the years like ordinary stone, Sacré-Cœur effectively washes itself whiter every time it rains. The dirtier the air, the harder it scrubs.

Turn around before you go in. From these steps all of Paris spreads out below you, flat and grey and endless, with the white church glowing at your back. The view is free, and it's one of the great ones.
