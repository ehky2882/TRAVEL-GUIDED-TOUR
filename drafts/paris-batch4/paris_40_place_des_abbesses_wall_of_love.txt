You're in Place des Abbesses, and if the rest of Montmartre felt like a crowded postcard, this is where it turns back into a village. Quieter streets, local cafés, a leafy square — this is the Montmartre that Parisians actually live in.

Start with the entrance to the Métro station here, because it's a small treasure. It's one of the original Art Nouveau station entrances designed by Hector Guimard around 1900 — green cast iron curling like plant stems, with a delicate glass canopy overhead shaped like a dragonfly's wing. Most of these were torn out over the decades; only a couple of the fully glass-roofed ones survive in the whole city, and this is one of them. Parisians once mocked them as ugly; now they're protected treasures and a symbol of the era. Down below, by the way, is the deepest station in Paris — a long spiral down into the hill.

Now find the small park just off the square, and in it, a wall: the Wall of Love. It's a panel of dark blue tiles covered in two words — "I love you" — handwritten more than three hundred times, in around two hundred and fifty different languages and dialects, from the everyday to the nearly extinct. A calligrapher and a musician collected the phrase from speakers all over the world and laid them out together on this single wall. Find your own language somewhere in the grid; almost everyone can.

There's something fitting about it sitting up here. Montmartre has spent more than a century being the city's romantic imagination — the hill of artists and lovers and late nights. A wall that says "I love you" in nearly every tongue on earth belongs exactly here.

So linger in this square a while. This is the village the tour groups miss, and it's the best of the hill.
