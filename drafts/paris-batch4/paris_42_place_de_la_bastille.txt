You're in Place de la Bastille, standing on the most famous spot in the history of the French Revolution — and there is almost nothing here to see. That's the point, and it's worth understanding.

On the fourteenth of July, 1789, a Paris crowd stormed the Bastille, a great medieval fortress-prison that stood right here. It was the spark of the Revolution, the day France still celebrates as its national holiday. You'd expect the building to be preserved, revered, queued around the block. But it isn't here at all. Within months of being stormed, the Bastille was completely demolished — pulled down stone by stone by the revolutionaries themselves, who wanted every trace of the hated royal prison gone. The stones were carted off; some were even carved into little souvenir models of the fortress and sold. The most famous building of the Revolution was erased by the Revolution.

All that marks where it stood is a line of paler stones set into the pavement, tracing the old foundations. Look down, and you can walk the outline of the vanished fortress. The birthplace of modern France is, fittingly, an absence — a shape in the ground where a building used to be.

Now look up at the tall bronze column in the centre of the square, with the gilded winged figure leaping from the top. Here's the twist most people get wrong: that column has nothing to do with 1789. It commemorates a completely different revolution — an uprising in 1830, decades later, that toppled another king. So the monument that does stand in Place de la Bastille honours a different revolution entirely, while the one everyone comes here for left no monument at all.

It's a strange, very French lesson in memory. Stand in the middle, find the outline in the stones, and picture the fortress that isn't there.
