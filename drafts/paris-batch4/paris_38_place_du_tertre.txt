You're in Place du Tertre, a small square at the top of Montmartre, and right now it's probably packed — easels everywhere, artists offering to paint your portrait or snip your silhouette, crowds threading between the café tables. It can feel like a tourist trap, and in a sense it is. But stand still a moment, because the ground under all of this is the real thing.

A little over a century ago, Montmartre wasn't part of the busy city at all. It was a half-rural village on a hill, covered in windmills and vineyards, with cheap rooms and cheaper wine — which is exactly why poor young artists flocked to it. In the years around 1900, some of the most important painters of the modern age lived and worked within a few streets of where you're standing: names that now fill the world's great museums learned their craft up here, when Montmartre was scruffy and affordable and crackling with new ideas. Down the hill, in a ramshackle warren of artists' studios, Picasso painted the picture that cracked European art wide open.

Then fame did what fame does. The artists became famous, the rents climbed, the village was swallowed by the city, and the real bohemia scattered. What's left in this square is, in a way, a performance of that vanished world — painters working for tourists where geniuses once worked for themselves.

But don't be too cynical about it. They really were here; this really was the place. And if you want proof that it was once countryside, walk a couple of streets over and find it: a small working vineyard still grows on the hillside, rows of vines in the middle of the city, the last green ghost of the rural village Montmartre used to be.

So enjoy the square for what it is, then go find the vineyard for what it was. Both are true at once.
