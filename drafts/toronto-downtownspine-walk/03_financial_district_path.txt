Now you're at King and Bay, the actual crossroads of Canadian money, standing under a cluster of plain black towers that look, at first glance, like the most anonymous buildings on the walk. Give them a second.

These are the towers of the TD Centre, and they were the last great work of Mies van der Rohe — the German-born architect whose entire philosophy fit into three words: less is more. No ornament, no flourish, nothing carved into the stone, because there's barely any stone at all — just black steel, bronze-tinted glass, and proportions so exact it took a genius to make them look like nothing in particular. When they went up in the 1960s, they swept away the older downtown that stood here and announced the modern city. This is the future side of the walk — the part that didn't keep the old thing.

But look down. Beneath your feet, and beneath most of the blocks around you, runs the PATH: a whole second downtown, underground. Roughly thirty kilometres of climate-controlled tunnels and shopping concourses, linking the towers so that office workers can cross the core in January without ever reaching for a coat. For years it was billed as the largest underground shopping complex in the world — a title it eventually lost, on length, to Montreal's rival network, which is a fact the city has decided not to dwell on.

So even here, on the future side, the old instinct sneaks back in: the city quietly built a second version of itself and tucked it underneath the first.

Keep heading north up Bay Street toward Queen, until the towers open onto a wide public square with a low, curved concrete city hall at the back of it. That's Nathan Phillips Square.
