You've walked out of the canyon of towers into open air: a big public square, a reflecting pool that turns into a skating rink when it freezes, a run of concrete arches, and that unmistakable three dimensional sign spelling out the city's name, which half the people here are busy photographing themselves in front of. This is Nathan Phillips Square, and the thing at the back of it is the city hall Toronto built when it wanted to feel modern.

Look at it properly. Two curved towers, one taller than the other, cupped around a low round chamber in the middle — the council chamber, which from above looks a little like an eye held between two parentheses. It's the opposite of everything else on this walk: no columns, no stone temple, nothing pretending to be older than it is. A Finnish architect named vilyo revell won an international competition to design it, beating out hundreds of entries from around the world, and when it opened in nineteen sixty-five it was the city announcing, in poured concrete, that it meant to be a serious modern place.

Off to one side of the square is the Spirit Garden, a memorial to the survivors of Canada's residential schools — a quieter, more recent addition to a space that is meant, above all, to belong to everyone.

Because that's what this square really is. The money on this walk has mostly been private, locked inside towers and vaults, but this ground is public: the city's living room, its skating rink, its protest site, its backdrop.

Now, one last thing — and it's the whole point of the walk.

Turn around, and look across Bay Street. That old sandstone building with the great clock tower, glaring back at this one? That's Old City Hall.
