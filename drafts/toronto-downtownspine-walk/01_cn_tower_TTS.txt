It's five hundred and fifty-three metres to the top, and for thirty-two years — from the mid nineteen seventies until two thousand and seven, when a tower in Dubai finally passed it — this was the tallest free-standing structure anywhere on earth. It held that title longer than most records survive, and it lost it quietly, the way this city tends to lose things: without much fuss.

Here's the part that usually gets left out. The see en tower wasn't built to be a landmark. It was built to be an antenna. By the nineteen seventies the downtown office towers had grown tall enough to block television and radio signals, and the railway — Canadian National, the see en in the name — happened to own a vast stretch of emptied-out rail land right here, so it poured the tallest broadcast mast it could into concrete. A fix for bad reception. The fact that it became the thing every postcard and every establishing shot reaches for whenever a film wants you to know you're in Toronto — that was never really the plan.

The railway sold it years ago, and nobody could bring themselves to change the name, so the see en quietly stopped standing for Canadian National and started standing for, more or less, nothing in particular. It's just the tower now.
