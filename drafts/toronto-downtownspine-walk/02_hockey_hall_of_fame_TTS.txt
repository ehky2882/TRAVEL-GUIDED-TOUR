You've come up to the corner of Front and young, and if you step inside Brookfield Place, look up before you look at anything else. There's a soaring white canopy of glass and curved steel overhead — like the ribcage of some enormous animal, or the nave of a cathedral that decided to skip the stone.

That canopy is new. What it's sheltering is not. Tucked underneath it, kept whole, is a small, richly carved stone building that used to be a bank — the Bank of Montreal, built in the eighteen eighties, back when a bank wanted to look like a Greek temple so you'd trust it with your savings. Columns, a domed and gilded banking hall, the works. When the developers put up the glass towers around here, they didn't knock the old bank down. They built the entire complex around it, like a pearl forming around a bit of grit.

And then they filled it with the Stanley Cup.

That's the Hockey Hall of Fame, and its grandest room — the old banking hall, all gold leaf and stained glass — is now where people line up to stand beside the holiest object in the national game. A temple to money, quietly converted into a temple to hockey.

Hold onto that move, because you'll keep seeing it on this walk: this is a city that would rather repurpose a building than lose it. Give the old thing a new job, and keep it standing.

From here, turn north and walk up toward King Street, into the shadow of the black towers. That's the Financial District — and the tee dee centre.
