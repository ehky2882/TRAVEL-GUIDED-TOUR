Cross Bay Street, or simply stand where you are and take it in from the square: a huge Romanesque pile of rose and brown stone, arched and turreted, with a clock tower rising more than a hundred metres over the street and pointing straight down the length of Bay like it owns the view. Which, for a long time, it did.

This is Old City Hall, finished in 1899, and when it opened it was one of the largest civic buildings on the continent — the work of an architect named E.J. Lennox, who, after years of fighting the councillors over the building's ballooning cost, took a small revenge in stone: he had his own name carved into the band of stonework running under the eaves, letter by letter, because the city had refused him a plaque. Look closely under the roofline and it's still up there — the architect signing his own building out of spite.

Here's why it's the end of the walk. When the space-age city hall behind you opened in the 1960s, this one was meant to come down. There were real plans to demolish it and drop a shopping centre on the site, keeping perhaps the clock tower as a keepsake. It was saved by a stubborn band of citizens who refused to let it go, and for the next half a century it served as the city's main courthouse.

Now even the courts have moved out — some of them, fittingly, into the rebuilt St. Lawrence Market North a few blocks east — and the building sits empty: a national historic site with no settled job, while the city argues gently about what it ought to become — a museum, perhaps, or a library, or a hall for weddings — and nobody's quite decided.

[beat]

So here they stand. The hall the city built to replace this one, and this one, which the city could never quite bring itself to lose — facing each other across a single street, old stone and new concrete, an argument nobody ever really won. That standoff might be the most Toronto thing in the whole downtown: a city that refuses to choose between its past and its future, and makes them share a corner instead, and look each other in the eye.

Stay a moment, and let them.
