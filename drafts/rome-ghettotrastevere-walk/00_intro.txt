Rome exists because of its river — and you can spend an entire day in the centre of the city without seeing it once. That vanishing act is real, it was deliberate, and this walk is about undoing it.

We're going to the water. From Largo di Torre Argentina we head south into the old Jewish Ghetto, cross the Tiber on the oldest bridge in the city, pause on the island in midstream, come ashore in Trastevere — the neighbourhood whose whole name means "across the river" — and finish on the Janiculum terrace, looking down on everything we've just walked. Along the way, keep an eye out for the massive stone walls that hold the river in its channel. They're the reason you never see the Tiber until you're standing on top of it, and by the end of the walk you'll know their story too.

Practical notes: it's about two and a half kilometres, flat and easy until the very end — the last stretch climbs the Janiculum hill, a stiff fifteen to twenty minutes uphill, and there's no shame in taking a taxi up from Trastevere instead. The route crosses two bridges; the second carries a little traffic, so keep to the side. Otherwise it's lanes, squares and riverbank all the way.

Start at Largo di Torre Argentina, and make for the railing overlooking the ruins.
