For more than three centuries, the people of this street were locked in at night.

Walk vee ah del por tee koh doht tah vyah slowly, toward the river. In fifteen fifty-five, a papal decree confined Rome's Jewish community — one of the oldest communities of any kind in Europe, present in this city since before the Caesars — to a few walled hectares right here, with gates that closed at dusk. And the ground chosen for the ghetto was not neutral ground. This was the lowest land in Rome, hard against the tee ber, and in the centuries before the flood walls the river came calling almost every winter — into the streets, into the lower rooms of the crowded houses, sometimes storeys deep. The walls kept people in; nothing kept the water out. Poverty was policy here, and the river was part of the sentence.

The gates and walls finally came down in the eighteen eighties — the same decade, as it happens, that the city began raising the great embankments that would end the floods for good. One set of walls fell as the other rose.

One thing more, and it asks for a quieter voice. The community that lives on this street has prayed in this city for more than two thousand years, and it endured its darkest morning in October of nineteen forty-three, when the occupying German forces came here for its people. It is still here. Carry that fact gently as you look around you now — because what you're walking through today is not a memorial but a living neighbourhood, loud with restaurant tables, bakeries and kids on bikes, and its persistence is the point.

At the end of the street, by the synagogue, turn toward the river. The small stone bridge ahead of you is the oldest in Rome, and it's ours: we cross here, to the island.
