trahs teh veh reh means, literally, "across the tee ber" — and the name has always been less an address than an attitude.

Come into the piazza and find a spot near the fountain, which has been marked on maps of this city since the fourteen hundreds. In ancient times this bank was the workers' side of the river — sailors, dockers, millers, migrants from every port the empire traded with — and it has kept a stubborn sense of itself ever since: its own accent, its own swagger, its own conviction that the real Rome lives over here, and that everything across the water is merely the city. The locals even have a word for it. Every July, this neighbourhood throws itself a festival called the fess tah deh noh ahn tree — the feast of "we others" — which is about as clear as an identity gets: one week a year, officially, trahs teh veh reh celebrates not being the rest of Rome.

And the festival's centrepiece belongs to this walk. Its patroness is a statue of the Madonna known as the fyoo mah roh lah — "of the river" — because, as the story goes, fishermen found her floating at the tee ber's mouth after a storm, some five centuries back, and brought her home to trahs teh veh reh. Take the legend as a legend, but notice what it says: even the neighbourhood's miracle came out of the water. On this side of Rome, the river was never scenery. It was the employer, the road, and occasionally the deliverer of small wonders.

The church on the piazza, sahn tah mah ree ah een trahs teh veh reh, is one of the oldest in the city, and its golden facade mosaics are the square's evening entertainment — they catch fire when the light goes low. It's been said that if trahs teh veh reh were a town on its own hill somewhere, this would be its cathedral square, and standing here, that's exactly how it feels.

Now for the climb. Take vee ah dehl lah pahl yah out of the piazza and follow the signs uphill toward the jah nee koh loh — fifteen to twenty minutes of switchbacks, or a quick taxi — and meet me at the terrace on top.
