Come to the railing and look down. The temples at the bottom of that pit stand on the ground level of the Roman republic — several metres below your shoes. The oldest of them was already ancient when the empire began; the four of them together are among the oldest things you can see anywhere in this city, and to see them, you look down. Always down.

It's worth asking why. Part of the answer is ordinary: cities rise on their own rubble, and Rome has been rebuilding on top of itself for longer than almost anywhere. But here there's another culprit, and it's the one this walk is about. You're standing in the ancient flood plain of the Tiber, and for two thousand years, every time the river rose — which was often — it left behind a layer of silt and debris when it fell. Flood by flood, century by century, the river quietly raised the street. The Rome of the republic didn't sink. The city grew up over it, on ground the Tiber helped deliver.

So this sunken square makes a fitting first stop: we're setting off to find a river you can already see the fingerprints of, several metres deep, right here in the middle of town. The temples, for the record, are so old that nobody has ever settled which gods most of them belonged to — they're lettered A to D, like exhibits — and they only came back to light in the demolitions of the 1920s, when a modern square was cut through and the republic turned up underneath.

Now let's go find the culprit. Walk south, away from the tram stops, and in a few minutes the lanes will bring you to Via del Portico d'Ottavia — the main street of the old Jewish Ghetto.
