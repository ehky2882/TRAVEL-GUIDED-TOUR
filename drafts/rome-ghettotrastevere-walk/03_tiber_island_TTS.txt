Sixty-two years before the birth of Christ, a Roman official named loo shus fah brish us built this bridge — and carved his name into it, more than once, so that nobody would ever forget who was responsible. Two thousand years of feet have crossed it since, and yours are next.

Walk it slowly. Halfway across, stop at the parapet and look at the river — your first proper sight of it all day. The island splits the tee ber into two narrow channels, and that, more than anything else, is why there's a city here at all. A wide river is a wall; a river split by an island is a doorway. Long before Rome had a name, the trade paths of central Italy — salt from the coast, herds and goods from the hills — funnelled toward this one easy crossing, and the settlement that controlled it never looked back. Emperors, forums, basilicas: all of it downstream, historically speaking, of the simple fact that this is where you could get across.

Two details before you move on. On the upstream face of the bridge's central pier there's a small arched opening — a flood vent, cut by builders who knew this river's temper and built for it, which is no small part of why the bridge is still here. And the weathered stone heads on the parapet — faces worn almost to nothing — have been watching crossings since antiquity.

On the island itself, most of what you see today is a working hospital; the little piazza by the church is a good place to stand a moment with water on both sides of you. Then take the second bridge, pon teh chess tee oh — rebuilt in the eighteen eighties when the flood walls went in, so mind the occasional car — and come ashore in trahs teh veh reh. The piazza we want, sahn tah mah ree ah een trahs teh veh reh, is a few minutes into the lanes.
