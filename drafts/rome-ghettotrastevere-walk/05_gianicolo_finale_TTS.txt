However you got up here — on foot, by taxi, breathing hard or not — take a moment at the balustrade before you look at anything in particular. This is the widest view in Rome: the whole city laid out below you, dome by dome, hill by hill, all the way to the mountains on a clear day.

Now try something. Find the river.

Go ahead — you've just spent the afternoon walking to it, along it and across it, so you know roughly where it runs. And yet from up here the tee ber is nearly invisible: no glint of water, just a winding line of plane trees threading through the rooftops. A city built by a river has managed to hide it — and that was a choice. In December of eighteen seventy, weeks after Rome became the capital of a newly united Italy, the tee ber rose into one of the worst floods anyone could remember, drowning the streets of the young capital metres deep. The new state answered with the biggest public works project the city had seen since antiquity: the moo rah lyoh nee, the great embankment walls, kilometres of them, high and sheer. They worked. The floods that had tormented Rome for twenty-five centuries — that raised the streets at lar goh ar jen tee nah, that ran through the lower rooms of the Ghetto — simply stopped.

But the cure had a cost. The walls that saved the city also severed it from the water: the ports, the boatmen, the floating mills, the whole riverside world that trahs teh veh reh lived off — gone within a generation, and the river sank out of Rome's daily sight, down between its stone banks. Nobody misses the floods. Some Romans, to this day, miss the river.

So stay a while, up here where you can see both truths at once — the safe, dry, magnificent city, and the thin green line it owes everything to and no longer looks at. If it's coming up on noon, don't let the cannon below the terrace startle you; it fires once a day, on schedule. Otherwise there's nothing you need to do but watch the light move across Rome — and keep the river company a little longer. Today, you're one of the few who did.
