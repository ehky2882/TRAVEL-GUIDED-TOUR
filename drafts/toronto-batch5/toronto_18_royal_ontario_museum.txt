Look up at the corner of Bloor Street West and Avenue Road, at the spot where this building seems to be having an argument with itself.

On one side, dark stone, carved detail, the calm face of a building from 1914 that was meant to look like a castle and largely succeeded. On the other, jammed right into it, a burst of angled glass and brushed aluminum that looks like it crashed in from somewhere else and stuck. That's the Michael Lee-Chin Crystal, and the seam where the two meet is the most honest thing on the block.

Start with the old building. The Royal Ontario Museum opened here in 1914, and for most of a century it did what grand museums did — it kept the world's cultures behind heavy walls and asked you to come in quietly. Today it holds something like six million objects, which makes it the largest museum in the country. For decades it was nicknamed, not always kindly, the castle.

Then, in 2007, the castle grew a crystal. The architect was Daniel Libeskind, chosen from fifty finalists, and his idea was that the addition should echo the gem-and-mineral collection inside — five interlocking volumes, a quarter glass and three-quarters aluminum, almost no right angles, and exactly one vertical wall in the whole thing. The canted walls lean out over the sidewalk and never quite touch the old stone.

Here's the part the city still argues about. When it opened, one major critic called it a monument; another said it raged at the world. Some lists put it among the ugliest buildings anywhere. It leaked that first winter.

[beat]

Most cities bury the fight over what they should look like. Toronto welded its argument to the corner of Bloor and left it there, in glass, for everyone to take a side.

So take one. Stand at the seam, where 1914 meets 2007, and notice that the old building looks better for the company — and the Crystal looks better for having something to crash into. Whatever you decide, you're not walking past it. Nobody does.
