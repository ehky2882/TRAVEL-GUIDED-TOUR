You're on Dundas Street West, standing under a wall of glass and wood that curves along the block like the hull of an upturned boat. Find the long timber-and-glass front — that's the Art Gallery of Ontario, and the architect who shaped it grew up a few blocks from where you're standing.

His name is Frank Gehry. You may know his other work: the titanium swoops of Bilbao, the steel sails of the Walt Disney Concert Hall in Los Angeles — buildings that announce themselves from a mile off. He was born in Toronto, into a Jewish family in a downtown immigrant neighbourhood, played as a kid in the park just behind this building, and first saw real art inside it when he was eight years old. When the gallery asked him to remake it, he called the job an emotional experience. It was his first building in Canada.

Everyone expected a Bilbao. What he built instead was restraint. Run your eye along that front — a corridor of warm Douglas fir and glass, about a hundred and eighty metres long, that the critics nicknamed the belly of a whale. Step inside later and you'll find a spiral staircase that bursts straight up through a glass roof and keeps climbing outside the building. Around the back, a wing clad in blue titanium leans over the park.

The whole thing was built to hold one extraordinary gift — the collection of Ken Thomson: more than two thousand works, everything from tiny ship models to a rediscovered Rubens. Tucked at the heart of it all is the Grange, an 1817 house that was the gallery's very first home.

[beat]

Here's the quiet genius of it. The old gallery looked inward, the way museums do, keeping your eyes on the walls. Gehry turned it to face the city — glass everywhere, the rooftops of Chinatown pressing against the windows. He could have built a spectacle. He came home and built something that looks at its hometown instead.

One last thing, out front: a great bronze by Henry Moore — the same sculptor whose Archer sits over at City Hall. Stand here a moment before you go in. This is the rare landmark more interested in the view out than the view of itself.
