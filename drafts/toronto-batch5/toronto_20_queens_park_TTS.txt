Stand on the south lawn of Queen's Park, with University Avenue running away behind you, and look north at the long pink building filling the end of the view. Torontonians call it the Pink Palace, after the rose coloured sandstone hauled in from the Credit Valley. It's the seat of Ontario's government, and it started with a scandal.

The province ran a competition for the design. One of the judges was an architect named Richard wait, working out of Buffalo. When the entries came in, the commission went to, wait. One of the judges. Ontario's architects were furious, and to make it worse, plenty of people complained the winning design looked too American. The building meant to embody the province had a conflict of interest at its foundation and an accent from across the border.

It went up anyway, between eighteen eighty-six and eighteen ninety-two, and opened in eighteen ninety-three. Look at the stonework. Those unusually large windows are only possible because the whole thing hangs on an iron frame hidden inside. The walls hold something like ten and a half million bricks, and most of them were made by prisoners. And if that heavy, rounded, fortress-like style looks familiar, it should: Old City Hall, a few blocks south, was built in almost exactly this manner six years later.

Then, in nineteen oh nine, fire gutted the west wing. The man brought in to rebuild it was E.J. Lennox, the same architect who carved his enemies' faces into Old City Hall, and he lined the new interior in pale Italian marble, so the two halves of the building still don't quite match.

Now the detail worth the walk. Carved over the legislative chamber inside, in Latin, is the building's motto: hear the other side. It sits above a room that has spent more than a century as one of the loudest, most partisan in the country.

The land under your feet, by the way, is leased from the university next door for almost nothing, a peppercorn deal, generations old. Stay a minute. Watch the pink stone go properly rose as the light drops. A building that began in an argument, still standing, still arguing. It suits the place.
