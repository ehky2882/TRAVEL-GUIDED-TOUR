Before anything else, look at the building, because whatever you think of what happens inside it, the outside is one of the finest things of its kind in Madrid. This is las ventas, the largest bullring in Spain and, by most reckonings, the most important in the world. And the argument about what it's for is one you should hear both sides of.

Start with the brick. The style is called nayo moodehar, a Spanish revival of the patterns Muslim craftsmen left across the peninsula centuries ago, all horseshoe arches and woven brickwork, set with glazed ceramic tiles. Look closely and the tiles carry the coats of arms of the Spanish provinces, one after another around the ring. It was finished in nineteen twenty-nine and opened with a charity bullfight in nineteen thirty-one, and it holds close to twenty-four thousand people in a perfect circle sixty metres across. Spaniards call it the cathedral, the place a bullfighter has to succeed to truly arrive.

And here's the thing to sit with, because Spain itself is sitting with it. To one side, bullfighting is defended as an art form and a piece of intangible heritage, protected by national law, drawing full houses here every day during the san iseedro fair in May and June. To the other, it's increasingly opposed as cruelty: attendance has fallen, some regions have turned away from it, and in twenty twenty-four the national government scrapped its official bullfighting prize. The country is genuinely divided, and the building stands right on the fault line, admired and contested in the same breath.

One detail before you go, and it captures the whole strange world of this place. Out front, among the statues of famous matadors, stands a monument to a Scottish scientist: Alexander Fleming, who discovered penicillin. The bullfighters paid for it themselves, because the antibiotic he found saved the lives of countless men wounded in this ring. A figure of a matador raises his cap to the doctor in thanks. Whatever you make of the spectacle, that small bronze salute tells you how seriously, and how literally, this was always a matter of life and death.
