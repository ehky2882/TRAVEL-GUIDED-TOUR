Go gently here. This is a cemetery, still in use, and people come to visit graves that matter to them. But it's also one of the most extraordinary places in London, and it's worth understanding why it looks the way it does.

Highgate opened in eighteen thirty-nine, and to understand it you have to know what came before it. By the eighteen thirties London's old parish churchyards were catastrophically full — bodies stacked beyond any decency, a genuine public health crisis. The answer was a ring of huge new private cemeteries on the city's edge, landscaped like parks, and Highgate became the most fashionable of them all. People didn't just bury their dead here. They came to walk, to be seen, to admire the monuments. Death, to the Victorians, was something you dressed up and displayed.

You can read that everywhere you look. The tombs are theatrical — broken columns, draped urns, sleeping stone angels, a whole grammar of mourning carved in marble. The most famous stretch is the Egyptian Avenue, a row of tombs built like the entrance to a pharaoh's chamber, leading to a sunken circle of vaults ringed by a great cedar tree. It's overgrown now, half-reclaimed by ivy and roots, and that wildness is part of why people love it — a Victorian fantasy of death slowly being taken back by the living world.

There are famous people resting here — the one most visitors look for is Karl Marx, whose great bearded bust sits on the eastern side, still drawing visitors from across the world more than a century after he died a few streets away in poverty. But try not to let it become a celebrity hunt. The quieter graves matter just as much.

What this place really preserves is an attitude — a century that believed grief deserved grandeur, that the dead should be remembered out loud, in stone, under trees. Walk slowly. Read a few of the ordinary names, not just the famous one. The whole point of a garden cemetery was that remembering could be beautiful rather than only sad, and standing here among the ivy and the leaning angels, you can feel them having got that right.
