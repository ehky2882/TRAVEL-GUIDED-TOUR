Find a spot near the edge of the park where you can see one of the long white terraces — a whole row of grand houses behind a single unbroken facade, columns and pediments running the length of the street, painted the colour of wedding cake.

Walk up close to one, and look carefully. It's a fake. Or rather, it's theatre.

These terraces were the work of John Nash, the favourite architect of the Prince Regent — the future George the Fourth — in the early 1800s. The Prince wanted a grand new park on the edge of town, ringed with palaces fit for the aristocracy, with a route running straight down to his own residence. Nash gave him exactly that, on paper. What actually got built is cleverer and slightly more dishonest. Behind those palatial fronts aren't single great houses at all — they're rows of ordinary terraced homes, dressed up to look, from across the park, like one enormous palace.

That's the trick worth seeing. Nash wasn't really designing buildings here so much as designing a view. He understood that almost nobody would ever stand where you're standing and inspect the brickwork — they'd see it from a carriage, from across the lawns, at a distance, where the columns and the symmetry read as sheer magnificence. So that's where he put all the money: on the surface, the silhouette, the bit you see from far away.

*[beat]*

Now turn and look at the park itself, because it's the other half of the same idea. It feels wild and rolling and natural, the lake curving off out of sight, trees placed as if they simply grew there. None of it is accidental. Every tree, every bend in the water, every gap that lets a terrace appear suddenly through the leaves was composed, the way a painter composes a landscape.

So this whole place is a stage set two hundred years old — the grandest backdrop in London, built to be admired from exactly the middle distance you're probably standing in. Step back. Let the terrace blur slightly into one great white sweep. That's the view Nash actually built. Up close was never the point.
