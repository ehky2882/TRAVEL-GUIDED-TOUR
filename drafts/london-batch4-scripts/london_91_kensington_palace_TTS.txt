From the gardens, Kensington Palace doesn't look much like a palace. No great dome, no vast facade — just a long, handsome brick house, the kind a very rich family might live in, set among lawns and avenues of trees. That modesty is the first clue to what this place actually is.

For three hundred years, Kensington has been where the royal family puts the relatives. The grand state business happens at Buckingham Palace, a couple of miles east. Kensington is the home — divided up into apartments, occupied by princes and princesses and minor royals, generation after generation. King George the Fifth is supposed to have called it the aunt heap, and the nickname stuck, because that's exactly what it was: a comfortable brick warren full of the wider family.

But one resident made it a place of pilgrimage. Diana, Princess of Wales, lived here for years, and after her death in nineteen ninety-seven the gates and railings in front of you vanished entirely under a sea of flowers — bouquets stacked metres deep, left by hundreds of thousands of strangers. If you stand at the main gates and picture them buried in cellophane and handwritten cards, you're picturing one of the most extraordinary outpourings of public grief Britain has ever seen.

Walk round to find the Sunken Garden, because it's the loveliest corner and it carries that story forward. It's a formal garden, planted in tiers around a still pool, blazing with colour in summer — and it was redesigned and replanted as a memorial, with a statue of Diana now standing in it. A quiet, deliberately beautiful place made out of remembering one person.

So here's Kensington, in a sentence: the least palatial of the royal palaces, the family's domestic back-stage for three centuries, that became, almost by accident, a national shrine. Most of it is still lived in, behind those windows, by people you'd recognise from the news. Have a wander through the parts that are open, then sit by the round pond in the gardens. The whole point of this place was always that it felt like somewhere people actually lived. It still does.
