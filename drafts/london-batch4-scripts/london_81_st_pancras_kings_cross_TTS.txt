Stand on the Euston Road, on the south side, and look up at the front of saint pankruss station — the red-brick palace with the spires and the clock tower, the one that looks far too grand to be somewhere you catch a train.

That's the point. It was meant to.

When it went up in the eighteen sixties, the railway companies were the richest, most aggressive businesses in Britain, and the Midland Railway wanted a London terminus that said so out loud. So they hired George Gilbert Scott, the most fashionable Gothic architect of the day, and told him to build a hotel that would shame every rival into silence. The result is the Midland Grand — all those pinnacles and pointed arches and that improbable roofline, Gothic detail borrowed from Venetian palaces and Flemish cloth halls and stacked up in front of a railway shed.

Look past the hotel for a moment, at the great curved roof of the shed behind it. When William Barlow threw that single span of iron and glass over the platforms, it was the widest enclosed space anywhere in the world. The fairy-tale front and the engineering miracle behind it — that's the whole Victorian century standing in one place.

Now here's the part that should stop you. By the nineteen sixties all of this had fallen so far out of fashion that there were serious plans to pull it down. yooston station, just up the road, had its famous arch demolished in nineteen sixty-two, and saint pankruss looked like it was next in the queue.

What saved it was, more or less, one man — the poet John betchuhmun, who loved Victorian buildings when almost nobody else did, and who led the fight to keep this one standing. There's a statue of him inside now, up on the platform concourse, cap in hand, gazing up at the roof he helped rescue.

So when you go in — and you should — head up to the platform level and find him. A slightly rumpled man in bronze, looking up. It's easy to walk straight past him on the way to a train, but he's the reason there's anything here worth walking past.
