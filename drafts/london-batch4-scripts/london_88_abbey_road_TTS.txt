On the eighth of August, nineteen sixty-nine, four members of the Beatles walked across this exact crossing while a photographer stood on a stepladder in the road and took six frames in ten minutes. One of them became the cover of the album they named after the street: Abbey Road. No title on the sleeve, no band name — just four men walking. It didn't need words.

Now turn and look at the building behind it, the white Georgian townhouse set back from the road. That's Abbey Road Studios, and it's the real reason any of this happened. It's one of the oldest purpose-built recording studios in the world, opened in nineteen thirty-one, and the Beatles recorded most of their music inside it. The crossing is famous because the studio was there, and the studio is still working — bands and film composers record here today, which is why you can't go in.

Here's the small modern absurdity worth knowing. There is a webcam, pointed at this crossing, live, twenty-four hours a day. At any moment, people anywhere in the world can be watching this exact spot — watching you, if you step out onto it now. The crossing itself is so beloved that it's been given protected status, a piece of ordinary road infrastructure listed for its cultural importance, which is a very strange and very British thing to do to some paint.
