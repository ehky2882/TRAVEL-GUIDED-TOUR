You've walked up through Hampstead Heath, and here at the top, white against the green, is Kenwood House. Before you go in, know the best thing about it: it's free, and inside hangs one of the most valuable paintings in Britain, which you can walk up to without paying a penny.

But take the house in first. This is the work of Robert Adam, the most fashionable architect of the late 1700s, who remodelled an older house into this cool, pale, perfectly balanced thing in the 1760s. Adam's whole genius was the interior — light, delicate, classical, every ceiling and doorframe part of one coordinated scheme. The room to find is the library, a long space with a curved ceiling painted in soft pinks and blues, gilded just enough, considered down to the last inch. It's one of the most beautiful rooms in England, and it's a room, not a roped-off shrine. You can stand in it.

*[beat]*

Now the painting. In the 1920s the house and its art were given to the nation by Edward Guinness, of the brewing family — the Iveagh Bequest — on the condition that it stay open and free forever. And among what he left is a small, quiet picture by Rembrandt: a self-portrait of the artist as an old man, and, hanging nearby, a Vermeer — the Guitar Player — one of only a tiny handful of Vermeers anywhere in the world, in a country house on the edge of a public heath, guarded by almost nobody, costing nothing to see.

Sit with that for a second. There are people who fly across the world and queue for hours to see a single Vermeer behind glass in a crowded gallery. Here's one you can walk up to on a Tuesday afternoon, on the way back from a walk, for free, because a man a century ago decided that was how it should be.

So that's Kenwood — a perfect Adam interior and a world-class collection, given away on the condition that nobody would ever be charged to enjoy it. When you've looked your fill, go back out and sit on the lawn facing the city. The view down the slope to the ponds is the same one the painting's owners had. They just had to share it eventually.
