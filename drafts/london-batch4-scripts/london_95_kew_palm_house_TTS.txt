In front of you is a building made almost entirely of glass and curved iron, swelling up in the middle like a great green bubble caught mid-rise. This is the Palm House at Kew, and it's one of the most important buildings in the history of architecture — which is a strange thing to say about a greenhouse.

Here's why. When it went up in the eighteen forties, nobody had built anything like it. Architects worked in stone; you could not make a building this size out of glass, because glass and iron behaved in ways the old rules couldn't describe. So the people who solved it weren't really architects at all. The structural genius behind it, Richard Turner, was an ironworker — and the form they landed on came straight from the shipyards.

That's the detail to carry inside with you. Look at the great curved ribs holding the glass up. They're built the way the hull of a ship is built — the same wrought-iron techniques, the same understanding of how to make iron curve and hold under stress. They essentially took the upturned hull of a boat, inverted it, glazed it, and filled it with the rainforest. When you stand inside, under that soaring ribbed ceiling, you're standing inside a ship turned upside down and pointed at the sky.

And then the purpose. This wasn't built for beauty, though it's beautiful. Kew was the botanical engine room of the British Empire — plants from every conquered and colonised corner of the world brought here to be studied, propagated, and sent on to wherever they could be made profitable. The Palm House was a machine for keeping the tropics alive in a cold country, so that the world's plants could be gathered, controlled, and understood in one place. That history is complicated, and worth sitting with as you walk among the palms.

So go in. Feel the heat and the wet hit you at the door — they keep it tropical year-round. Climb the spiral stair to the high walkway if it's open, up among the tops of the palms, and look at those iron ribs from close. A Victorian shipwright's solution to an impossible problem, still standing, still doing its job, nearly two centuries on.
