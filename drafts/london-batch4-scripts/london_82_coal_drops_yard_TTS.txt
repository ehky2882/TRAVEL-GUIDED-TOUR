You're standing in the middle of what used to be one of the dirtiest places in London.

It doesn't look it now. Granary Square, where you're standing, is all clean stone and open water, and if you've timed it right there are a thousand little fountains rising and falling out of the ground in front of you, lit up after dark, children running through them in summer. But pull the gloss away and the names tell you what this was. Coal Drops. Granary. Goods Yard. This whole quarter behind King's Cross station was where the railways unloaded the things that kept Victorian London alive — coal, grain, ice, fish — straight off the trains and onto the canal barges waiting beside it.

Turn and look at the long brick buildings to one side, the ones with the great swooping roofs that seem to lean towards each other across a narrow gap. Those are the Coal Drops — built in the eighteen fifties so that trains could run along the top and tip their coal down through the floors into wagons and barges below. Industrial machinery, essentially, the size of a street. For most of the twentieth century, once the coal stopped, they sat derelict, and for a while in the nineteen eighties and nineties the only thing happening in here was a legendary, slightly lawless nightclub scene.

Now look at what's been done to them. The architect Thomas Heatherwick took those two Victorian roofs and stretched them towards each other until they kiss in the middle, making a single covered space out of two old sheds, and filled the lot with shops and restaurants.

That's the move worth noticing here. Nobody knocked the coal yards down and started again. They kept the brick, kept the bones, kept the names, and let the new thing grow out of the old one. So when you walk through, you're not in a shopping centre that's pretending to have history — you're in the actual machinery of Victorian London, still standing, still useful, just asked to do something gentler with its old age. Walk the canal towpath behind it if you've time. The barges are gone, but the water's exactly where it was.
