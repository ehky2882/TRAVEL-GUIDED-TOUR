From the street, Leighton House looks like a fairly stern red-brick Victorian villa — handsome, but not the kind of thing you'd cross London for. That's deliberate. The whole point of this house is what it hides, and the gap between the plain outside and the extraordinary inside is the entire experience.

This was the home of Frederic Leighton, the most successful, most establishment painter in Victorian Britain — president of the Royal Academy, eventually made a lord, the absolute summit of the respectable art world. And he built himself, behind that sober brick, a private pleasure-palace devoted entirely to beauty. A bachelor with no family to house, he spent thirty years turning his home into a single, growing work of art.

The room you've come to see is the Arab Hall. Step into it and the temperature of the place changes completely. It's a domed chamber modelled on a palace hall in the Islamic world, with a fountain murmuring in a black marble pool at its centre, and every wall covered floor to ceiling in antique tiles — genuine sixteenth and seventeenth-century tiles that Leighton collected on his travels through Syria, Turkey and beyond, brilliant blues and greens, gilded ceiling glinting above. It is one of the most sensuous interiors in London, built by the most proper man in British art.

*[beat]*

That contradiction is the thing to hold on to. The Victorians are caricatured as buttoned-up and repressed, and Leighton was their ultimate insider — and yet here, in his own home, he built a space of pure escape, an Arabian fantasy soaked in colour and water and silence, the opposite of everything that public respectability was supposed to be. The house was part of a whole movement of artists who believed your surroundings should be works of art in themselves: the cult of the House Beautiful.

So take your time in here. Notice how the dim, jewel-box rooms make you lower your voice. This was never built to impress committees or sell paintings — it was built for one man to live inside his own idea of beauty. Standing in the Arab Hall, with the fountain going and the tiles glowing, you're standing in the most private wish of a very public man.
