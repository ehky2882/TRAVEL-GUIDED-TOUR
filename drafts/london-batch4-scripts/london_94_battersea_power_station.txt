Four white chimneys, one at each corner, rising off a vast brick box the colour of dried blood. You've almost certainly seen this building before you ever came to London — on a record sleeve, in a film, in the background of the city's imagination. This is Battersea Power Station, and for most of its life it didn't make a single beautiful thing. It made electricity.

Look at the brick. There's an absurd amount of it — this is one of the largest brick buildings in Europe — and it's worked like architecture, not just stacked like a factory. That's the hand of Giles Gilbert Scott, who was brought in to make a power station that London could bear to look at. And here's a detail worth carrying: Scott is the same man who designed the red telephone box you've been photographing all over the city, and his cousin's firm later shaped Bankside Power Station downriver — the building that's now Tate Modern. Once you know that, you start seeing the family resemblance: the same instinct that an industrial brick monster could be made monumental, even noble.

It generated power into the 1980s, then fell silent — and then came the strange decades. For more than thirty years it stood empty, roofless for much of that time, slowly rotting, one of the most famous derelict buildings in the world. Plans came and went; nothing stuck.

*[beat]*

The pop-culture moment that fixed it forever: in 1976, the band Pink Floyd floated an enormous inflatable pig between those chimneys for an album cover. The pig broke loose, drifted off into the flight path of Heathrow airport, and grounded planes. The building has been impossible to take entirely seriously ever since, which is part of its charm.

Now look at what's happened. After all those derelict years it's finally been brought back — the shell restored, the interior turned into shops and flats and offices, one chimney rebuilt as a glass lift you can ride to the top. Some people love it, some mourn the romantic ruin it used to be. Either way, the four chimneys still stand exactly where they always did. Walk up to the brick and put your hand on it. Whatever's inside now, that wall was built to outlast all of us.
