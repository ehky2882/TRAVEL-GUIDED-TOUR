Everyone comes to Camden for the market — the stalls, the food, the leather and the band t-shirts, the crowds three deep on a Saturday. But stand for a moment by the water, at the lock itself, because the water is the reason all the rest of it is here.

This is Camden Lock, and the clue's in the name. That gentle drop in the canal in front of you, where the water steps down between two sets of wooden gates — that's the lock that gave this whole place its identity. The canal is the Regent's Canal, cut in the 1810s and 20s to carry cargo across north London between the docks and the Midlands. Camden was a working interchange: goods came off the canal boats here, onto carts and later onto the railway, and the warehouses you can see around you were built to store all of it.

Look at those buildings now, because they tell the story of what happened next. By the second half of the twentieth century the canal trade was finished, the warehouses were empty, and into that emptiness — cheap rent, big spaces, nobody minding — came craftspeople, then market stalls, then a music scene that turned Camden into one of the engines of British youth culture for decades. Punk, then everything after it, played the pubs and venues a few minutes' walk from where you're standing.

There's one lovely detail to hunt for. Tucked into the market there's a place called the Horse Hospital — or stables, depending which corner you find — a reminder that this was a transport hub long before lorries, when the whole operation ran on horses hauling boats and carts, and the animals needed somewhere to be stabled and treated. Look for the ramps and the cobbles built for hooves, not feet.

So here's Camden in one frame: a piece of Georgian shipping infrastructure that lost its job, sat empty long enough for something stranger and louder to move in, and turned into a marketplace for a kind of London that doesn't fit anywhere tidier. Buy something you didn't plan to. Then come back to the lock and watch a narrowboat work through it — slow, patient, exactly as it did two hundred years ago.
