You've climbed the hill, so catch your breath, turn around, and look south across the city.

That view is the reason you came up here, and it's also the reason this hill looks the way it does — open, grassy, undeveloped, when it sits on some of the most valuable land in London. Somebody decided, long ago, that what you're looking at mattered more than what could be built here.

Take the skyline in for a second. St Paul's dome, off to your right, low and pale. The cluster of glass towers in the City beyond it. The Shard further round to the left, standing on its own south of the river. The whole of central London laid out below you like a model, the parks stitching the green between the grey.

Here's the thing that holds it all in place: this is a protected view. There's an actual law — a sightline drawn through the air — that says nothing may be built in the gap between this hill and St Paul's Cathedral that would block the dome. Developers have to prove their tower won't intrude on it. So the view in front of you isn't just nice. It's legally defended, an agreement that some things are worth keeping clear even when there's money to be made by filling them in.

*[beat]*

There's a softer history here too. This hill has been a poets' hill for two centuries — Blake claimed he had a vision of the spiritual sun up here, and writers and walkers have come for the same reason you have: it's the closest big view to the centre that you can reach on foot, with no ticket and no queue. On the railings near the top you'll find William Blake's words carved into the stone, about the spiritual fourfold vision he said he saw from this spot.

So stay a while. This is one of the few places in London where the whole city agrees to hold still and let you look at it — and where, by law, it has to keep doing so. Come back at dusk if you can. The towers light up one by one, and the dome holds its ground in the middle, exactly where the law says it must.
