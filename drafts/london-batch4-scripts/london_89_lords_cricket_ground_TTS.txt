If you know cricket, you don't need me to tell you what this place is. If you don't, here's the only thing you need: this is the closest the sport has to a cathedral, and people say Lord's the way they might say the Vatican.

It's called the home of cricket, and the name on the gate is just a man's — Thomas Lord, who set up the ground in the early eighteen hundreds. But the institution behind it, the marlibun Cricket Club, spent two centuries as the body that wrote and guarded the laws of the game for the entire world from this one address in north London.

There are a few things to look for. Inside the pavilion — the grand old building with the long windows — is the Long Room, a wood-panelled hall the players walk through on their way out to bat. Imagine it: you're about to play in front of thirty thousand people, and the path takes you through a quiet room full of members in ties and portraits of the dead greats watching you go. It is, by reputation, the most nerve-shredding short walk in sport.

Then there's the slope. The whole playing field tilts — it drops by around two and a half metres from one side to the other, enough that the ball behaves differently depending on which end you bowl from. The most famous ground in cricket is, gloriously, not flat.

And then the strangest treasure of all. Cricket's biggest contest between England and Australia is called the Ashes — and the trophy they supposedly play for is a tiny terracotta urn, just a few inches tall, said to contain the burnt remains of a cricket bail, created as a joke in a newspaper after England lost in eighteen eighty-two. That urn lives here, in the little museum, and here's the twist: it's so fragile and so precious that it almost never leaves. The teams play for it, but the actual urn stays at Lord's. They win a replica.

So that's this place — laws, a sloping pitch, a terrifying walk through a quiet room, and a national obsession that comes down to a joke urn smaller than your hand. Even if you never watch a match, that's a very English kind of greatness.
