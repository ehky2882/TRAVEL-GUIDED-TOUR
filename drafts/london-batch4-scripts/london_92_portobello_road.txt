The first thing to know about Portobello Road is that it's really two streets wearing the same name, and which one you get depends entirely on the day you came.

Come on a Saturday, and this is the most famous antiques market in the world — stalls and shopfronts running for the better part of a mile, packed shoulder to shoulder with silver, old jewellery, prints, cameras, coins, the lovely and the dubious side by side, dealers who've been here for decades next to people who set up that morning. Come on a Tuesday, and the antiques largely vanish, and you find a working neighbourhood street market instead — fruit and veg, fish, household bits, the ordinary shopping of the people who actually live here. Same paving stones, two completely different streets.

That doubleness is the real story of Notting Hill, the area you're walking through, because this place has changed identity more than once. After the Second World War, this was a poor, run-down quarter, and it became one of the main places where families arriving from the Caribbean settled — part of the Windrush generation who came to help rebuild Britain and met, too often, hostility and terrible housing.

*[beat]*

Out of that community came something the whole city now claims as its own: the Notting Hill Carnival. Every August, these streets fill with sound systems and steel bands and costume and a couple of million people, in what's grown into one of the biggest street festivals on the planet — a celebration rooted directly in Caribbean culture and in this neighbourhood's history of being told it didn't belong.

So as you walk the market, hold both pictures at once. The pastel houses are achingly desirable now, some of the priciest in London, and the street's been polished by films and tourism into something charming. But underneath the antiques and the photo-op facades is a tougher, richer story about who built this place and what they made here. Buy something small if the spirit moves you — haggle gently, it's expected. And if you can possibly come back for Carnival, do. That's when the street tells you the truth about itself.
