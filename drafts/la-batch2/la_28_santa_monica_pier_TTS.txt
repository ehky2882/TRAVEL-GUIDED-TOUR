Santa Monica Pier

The Santa Monica Pier is the last of the great Pacific pleasure piers, the symbolic end of Route sixty six, and quite possibly the most beloved structure in Southern California — and it began, in nineteen oh nine, as a utility project. The city built the original concrete pier largely to carry a sewer line out past the breakers. That is the least romantic origin of any adored landmark in America, and the pier has spent over a century living it down in style.

The fun arrived in nineteen sixteen, when amusement-park builder Charles loof added a pleasure pier alongside, anchored by the hip uh drohm — the fairy-tale carousel building near the entrance, now a National Historic Landmark. Inside spins a carousel of hand-carved horses that has been delighting riders, and moonlighting in the movies, for the better part of a century. Beyond it the pier unrolls the full classic menu: arcade, aquarium, fishermen at the rails, street performers, and the little amusement park whose Ferris wheel — said to be the world's first solar-powered one — has become the postcard of this entire coastline, especially lit up after dark.

The pier very nearly didn't make it. In the early nineteen seventies the city council voted to tear it down, and the residents of Santa Monica staged a revolt, packing meetings until the plan died. A decade later, brutal winter storms chewed off a third of the structure, and the town rebuilt it. This pier exists today purely on affection, twice proven.

And then there's the sign — Route sixty six, End of the Trail. Full honesty: the highway's official terminus was about a mile inland, where the road had to junction with the coast highway, and this particular famous sign only went up in two thousand nine. But nobody ever drove twenty-four hundred miles from Chicago to finish at an intersection. The pier is where the continent runs out — where generations of arrivals walked to the rail and looked at the Pacific because there was no more west left to want. It's the honest end of the American road, whatever the paperwork says.

Stay for sunset if you possibly can. Everyone claps. Nobody's embarrassed. It's that kind of place.
