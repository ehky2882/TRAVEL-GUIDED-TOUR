Griffith Observatory

Griffith Observatory is the best balcony in Los Angeles — a white art deco temple on the south slope of Mount Hollywood, with the whole basin spread below, the Hollywood Sign across the canyon, and, on a clear day, the Pacific glittering at the far edge. It is also, and has been since nineteen thirty five, completely free — and thereby hangs the story.

Griffith J. Griffith was a Welsh-born businessman who made a fortune in mining and Southern California real estate, and in eighteen ninety six he gave the city just over three thousand acres of hillside — Griffith Park, still one of the largest urban parks in North America. Later he offered money for a great public observatory on the peak, convinced that looking through a telescope could enlarge a person; he had done it himself and called the experience life-changing. The city said no. Griffith by then was a scandal — he had shot his wife, a crime for which he served prison time — and Los Angeles refused to take his money while he lived. So he left it in his will, and sixteen years after his death the observatory opened on his terms: admission free, forever, for everyone.

Those terms built something genuinely rare — a serious public science institution that functions like a park. Inside you'll find a foo koh pendulum patiently proving the earth rotates, a Tesla coil that snaps to life on schedule, and a planetarium under the great center dome. Up on the roof, the twelve-inch zice refracting telescope opens to the public on clear evenings, and the observatory claims more people have looked through it than any other telescope in history. The line is long, it moves slowly, and it is entirely worth it — the queue for the eyepiece is the whole point, exactly as the donor ordered.

A word about timing: sunset draws half the city up here, and parking becomes a contact sport. Come late morning for the quiet version, or embrace the crowds and watch the basin light up grid by grid as the sky goes orange. Perspective improves people, Griffith believed. The jury may still be out on that one, but the view argues his side every single night.
