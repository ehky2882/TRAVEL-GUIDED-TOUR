lah bray ah Tar Pits

In the middle of the city — mid-boulevard, wedged between office towers and a world-class art museum — the Ice Age is still bubbling. The lah bray ah Tar Pits are natural asphalt seeps that have been oozing up through this ground for tens of thousands of years, and they are, quietly, one of the most important fossil sites on earth.

Start with the name, because it's a gift: lah bray ah is Spanish for "the tar," which means "the lah bray ah Tar Pits" translates, more or less, to "the the tar tar pits." The city knows. The city has decided not to fix it.

Here's how a puddle becomes a scientific treasure. An animal — say, an Ice Age camel, because yes, Los Angeles had camels — wanders in for a drink and gets a foot stuck in asphalt hiding under water or leaves. Its distress calls carry across the plain, and the predators arrive: dire wolves, saber-toothed cats, American lions, teeth first, questions later. They get stuck too. One trapped herbivore could take a dozen carnivores down with it, which is why this site preserves predators in numbers found almost nowhere else on the planet. Over the millennia, that grim arithmetic stacked up millions of bones — thousands of dire wolves, mammoths, mastodons, giant ground sloths — along with the fine print of a lost world, the beetles and seeds and pollen, all of it pickled beautifully in asphalt.

The museum on the grounds shows off the haul, including a glowing amber wall built from hundreds of dire wolf skulls, and the saber-toothed smy luh don, the official state fossil of California. Outside, the Lake Pit steams gently behind its fence, watched over by a famously heart-wrenching sculpture of a mammoth family — one parent trapped in the black water, the others watching from shore. And here's the detail that gets people: the excavation never ended. Scientists are still out here working active digs, pulling new specimens from the ground on ordinary weekdays while traffic idles past on wil sher.

Watch the pavement as you wander the park. The asphalt still breaks through the grass in slow black puddles, utterly indifferent to the city on top of it. It was here first, and it intends to stay.
