LACMA & the David Geffen Galleries

The Los Angeles County Museum of Art is the largest art museum in the western United States — a collection of roughly one hundred fifty thousand works covering some six thousand years of human effort — and as of 2026 it lives in one of the strangest and most talked-about museum buildings on the planet: a single enormous concrete ribbon, lifted on pavilions, floating clean across Wilshire Boulevard.

[beat]

The David Geffen Galleries are the work of Peter Zumthor, the famously exacting Swiss architect, and this is his first building in the United States — nearly twenty years in the making, nine hundred feet long, with the entire exhibition floor raised one level above the street. Cars pass underneath the art. There is no grand staircase and no prescribed route: one continuous level, around a hundred ten thousand square feet of galleries, where ancient bronzes and European paintings and contemporary work share the floor without hierarchy, while floor-to-ceiling glass around the perimeter keeps pulling the city itself into view. Museums usually tell you where to start; this one shrugs, generously, and lets you wander. Some people find that liberating, some find it maddening, and the building has been an argument since the day the design was unveiled — which, for Los Angeles architecture, is a tradition in its own right.

[beat]

Down at street level, the museum spills into the park: restaurants and a theater tucked under the concrete, sculptures scattered across the grass, and at the corner of Wilshire the piece everyone knows even if they don't know its name — Urban Light, Chris Burden's forest of two hundred two restored cast-iron streetlamps, glowing in tidy rows after dark. It may be the most photographed artwork in the city, and it's free, always, no ticket required.

One more thing to notice, because only Los Angeles would arrange it this way: the museum's next-door neighbor is the La Brea Tar Pits, actively bubbling Ice Age asphalt, mid-city. Six thousand years of art on one side of the lawn, an epoch of dire wolves on the other. Give the lampposts your evening and the ribbon your afternoon, and budget more time than you think — a nine-hundred-foot room takes a while.
