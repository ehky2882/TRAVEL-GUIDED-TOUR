Officially, you're in Rush Lane. Nobody calls it that.

Duck into the alley that runs behind the south side of Queen Street West, somewhere between Spadina Avenue and Portland Street, and keep walking. For about three blocks — close to a kilometre of brick, if you follow every offshoot — every wall, door and drainpipe is painted, and most of it will be different the next time you come.

That impermanence is the point. Some pieces vanish in a week, painted over by whoever's next. A few have survived for years, left alone by an unwritten code among the artists about what you don't touch. Look for the big tribute wall crammed with local characters — one artist's cheerful yellow bird presiding over a crowd of Toronto in-jokes. If it's still there.

Here's the Toronto part. For years, this was simply illegal — property owners kept getting ticketed for graffiti they hadn't asked for and couldn't keep up with. So the local business association made an argument that only really works in this city: the wall had become an attraction. A comedian was filming his national television rants against these bricks. Tourists were coming to photograph it. In 2011, the city agreed to designate the lane and stop fining people.

Notice what that is, though. The city didn't declare the art legal. It's still, technically, not a legal wall. What happened is more Canadian than that: everyone quietly agreed to stop fighting about it.

[beat]

So walk the whole length, slowly. Take a photo of something you like, and know it's a photo of a thing that won't exist much longer. That isn't a flaw in the gallery. It's the only gallery in the city that repaints itself.
