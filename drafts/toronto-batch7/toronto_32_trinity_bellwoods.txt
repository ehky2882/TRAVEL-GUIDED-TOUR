You've probably arranged to meet someone at these gates without ever asking what they're the gates to.

Stand at the top of Strachan Avenue where it meets Queen Street West, in front of the stone-and-iron gateway into the park. There's no building behind it. That's the first ghost. These gates once opened onto Trinity College — a grand Gothic pile that stood here from the 1850s until the city bought the grounds and knocked it down in the 1950s. The gates are pretty much all that's left, and even they're a copy; the original ironwork was carted off to a school east of the city.

The college has a small, sharp irony buried in it. A bishop named Strachan founded Trinity in 1851 precisely because he thought the new University of Toronto had gone godless and secular. His whole point was to be the anti-university. And within a few generations, Trinity gave up and folded itself into the very university it was built to oppose. It sits on that campus now. Only the gates stayed behind.

Now walk in and look at the big grassy bowl. That dip in the ground is another ghost — it's the old ravine of Garrison Creek, a real river that once ran down to the lake. The creek was buried in a sewer pipe over a century ago, and the ravine was later filled in with the dirt dug out of the Bloor subway tunnel — the same great dig, more or less, that the viaduct across the Don Valley was built to carry.

[beat]

And keep an eye out, while you're here, for a white squirrel. There are a few — pale little things people swear bring good luck. The stories insist there's only one, impossibly old and impossibly fast. There isn't. But in a park built on this many buried things, it's a nice idea to let stand.
