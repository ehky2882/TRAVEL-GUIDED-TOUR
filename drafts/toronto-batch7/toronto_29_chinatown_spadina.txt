Listen for the streetcar first. That rattle and clang coming up Spadina Avenue is the sound of this whole neighbourhood, and it's a good place to start.

Stand at the corner of Spadina and Dundas Street West, where four blocks of Chinese, Vietnamese and Thai signage crowd every window and the fruit sellers stack lychees and durian out on the sidewalk. This is one of the largest Chinatowns in North America. But the thing worth knowing is that it's in the wrong place — or rather, the second place.

Toronto's first Chinatown wasn't here. It grew up in the early 1900s about a kilometre east, around Elizabeth Street, in the crowded immigrant district people called the Ward. Laundries, restaurants, family associations, a couple of thousand people. And in the 1950s, the city expropriated it and knocked most of it flat to build the new City Hall and the square in front of it. If you've stood in Nathan Phillips Square, you've stood on top of the first Chinatown.

The families who could afford to move came here, west along Dundas to Spadina, into what had been a Jewish garment district as that community moved north. A second Chinatown, rebuilt from the pieces of the first.

And then, in the 1960s, the city came back. A plan proposed clearing the rest for office towers. This time the community organised — a restaurateur named Jean Lumb helped lead a Save Chinatown campaign, arguing, simply, that a city this size ought to have a place where newcomers could land among people who spoke their language. This time, the city backed down.

[beat]

So this is a neighbourhood that got moved once and refused to be moved twice. Grab something steamed from a window counter, stand in the noise for a minute, and notice that nothing here is trying to be quaint. It's just working — the way it has, in one spot or another, for well over a hundred years.
