VENICE BEACH BOARDWALK

Everything you're looking at is what's left of a utopia that failed in the most Los Angeles way possible: it became famous instead.

You should be on Ocean Front Walk, the wide boardwalk running between the beach and the storefronts — murals, vendors, skaters, and somewhere within earshot, at least one person performing something. To find the origin of it all, look for Windward Avenue, where a row of columned arcades meets the walk. Those Venetian colonnades are more than a hundred years old, and they're the last standing piece of the original dream.

The dreamer was Abbot Kinney, who had made a fortune in tobacco and decided this stretch of salt marsh should become the Venice of America — a real one, with canals dredged where the marsh had been, gondolas and gondoliers brought over from Italy, and a program of lectures and orchestras meant to seed a cultural renaissance on the Pacific. It opened on July 4, 1905, and tens of thousands came on the first day.

They came, they rode the gondolas — and then they made it plain they'd rather have fun. The lecture halls emptied while the amusement pier filled, and Kinney, whatever he privately thought, was too good a businessman to argue. Venice pivoted into the Coney Island of the Pacific: roller coasters, sideshows, dance halls, a miniature railroad threading through it all. The renaissance never arrived. The crowds never left.

Kinney died in 1920, Los Angeles absorbed Venice in the mid-twenties, and most of his canals were paved into streets in 1929. What survived the respectable decades that followed wasn't the culture — it was the carnival. Beat poets took the cheap bungalows, bodybuilders claimed a patch of sand just down the walk, skateboarding got reinvented in this neighborhood's empty pools, and today's boardwalk economy of performers, artists, and vendors runs on the same fuel Kinney's midway did: hold a crowd, then pass the hat.

[beat]

So the utopia failed — but look around, because something stranger succeeded. Kinney wanted a place unlike anywhere else in America. A century on, whatever else has changed, nobody has ever accused Venice of being like anywhere else.
