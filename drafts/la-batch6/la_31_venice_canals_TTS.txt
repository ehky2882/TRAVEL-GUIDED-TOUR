VENICE CANALS

One block from one of the loudest places in Los Angeles, everything goes quiet — and the quiet is an accident.

You should be in the Venice Canal Historic District, a few blocks inland from the boardwalk, standing on or near one of the white arched footbridges. Six narrow canals, low houses with little docks, ducks working the banks, kayaks tied up like parked cars. It reads as a miniature of Venice, Italy, and it is — but not in the way most visitors assume.

Here's the twist: these are not the famous canals. When Abbot Kinney opened his Venice of America in nineteen oh five, his showpiece canals — the ones with the imported gondoliers — were dug a few blocks north of here, around what's now Windward Avenue. This set came just afterward: a second tract, dug next door to ride his success, joined to the original network by the Grand Canal. The annex, in other words. The sideshow.

Then the car happened. Venice was absorbed into Los Angeles in the mid twenties, the canals were declared obsolete, and in nineteen twenty nine Kinney's originals were drained and paved into ordinary streets. If you've ever stood in the traffic circle where Windward meets Pacific, you've stood in what used to be the lagoon at the heart of the whole dream. And the reason these six survived is almost comically unromantic: this tract didn't have enough residents to pay the assessments that funded the paving. Too poor to become roads — so they stayed water.

They spent decades in genteel decay after that, sidewalks crumbling, the houses cheap and bohemian, until the early nineteen nineties, when the city finally drained and rebuilt them — new walls, new walkways, the arched bridges restored. The district now sits on the National Register of Historic Places, which is its own quiet irony: the afterthought became the protected landmark, while the main event lies under asphalt, with street signs for a headstone.

So walk it slowly. This is the last place where you can feel what Kinney actually meant — a neighborhood built to move at the speed of water, in the city that chose the freeway. It survived by being overlooked, which may be the most Venice thing about it.
