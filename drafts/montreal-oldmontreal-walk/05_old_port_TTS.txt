You've reached the water — the reason for everything you've seen this past hour. roo duh lah kom oon curves along the harbourfront behind you, the quays stretch into the river ahead, and upstream, out of sight, are the rapids that stopped every ocean ship for three centuries and made this exact shoreline one of the great inland ports of the world. Cargo from Europe came off the boats here; grain from the Prairies went on, bound the other way. For a long stretch of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, this waterfront was the loudest place in Canada.

Look east along the water and you'll see a tall white tower with a clock face near the top. That's the Clock Tower, finished in nineteen twenty-two as a memorial to the sailors who left this harbour for the First World War and never came back — a working clock and a quiet monument at once, which suits this city.

Then, in nineteen fifty-nine, the Saint Lawrence Seaway opened. Ships could finally sail past the rapids, the port that made Montreal simply moved downstream, and the quays went silent for a generation. What you're standing in now is the city's answer: the working harbour reinvented as its front porch — cyclists and skaters on the promenade, a ferris wheel turning where the freight once moved, families out at the end of piers built for cargo.

And that's the whole biography, walked in an afternoon. Founded on a point of land, kept alive by a nurse and a peace treaty, fed by a market, run for a while out of a building with a silver dome — and always, always, tilted toward this river. The city grew up here, moved on, and then came back, the way people return to the house they grew up in: not to work, just to sit out front and watch the water.

Stay a while. Watch for a ship, if one obliges — they still pass, now and then, on their way to somewhere else. The port moved on. The water never did.
