The silver dome ahead of you was built to be seen from the water. In the days when everyone important arrived by ship, bon suh koor market was Montreal's calling card — a long neoclassical block modelled loosely on the Customs House in Dublin, finished in eighteen forty-seven, and deliberately grander than a vegetable market had any right to be.

But here's what the building actually is: the city's understudy. Whenever Montreal needed a room, this was the room. In eighteen forty-nine, after rioters burned down the parliament building a few streets west, the Parliament of United Canada sat here for a session. For twenty-six years it doubled as City Hall. It hosted concerts, banquets, and exhibitions, and through all of it kept working as a farmers' market for more than a century — hay, cabbages, and statecraft under one dome. When the market finally closed in nineteen sixty-three, the building was slated for demolition, and it survived — narrowly — into its current life of boutiques and galleries. Step inside if it's open; the bones of the old market halls are still legible under the retail.

Next door, the small stone church with the spire is noh truh dahm duh bon suh koor, the sailors' chapel — the place ships' crews came to pray before crossing the Atlantic. From the harbour side, a statue of the Virgin stands on its roof with her arms open to the water, still facing the departures.

Market and chapel, side by side: one fed the city, and the other steadied its nerve before the long crossings.

Last stop now. Any of the small streets on your right will drop you down to roo duh lah kom oon and the waterfront. Cross over to the quays. That's the Old Port — where this whole story ends, or turns into another one.
