You should be in plahs rwah yal, the small square where the sharp modern museum building meets the oldest stone in the city — and this wedge of land is the oldest address in Montreal. Not the oldest building; the oldest idea. In May of sixteen forty-two, a boatload of French settlers led by may zoh nuv — the man on the monument you just left — came ashore at this point and founded a mission colony called veel mah ree. The museum in front of you, pwant ah kal yair, stands directly on that founding ground, and the archaeology is in the basement: you can walk among the actual foundations later, if you give it the hour it deserves.

But this point matters twice, and the second time is the summer of seventeen oh one. That August, around thirteen hundred delegates from close to forty Indigenous nations travelled here — some paddling for weeks — to negotiate an end to decades of war with the French. The agreement they signed is called the Great Peace of Montreal, and the man who did more than anyone to carry it over the line was kon dee ah ronk, the wehn daht chief whose name you may recognize from the lookout on Mount Royal, the one with the best view in the city. He fell gravely ill during the negotiations, rose to give one last speech that witnesses said held the whole assembly still, and died before the treaty was signed. He was buried with full honours, French officers and Indigenous leaders walking together in the procession. The peace he argued for held.

So this ground carries both stories — the city's founding, and, fifty-nine years later, the agreement that gave it a future. One point of land, two beginnings.

Back up on roo san pol, head east past the galleries and the grey stone facades until the street opens on your left into a long square climbing the hill, full of terrasses and easels. That's plahs zhak kar tyay, our next stop.
