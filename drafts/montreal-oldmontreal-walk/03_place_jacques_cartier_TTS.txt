Notice the tilt first. plahs zhak kar tyay runs downhill from roo noh truh dahm to the harbour, and that slope is the whole history of this city in a single gesture — everything here once leaned toward the river, because the river was the economy, the highway, and the reason anyone was here at all.

The square began as somebody's backyard. In seventeen twenty-three, the mar kee duh voh druh yuh, governor of New France, built his château at the top of this slope and laid out the grounds below as his private formal gardens — clipped hedges and gravel walks running exactly where the terrasses now spill their tables. The château burned in eighteen oh three, and within a few years the land had been turned over to the city as a public market square. The governor's garden became the people's square, and it has never really stopped being one: the produce stalls gave way to buskers, caricaturists, and flower carts, but the square still trades in something, every single day.

At the top stands Nelson's Column, raised in eighteen oh nine — the oldest public monument in the city, and older than the far more famous Nelson's column in London by more than three decades. If you've heard the tale of the two admirals on your travels through Montreal, this is where it lives.

In summer the whole slope goes car-free, and you can hear the difference — footsteps and violins where the engines used to be.

From the bottom of the square, continue east along roo san pol, the oldest street in the city, and within a few minutes a long silver dome rises ahead of you on the right: bon suh koor market, a building that has been nearly everything this city ever needed. I'll explain when you get there.
