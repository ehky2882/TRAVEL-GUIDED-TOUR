Stand near the monument at the centre of the square, where you can turn a slow circle: Notre-Dame Basilica filling the south side, and directly opposite it, the domed head office of the Bank of Montreal — God and money facing each other across the cobbles, which is about as honest as urban planning gets.

The bronze figure above you is Paul de Chomedey de Maisonneuve, the soldier who founded this city, but the monument earns its keep down at the base. Walk around it. There's Jeanne Mance, the nurse who ran the colony's first hospital and arguably kept the whole enterprise alive; Charles Le Moyne, the farmer-turned-seigneur whose sons went on to found New Orleans; Lambert Closse, the garrison commander, with his dog Pilote at his heel — legend has it her barking warned the settlement of night raids, and legend is doing some heavy lifting there, but the sculptor gave her a place on the monument anyway. And facing outward, an Iroquois figure — the sculptor's nineteenth-century acknowledgment that this ground was inhabited, and contested, long before anyone else on the pedestal arrived. The founding of Montreal was a group project, and this is one of the rare monuments willing to admit it.

Now look at the basilica. Its twin towers were designed by James O'Donnell, an Irish-American Protestant from New York who, somewhere in the building of it, was won over by his own creation — he converted to Catholicism before he died, partly, the story goes, so he could be buried inside it. He's still there, down in the crypt, the only person interred in the building. The architect became part of the architecture.

[beat]

When you're ready, we go back before all of this — before the basilica, before the banks. Walk down Rue Saint-Sulpice toward the water, then turn right along Rue Saint-Paul until it opens onto a small square with a sharp-angled modern building rising above old stone. That's Pointe-à-Callière, and underneath it is the exact ground where Montreal began.
