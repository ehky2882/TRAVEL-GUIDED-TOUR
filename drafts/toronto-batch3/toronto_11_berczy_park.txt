Within a single block, this neighbourhood keeps both its most solemn thing and its most ridiculous one. And somehow they belong together.

Start in Berczy Park, the small triangular green space tucked right behind the flatiron building. Its centrepiece is a tall, tiered cast-iron fountain covered in dogs — a whole pack of them, every one gazing up at a single golden bone balanced at the very top.

Count the dogs first.

There are twenty-seven of them, all fixed on that bone. Look a little harder and you'll find a cat who refuses to join in — perched off to the side, staring instead at two birds on a nearby lamppost. That cat is the punchline of the whole design. When the fountain was first proposed, around 2017, the city nearly turned it down — a fountain of dogs seemed far too silly for serious public art — and only approved it once the cat and the birds were added to give it a story. Even the park's name is contested: people argued over it for years, until the city officially settled on "bear-ski." Think of a bear on skis, and you've got it.

Now look north, a couple of blocks up Church Street, to a tall stone spire rising over the rooftops. That's the Cathedral Church of St. James. Its congregation is the oldest in the city — it goes back to 1797, when this was still the town of York. The spire climbs to about ninety-three metres, the tallest church spire in the country, and for decades it was simply the tallest thing in Toronto: the first landmark newcomers saw as they stepped off the train at the old station. The main church opened in 1853; the spire and its ring of bells took another twenty years to finish.

[beat]

One block, two ideas of what a place is for. A spire built to make you feel small and reverent, and a fountain built to make you laugh — and yet both of them are exactly the same thing underneath: somewhere people gather and look up together. Sit by the dogs a while, glance north at the spire, and notice the city left room for the sacred and the ridiculous within a two-minute walk.
