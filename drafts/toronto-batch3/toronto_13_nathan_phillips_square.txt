When Toronto needed a new city hall, it held a contest open to the entire world. And then it picked the strangest design on the table.

You should be standing in Nathan Phillips Square, the big open plaza off Queen and Bay Streets. Ahead of you is the building everyone photographs: two curved towers of different heights, leaning toward each other, with a low round chamber cupped between them. From street level it's striking. From the air, it reads as an eye — two hands holding a single watchful pupil. That's City Hall. Behind you, a reflecting pool that becomes a skating rink in winter, and a row of tall concrete arches.

Start with the towers.

They're the work of a Finnish architect, Viljo Revell, whose design beat out hundreds of entries from dozens of countries back in 1958, and opened in 1965. Almost nobody expected it to win — plenty of people wanted something safe and grand and columned. Instead the city got this: curved, spare, and a little alien. It was the moment a cautious place decided, just once, to be bold.

Now look for the lone abstract sculpture out on the plaza — that's The Archer, by Henry Moore. Revell flatly refused to call the building finished without a Moore in front of it. City Council, in turn, refused to pay for it. So the mayor went out and raised the money from the public instead. The residents bought the art their own council had turned down.

And the square keeps adding to itself. There's a Peace Garden here for the victims of Hiroshima. And in the southwest corner, opened in 2024, is the Spirit Garden — an Indigenous space honouring the survivors of Canada's residential schools and the children who never came home. At its heart sits a limestone turtle in a quiet reflecting pool, and a wall nearby carries the names of all eighteen residential schools that once operated in Ontario. Take it gently if you walk over.

[beat]

The building behind you was 1965's idea of the future. But the square in front of it has never stopped being rewritten — each generation adding what it believes a city should stand for. Skate here in winter, or just stand a while under the arches. This is the city's living room, and it's still being furnished.
