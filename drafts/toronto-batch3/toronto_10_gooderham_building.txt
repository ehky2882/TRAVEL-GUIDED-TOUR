New York gets the credit for the flatiron building. Toronto built one first — a full decade earlier — and it's right in front of you.

You should be standing on Front Street East, looking west to where Front and Wellington Streets come together. Right at that junction sits a narrow red-brick building shaped like a wedge, its front rounded into a slim tower that comes to a point. This is the Gooderham Building — Toronto's flatiron.

Start with the shape, because the shape is the whole story.

It's triangular because the lot is triangular, and the lot is triangular because of the lake. Front Street once ran right along the shoreline of Lake Ontario, and the early town's grid was bent to follow the curve of the water. Where that bent street met a straight one, it left this odd sliver of land. So the building's famous wedge isn't a clever design flourish — it's the ghost of a coastline that now sits buried, several blocks inland.

It went up in 1892, built for George Gooderham, who ran the city's enormous whisky distillery, as offices for the company — with his own perched at the top of that rounded tower, looking out over the neighbourhood. The much taller, much more famous Flatiron in New York came ten years afterward. This one cost about eighteen thousand dollars and had one of the first manually operated elevators in the city.

Now walk around to the back — the west wall, the one facing the little park. There's an enormous painted mural there, a trick of the eye: it copies the windows of the building across the street, then makes the whole surface appear to peel up at the corners, like a giant sheet caught in the wind. It was painted in 1980, and it's become one of the most photographed walls in Toronto.

[beat]

Look at the building straight on from the east and you get the picture everyone takes home: a Victorian brick wedge with a wall of glass towers rising behind it — the old shoreline and the modern skyline in a single frame. Most people photograph it by accident, on their way somewhere else. Stay a second longer, and notice it was shaped by water that isn't there anymore.
