If it's a Sunday morning, you can probably already hear it — the low roar of thousands of people haggling downhill. If it's any other day, you're standing on a quiet, steep street and you'll have to take the noise on faith. Either way, this is El Rastro, the great open-air market that has tumbled down this hill for the better part of three hundred years.

Start with where you are. The street is called Ribera de Curtidores — the riverbank of the tanners — and that name is the key to everything. Centuries ago this edge of the city was where animals were slaughtered and their hides were turned into leather. The carcasses were dragged downhill from the old slaughterhouse to the tanning workshops, and they left a long stain on the ground as they went. A trail. In Spanish, un rastro. The most charming market in Madrid is named, quite literally, after a trail of blood.

These days the only thing being hunted is a bargain. From around nine in the morning until the middle of the afternoon on Sundays and public holidays, hundreds upon hundreds of stalls unfold down the slope — old vinyl, leather, fans, postcards, brass odds and ends, clothes new and ancient, and a great deal of cheerful junk. The serious collectors arrive at opening to skim the best finds; by late morning it's less about buying and more about the crush and the spectacle.

Here's the local wisdom, and the gift of doing it right. Start at the top, near Plaza de Cascorro, and let gravity carry you down — fighting uphill through that crowd is a beginner's mistake. Keep a hand on your bag; the pickpockets work the tightest spots. And know that the market is only half the ritual. When the stalls pack up, everyone pours into the bars of La Latina just around you for a beer and a plate of something — the tapas crawl that turns a morning's shopping into a whole Sunday.

So don't treat it as shopping. Treat it as the city's weekly habit. The hides and the tanners are long gone, but the trail they left still fills, every Sunday, with people following it down the hill.
