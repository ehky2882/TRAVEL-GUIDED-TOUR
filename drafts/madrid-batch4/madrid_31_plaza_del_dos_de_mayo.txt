In the middle of this square stands a stone archway with two figures beneath it, and almost everyone walking past treats it as scenery. It isn't. That arch is a survivor — the gateway of a military barracks that once stood here — and it marks the spot where this neighbourhood earned its name in blood, twice over, two centuries apart.

Go back to the second of May, 1808. Napoleon's army held Madrid, and on that morning the city rose against them. Here, at the Monteleón artillery barracks, a small group of Spanish soldiers and ordinary townspeople made a stand. Two officers, Daoíz and Velarde, are remembered as its heart; they died defending the guns, and the two figures under the arch honour them. The uprising was crushed, and the reprisals that followed were brutal — you can see both days, the fighting and the executions, in two of Goya's most famous paintings hanging in the Prado, a short walk from here. He painted what this square remembers.

The neighbourhood took its name from one of the day's dead — Manuela Malasaña, a young seamstress killed in the chaos. So the whole district is, in effect, named after a teenager who didn't survive the second of May. That's worth carrying for a moment.

Now look around at what the same streets became. Because the second thing Malasaña is famous for is a rebellion of a completely different kind. In the nineteen eighties, after decades under Franco, this is where Madrid's youth exploded into colour — music, clubs, comics, film, a wave of creative noise called La Movida. The bars and record shops you can see grew out of that. Two uprisings, then: one against an emperor's soldiers, one against grey years of silence.

So the square holds both at once. The arch is genuinely old stone, genuinely scarred history; the streets fanning out from it are loud, painted, and very much alive. Stand here a moment and let the distance close — the same few corners that watched a city fight for its life later watched it learn, very loudly, how to enjoy being free.
