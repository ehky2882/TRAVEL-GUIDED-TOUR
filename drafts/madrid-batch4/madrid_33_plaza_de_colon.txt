Three things compete for your eye in this square, and they don't quite agree with each other. A tall stone column with a figure on top. An enormous Spanish flag on a white pole. And, off to one side, a cluster of huge rough concrete blocks. Together they make this one of Madrid's great civic stages — and one of its quietest arguments.

The column first. On top stands Christopher Columbus — Cristóbal Colón, which gives the square its name. It was raised in the eighteen eighties, when Spain looked back on his voyages with uncomplicated pride and built him a monument to match: white marble, neo-Gothic, the explorer gazing out with a Castilian flag in his hand. In 2009 he was shifted to his present perch in the middle of the traffic, so the discoverer of a new world now also directs the flow of cars from north to south.

The flag came much later, going up around the year 2001 — and it's deliberately the largest in the country, fourteen metres by twenty-one, on a fifty-metre mast that turns with the wind so it never tangles. This square has long been the place Madrid comes to wave that flag: for football wins, for national days, for protests of every stripe. A stage built to be filled.

Behind it lie the Gardens of Discovery, and the great concrete blocks within them carry carved inscriptions about the voyages, often read as standing in for Columbus's three ships.

Here's the part worth holding lightly, and even-handedly. The very word carved all over this square — "discovery" — is now contested. For centuries it was simply Spain's proudest story. But fourteen ninety-two was also the beginning of conquest and colonisation for the peoples already living across the Atlantic, and many now prefer to call it an encounter, or worse. Monuments to Columbus have become flashpoints across the world. This one stands serenely above the traffic, from an age that felt no doubt, in a city that now holds several views at once.

So read the square as exactly that: a confident answer from one century, standing in the middle of another century's questions. You don't have to settle it. Just notice that the stone is sure, and the conversation around it no longer is.
