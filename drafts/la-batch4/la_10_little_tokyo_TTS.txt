First Street has its history written into the sidewalk, and most people walk right over it.

You should be on First Street between san pee droh Street and Central Avenue, along the block of low brick storefronts, with a wooden fire tower rising over the plaza entrance nearby. This is Little Tokyo — one of only three Japantowns left in the United States, all of them in California, and this is the largest.

Start underfoot. Along this block, a public artwork runs the length of the sidewalk — the names of old businesses, remembered voices, dates — set into the pavement itself. It's the neighborhood keeping its own record, and it's the most honest introduction Los Angeles offers to any of its districts.

The record needs keeping. Japanese immigrants began settling here in the eighteen eighties, barred by prejudicial laws from living in most of the city, and built the heart of Japanese America — by nineteen forty-one, a community of some thirty thousand people. In nineteen forty-two, a wartime order forced Japanese Americans on the West Coast from their homes into incarceration camps, and this neighborhood emptied. It did not sit empty. Black families arriving from the South for defense work, shut out of most of Los Angeles by restrictive housing covenants, moved in — and for a few years this was Bronzeville, a jazz district whose clubs drew some of the great players of the era. After the war, Japanese American families returned and rebuilt, though the community numbered a fraction of what it had been. The sidewalk marks all of it, including the line for nineteen forty-two.

Today the neighborhood holds its institutions close: the Japanese American National Museum anchors the east end of the street, legacy businesses run generations deep, and the plaza fills for festivals that have outlasted everything the twentieth century did to this place.

Most neighborhoods get one history. This one has carried several — building, forced removal, another community's brief flourishing, return — and instead of smoothing that over, it set the whole account into the pavement where you can't miss it. Walk the block slowly and read as you go. In a city famous for paving over its past, this is the block that paved its past in.
