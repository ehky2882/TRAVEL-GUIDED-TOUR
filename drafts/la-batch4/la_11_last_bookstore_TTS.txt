The name started as a joke — the kind you make when you're fairly sure you're doomed.

You should be at Fifth and Spring, at the ground floor of the Spring Arts Tower. Step inside and the joke gets its punchline: marble columns rising to a high coffered ceiling, because this room was a bank — Citizens National, in a building from nineteen fourteen, back when Spring Street called itself the Wall Street of the West.

Josh Spencer started selling books online in two thousand five and opened a storefront in two thousand nine, right as bookstores everywhere were closing — hence the name, equal parts gallows humor and prophecy. Two years later he moved into this banking hall, and the city responded by making a former bank one of the most photographed bookstores in the world.

Look around the ground floor first — hundreds of thousands of new and used books shelved under those columns, plus a wall of vinyl. Then find the vault: the bank's original steel door still swings open, and the crime and mystery novels are shelved inside, which is exactly where they belong. Upstairs is the Labyrinth — a maze of used books where the shelves turn corners that don't quite make sense, books arranged by color, a window built of books, and the famous tunnel: an archway of stacked volumes you walk straight through.

Everyone repeats the line that Los Angeles doesn't read. Here's a bookstore that opened at the worst possible moment, in a downtown people had written off, and named itself for the end of its own industry — and became one of the reasons people come downtown at all. The bank moved on decades ago; the joke never came true. Both failures turned into this. Give it an hour. The tunnel is upstairs, but the vault is the better secret.
