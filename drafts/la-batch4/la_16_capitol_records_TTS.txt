Everyone can see it's a stack of records. Nobody who built it meant it to be.

You should be on Vine Street, just north of Hollywood Boulevard, looking up at a round white tower — thirteen circular floors with curved sunshades ringing each one, and a tall needle of a spire on top. The Capitol Records Building, finished in nineteen fifty-six: the first circular office building in the world, and one of the most recognizable towers in America.

Now the story, because the truth is better than the myth. In the mid nineteen fifties, a twenty-four-year-old architect named Louis ny dorf was handed an assignment his firm called Project X — a thirteen-story office building for a client so secret even he wasn't told who it was. He designed it round for entirely practical reasons: circular floors waste less wall, cost less to build, and stay cooler in the sun. It was an idea he'd been carrying since his graduate thesis. He had no idea he was designing for a record company.

When Capitol's president saw the model, he hated it — for exactly the reason you love it. He worried a round tower would look like a stack of records, a cheap gimmick in a city where hot dog stands were shaped like hot dogs, and demanded a rectangular building instead. It was the lender who overruled him, arguing that a tower people noticed was a tower that leased. So the resemblance everyone assumes was the whole point is, on the record, a coincidence the client tried to prevent.

Two details before you go. The light blinking at the tip of the spire has been spelling the word Hollywood in Morse code since the building opened. And below the parking lot, underground, are echo chambers designed with the help of the guitarist Les Paul — studios where some of the most treasured American recordings of the last century were made.

So Hollywood's perfect visual pun was never intended by anyone: an accident of geometry that the city decided meant something, and now it does. That may be the most honest monument this neighborhood has — a building that became its own legend without ever asking to.
