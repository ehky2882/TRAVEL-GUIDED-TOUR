For seventy-three years, the most famous awards ceremony on earth didn't have a home.

You should be on Hollywood Boulevard at Highland Avenue, facing the grand archway and the wide staircase climbing into the Ovation Hollywood complex. At the top of those stairs, behind the columns, is the Dolby Theatre — and if the staircase looks familiar, that's because you've watched people in gowns and tuxedos climb it on television.

Here's the problem this building solved. From nineteen twenty-nine onward, the Academy Awards wandered — hotel ballrooms, movie palaces, auditoriums, nine different venues over seven decades, the ceremony growing larger than any room built for other purposes could gracefully hold. So at the turn of this century, Hollywood finally built the show a machine of its own. The theater opened in two thousand one and has hosted the ceremony since two thousand two — designed by David Rockwell not as an opera house that gets televised, but as a television broadcast wearing an opera house costume.

The details give it away. The stage is one of the largest in the country — over one hundred feet wide, big enough to build a small city of scenery on. There are camera positions engineered into the architecture, and cabling runs under the theater to the trucks outside. Best of all: the seating is arranged so that every seat is within a few seats of an aisle, because on live television, a winner's walk to the stage is dead air, and dead air costs money.

One more detail, inside the entrance: columns naming every Best Picture winner since the beginning — with blank spaces left above, reserved for winners decades into the future. The industry built its optimism into the stonework.

Hollywood Boulevard sells fame in every direction — stars in the sidewalk, handprints in concrete, tours to the sign. This building is where the selling stops for one night a year and the industry performs the ritual on itself: the gowns, the staircase, the envelope. The rest of the year you can tour it, or just stand here at the bottom of the staircase — which is, after all, exactly where the cameras put you.
