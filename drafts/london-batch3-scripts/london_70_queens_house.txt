The Queen's House

This modest white box is the most revolutionary building in England — the first piece of true Classical architecture in the country, and the ancestor of every white-columned house from here to Virginia.

You're in Greenwich, looking at the Queen's House — a small, perfectly plain white villa, square and serene, sitting between the Old Royal Naval College and the rising green of Greenwich Park. To modern eyes it's almost invisible: a nice white house. To understand it, you have to see it as people did when it was finished around 1635 — when everything else in England was brick gables, twisted chimneys, and timber framing. Into that world, Inigo Jones — back from Italy, soaked in the architecture of Palladio — dropped this: pure proportion, white render, flat roofline, a perfect cube of Classical logic. It would have looked like a spaceship.

This is the building where English Classicism starts. Before the Banqueting House in Whitehall, Jones began here, with a villa for Anne of Denmark, queen of James the First — by one story, a gift of apology from the king after he swore at her in public when she accidentally shot one of his favourite hunting dogs. Royal marriages have produced stranger architecture for stranger reasons, but not many.

Inside is the Great Hall, a perfect forty-foot cube, and the Tulip Stairs — the first self-supporting spiral staircase in Britain, an elegant helix of iron and stone curling upward with no central column, each step resting on the one below. It's also, for connoisseurs of such things, the site of one of the most famous "ghost photographs" ever taken, a nineteen-sixties image of a shrouded figure on these stairs that has never been satisfactorily explained — the house wears the story lightly.

[beat]

Look at it one more time, then mentally line it up with every white-porticoed bank, courthouse, and plantation house in the English-speaking world. The line runs through St Martin-in-the-Fields, through Georgian London, across the Atlantic. It starts at this quiet white box by the park.

It's free to enter, and full of art. Climb the Tulip Stairs yourself — carefully, and with an open mind.
