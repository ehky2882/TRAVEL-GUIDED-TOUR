Cutty Sark

This ship was built for one job — getting tea from China to London faster than anyone else — and she was made obsolete almost the moment she launched.

You're at Greenwich riverside, looking at the Cutty Sark — a tall sailing ship, all masts and rigging, sitting raised in a dry dock with a sweep of glass around her hull, so she seems to float on a glass sea. She's a tea clipper, launched in 1869, one of the fastest sailing ships ever built. The clippers were the thoroughbreds of commerce: the first tea of the season fetched premium prices in London, so the trade became an annual race from China — and the ships were honed for nothing but speed, knife-thin hulls under acres of sail.

Her timing was terrible. The very year she launched, the Suez Canal opened — a shortcut to Asia that sailing ships couldn't easily use but steamships could. The tea race was over almost before she joined it. She found a second career hauling wool from Australia, where for a decade she made some of the fastest passages ever recorded under sail, before steam swallowed that trade too. The fastest of her kind, and the last: she's the only intact tea clipper left in the world.

The name is pure Scottish mischief. "Cutty sark" means a short shirt — it's what the young witch wears in Robert Burns's poem Tam o' Shanter, chasing Tam through the night. The figurehead under the bowsprit is that witch, arm outstretched, gripping a horse's tail — the trophy she snatched in the poem.

She nearly didn't survive. Preserved at Greenwich since the nineteen-fifties, she caught fire during restoration in 2007 — though, by luck, much of her original fabric had been removed for conservation and was spared. The restoration that followed raised her three metres into the air, which is why you can now do something no sailor ever could: walk directly underneath her, and run your eye along the gleaming hull lines that made her fast.

Walk under the hull and look up. Built for a race that ended as she entered it — and still, a century and a half on, the most beautiful machine in Greenwich.
