The Old Royal Naval College

Christopher Wren split this building in two — cut his own design down the middle — because a queen refused to lose her view of the river. The gap is the masterpiece.

You're at the Old Royal Naval College in Greenwich, among a set of grand Baroque buildings ranged along the Thames — twin domes, colonnades, courtyards of pale stone. This is Wren again, at full imperial scale. It was built from the sixteen-nineties as the Royal Hospital for Seamen — not a hospital in the modern sense, but a home for retired and wounded sailors, the navy's answer to the army's Chelsea pensioners.

Notice the strangest thing about the composition: it's split. Two matching halves with a wide avenue of open space between them, running from the river up toward the Queen's House and the park behind. Wren wanted one great unified building, but Queen Mary insisted the view from the Queen's House to the river be preserved — so the architect cut his composition in two and turned the constraint into the design. The result frames the little white Queen's House like a jewel and gives Greenwich the most theatrical sightline in London. It's a masterclass in what a good architect does with a "no."

Inside one wing is the Painted Hall — sometimes called Britain's Sistine Chapel — the sailors' dining room, every inch of its ceiling and walls covered in a vast Baroque painting by James Thornhill glorifying Britain's monarchs and naval power. It took him nineteen years. The pensioners eventually found it too grand to eat in. And it was here, in 1806, that the body of Admiral Nelson lay in state after Trafalgar, with enormous crowds filing past.

[beat]

The site keeps one more secret: this was the birthplace of the Tudors' world — Greenwich Palace stood here, where Henry the Eighth and Elizabeth the First were both born. The palace is gone; the navy's baroque masterpiece stands on its footprint. Power handed the site from dynasty to fleet.

Stand in the central avenue, the gap the queen demanded, and look both ways — river one way, Queen's House and park the other. The hole in the design is the best thing in it.
