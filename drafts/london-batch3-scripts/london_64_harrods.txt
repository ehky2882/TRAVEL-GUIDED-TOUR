Harrods

This shop's old motto was Latin for "all things, for all people, everywhere" — and it once sold, among other things, lion cubs, aeroplanes, and an alligator.

You're on Brompton Road in Knightsbridge, looking at Harrods — a vast terracotta palace of a department store, a whole city block of ornate brick and stone, picked out at night by eleven thousand light bulbs tracing its outline. It began in 1849 as a small grocery shop run by Charles Henry Harrod, and grew, floor by floor and department by department, into the most famous department store in the world: over a million square feet, hundreds of departments, its own dress code, and a food hall so ornate — Edwardian tiles, painted ceilings — that it's worth visiting as architecture even if you buy nothing.

The old motto, Omnia Omnibus Ubique — all things, for all people, everywhere — was meant almost literally. For much of the twentieth century Harrods ran an "anything" service: the store famously sold and delivered practically whatever you asked for. Customers ordered exotic pets from its pet department — lion cubs, alligators, a baby elephant on at least one famous occasion — alongside aeroplanes, yachts, and funerals. The store's boast was that it could source anything legal, and for decades it more or less could.

Two further pieces of history hide in the building. In 1898 Harrods installed the first escalator in England — a moving belt that so alarmed customers that staff reportedly stood at the top offering brandy to the shaken. And the store's ownership has tracked the world's money: family firm, then public company, then the Egyptian businessman Mohamed Al-Fayed, and now the sovereign wealth fund of Qatar — a fair map of where wealth has lived over the past century.

[beat]

That's really what Harrods is: a barometer. What it sells, who shops there, who owns it — each era's answer tells you who has money and what they want. The lion cubs are long gone, banned by law and by conscience; the marble halls of luxury brands are what "everything" looks like now.

Step into the food hall at least, brandy optional. The tiles alone are worth the crowds.
