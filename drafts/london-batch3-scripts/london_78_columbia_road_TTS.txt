Columbia Road

Six days a week this is a quiet East End street of Victorian shopfronts. On Sunday mornings it disappears under flowers — a market tradition with roots in refugee gardeners and a failed philanthropic dream.

You're on Columbia Road in Bethnal Green — a short street of small two-storey Victorian shops, painted in deep colours, housing independent galleries, bakeries, and curiosity shops. If it's Sunday morning, you'll know: the entire street becomes a flower market, stalls running its full length, banked with cut flowers and plants, the air full of scent and the trademark shouts of the traders — "everything a fiver!" — crowds shuffling shoulder to shoulder. By mid-afternoon it's all gone, packed up until next week.

The flowers have deep roots here. The Hyooguhno silk weavers who settled this part of East London in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries brought with them a passion for growing things — they were famous for their gardens and especially their competitive flower growing, prized orikyoolas and tulips raised in tiny East End yards. The area's flower trade grows out of that inheritance, formalised into a market in the Victorian era and shifted to Sunday in deference to the area's Jewish traders, for whom Saturday was the Sabbath.

There's a ghost story in the street's name. In the eighteen sixties the philanthropist Angela Burdett-Coutts — the richest woman in England, Dickens's friend — built Columbia Market here: a colossal Gothic market hall, like a cathedral for costermongers, intended to give street traders a grand, hygienic home. It was a noble, total failure — the traders preferred the street, the hall stood mostly empty, and after decades of half-uses it was demolished. The street kept the name; the philanthropy is a vanished Gothic phantom.

What survived instead was the thing nobody designed: the street itself as the market, one morning a week, governed by the rhythm of the traders and the seasons. The grand hall failed; the informal habit has now run for well over a century.

Come before nine for the flowers or near closing for the bargains, then let the crowd carry you. It's London at its best trick — transforming completely, then putting itself back by tea-time.
