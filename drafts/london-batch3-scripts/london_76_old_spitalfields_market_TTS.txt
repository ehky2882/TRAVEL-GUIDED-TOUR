Old Spitalfields Market

A market has traded on this ground since the thirteen eighties — and the handsome Victorian hall it lives in now was very nearly demolished for offices, twice.

You're at Old Spitalfields Market, just east of the City's glass towers — a large covered market hall of brick, iron, and glass, full of stalls selling food, clothes, records, crafts, and antiques, ringed by restaurants. The name tells you where you are: "Spitalfields" is a worn-down contraction of "hospital fields," the land of the medieval priory and hospital of St Mary Spital that stood here from the twelfth century. A market charter was granted in sixteen thirty-eight, but informal trading on the ground goes back further still — call it the better part of seven centuries of selling on this spot.

For most of its modern life this was a wholesale fruit and vegetable market, the East End's answer to Covent Garden — the elegant hall you're standing in was built in the eighteen eighties by Robert Horner, a former market porter who'd risen to own the lease, which is its own little parable. Lorries, crates, and pre-dawn chaos defined the place until nineteen ninety-one, when the wholesale market moved out to bigger premises, leaving the question every Victorian market eventually faces: demolish or reinvent.

It nearly went. With the City expanding hungrily eastward, the site was earmarked for office development, and for years the market survived on temporary reprieves while campaigners fought for it. The compromise you see today gave part of the site to new buildings while saving the Victorian hall — which then filled with the stall-and-street-food life that now defines it.

There's a neat symmetry in what it became. This corner of London has always lived off the meeting of City money and East End hustle — the market literally stands on the seam between the two, glass towers on one side, Brick Lane on the other. Seven hundred years in, it's still doing what markets at boundaries do: letting both sides mix.

Come hungry, ideally at the weekend. The traders change, the goods change, the centuries change — the selling, on this exact ground, has barely paused since the Middle Ages.
