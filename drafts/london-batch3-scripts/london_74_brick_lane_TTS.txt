Brick Lane

There's a building on this street that has been a Protestant chapel, a Methodist chapel, a synagogue, and a mosque — the same building, changing faiths as the street changed people. That building is Brick Lane's whole story.

You're on Brick Lane in the East End — a long, narrow street of curry houses, bagel shops, vintage clothing, street art, and market stalls, the heart of London's Bangladeshi community, with street signs in English and Bengali. The name comes from the brick kilns that worked here when this was the city's edge. What the street became is London's great landing strip: for three centuries, wave after wave of new arrivals has settled here, made the street theirs, prospered, and moved on — leaving the street to the next wave.

The sequence runs like geological strata. First the French Hyooguhnoze, Protestant refugees fleeing persecution in the sixteen eighties, who built the silk-weaving industry — their grand weavers' houses, with wide attic windows built to light the looms, still stand on the side streets. Then Irish labourers. Then, from the late nineteenth century, Jewish refugees fleeing pogroms in Eastern Europe, who made this the most densely Jewish quarter in Britain — the famous beigel shops at the top of the street are the living remnant. Then, from the mid-twentieth century, Bangladeshi migrants, largely from Silhet, whose community defines the street today and gave it the name "Banglatown."

And the proof stands at the corner of Fornyay Street: the Brick Lane Mosque. Built in seventeen forty-three as a Hyooguhno chapel, it later served Methodists, became the Great Synagogue of the Jewish East End, and in nineteen seventy-six became a mosque. One building, four communities, every change of faith tracking a change of population. On its wall is a sundial with a Latin motto: Umbra soomus — "we are shadows."

We are shadows — meaning we pass. Every community that has owned this street believed itself permanent; each in turn handed it on. Gentrification is simply the current wave, and the street is mid-change again, as it always is.

Walk it end to end — beigels at one end, biryani in the middle, street art everywhere — and find the sundial. The street will outlast its current self too. It always has.
