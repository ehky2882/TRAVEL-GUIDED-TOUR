Whitechapel Gallery

In nineteen thirty-nine, Picasso's Gairneeka — the most famous protest painting of the century — hung not in a national museum but here, in a gallery built to bring art to the East End poor. The entry fee was a pair of boots, for the men fighting Franco.

You're on Whitechapel High Street, at the Whitechapel Gallery — a handsome Arts and Crafts building of pale stone with an arched entrance, modest in scale among the bustle. It was founded in nineteen oh one by Samuel and Henrietta Barnett, a vicar and social reformer working in what was then the poorest quarter of London. Their conviction was straightforward and radical: that the people of the slums had as much right to great art as anyone in the West End, and that beauty was not a luxury but a need. The gallery was built with no permanent collection — just changing exhibitions of the best art that could be borrowed, free to all comers.

It went on to a century of punching far above its weight. It gave many landmark artists their first major London shows, and it staged exhibitions that changed British art history — including the nineteen fifty-six show often credited with launching Pop Art in Britain.

But the defining moment came in January nineteen thirty-nine. Picasso's Gairneeka — his vast, howling canvas painted in response to the bombing of the Basque town of Gairneeka during the Spanish Civil War — was touring to raise support for the Spanish Republic. In London it was shown here, in the East End, among communities that had sent volunteers to fight in Spain. Admission was famously by donation — including, at one showing, a pair of usable boots for the Republican front. Workers queued after their shifts to see it.

Hold that image: the century's greatest anti-war painting, hanging a few streets from the docks, paid for in boots. It vindicates the founders' whole premise — that the East End didn't need art watered down; it needed the strongest art there was.

The gallery is free to this day. Go in and see what's on. The premise hasn't changed in a century and a quarter: the best art, where the people are.
