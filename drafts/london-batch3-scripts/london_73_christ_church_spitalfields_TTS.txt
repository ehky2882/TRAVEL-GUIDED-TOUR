Christ Church Spitalfields

This church was built to intimidate — a great white monolith planted among dissenters and immigrants to remind them who was in charge — and it has loomed over every community that's lived in its shadow since.

You're on Commercial Street in Spitalfields, looking up at Christ Church — and "up" is the word. A massive white Portland stone front: a huge arched portico like a gateway, and above it a great broad tower narrowing into a spire, the whole thing wildly oversized for a parish church, less a building than a presence. This is Nicholas Hawksmoor, Wren's brilliant, strange pupil, and it's usually counted his masterpiece.

It exists because of an act of parliament. In seventeen eleven, alarmed that fast-growing East London was full of nonconformists — Protestants outside the official church, including the French Hyooguhno silk weavers settling in Spitalfields — the government funded a series of grand new churches for the capital's edges. The intent was frankly assertive: plant monumental Church of England architecture among the dissenters and the poor, and let the stone do the preaching. Hawksmoor delivered exactly that — a church that doesn't welcome so much as preside.

His style is like nobody else's: massive, severe, abstract, assembled from pure geometric forms — people reach for words like "primitive" and "elemental." Centuries later that strangeness made him a cult: writers and psychogeographers spun dark mythologies around his churches, casting him as the "devil's architect" — entertaining nonsense, but a measure of how unsettling this architecture genuinely is.

And the neighbourhood kept changing beneath it. Hyooguhno weavers, then Irish labourers, then one of the largest Jewish communities in Europe, then the Bengali community of today — wave after wave arriving poor at the same streets, each in turn living under this same white tower. The church decayed almost to ruin in the twentieth century and was magnificently restored. The communities changed completely; the monolith stayed.

Cross the street for the full height of it, then step inside the restored interior if it's open — flooded with light, all that severity suddenly serene. It was built to overawe outsiders. The outsiders, generation after generation, turned out to be the story.
