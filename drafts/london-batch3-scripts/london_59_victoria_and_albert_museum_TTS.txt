The Victoria and Albert Museum

This museum was founded on a strange, practical idea: that British design was falling behind the world's, and the cure was to show ordinary people beautiful things.

You're on Cromwell Road in South Kensington, looking at the Victoria and Albert Museum — a vast, ornate palace of red brick and pale stone, with a crown-topped central tower over the entrance. The V and A is the world's largest museum of decorative arts and design: not paintings on walls so much as the made world itself — furniture, fashion, ceramics, jewellery, ironwork, textiles, sculpture, theatre, photography — some two and a quarter million objects spanning five thousand years.

It was born out of national anxiety. After the Great Exhibition of eighteen fifty-one — the giant world's fair in Hyde Park that showed Britain both its industrial power and, uncomfortably, how much better French and other foreign design often was — the profits were used to buy land here and build institutions to fix the problem. The museum's founding mission was bluntly educational: collect the best-designed objects from every age and country, show them to manufacturers, students, and the public, and raise the standard of British taste and trade. It even opened in the evenings, with gas lighting, so working people could come after their shift — and it housed the world's first museum restaurant, on the logic that a fed visitor stays longer.

Inside, the range is gloriously disorienting. The Cast Courts hold full-size Victorian plaster casts of European masterpieces — including a replica of Michelangelo's David and a two-piece cast of Trajan's Column from Rome, so tall it had to be displayed cut in half. There are whole rooms of iron keys, of theatre costumes, of medieval reliquaries; the fashion galleries run from court dress to the catwalk.

The founding bargain still holds: it's free to enter, and it's still, at heart, a working library of human making — a place designers actually come to study. The Victorians built it to teach a nation taste. Whether it managed that is debatable; what it certainly became is the world's attic of beautiful things.

Go in without a plan and turn left when you'd normally turn right. The V and A rewards getting lost better than any museum in London.
