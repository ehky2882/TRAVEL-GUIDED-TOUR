The Royal Albert Hall

Every summer this great round hall holds the world's biggest classical music festival, where the cheapest tickets put you closest to the orchestra — standing, for the price of a sandwich.

You're at the Royal Albert Hall in South Kensington, looking at a vast elliptical drum of red brick and terracotta, wrapped in a continuous mosaic frieze and capped with a shallow dome of glass and iron. It opened in 1871, named for Prince Albert, Queen Victoria's husband, who had died a decade earlier — the man whose vision of an education quarter funded by the Great Exhibition built everything around you here. Victoria, still deep in mourning, opened the building; by some accounts she was too overcome to speak.

Walk a little of the circumference and read the frieze running around the top — "The Triumph of Arts and Sciences," a Victorian mission statement in mosaic. The hall beneath it has hosted practically everything: symphonies, boxing matches, sumo tournaments, rock concerts, film premieres, political rallies, tennis. It's less a concert hall than the nation's great all-purpose indoor amphitheatre.

But its heart is the Proms — the summer festival of nightly classical concerts running for eight weeks, held here since the Second World War. The founding idea, from 1895, was radical: bring classical music to people who couldn't afford it, with cheap tickets and an informal atmosphere — you could originally eat, drink, and smoke during the music. The tradition survives in the "Prommers": for each concert, hundreds of standing places in the arena, right at the orchestra's feet, are sold on the day for a few pounds. The best spot in the hall, for the least money, to whoever turns up.

One engineering confession: the hall opened with notoriously bad acoustics — a wicked echo, so pronounced that wags said it was the only place a British composer could be sure of hearing his work twice. It took until 1969 to fix it properly, with the fleet of mushroom-shaped acoustic discs you can see floating beneath the dome.

Stand back and take in the whole drum. A memorial to one man's belief that art and science belong to everyone — which, every summer, for the price of a sandwich, it briefly makes true.
