Bevis Marks Synagogue

This is the oldest synagogue in Britain in continuous use — over three hundred years of unbroken worship — and it exists because England, having expelled its Jews for three and a half centuries, finally let them return.

You're at Bevis Marks, on the eastern edge of the City of London, at the entrance to a courtyard — the synagogue sits back from the street, behind buildings, as the law of its day required. Inside the courtyard is a dignified brick building of seventeen oh one that looks, from outside, rather like a Protestant chapel of its period. That resemblance has a simple cause: it was built by a Quaker carpenter, working in the style he knew.

The deeper story starts with an absence. In twelve ninety, England expelled its entire Jewish population — the first such national expulsion in medieval Europe — and for three hundred and sixty-five years there was officially no Jewish community here at all. Resettlement came in the sixteen fifties, under Oliver Cromwell, when a community of Sefardic Jews — families of Spanish and Portuguese origin, many of whom had lived outwardly as Christians under the Inquisition — was quietly permitted to live and worship openly in London. Bevis Marks is that returned community's permanent home: built within two generations of readmission, and in continuous use ever since — through every war, including the Blitz, services have never stopped for long.

Step inside if it's open to visitors. The interior is largely original: dark oak benches, brass candelabra hanging low — the great candelabrum from Amsterdam, sister community to this one — and candlelight still used for some services. Three hundred years of the same families, the same prayers, the same room.

Look up when you leave the courtyard. The synagogue now sits in a canyon of glass — the Gherkin and the City's towers crowd directly over it, and battles over new skyscrapers blocking its ancient right to daylight have run for years. A building made deliberately modest, so as not to provoke, now holds its small ground against the tallest neighbours in Britain.

It's the quietest kind of monument: not to an event, but to a continuity — worship, unbroken, in one room, since seventeen oh one.
