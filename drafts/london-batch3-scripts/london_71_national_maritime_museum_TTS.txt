The National Maritime Museum

Britain is an island that built its entire history on the sea — and this is where the evidence is kept, down to the coat a national hero was wearing when he was shot.

You're in Grennitch, at the National Maritime Museum — the largest museum of its kind in the world, sitting at the foot of the park among the colonnades that once served the old naval hospital's school. The premise is simple and enormous: for an island nation, the sea was everything — food, trade, war, empire, exploration, migration — and you cannot tell Britain's story without telling its maritime one. This museum holds that story in objects: ship models, charts, instruments, uniforms, paintings, figureheads, millions of items.

The single most famous object is a coat. The undress uniform coat Admiral Nelson was wearing at the Battle of Trafalgar in eighteen oh five, when a French marksman's musket ball struck him down at the moment of his greatest victory — the bullet hole is clearly visible in the left shoulder. Few objects in any museum collapse the distance to a historical moment so completely: not a painting of the event, but the actual cloth, the actual hole.

But the museum's honesty is in its breadth. Alongside the heroics, it deals squarely with what the sea also carried: the galleries cover the transatlantic slave trade and the empire that British sea power built and enforced — the trade in human beings that ran through British ports and British ships for centuries. The maritime story is told here as triumph and as harm, navigation and exploitation in the same fleet.

That's what makes it more than a hall of ship models. The sea made Britain rich, fed it, defended it, and connected it to the world — and the same ships carried conquest and cruelty. An island can't choose only the flattering parts of its relationship with the water; this museum, at its best, doesn't try.

It's free, and bigger than it looks. Find Nelson's coat first — and then give equal time to the Atlantic galleries. Both are the truth, and they belonged to the same navy.
