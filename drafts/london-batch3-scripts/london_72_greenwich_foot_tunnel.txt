The Greenwich Foot Tunnel

You can walk under the Thames here — fifty feet down, through a white-tiled Victorian tube, for free, exactly as dock workers have done since 1902.

You're by the river at Greenwich, next to the Cutty Sark, looking at a round red-brick building with a glass dome — like a small mausoleum or an oversized pepper pot. Its twin stands directly across the water on the Isle of Dogs. These are the entrance rotundas of the Greenwich Foot Tunnel, and the glass domes cap the shafts: inside each one, a lift and a spiral staircase drop about fifty feet down to a tunnel that runs under the riverbed to the other side.

It was built for workers. At the turn of the twentieth century, thousands of people living south of the river worked in the docks and shipyards on the north bank, and their only way across was an unreliable ferry — miss it, or find it cancelled by fog, and you lost a day's pay. The tunnel, opened in 1902, was a piece of plain municipal decency: a free, permanent, weatherproof crossing so working people could get to work. It has stayed free, and open, ever since.

Go down and walk it. The experience is wonderfully strange: a long, gently dipping tube lined with glazed white tiles — around two hundred thousand of them — cool in all seasons, the sound of your footsteps running ahead of you, with the full weight of the Thames moving somewhere overhead. At the far end you surface on the Isle of Dogs, where a riverside path gives you the classic postcard view back across the water: the Old Royal Naval College, the Queen's House, the park rising behind — Greenwich arranged as a single composition, the view Canaletto painted.

[beat]

Near the northern end, look for where the tunnel lining changes: a stretch repaired with iron segments after a wartime bomb damaged the tube. The tunnel kept working through the Blitz, like the people it was built for.

It's a fifteen-minute round trip and one of London's best free experiences: under the river, into another borough, and back — through a tube of white tiles built so that nobody would ever again lose a day's pay to fog.
