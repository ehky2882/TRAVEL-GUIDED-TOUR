Dennis Severs' House

Inside this house, a family of eighteenth-century silk weavers seems to have just left the room — the fire is lit, the tea is half-drunk, the bed is warm — and none of them ever existed.

You're on Folgate Street in Spitalfields, outside number eighteen — one of the old Huguenot weavers' houses, dark brick, shutters, a gas lamp burning above the door. From the street it's merely handsome. Inside is one of the strangest experiences London offers, and it's the life's work of one man.

Dennis Severs was a Californian who fell in love with old London, bought this derelict house in the nineteen-seventies, and lived in it without electricity, gradually composing each of its ten rooms into a single continuous fiction: the home of the "Jervises," an invented family of Huguenot silk weavers, whose fortunes rise and fall through the rooms across two centuries. It is not a museum. Severs called it a "still-life drama," and his rule for visitors was blunt — you pass through in silence.

The conceit is that the family has just stepped out of whichever room you enter. Candles burn; a fire crackles in the grate; there's half-eaten bread on the table, tea cooling in cups, a wig tossed on a chair, an unmade bed still dented; you hear footsteps and voices from the floor above, church bells, a carriage outside. Every sense is staged. You move through someone's life mid-moment, room by room, from prosperous parlour to the cold, peeling garret of the family's decline — the whole arc of Spitalfields itself, told through one imaginary household.

[beat]

What makes it art rather than waxwork is that nothing is explained. No labels, no ropes, no guide — just you, in silence, assembling the story from the evidence of the rooms. Severs died in 1999; the house is kept exactly as he composed it, candles and all.

It's open on limited days, often by candlelight, and it books out. If you can get in, go — and keep the silence. It's the only house in London where the past isn't displayed but interrupted.
