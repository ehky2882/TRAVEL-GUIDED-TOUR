The Natural History Museum

This building was designed as a cathedral — but a cathedral to nature, with monkeys climbing the arches and a blue whale where the altar would be.

You're on Cromwell Road, looking at the Natural History Museum — a long, twin-towered building in striped blue-grey and honey-coloured terracotta, with a great rounded Romanesque entrance like the door of a cathedral. That resemblance is completely deliberate. The architect, Alfred Waterhouse, built it in the eighteen-seventies as a "cathedral to nature," and the religious architecture carries a quiet argument from its founder, the great anatomist Richard Owen — the man who, incidentally, invented the word "dinosaur" — that the study of nature was a form of reverence.

Get close to the walls before you go in, because the building itself is part of the collection. The terracotta is crawling with sculpted animals and plants — monkeys scrambling up arches, fish, birds, lizards, leaves — and there's a code: living species decorate one wing, extinct species the other. The whole facade is a catalogue of life, modelled in clay.

Inside, the centrepiece of the great vaulted central hall is a real blue whale skeleton, twenty-five metres of her, diving from the ceiling — she's named Hope, and she replaced the museum's famous cast diplodocus, Dippy, in 2017. The collection behind the galleries is staggering: some eighty million specimens, including ones collected by Darwin himself on the voyage of the Beagle. This is not just a display; it's one of the most important scientific research collections on the planet, with hundreds of working scientists behind the walls.

There's a good irony in the cathedral idea. Owen, who built it, was a fierce opponent of Darwin's theory of evolution. Yet the museum he created became one of the great engines and showcases of evolutionary science — his temple to creation now tells, room after room, the story he resisted.

Join the queue with the school parties and go in. Look up in the main hall — at the whale, and past her at the painted ceiling of plants. It's the grandest room in London with a skeleton in it, and it was built to make you feel exactly the awe you'll feel.
