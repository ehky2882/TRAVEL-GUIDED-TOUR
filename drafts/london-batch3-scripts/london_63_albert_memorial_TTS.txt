The Albert Memorial

A queen lost her husband and answered with this: a hundred and seventy six feet of gilded Gothic excess, with the dead man sitting golden at the centre, holding a catalogue.

You're in Kensington Gardens, opposite the Royal Albert Hall, looking at the Albert Memorial — an enormous Gothic canopy, like a cathedral spire stood on the ground, glittering with gold, mosaic, and enamel, sheltering a giant gilded statue of a seated man. The man is Prince Albert, Queen Victoria's husband, who died of illness in eighteen sixty-one at just forty-two. Victoria's grief was total and famous — she wore black for the remaining forty years of her life — and this monument, finished in the eighteen seventies to designs by George Gilbert Scott, is that grief made architecture: the most elaborate memorial ever raised in Britain to someone who wasn't a monarch.

Look at what golden Albert is holding. Not a sword, not a sceptre — a book. It's the catalogue of the Great Exhibition of eighteen fifty-one, the world's fair he championed, whose profits built the entire museum quarter around you. The memorial's makers chose to immortalise him not as a prince but as the patron of arts, science, and industry — a man holding, essentially, a programme.

Walk around the base. The white marble frieze wrapping the podium carries a hundred and sixty-nine life-size carved figures — poets, musicians, painters, architects, sculptors from across history, a sort of stone encyclopaedia of Western culture. At the four outer corners sit marble groups representing the four continents as the Victorians saw them — Europe, Asia, Africa, the Americas, each with its emblematic animals — a piece of imperial worldview now read very differently than intended, and worth looking at with that in mind.

Taste has whiplashed about this thing for a century and a half. The Victorians thought it sublime; the twentieth century found it ludicrous, and it was left to blacken and decay — the gold vanished under grime so long that many Londoners never knew the statue was gilded at all. A vast restoration in the nineteen nineties re-gilded Albert, and opinion has mellowed into affection.

Stand back where you can see the Albert Hall behind it. Memorial and hall, grief and purpose, facing each other — a marriage commemorated on a truly imperial scale.
