Chelsea Physic Garden

Behind a high brick wall by the Thames is London's oldest botanic garden — founded to train apothecaries, and quietly responsible for changing what half the world farms.

You're at the Chelsea Physic Garden, on the Chelsea Embankment — though from the street you'd hardly know it. A high wall hides nearly four acres of garden that have been here since 1673, when the Worshipful Society of Apothecaries founded it as a teaching garden. "Physic" means healing: this was a working classroom where apprentice apothecaries — the pharmacists of their day — learned to identify, grow, and prepare medicinal plants, in an age when botany and medicine were the same subject and a misidentified leaf could kill your patient.

The site was chosen shrewdly. The walled riverside plot traps warmth — the garden has its own gentle microclimate, noticeably milder than the streets around it — which lets it grow things that shouldn't survive outdoors in England, including what's claimed as the largest outdoor fruiting olive tree in Britain. Inside, the beds are still arranged the old way: by use and by family — plants for medicine, poisons clearly labelled, dye plants, edibles — a living pharmacy laid out in soil.

Its reach went far beyond Chelsea. In the seventeen-thirties the garden sent cotton seed to the new colony of Georgia, a contribution to establishing cotton growing in America — a small botanical favour with a vast and ultimately terrible economic history attached. Through its seed-exchange network, set up in the same era, it still swaps seeds with botanic gardens worldwide, one of the oldest such exchanges on earth. And its early curators — men like Philip Miller, the most famous gardener of the eighteenth century — trained and supplied the botanists who went out with the great voyages.

What makes it special now is that it's still itself. Kew is vast and imperial; this is small, walled, specific — a working medicinal garden on its original site, three and a half centuries on, where every bed has a purpose and most plants have a job.

It's open to visitors most of the year. Go in, find the poison bed, and read the labels. Medicine started here — leaf by leaf, carefully labelled, behind a warm brick wall.
