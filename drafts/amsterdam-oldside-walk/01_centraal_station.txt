Stand on the square with your back to the water, facing the station's long red-brick front. If it reminds you of a palace, or a cathedral, that's no accident — and if you've already been to the Rijksmuseum, you may feel you've seen this building before. Same architect: Pierre Cuypers, who gave the city its two great gateways, one for art and one for arrivals, and dressed them both in the same Gothic-Renaissance finery. Amsterdam is held, more or less, between two buildings by the same man.

But here's the sly part — the part that sets up everything on this walk. For six hundred years, the spot you're standing on didn't exist. This was open water: the harbour mouth, the city's actual front door, where ships came in from the sea and tied up along the quays. When the railway age arrived and the city had to choose where to put its terminus, it chose, of all places, here. Three artificial islands were raised in the harbour itself, thousands of wooden piles went down, and the new front door was built squarely in the middle of the old one. Amsterdam plugged its own harbour to welcome the train.

Which means every arrival since has stepped ashore exactly where sailors did for centuries. And that's who this walk follows: the arrivals. Sailors, traders, refugees, believers of inconvenient faiths — and the famously practical way this city decided to accommodate them all.
