Every walk in this city eventually touches the canal ring and its postcard calm. This one doesn't — or only at the very end. This is the walk through what the ring was built to escape: the oldest square kilometre of Amsterdam, the medieval port, where the city learned the trick that made everything else possible.

Call it the tolerance ledger. Amsterdam's oldest quarter has spent seven centuries absorbing arrivals — sailors, traders, refugees, believers of inconvenient faiths — and it managed them not with warmth, exactly, but with a bookkeeper's genius for accommodation: everything has its place, its conditions, and its price. Sin, zoned. Faith, licensed. We'll read the entries one by one — a railway cathedral built in the harbour mouth, a sea dike turned Chinatown, the oldest building in the city with graves for a floor, the most argued-about neighbourhood in Europe, a church hidden in an attic — and finish on the Dam, where the whole arrangement is carved in stone.

Practical notes: the route is short and flat — about two kilometres, an hour at an easy pace, more if the church or the temple draws you in. Parts of it pass through the red-light district; there are ground rules there, and I'll give them to you clearly when we arrive.
