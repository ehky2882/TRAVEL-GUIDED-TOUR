From the outside: a canal house. Seventeenth century, handsome, narrow, nothing to see. That is the entire idea.

Climb through it, though — and you can; it has been a museum for well over a century — and the top floors open into something impossible: a complete Catholic church in the roof, with a marble altar, an organ, galleries on two levels, and seating for a congregation of about a hundred and fifty, all folded invisibly into the attics of this house and the two behind it. It was built in the 1660s by the merchant who lived downstairs, a Catholic named Jan Hartman, and it served its parish for two centuries.

Here's the arrangement it reveals. After the city went Protestant in the 1570s, Catholic worship wasn't hunted — it was delisted. You could believe what you liked, gather as you pleased, even build something as elaborate as this, on one condition: the street must never see it. No towers, no bells, no door that admitted to being a church. Everyone knew, of course. The authorities knew; the neighbours knew; the sheriff could usually be persuaded to keep knowing nothing. Faith became one more thing the ledger could accommodate — permitted, but behind glass, which by now should sound familiar.

That, more than any canal or gable, is the local invention: freedom as a private arrangement, discretion as the price of peace.

One last stretch. Cut through to Warmoesstraat, the busy street running parallel just to the west, turn left, and let it carry you south for a few minutes until it opens onto the great square. The Dam — where this city began, and where our walk ends.
