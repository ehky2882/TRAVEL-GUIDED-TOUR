First, the ground rules, because they matter more here than anywhere else in the city. Never photograph or film the women in the windows, or the windows themselves when they're occupied — not casually, not from a distance, not "just of the street." Keep moving when you're close to the windows rather than lingering in front of them. And remember that people live here: above the neon are ordinary flats — families, students, residents who have watched the world troop past their doors for decades. Be the visitor they don't have to mind.

Now the place. These few streets along the two old canals — the ow duh zides foor burg vahl and the ow duh zides ahk ter burg vahl — are the oldest neighbourhood in Amsterdam, and what happens in the windows is not some modern indulgence but the city's most venerable industry: older than the canal ring, older than the tulips, nearly as old as the dike you walked in on. A port fills every night with sailors, and this city, being this city, concluded early that appetites you cannot abolish are better managed than denied. Today that logic is written into law — sex work here is legal, regulated labour, with rents and taxes and a union — and the women behind the glass are working, in the plainest sense of the word.

That's the tolerance ledger this walk keeps returning to, and it rewards close reading, because the entries always come with conditions. Permitted, but zoned. Legal, but behind glass. Managed, never celebrated. Amsterdam's famous tolerance was never quite a warm embrace — it was a ledger, kept by pragmatists, and it balanced.

The next stop shows the same arrangement from the other side — what tolerance looked like when it was your faith, rather than your trade, that needed a licence. Follow the ow duh zides foor burg vahl north, back toward the station end, and watch the left side for a narrow canal house with a museum entrance. It's called Our Lord in the Attic — and from the street you'd never know, which is exactly the point.
