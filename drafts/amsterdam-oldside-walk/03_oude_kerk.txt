Start with the floor. Whatever else this building is — and it has been many things — it is, first, the city's memory bank: the oldest building in Amsterdam, consecrated around 1306, standing here since the settlement was a fishing village on a dike. And underfoot, the entire floor is made of gravestones — roughly two and a half thousand slabs, covering the remains of some ten thousand Amsterdammers buried across five centuries. There is no path around them. In the Oude Kerk you walk on the dead, and the city made its peace with that long ago.

Look up and you'll find the other half of the trick: a vast timber ceiling, one of the largest medieval wooden vaults in Europe, curved like the hull of a ship turned upside down — fittingly, since this was above all the sailors' church, where crews were blessed before the long voyages east and mourned when they didn't come back. Rembrandt knew this floor well. His wife Saskia is buried here, one name among the ten thousand.

And then there's the setting, which you will certainly have noticed by now. The oldest sacred building in the city stands in the middle of the red-light district, and has for centuries — the church and the neighbourhood grew up together, serving, you could say, adjacent needs of the same arriving sailors. These days the church spends much of its life as a contemporary-art space, which it wears surprisingly well.
