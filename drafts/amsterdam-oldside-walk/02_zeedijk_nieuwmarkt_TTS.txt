You're walking on a sea wall. The zay dike — the name means exactly what it sounds like — is the dike that held the water out of medieval Amsterdam, which is why it curves, and why it sits a shade higher than the streets around it. For most of the city's life this was the first street a sailor touched after months at sea, and it offered everything a sailor might want, in roughly the order he wanted it.

The house at number one, leaning companionably into the street, is in ut ahp yun — "At the Monkeys" — one of only two wooden facades left in the city centre, and an inn since the sixteenth century. The story attached to the name, and it is a story, says that sailors short of cash would settle their bills with monkeys brought back from the East, until the inn was so full of them that guests left scratching. A legend, almost certainly. But it tells you precisely what kind of street this was.

The street nearly died once — these blocks were the most notorious address of the city's heroin years, in the nineteen seventies and eighties — and its recovery is one of Amsterdam's quiet successes. Today it's the heart of the city's small Chinatown, and halfway along you'll pass something genuinely unexpected: the huh hwah temple, its golden roof rising between the gables, the largest Buddhist temple in Europe built in the traditional Chinese palace style, opened in the year two thousand by Queen bay ah triks herself. The newest arrivals' church on the oldest arrivals' street.

The dike delivers you into nyoo markt, the broad square ahead; the turreted castle in the middle is the vahg. It began as a city gate — Saint Anthony's, holding the eastern wall — and when the wall came down, the city recycled it as a weigh house, with guild rooms upstairs. The surgeons' guild that met up there is the same one that commissioned rem brant's Anatomy Lesson — decades before they built their famous domed theatre in the tower.

From the vahg, take the lane leading west out of the square, cross two canals, and the great Gothic church rising ahead is the ow duh kairk — next.
