And here it all comes together — the dam on the am stel, the square the city is named for, and, for our purposes, the ledger's final page.

Look at the two great buildings sharing the west end of the square: the Royal Palace, and beside it the nyoo wuh kairk, the "New Church" — new, in Amsterdam terms, meaning fourteen oh eight. Six centuries ago that church was the pride of the city, and it was meant to be prouder still: plans were drawn for a tower that would have out-topped everything in the Low Countries. It was never finished — fires, money troubles, and, in the end, a city that preferred to spend on the building next door. Stand back and look at the pair of them honestly. In almost any other European capital of the age, the church wins that contest. Here, the city hall does, without visible embarrassment.

That's the whole walk in a single view. A port that took in anyone — sailor, trader, refugee, heretic — not out of saintliness but out of arithmetic, and that managed what it could not abolish: appetite behind glass on one canal, faith behind a plain front door on another, and God himself politely out-built on the main square.

Before you go, walk to the white stone pylon at the far end of the square — the National Monument, which remembers the Dutch dead of the Second World War. The country gathers here every year on the fourth of May, and falls silent. It's worth a quiet moment of your own.

You've now walked the oldest square kilometre of the city. Everything else Amsterdam became — the ring, the museums, the whole planned miracle — began as entries in the ledger they kept here.
