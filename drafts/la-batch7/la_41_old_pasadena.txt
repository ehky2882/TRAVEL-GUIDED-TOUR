OLD PASADENA

Every old building on this street was supposed to be gone by now.

You should be near Colorado Boulevard and Fair Oaks Avenue, the heart of Old Pasadena — about twenty blocks of brick and stone storefronts from the late 1800s and early 1900s, with restaurants and shops behind the old façades and narrow alleys threading between them. It looks inevitable, the way well-loved places always do. It was nearly demolished.

This was Pasadena's original downtown, built in the boom after the railroad arrived in the 1880s, back when the city was a winter resort for wealthy Easterners. Then the twentieth century did to it what it did to old main streets everywhere: business drifted away, the buildings emptied, and by the 1960s and 70s the vacancy rate had climbed toward seventy percent. The district had become the kind of skid row that city planners solved, in that era, with a wrecking ball — and plans circulated to level much of it for redevelopment.

What happened instead was a fight, and the preservationists won it. The city passed one of California's earliest historic preservation ordinances in 1975, a citizens' group formed to defend the buildings, and through the 1980s the unglamorous work got done — seismic retrofits, restored façades, new tenants coaxed into old rooms. The gamble was that history itself could be the draw. It paid off so completely that Old Pasadena is now cited nationally as the model: proof that a left-for-dead main street can be revived rather than replaced. Notice the alleys as you walk — reclaimed as lanes of doorways and patios, they're the district's best trick, an entire second street system hiding behind the first.

Two through-lines cross here, both older than the buildings. The Rose Parade has rolled down this stretch of Colorado every New Year's since the 1890s. And for decades, Route 66 ran right along it — this street was the triumphant last leg into the promised land.

[beat]

So here's the reframe: this is the rarest kind of Los Angeles landmark — not a thing that was built, but a thing that was kept. In a region that solves problems with demolition, Old Pasadena is what stubbornness looks like, a century on, with the lights back on.
