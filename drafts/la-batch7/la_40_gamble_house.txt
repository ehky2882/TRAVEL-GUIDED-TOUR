GAMBLE HOUSE

This is what happened when soap money decided the most luxurious thing on earth was a piece of wood, joined perfectly.

You should be on Westmoreland Place, a quiet private lane just off Orange Grove in Pasadena, facing the Gamble House — a long, low, deep-eaved house of dark shingles and timber, sitting on its rise like it grew there. It was built in 1908 as a winter home for David and Mary Gamble, of the Procter and Gamble fortune, back when Pasadena was where wealthy Midwesterners came to escape the cold.

Here's what makes it radical. In 1908, American money announced itself in marble — columns, gilt, imported ballrooms. The Gambles hired two brothers, Charles and Henry Greene, who believed the opposite: that craft itself was the luxury. So the house shows you its construction. The joinery is exposed and celebrated, the pegs and straps polished like jewelry, the teak and mahogany rubbed until the wood glows from within. Look at the front doors and you'll see the signature piece — a spreading oak tree rendered in stained glass across all three panels, so the entrance itself is a landscape.

And the Greenes designed everything. Not just the house: the furniture, the light fixtures, the rugs, the fireplace tools. Two Swedish-born craftsmen, the Hall brothers, built it all by hand. The family clearly understood what they had, because they kept it, unchanged, until 1966 — and then gave it to the city and a university rather than sell. As the story goes, a prospective buyer mused about painting all that woodwork white, and the family quietly took the house off the market. Whether or not it happened exactly that way, the result is real: almost nothing here has changed in over a century, down to the chairs.

One more layer: the second floor has open-air sleeping porches, because the whole point of a Pasadena winter house was the air itself. This is architecture designed around a climate — the most Californian idea in the building.

[beat]

Movie fans may recognize it as the home of a certain eccentric inventor in a beloved 1985 time-travel film. Fitting, in a way. Walk up the drive and you really have gone back — to the last moment when the height of American luxury was something made slowly, by hand.
