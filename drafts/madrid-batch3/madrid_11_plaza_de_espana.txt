For most of the last century, this was a place you crossed, not a place you stayed — a giant roundabout with traffic on every side, marooning the monument in the middle. Then Madrid did something quietly radical: it put the cars underground.

You're in the Plaza de España, at the western end of the Gran Vía, and what you're standing in is brand new and very old at once. The square reopened in 2021 after two and a half years of work that buried the through-traffic in a tunnel and handed the surface back to people — more than a thousand trees, wide walking paths, and a clear green route that now links the Royal Palace, the Sabatini Gardens, the Templo de Debod and the river beyond. You can walk from here to any of them without meeting a car.

But come to the centre, to the monument that all of this was rearranged around. It's dedicated to Miguel de Cervantes — Spain's Shakespeare, the man who more or less invented the modern novel with Don Quixote. He sits in stone, looking out. And below him, cast in bronze and riding out ahead of him, are the two figures he created: the deluded knight Don Quixote on his bony horse, and his round, earthbound squire Sancho Panza. The author, raised up and still; his characters, down at eye level and in motion.

That's the quiet joke of the monument, and it's a good one. The man is remembered in cold stone, while the people he made up get the warm bronze and the movement — as if the invention had outrun the inventor. Which, in a sense, it has: more people the world over could sketch Don Quixote than could tell you a thing about Cervantes's life.

[beat]

So don't just pass through this time. The whole square was rebuilt so you wouldn't have to. Sit by the pool below the knight, with the two mismatched towers of the 1950s behind him, and notice that you're at a hinge — the modern city's grandest street at your back, and the green road to the old royal heart of Madrid opening out in front of you.
