Look down at the ground beneath your feet. Among the paving you'll find lines of writing set into the stone in gilded letters — sentences from old plays and poems, running along the street like a trail. You're in the Barrio de las Letras, the Quarter of Letters, and the writers under your shoes once lived in the houses over your head.

This was the neighbourhood of Spain's Golden Age — the great flowering of Spanish writing in the fifteen and sixteen hundreds. Within these few narrow streets lived the giants all at once: Cervantes, who gave the world Don Quixote; Lope de Vega, who wrote plays faster than theatres could stage them; and their sharp-tongued rivals Quevedo and Góngora, who feuded in verse the way other men duelled with swords. The lines set into the pavement are theirs — fragments of what they wrote, laid where they walked.

Here's the gift, and it's a strange neighbourly fact. Cervantes and Lope de Vega lived almost on top of each other and could not stand one another — and the geography of their afterlives kept the joke going. Lope's house still stands a couple of streets away, kept as it was. And Cervantes, the more famous of the two, lies buried in a convent right here in the quarter, behind a plain wall most visitors pass without a glance. The man whose words are underfoot all over this district rests, quietly, in the middle of it.

Now look at the square itself, Plaza de Santa Ana. One side is held by the Teatro Español, a theatre that has stood on this spot, in one form or another, for centuries — open-air playhouses once filled this ground, where audiences shouted plays down or carried them on their shoulders. The bars around the edges have their own literary ghosts; one was a favourite of Hemingway, decades later, when the writers drinking here had swapped Spanish for English.

So take a slow loop and read the ground as you go. Most cities hang plaques on walls to remember their writers. This one did something better — it pressed their words into the street, so that to cross the neighbourhood at all, you have to walk straight through their sentences.
