Stand with your back to the Royal Palace and look at the cathedral across the square. Pale stone, columns, a dome — it could be three hundred years old. It was finished in nineteen ninety-three.

That's the strange thing about the almoodayna. Madrid became the capital of Spain in fifteen sixty-one, and then went more than four centuries without a cathedral of its own. There was always a reason to wait — the cost of running an empire, then the cost of losing one — and the plans kept being shelved. The first stone wasn't laid until eighteen eighty-three, by Alfonso the Twelfth, who meant it at first as a tomb for his young wife. Then the work crawled: the architect died, a civil war stopped everything, the money ran out. Start to finish, it took a hundred and ten years.

Look at the front first. That grey-and-white neoclassical face isn't an accident, and it isn't really the cathedral's own taste. When a new team picked up the unfinished project in the nineteen fifties, they deliberately dressed the exterior to echo the palace it faces — and they lowered the whole building so it wouldn't loom over the king's house across the square. A cathedral built, in part, to defer to its neighbour.

Now step inside, if the doors are open, because the contrast is the gift. After that sober grey shell, the ceiling overhead is painted in hot geometric colour — reds, blues, gold — bright and frankly modern, far more nineteen ninety-three than fifteen sixty-one. The bones are Gothic; the skin is contemporary. Most cathedrals took so long to build that they drifted through styles by accident; this one wears its centuries on purpose.

One more thing, easy to miss: when Pope John Paul the Second consecrated it in nineteen ninety-three, it became the first cathedral anywhere consecrated by a pope outside Rome — a very old honour, handed to one of the youngest great cathedrals in Europe.

So don't come here for age. Come for the opposite — a building that waited four hundred years for its moment, then chose, in stone, to stand a little lower than the palace beside it.
