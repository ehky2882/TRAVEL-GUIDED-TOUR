You're standing between two kinds of power. Behind you, the Royal Palace. Ahead, across the gardens, the Teatro Real — the opera house. One built to be lived in by a monarch, the other built to applaud one. They've been staring at each other across this square since the middle of the nineteenth century.

Start with the statues along the edges of the gardens. A long line of stone kings — Visigothic and medieval rulers of Spain, solemn on their plinths. Here's the gift: they were never meant to stand down here at all. They were carved to crown the roofline of the Royal Palace, a whole skyline of monarchs looking down on the city. Then someone did the arithmetic and got nervous — all that stone, that high, on those walls — and the kings were quietly brought down to ground level. So they ended up here, at your eye line, close enough to read their faces. A royal skyline that lost its nerve.

Now walk to the centre, to the man on the rearing horse. That's Philip the Fourth, and the statue is doing something that, when it was made in the sixteen forties, almost nobody had managed. The horse is balanced entirely on its two back legs — no tail touching the ground, no clever prop. Getting a bronze that heavy to stand on two points without toppling is a genuine engineering problem, and the sculptor, Pietro Tacca, is said to have written to Galileo for help with the maths. The solution was to make the front of the horse hollow and pack the rear solid, shifting all the weight back over the legs that carry it. A statue standing up thanks to physics.

Then turn to the opera house. There's been a Teatro Real here since 1850, though it has been closed, reopened, turned into a concert hall, and reopened again — a building almost as restless as the operas inside it.

So this square is really one quiet joke about permanence: kings meant for the sky, brought down to earth; a horse held up by calculation; an opera house that keeps having to be saved. Stay a while. Even the pigeons here look like they're posing.
