Look at the building in front of you and try to work out what's holding it up. The brick mass seems to hover, its base cut away into shadow, as if someone slid the ground floor out and the whole thing forgot to fall. That's the trick the architects wanted — and it's the smaller of the two surprises here.

This is kysha forum, and it started life as a power station, built around the turn of the twentieth century to feed electricity to this part of Madrid. By the time the kysha foundation took it on, only the brick shell was worth keeping. The Swiss architects hertsog and de moyron kept that old skin, carved away the ground floor so the building appears to float, and crowned it with a rusted-steel top that reads almost like weathered iron lace. If that move sounds familiar, it should: the same pair turned a London power station into Tate Modern. Two dead generators, two of the most-visited art spaces in Europe.

Now turn to the wall beside it — the green one. That's the real showstopper: a vertical garden by the French botanist patreek blon, the first of its kind in Spain. Around fifteen thousand plants, something like two hundred and fifty different species, all growing straight up a sheer wall four storeys high. And here's the gift detail — there's no soil. The plants are rooted in felt and fed by water trickling down from the top, which is blon's whole idea: plants don't actually need earth, just water, light and something to cling to. From across the square it looks less like gardening and more like a painting that happens to be alive.

The square itself is free and open, so you can walk right up, stand under the floating brick, and put your hand near the living wall.

So here's the pattern worth carrying with you down the pasayo del prado: this whole avenue is about looking after the past. The Prado guards old paint, the teessen guards a collection — and here, two outsiders took a discarded power station and a bare wall and proved that the future of a building can be greener, and stranger, than its first life ever was.
