Walk a little way into the Retiro and you reach it through the trees: a palace made almost entirely of glass, mirrored in a small lake, with a few cypresses leaning over the water. On a bright day it barely looks solid — more like a drawing of a building than the thing itself.

This is the Palacio de Cristal, the Crystal Palace, and the first thing to know is that it was never anyone's home. It was built in 1887 as a giant greenhouse — iron frame, glass walls — to hold living plants. Stand back and look at the proportions: the great domed centre, the wings, the way the whole structure exists to let light pour straight through it.

It was built for an exhibition about the Philippines, then a Spanish colony on the far side of the world. The idea was to bring a piece of the tropics to Madrid — exotic plants and flowers under glass for crowds who would never travel there. It's worth being honest about the rest, briefly: these colonial exhibitions also put people on display, men and women brought from the colony to be looked at by visitors. We find that deeply troubling now, and rightly; at the time, much of Europe treated it as ordinary. The beautiful building and that uncomfortable history come from the same moment.

Today the Crystal Palace does something quietly fitting. It belongs to the Reina Sofía museum and is used for contemporary art — but it's usually nearly empty, holding a single installation, sometimes almost nothing at all. After a history of being crammed with things to gawp at, it has become a space whose finest exhibit is the light itself, moving across the glass through the day.

So step inside if it's open, and notice how little there is to see, and how much. The trees outside throw shifting shadows on the floor; the lake bounces ripples up onto the dome; the whole place breathes with the weather. A building made to show off an empire, now content to show you almost nothing but the afternoon.
