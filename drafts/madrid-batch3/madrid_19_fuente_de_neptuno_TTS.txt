In the middle of the roundabout, a sea god rides a chariot pulled by horses with fish tails, a trident in one hand and a writhing sea snake in the other. This is the fwentay day neptoono, and on certain nights of the year it disappears under a sea of scarves and flares as half of football-mad Madrid climbs in to celebrate.

First, the fountain itself. It's one of three that a king commissioned to dress up this boulevard in the seventeen eighties — Charles the Third, turning a scruffy edge of the city into the grand pasayo del prado you're standing on. neptoono is carved from white marble, and like a surprising number of great statues, the man who began him didn't live to finish him: the sculptor died partway through, and his pupils completed the god after his death. Look at the detail in the water around the base — small creatures spouting, the surface always moving. It's theatre in stone.

Now the part locals will tell you. This fountain belongs, by long tradition, to one of the city's two great football clubs — atletiko day Madrid — while their rivals, rayal Madrid, celebrate at the thibayles fountain just up the road. Win a trophy, and the players are paraded to the family fountain to wave at the crowd.

Here's the gift, though, and it's a lovely one: it wasn't always this way. Go back far enough and the allegiances were the other way round — the club that owns this fountain today once belonged to the other one. Loyalties that feel carved in marble turned out to be as movable as everything else in a living city.

And the statue has wandered too. neptoono wasn't always here in the centre of the traffic; he was shifted and turned to his current spot at the end of the nineteenth century, when the city rearranged its boulevards.

So whichever colours you wear, or none, the fountain has the same quiet lesson. The trident, the snake, the rearing sea horses — they look eternal. But the god has been moved, finished by other hands, and claimed by changing crowds. Even neptoono, it turns out, has switched sides.
