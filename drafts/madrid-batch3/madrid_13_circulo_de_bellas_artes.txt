Here's a small secret about Madrid: one of the best views in the city costs about the price of a coffee, and almost no tourist knows to ask for it.

You're at the foot of the Círculo de Bellas Artes, where Calle de Alcalá runs into Gran Vía. From the street it reads as a grand, slightly severe stone building — and it's easy to walk straight past. Don't. This has been a private arts club since 1880, one of the oldest in Europe, the kind of place where painters, writers and filmmakers came to argue and show their work. A very young Picasso took drawing classes inside these walls before anyone knew his name.

But the reason to stop is the roof. Go in, find the lift with the glass doors, pay the few euros, and ride to the top. What opens up is close to the whole city at once: Gran Vía curling away with the Metrópolis building and its winged statue in the foreground, the rooftops of the old centre, Cibeles and the Retiro greenery off to one side, and on a clear evening the mountains behind it all.

Now look up, at the figure crowning the building above you. That's Minerva — the Roman goddess of wisdom and the arts, cast in bronze. She's been the emblem of this club since the beginning, and there's a quiet wit in the choice: most buildings put a saint or a king on the roof, and the artists put up the patron of clever thinking instead. She's looking out over exactly the view you're looking at.

The terrace works as a café and bar, so you can sit with a drink and let the light change — and the light is the point. Come about an hour before sunset and the whole city turns gold, the kind of slow show you can't rush.

So this is the trick worth remembering: in a city of paid viewpoints and rooftop hotels charging for the privilege, the artists' club quietly kept the finest one and let anyone climb up for the price of a ticket. Take the seat. Let Minerva keep watch.
