The line you've been following for two kilometres runs into this hillside — and turns into a park.

You're at mow er park. The name is as blunt as this city gets: Wall Park. All of this open ground was border strip, and when the Wall fell, it lay empty — a dead zone through a dense neighbourhood. Instead of letting it be built over, the people around it pushed, for years, until the strip became this: a long lawn, a scruffy hillside, one of the best-loved parks in Berlin.

Climb the slope. Along the crest runs a surviving stretch of the old inner wall, and here anyone with a spray can is welcome to answer it — the paint changes week by week, layer on layer, a border retaken by amateurs. On Sundays the park below fills with a sprawling flea market, and in the warmer half of the year the stone amphitheatre cut into the hill hosts open-air karaoke, where several hundred strangers will cheer for a stranger who can't quite hit the note. It's one of the kindest crowds you'll ever stand in.

An hour ago you stood at a wall of portraits — the faces of people this border killed. Now you're standing on the border itself, and it's a lawn full of kites and second-hand records and brave, wobbly singing. Berlin refuses to choose between those two truths. It keeps its grief and its Sunday afternoon a short walk apart, on the same line of cobblestones, and it asks you to hold both at once. That's the ghost line, walked end to end — the only place in the city where you can follow the Wall from its saddest ground to its happiest and understand that they are the same ground.

Find a spot on the slope and stay a while. And if someone hands you the microphone — that's between you and the city.
