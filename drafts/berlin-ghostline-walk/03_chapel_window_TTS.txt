The gentlest building on this walk stands where a church was blown up.

The oval structure in front of you, wrapped in a screen of wooden slats, is the Chapel of Reconciliation. Its predecessor, the Church of Reconciliation, ended up stranded inside the border strip in nineteen sixty one — its parish split in two, its doors unreachable, a brick church standing empty between the walls. For years the guards worked around it. Then, in nineteen eighty five — not in war, but in nineteen eighty five — the East German state dynamited it to clear the sightlines. The bells were saved. In two thousand the parish built this in its place: a small chapel of rammed earth, with the rubble of the old church folded into its walls. On most weekdays at midday, a short service here remembers one of the people who died at this border — one person at a time, by name.

Nearby stands the Window of Remembrance: a long wall of rusted steel holding rows of portraits. These are the faces of people who died at this Wall, trying to reach the other side of their own city. The memorial names them, so it can be said plainly here. No figure is agreed on, and this walk won't pretend to one; the honest arithmetic is to stand at the window and meet the faces, one by one, for as long as feels right.

Take whatever time you need. When you're ready, continue north-east along the strip, the direction you've been walking. The memorial runs on for several hundred metres more — and the street around it, you'll notice, gets more and more ordinary as you go. That's the next part of the story.
