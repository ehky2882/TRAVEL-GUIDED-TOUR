All over this city there's a line in the ground. Two parallel rows of cobblestones, set flush into the asphalt, running through streets and squares, under parked cars and café tables, marked here and there with a small bronze plate: Berliner Mauer, 1961 to 1989. It traces where the Wall stood. If you've spent any time in Berlin, you've crossed it a dozen times without noticing — which is how the city wants it. A scar you step over on the way to lunch.

This walk is different. This time you don't cross the line. You follow it.

We'll cover a little under two kilometres, from Nordbahnhof up Bernauer Strasse to Mauerpark — level ground the whole way, open to everyone, and free. Bernauer Strasse is where the border did its worst, and where the city now remembers it most carefully. Fair warning: this is the hardest ground in these tours. It is also, deliberately, a walk that ends somewhere joyful — and the fact that both things sit on the same line is the whole story.

Start at Nordbahnhof; the S-Bahn brings you right to it. And the first stop is the station itself, because the story of this border begins underground.
