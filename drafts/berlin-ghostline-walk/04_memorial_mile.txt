From here, the memorial thins out — and ordinary life thickens.

Walk the strip north-east and the two start to interleave. Information steles stand among the trees. Flat steel plates set into the ground trace the lines of tunnels that were dug beneath this street, marked and left at that. Patches of the inner wall survive in the grass. And on both sides of all of it: the tram ringing past, bakeries opening their doors, laundry on balconies, someone jogging the length of the border with headphones in.

None of that is disrespect. It's the design. This memorial has no gate and no exit; it simply dissolves, block by block, back into a street being a street — which, if you think about it, is the outcome everyone on this ground was hoping for all along. The city doesn't fence off its past here. It lives on top of it, carefully.

Keep going until the memorial strip runs out. Ahead of you the ground rises into a long green slope, with a wall along its crest that looks nothing like the sombre concrete behind you — this one is a riot of colour. Cross toward it. That's Mauerpark, the end of the line, and the walk saves its answer for there.
