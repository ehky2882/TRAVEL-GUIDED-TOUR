For twenty-eight years, this was a station that trains passed through but never stopped at.

You're at Nordbahnhof, at the southern end of Bernauer Strasse. Before you head for the memorial, give the station itself a minute. It has earned it.

When the border closed in 1961, a few Western S-Bahn and U-Bahn lines had a problem: on their way between two halves of West Berlin, they ran under the East. The trains kept running — East Germany, which needed the transit fees, allowed it — but every station along those stretches was sealed. Entrances bricked up, platforms dimmed, armed guards posted in the gloom. Western trains slowed to walking pace and rolled through without stopping, their passengers peering out at platforms lit like cellars, while above ground East Berliners walked past bricked-up stairways that officially led nowhere. West Berliners had a name for these places: Geisterbahnhöfe. Ghost stations. You are standing in one.

Here is the detail worth keeping: even the guards posted down on those platforms were locked in while they worked, and watched by other guards. The state would not trust anyone that close to a moving train to the West — not even the men it sent to stand there.

The Wall on the surface was only the visible part. The border ran through timetables and tunnels, cellars and platforms — a city divided not just east from west, but above ground from below: three dimensions of border. Panels inside the station hall tell the fuller story if you want it.

Now come up to the street level and out onto Bernauer Strasse. Across the road you'll see a long strip of grass with rows of rusted steel rods rising out of it. That's the memorial ground. Walk in anywhere along it — the next stop is the border itself, or what the city has chosen to keep of it.
