The grass under your feet was once some of the most heavily watched ground in Europe.

You're in the memorial strip that runs along bair now er shtrah suh — a long, open band of green where the border installations stood. Rows of rusted steel rods rise out of the lawn: where the Wall itself is gone, the rods trace it at full height, a wall you can see through.

bair now er shtrah suh was cruel in a very particular way. The buildings on this side stood in East Berlin; the street in front of them lay in the West. The border ran along the house fronts — a front door opened into a different country from the bedroom behind it. In the first days after August nineteen sixty one, people climbed out of these buildings into the West while the ground floors were being sealed beneath them. Then the facades were bricked up. Then the houses were emptied, and finally torn down, until only this open ground remained. Look into the grass as you walk: you can still find their foundations, the outlines of hallways and stairwells, cut off at the ankle.

Further along, one stretch of the border has been preserved at full depth, complete between its two concrete skins — the raked ground, the lamp posts, the patrol road, the strip the guards themselves called the death strip. Stand at it, but there's no need to linger over its mechanics.

Most memorials replace the thing they mourn with something finer. This one refuses. Fragments, absences, rusted rods, foundations in the grass — the wound kept open on purpose, in daylight, in the middle of a working city. Keep walking along the strip, past the rods, until you reach a small oval building wrapped in wooden slats. That's the Chapel of Reconciliation, and it's where this walk goes quietest.
