All over this city there's a line in the ground. Two parallel rows of cobblestones, set flush into the asphalt, running through streets and squares, under parked cars and café tables, marked here and there with a small bronze plate: bair lee ner mow er, nineteen sixty one to nineteen eighty nine. It traces where the Wall stood. If you've spent any time in Berlin, you've crossed it a dozen times without noticing — which is how the city wants it. A scar you step over on the way to lunch.

This walk is different. This time you don't cross the line. You follow it.

We'll cover a little under two kilometres, from nort bahn hohf up bair now er shtrah suh to mow er park — level ground the whole way, open to everyone, and free. bair now er shtrah suh is where the border did its worst, and where the city now remembers it most carefully. Fair warning: this is the hardest ground in these tours. It is also, deliberately, a walk that ends somewhere joyful — and the fact that both things sit on the same line is the whole story.

Start at nort bahn hohf; the es bahn brings you right to it. And the first stop is the station itself, because the story of this border begins underground.
