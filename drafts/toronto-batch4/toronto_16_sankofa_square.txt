Most public squares are named to honour someone. This one was renamed to reckon with someone.

You should be standing near the corner of Yonge and Dundas Streets — one of the busiest intersections in the country, where the crowds cross in every direction at once beneath a wall of giant glowing screens. The open plaza on the southeast corner, with its stage and stepped paving, is Sankofa Square.

Start with the name, because the name is the whole story.

For years, this was Yonge-Dundas Square — named for the street, which was named for a man. Henry Dundas was a Scottish politician of the late seventeen hundreds. When Britain was debating an end to its slave trade, Dundas put forward a motion to make abolition gradual rather than immediate. To his critics, that delay kept people enslaved for years longer than necessary. His defenders, including some of his descendants, argue he was being strategic — softening the measure so it could actually pass. Historians still genuinely disagree about his intent.

In 2025, after years of public petitions and a community review led by Black and Indigenous leaders, the city adopted a new name. Sankofa comes from the Akan people of Ghana, and it means, roughly, "go back and get what you left behind" — the idea that you learn from the past in order to move forward. Its symbol is a bird walking ahead while turning to look back over its shoulder.

[beat]

The change has been contested from the start. Some see it as an overdue reckoning; others questioned the cost, or the process, or whether renaming a square settles anything at all. And in a way, that argument is the point. A square named for the act of looking back has become, fittingly, a place the city is still arguing about how to look back.

Stand in the rush of the crossing, glance up at the screens, and consider that the ground under your feet was renamed to ask a question, not to deliver an answer. Whatever you make of it, it's doing exactly what its name says — making people stop, and reconsider.
