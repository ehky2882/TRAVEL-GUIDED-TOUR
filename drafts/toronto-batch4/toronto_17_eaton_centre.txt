This is a shopping mall with a flock of sixty geese frozen in the air above your head — and a church nearly two centuries old hiding behind it that the mall could not manage to kill.

You should be standing in the central galleria of the Eaton Centre, the long glass-roofed arcade that runs north to south through the building. Look straight up: suspended in the light is a flock of Canada geese, wings spread, captured in the act of coming in to land.

Start with the geese.

The piece is called Flight Stop, by the artist Michael Snow, and it's hung here since 1979. It looks like sixty different birds breaking formation. But here's the trick: it isn't sixty geese. It's one. Snow photographed a single goose, then reproduced it at three different sizes — deliberately making the highest ones smallest, to fake the perspective and trick your eye into thinking the glass ceiling is even higher than it is. The title itself is a pun: a flight, stopped, the way a photograph stops time.

One Christmas, the mall's decorators tied red ribbons around the geese's necks. Snow was not amused — he compared it to hanging earrings on a famous statue — and he took the mall to court. He won. The case became a landmark in Canadian law on an artist's right to protect their own work, and it's studied in law schools to this day.

Now walk out the west side, behind the mall, into a small quiet square. There's a Gothic church there, built in 1847 — the Church of the Holy Trinity. When this mall was planned in the 1960s, the design would have flattened both this church and much of Old City Hall. The public refused, and the mall was forced to build itself around the church instead. And that church was founded on a single condition: that its seats be free to anyone, forever — no rented pews — at a time when most churches charged you for a place to sit.

[beat]

The galleria was modelled on a grand European arcade — a glass cathedral for shopping, with sixty geese where the altar might be. But the real church is the small, stubborn one out back: the building that simply would not move. Stand under the geese a moment, then step outside to it. The mall is what the city built. The church and the geese are what the city refused to let it erase.
