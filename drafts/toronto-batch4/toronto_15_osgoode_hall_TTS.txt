Everyone will tell you the gates were built to keep the cows out. Everyone is wrong, and the truth is better.

You should be standing on Queen Street West, at the long iron fence in front of ozgood Hall, the elegant pale building set back behind a green lawn, with grand porticos and a calm, classical symmetry that feels a world away from the traffic behind you. But look at the fence itself first, and especially at the gates.

They're narrow. Strangely narrow, built so that only one person can squeeze through at a time, turning sideways through a tight iron pinch. For generations, Torontonians have explained these cow gates the same way: the lawn was once pasture, and the gates were designed to keep wandering cattle off the grass.

It's a lovely story. It's also almost certainly a myth. By the time this fence went up, in the eighteen sixties, this was no farm field, the city already had gas lamps, piped water and streetcars. The narrow gates weren't about keeping animals out. They were about controlling people: slowing a crowd to a single file, one body at a time. Students did once try to push a cow through one in the nineteen fifties, just to test the legend. It didn't go well for them.

And what they guard is fitting. ozgood Hall is the home of the law in Ontario, the headquarters of the body that has governed the province's lawyers since seventeen ninety-seven, and home to its highest courts and a library some call the most beautiful room in the country. The original wing went up in the early eighteen thirties, when this stood at the very edge of town.

So here's the quiet joke of the place: a building entirely about the law, ringed by a fence whose famous gates exist to decide, very precisely, who gets to pass and how. Everyone remembers the cows. Almost no one remembers it was really about us. Squeeze through one of the gates yourself, sideways, one at a time, exactly as intended, and you've already understood the building before you reach the door.
