Step off Calle Mayor, into the small square on the south side, about halfway between Puerta del Sol and the Royal Palace. Most people walk straight past the opening. Turn in.

You're standing in one of the oldest squares in Madrid — older than the grand boulevards around it, older than the capital itself. Back when Madrid was a small Castilian town, the council met right here, and the square took its name when the king granted the town the title of Noble and Loyal: la villa, the town. This was the centre of it.

Three buildings frame you, and here's what's worth slowing down for — each comes from a different century, and together they read like a timeline you can take in without moving your feet.

Start with the tower on your left, the Torre de los Lujanes. Fifteenth century, brick and stone, the oldest surviving civil building in the whole city. Tradition says a king of France was held prisoner inside it — Francis the First, captured at the Battle of Pavia in 1525 and brought here. There's almost no evidence for it, but the story has clung to these stones for five hundred years, which tells you how good a story it is.

The building in the middle is the Casa de Cisneros — sixteenth century, delicate carved stonework, the Renaissance arriving in a town about to get much bigger. Look for the little covered bridge linking it to its neighbour, slung over the lane below, so the city's officials could cross without touching the street.

And the third, the Casa de la Villa — seventeenth century, heavier, with dark slate spires, built by the same architect who gave Madrid its Plaza Mayor. This was the town hall.

Three buildings, three centuries — and you can watch the whole climb from village to royal capital just by turning your head.

[beat]

Here's the quiet part. Madrid governed itself from this square for roughly three hundred years; the city hall only moved out in 2007, across town to a grander palace. So you're standing where the capital grew from, now mostly left to the pigeons and the occasional wedding photo. The boulevards moved on. This stayed.
