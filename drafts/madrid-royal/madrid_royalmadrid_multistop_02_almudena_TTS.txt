Turn and face the almoodayna — the cathedral filling the south side of the forecourt, pale stone and a dome, directly opposite the palace's grand front. The two buildings stare at each other across this open space, and that face-off explains the cathedral's whole strange character.

Madrid was an oddity among great Catholic capitals: for centuries it had no cathedral at all. The capital was planted here in the sixteenth century, but the city's main church wasn't begun until the eighteen eighties, and then it crawled — stalled by money, by war, by changing tastes — and wasn't finished and consecrated until nineteen ninety-three, by Pope John Paul the Second. A cathedral that took over a hundred years, completed within living memory.

And look at how careful it is. It was deliberately built low and restrained, in a style chosen to harmonise with the palace rather than challenge it. A cathedral would normally be the boldest thing in a city — soaring, dominant, the building everything else defers to. This one defers instead, sitting quietly across from the crown's house and keeping its head down. Even the interior is unusually modern and plain, because so much of it was finished late, on a budget, in a hurry.

That's the gift of standing here: two huge buildings facing off, and the church being the polite one. In Madrid, the palace won the staring contest before the cathedral was even built.

When you've taken in the pair of them, head out to kaye bylen and follow it north along the eastern side of the palace to the open square just beyond — the platha day oryentay. It's full of kings.
