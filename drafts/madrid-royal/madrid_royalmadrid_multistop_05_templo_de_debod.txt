You've come up onto an open rise above the river, and ahead of you, sitting in a shallow reflecting pool, is something that has no business being in Madrid: a genuine ancient Egyptian temple. The Templo de Debod is real, and it's old — more than two thousand years old, built when Egypt was ruled by the last of the pharaohs and the early Romans.

It's here because of a rescue. In the 1960s Egypt built the great Aswan dam, and the rising water threatened to drown a whole stretch of ancient monuments. An international effort moved the most important of them to high ground; Spain helped, and in 1968 Egypt gave this temple to Spain in thanks. It was shipped over stone by stone and rebuilt here, on this hill, opening in 1972.

The hill itself carries its own heavy history — this is where Madrid's defenders made a stand against Napoleon's troops on the second of May, 1808, the uprising Goya painted and that you can stand in front of in the Prado. But that's another walk. Tonight, this hill is for the temple.

Because this is where you end, and you've timed it if the sun is low. This is the finest sunset in the city: the temple in silhouette, doubled in its pool, the sky going copper behind it, the whole western horizon open over the Casa de Campo. We started at the hard stone heart of the monarchy and walked out through its ring of green, and we finish here — on the crown's furthest green edge, watching the sun go down behind a two-thousand-year-old temple that a desert kingdom once gave away. Stay for the last of the light. There's no better place in Madrid to lose half an hour.
