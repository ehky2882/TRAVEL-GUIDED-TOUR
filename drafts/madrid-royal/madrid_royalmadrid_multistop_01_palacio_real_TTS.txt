You're in the platha day la armaireea, the wide paved court on the south side, with the full front of the Royal Palace rising ahead of you — pale stone, rows of columns, the whole thing balanced and severe and very large. By floor area it's the biggest royal palace in Western Europe, thousands of rooms behind that facade.

Here's the useful thing to know, and then we'll move, because the palace is the one part of this walk we don't go inside. The Spanish royal family doesn't actually live here. The king works here — state ceremonies, official receptions, the formal business of the crown — but he and his family live in a far plainer house, the tharthwayla, out on the edge of the city. So this enormous building is less a home than an office and a stage: the crown's public face, kept up for the occasions that need grandeur.

Now turn your attention to what's around it rather than what's in it. You're standing in a vast empty forecourt. Behind the palace, to the west, a whole wooded garden runs down toward the river. To the north, a formal garden of hedges and fountains. To the east, a landscaped square. The palace sits in the middle of all of it like a stone in a setting — a hard centre ringed on every side by green. That ring is the real subject of this walk, and we're about to start tracing it.

Begin by turning to face south, across the forecourt. The pale cathedral closing off the far side is the almoodayna — and it had a difficult job, standing this close to a palace.
