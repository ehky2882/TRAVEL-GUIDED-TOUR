A palace is only the hard centre of a monarchy. The soft part is the green it keeps around it.

This short walk circles the seat of the Spanish crown — the Royal Palace — but it spends most of its time in the gardens and squares wrapped around it. That's the idea worth carrying: the monarchy didn't just build itself a vast stone house, it surrounded that house on every side with landscaped ground — a formal garden to the north, a wild one to the west, a sculpted square to the east — a ring of green insulating the crown from the city. We're going to walk that ring, and finish on its far edge at something the crown never built and could never have imagined: a genuine ancient Egyptian temple, two thousand years old, set up on a hilltop to watch the sun go down.

So: we start at the palace, take in the cathedral that had to be built without upstaging it, round the eastern side to the opera house and its square of statue-kings, climb to the formal gardens on the north flank, and walk on up to the temple for sunset.

It's tightly clustered and easy — everything but the final stretch is within a few minutes' stroll, and the last leg is a gentle rise. Begin in the great paved forecourt on the south side of the palace, the platha day la armaireea, where you can see the full front of the building.
