You're in the platha day oryentay now — a curved, formal square between the palace and the opera house, laid out with hedges, gravel walks and a great many statues. At its centre, a king on horseback: Philip the Fourth, the bronze balanced improbably on the horse's two back legs, a piece of seventeenth-century engineering that, the story goes, took advice from Galileo to keep it upright.

But look around the edges of the square at the other statues — a long line of stone kings and queens, plainly carved, standing at eye level among the hedges. There are around twenty of them, early rulers of the Spanish kingdoms, and they were never meant to be down here with you. They were carved to stand high along the roofline of the Royal Palace, a crowd of monarchs ringing the top of the building. They were judged too heavy to hoist safely up there — and a popular story says a queen had them taken down after dreaming the palace collapsed under their weight. Either way, the kings came down to the ground, and here they've stayed, close enough to touch.

On the far side of the square is the tayatro rayal, the royal opera house, opened in the eighteen fifties and facing the palace directly — court and culture set up across a garden from each other.

This is the first of the palace's green edges: not wild but sculpted, a formal garden with the monarchy's whole back catalogue of kings standing around in it.

From here, return to kaye bylen and head north, up the side of the palace. A set of gates leads into the formal gardens on its northern flank — the hardeeness day sabateenee.
