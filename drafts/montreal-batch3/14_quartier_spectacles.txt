After dark, look down. Set into the pavement around this district, dots of red light glow underfoot — a trail of them, running past theatres and concert halls. The neighbourhood calls it the Luminous Pathway. Keep the colour in mind; we'll come back to it.

You're in the Quartier des Spectacles — the "district of shows" — centred on Place des Festivals, the big open plaza facing the Place des Arts complex. The numbers behind the calm are startling: packed into roughly one square kilometre are some thirty performance halls holding close to twenty-eight thousand seats, and through the year the district hosts around forty festivals. The fountain built into the plaza floor, with its two hundred and thirty-five in-ground jets, is the largest interactive fountain in Canada — in summer it soaks delighted children, and in festival season it makes way for stages.

The biggest of those festivals owns this ground each summer: the Montreal International Jazz Festival, certified by Guinness as the largest jazz festival in the world, with millions of visits and hundreds of concerts — a huge share of them outdoors and free. That's not an accident of programming; it's the city's oldest entertainment instinct. When Prohibition dried out the United States in the 1920s, Montreal stayed defiantly wet, and Americans poured north to a city of clubs and cabarets — the era that put jazz in Montreal's bloodstream and gave a young Oscar Peterson his first stages.

And where did all that nightlife concentrate? Right here. These blocks were Montreal's Red Light district — for decades the most notorious stretch in Canada. When the city rebuilt the area into a cultural district in the 2000s, it chose, of all colours, red as its signature — the glowing dots, the red banners, the red-washed facades at night. Officially it's branding. But most cities scrub that kind of history off the map; this one turned the red light into the logo.

If a festival is on, you already know what to do. If it's a quiet night, follow the red dots for a block or two anyway — the district shows off best when it's lit and half-empty, humming to itself between shows.
