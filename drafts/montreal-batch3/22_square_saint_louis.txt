Square Saint-Louis is not named for a king, and not for a saint. It's named for two developers — the Saint-Louis brothers, businessmen who built up the blocks around it. Hold onto that: the most romantic square in Montreal was a real estate play from the very beginning.

You'll know you're in the right place by the houses. Around a leafy rectangle of park off Saint-Denis Street stand rows of Victorian homes from the 1880s — greystones dressed up with turrets, gingerbread trim, and paint in colours no committee would ever approve: pink, mauve, sea green. In the middle, under the big maples, a cast-iron fountain — ordered, in the practical Victorian manner, from a foundry catalogue, and moved here from another square entirely.

The square opened in 1876 on the site of an old city reservoir, and the houses that rose around it belonged to the French-Canadian upper middle class — the francophone answer, in miniature, to the anglophone money piling up a kilometre west on the other side of the mountain's slope. But what the square became is more interesting than what it was built to be. Writers and artists moved in as the money moved out, and the roll call of the surrounding streets reads like a syllabus: the playwright Michel Tremblay, the poet Gaston Miron, the poet-politician Gérald Godin and the singer Pauline Julien, and above all Émile Nelligan, who lived just there, on Laval Avenue facing the park.

Nelligan is worth a moment. A prodigy who wrote some hundred and seventy poems, sonnets, and songs before the age of twenty, he suffered a breakdown at nineteen and spent the remaining four decades of his life in institutions — his entire body of work already complete. Quebec never stopped reading him. His bust stands in the square's corner, facing the street where he lived, and people still leave flowers.

The Golden Square Mile turned its wealth into universities and museums. This square turned its own into literature.

[beat]

Take a bench near the fountain before you go — preferably with something to read. It's the local custom, a century and a half running, and the square seems to expect it of you.
