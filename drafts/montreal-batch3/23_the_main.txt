Check any street sign in Montreal — Sainte-Catherine East, Sherbrooke West — and every one of those easts and wests is measured from the pavement under your feet. This is Boulevard Saint-Laurent. The whole city counts from here. Montrealers just call it the Main.

Zero point of the addresses, and for a very long time, the zero point of something heavier. You've already met the divide, down in the old city, where a British admiral and a French one still stand a square apart. Here is where that divide was drawn on the map: for generations, the city east of this boulevard spoke French, the city west of it spoke English, and the two halves ran their own schools, banks, newspapers, and lives. Two cities on one island — a novelist here famously called it two solitudes — with this street as the border between them.

And then the border did something no one planned: it filled up with everyone else. From the 1890s on, the Main was where new arrivals stepped off the boats and started climbing — first the Yiddish-speaking Jewish community, whose garment factories made this street hum and whose language was for a time among the most spoken in Montreal, then Chinese, Italian, Greek, Portuguese, Hungarian, Haitian, wave after wave settling directly on the line, belonging fully to neither side and eventually to both. In 2002, Canada declared the boulevard a National Historic Site — not for a building, but for that story: six kilometres of arrival.

The proof is edible. Schwartz's, a few steps along, has been brining its smoked meat for ten days at a stretch since 1928, founded by a Romanian Jewish immigrant, and the lineup outside now speaks every language the city has. The border became the seam — the one place the two solitudes had no choice but to meet, over a sandwich if nothing else. The divide today is mostly symbolic; the street that marked it is what stitched the city together.

Walk a few blocks north when you're ready — no destination required. The Main changes languages faster than you can walk, and eavesdropping is, on this street, a form of sightseeing.
