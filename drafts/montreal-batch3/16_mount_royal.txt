However you made it up here — the stairs from Peel, the long winding paths — you've just done the thing that named a city.

You should be on the broad stone terrace in front of the chalet, at the Kondiaronk Belvedere, with Montreal spread out below you and the St. Lawrence glinting beyond the towers. In 1535, Jacques Cartier climbed this hill with hosts from Hochelaga, the Iroquoian village that stood near its base, looked out at some version of this view, and named the place Mont Royal. Say it quickly for five hundred years and you get Montreal. The city is named after the ground you're standing on.

The park itself is younger and very deliberate. It opened in 1876, designed by Frederick Law Olmsted — the man behind New York's Central Park — who insisted the paths climb so gently that the mountain would reveal itself gradually, as an experience rather than an obstacle. If the walk up felt unhurried, that was the design working.

Now the name of this terrace, because it's the best-chosen name in the city. Kondiaronk was a Wendat chief and one of the great diplomats this continent has produced. In 1701, on the ground below you, he helped negotiate the Great Peace of Montreal — dozens of Indigenous nations and New France, ending decades of war. He fell ill and died during the gathering itself, and was buried with full honours. Long before any of that, this island already had a name in Kanien'kéha, the Mohawk language: Tiohtià:ke — often translated as "where the group divides," a place of meeting and parting where the waters split around the island. A gathering place, in other words, for a very long time before it was a city.

Look at the skyline again, and notice something easy to miss: none of the towers stand taller than where you are. That's law, not accident — Montreal decided long ago that no building may rise above its mountain. The skyline defers to the hill. One single building has ever been granted an exception, and it isn't a bank tower; it waits on the mountain's far slope, and it has its own story.

To the east along the ridge stands the great illuminated cross, marking — so the story goes — a wooden one carried up by the city's founder in 1643. Find it after dark, from anywhere in the east end. Then find this terrace again in your memory. The mountain is small, as mountains go. The city just agreed never to look down on it.
