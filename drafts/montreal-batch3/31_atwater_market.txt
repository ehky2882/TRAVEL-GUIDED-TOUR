In 1933, in the worst year of the Depression, Montreal opened two great public markets to keep the city fed. One went up beside Little Italy in the north end. The other is the one whose clock tower is rising in front of you.

This is Atwater Market, on the bank of the Lachine Canal, and its building is the most elegant thing anyone ever put up to sell carrots. The city built it as a relief-era public works project on the site of an old lumberyard, and the architect, Ludger Lemieux, gave a food hall the full Art Deco treatment — a long, dignified brick pavilion with a clock tower that has become the signature landmark of the entire canal. Boaters navigate by it; cyclists measure their rides against it; photographers can't leave it alone.

The arrangement inside has a pleasing logic. The hall itself belongs to the serious trades — butchers of long standing, a fromagerie dense with Quebec cheeses, bakers — while the flower and produce stalls ring the outside, swelling in summer and huddling in winter. It's smaller and more concentrated than its northern sibling: less a wander, more a mission.

But the market's best trick is where it stands. When it opened, the canal out front was a working artery — barges, freight, factory whistles — and the market fed the industrial neighbourhoods packed around it. The factories closed, the canal fell silent, and Atwater could easily have gone down with them. Instead it held on, and when the canal came back to life as a park and a waterway for pleasure boats, the market found itself perfectly placed for a job nobody designed it for: provisioning the picnics. The grass banks where cargo once queued are now covered, on any warm weekend, with people eating things they bought fifty metres away.

A Depression relief project that outlived its harbour and inherited a better one — the canal district's whole story, told in one building with a clock on top.
