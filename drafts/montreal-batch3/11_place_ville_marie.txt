On a hard winter day, half a million people cross downtown Montreal without ever going outside. You're standing on the roof of their city.

This is the esplanade of Place Ville Marie, under the cross-shaped tower that changed everything. Until the late 1950s, this exact spot was a smoking open trench — the rail cut where trains dove into the tunnel under Mount Royal — a wound running straight through the middle of downtown. The developers didn't fill it in so much as build over it: a forty-plus-storey cruciform skyscraper, designed by the firm of I.M. Pei, finished in 1962, with shopping concourses below the plaza where the trains still run deeper down. At night, the beacon on its roof sweeps the sky like a lighthouse that lost its ocean.

But the tower is the visible half of the idea. Below your feet, those concourses kept connecting — to the train station, to hotels, to office towers, then to the métro when it opened in 1966 — and grew into the RÉSO: more than thirty kilometres of corridors linking metro stations, universities, museums, a hockey arena, and thousands of shops, with well over a hundred doors down from the street. Toronto's PATH network held the world record for years; in 2023 the title quietly moved here — Toronto kept the record for underground shopping, Montreal took it for sheer length. Neither city talks about it much, which is very Canadian of them both.

This is also where you should meet the man whose fingerprints are all over modern Montreal. Jean Drapeau was mayor, on and off, for nearly three decades, and he governed like a showman with a drafting table: the métro, the 1967 world's fair, the Olympics — projects sized for a city he insisted on seeing as a world capital. The underground city grew up around his métro like roots around a water line. Keep his name in your pocket; this city will hand it back to you more than once.

Montreal never beat its winter. It conceded the surface for five months a year and built a second city underneath — and then made the second city pleasant enough that people use it in July. Find a door, and go down.
