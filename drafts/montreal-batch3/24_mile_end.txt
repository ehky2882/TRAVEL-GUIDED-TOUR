Breathe in. If the wind is doing its job, somewhere on this street there's wood smoke and warm sesame in the air, because Mile End runs on two rival wood-fired ovens that have scarcely cooled since the middle of the last century.

You should be on Saint-Viateur Street, in the blocks around Saint-Urbain. The storefront with the perpetual line is St-Viateur Bagel, rolling and boiling and baking here since 1957 — founded by Myer Lewkowicz, a survivor of Buchenwald who arrived in Montreal with almost nothing and later said that in the camp, bread was the thing he had dreamed of. A few blocks south, on Fairmount Avenue, stands the older rival: Fairmount Bagel, founded in 1919, open twenty-four hours a day, and — this is true — the first bagel bakery to send its product into orbit, when an astronaut nephew of the owner carried a batch to the International Space Station in 2008. Montrealers divide into camps over which one is better. The correct answer is whichever is warm in your hand.

Now find the dome. At Saint-Viateur and Saint-Urbain rises a church modelled on Hagia Sophia in Istanbul — a great Byzantine dome beside a tower shaped like a minaret. It was built in 1914 and 1915 by Irish Catholics, and there are still shamrocks carved into the facade; today it's the heart of Polish Catholic Montreal, and every July an Italian saint is paraded past its doors along this very street. One building, four communities, and nobody ever felt the need to start over.

That, in one corner, is how Mile End works. The Irish built here, then the Jewish community — Mordecai Richler grew up a few streets over on Saint-Urbain — then Greeks, Poles, Italians, Portuguese, and lately the musicians, artists, and studios. Most neighbourhoods erase their previous tenants; this one keeps everything running under new management.

And the songwriter you met down at the sailors' chapel — Leonard Cohen — lived a short walk south of here for more than forty years, in a plain grey house facing Parc du Portugal, just off the Main. He bought his bagels on this street, and he and the owner of St-Viateur were friends. The city let him be a neighbour instead of a monument, and he stayed for it.
