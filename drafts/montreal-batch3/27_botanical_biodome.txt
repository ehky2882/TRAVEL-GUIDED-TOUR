One of the finest rainforests in Canada lives inside a bicycle stadium.

You're at Olympic Park, and the building in question is the Biodome — the swooping structure beside the stadium. In 1976 it was the Olympic velodrome, built for track cycling and judo, its riders whirring around a banked wooden oval. When the Games moved on, Montreal did something quietly brilliant with it: in 1992 the velodrome reopened as a walk-through world, five living ecosystems of the Americas under one roof. You pass from a humid tropical forest — capybaras, golden lion tamarins, a river you can smell — through a maple forest and the Gulf of Saint Lawrence, down to the sub-Antarctic, where penguins now occupy the building's cold end. Penguins, in a velodrome. It remains one of the best afternoons in the city, especially in January.

Across Sherbrooke Street spreads the other half of the visit: the Botanical Garden, founded in the depths of the Depression in 1931, largely through the vision of a botanist monk, Brother Marie-Victorin. It has grown into one of the great botanical gardens of the world — a Chinese garden built with artisans from Shanghai, a serene Japanese garden, and a First Nations Garden that walks you through the trees and plants this island's first peoples knew long before any of the rest.

You can't miss the tower leaning over everything — the tallest inclined tower of its kind in the world, tilted at an angle no straight tower would dare. It and the stadium below it were the grand gestures of the mayor you met downtown, the one who dreamed in world's fairs and Olympiads. But notice where the life actually is. The park built for two weeks of Games found its permanence in the quieter tenants — the gardens, the greenhouse worlds, the penguins. Montreal's Olympic legacy turned out greenest exactly where it's least Olympic.

[beat]

Give the Biodome its hour and the garden its two, and take the seasons in whatever order pleases you — this is the one corner of Montreal where January and the Amazon are separated by a door.
