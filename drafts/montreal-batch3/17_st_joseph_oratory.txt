The largest church in Canada was started by a doorman.

You should be at the foot of the grand stairway off Queen Mary Road, looking up — a long way up — at a green copper dome riding the shoulder of Mount Royal, one of the largest church domes anywhere in the world. This is Saint Joseph's Oratory, and the whole improbable mass of it traces back to one small, frail man.

His name was Alfred Bessette — Brother André, once he took his vows — and for some forty years he worked as the porter at the college across the street: answering the door, greeting visitors, sitting with the sick. People began to say that the sick he prayed with got better. He always waved the credit away, insisting it was the work of Saint Joseph, and in 1904 he put up a tiny wooden chapel on the mountainside — paid for, the story goes, partly with the nickels he earned cutting students' hair. Pilgrims came. Then more pilgrims. The chapel grew into a church, the church into a basilica begun in 1924 and not finished for another four decades. Brother André died in 1937, at ninety-one, long before it was done. A million people filed past his coffin in the January cold.

Look at the centre of the stairway ahead of you: three flights climb to the church, and the middle one, in wood, is reserved for pilgrims who ascend on their knees, pausing to pray on each of its ninety-nine steps. On most days you'll see someone making that climb.

Two more things. Montreal, as a rule, allows no building to rise higher than Mount Royal — the skyline bows to the mountain, with exactly one exception granted in the city's history, and you're looking at it. The cross on this dome is the highest point in Montreal. Not a bank, not a tower of condominiums — the doorman's church. And inside, in the votive chapel, walls of canes and crutches left behind by pilgrims hang beside thousands of candles; Brother André's heart, preserved at his own request to watch over the basilica, rests upstairs in the museum. It was stolen in 1973 and returned a year later after an anonymous tip — even the crime ended gently here.

In a province that walked away from the church in a single generation, this is the place where the old faith is still visibly, unmistakably alive. Climb at whatever pace suits you. The man this mountain of granite remembers took sixty years; you can take twenty minutes.
