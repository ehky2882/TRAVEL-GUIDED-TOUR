In 2012, a Montreal museum asked the city to vote on the symbol that best represents it. The Olympic Stadium was on the ballot, and so were the Canadiens, Old Montreal, and the cross on top of Mount Royal. Every one of them lost — to the outdoor staircase. And the museum that ran the poll was Pointe-à-Callière, the one built over the very ground where the city was founded. Even the institution guarding Montreal's birthplace concluded that the truest thing about this city is bolted to the front of its houses.

You're standing in the heart of staircase country. Pick any residential street on the Plateau and look down the row: duplexes and triplexes shoulder to shoulder, and spilling from their upper floors, wrought-iron staircases twisting their way to the sidewalk — some straight, some curled like an S, some splitting partway down like the branches of a tree.

The reason, of all things, is regulation. In the decades around 1900, Montreal's population was exploding, and the city wrote rules to manage the boom: buildings had to stand back from the street to leave room for a strip of green, and firewalls had to break up the rows every twenty-five feet. Builders, squeezed for interior space, moved the staircases outside — which also meant no hallways to heat through a Montreal winter, and a full ground-floor flat left over for the landlord. The city's ironworkers did the rest, bending metal into loops and scrolls and leaves, so that each staircase carries its maker's signature.

There's also a story that the Catholic clergy favoured outdoor stairs to keep temptation out of shared hallways. It's a legend — repeated for a century, never proven — but the fact that Montrealers keep telling it says something about who they believe ran this city back then.

None of this was designed to be beautiful. The staircases are a workaround — a building code answered by craftsmen who couldn't help themselves. Montreal chose as its symbol not a monument built to impress anyone, but the accidental poetry of people solving a problem.

Every first of July, when the city's leases turn over all at once, entire households come winding down these spirals — mattresses, dressers, refrigerators, all of it. If a staircase could hold a job, that's the day it clocks in.
