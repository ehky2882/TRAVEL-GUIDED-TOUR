It's 1933. The Depression has its grip on Montreal, the city is struggling to keep food affordable, and the answer it lands on is a market — a place where farmers from the Quebec countryside can sell directly to the people who need to eat. They build it on an old lacrosse field in the north end, where an Irish sports club called the Shamrocks used to play, and they call it the Market of the North. You're standing in it.

Walk the aisles. Jean-Talon is one of the largest open-air markets in North America, and it never closes for the season — it just changes shape. From late spring the stalls spill outdoors, and on summer weekends the lanes go car-free; when winter comes, the market puts up its walls and pulls itself indoors, trading strawberries and corn for cheese, game, and root vegetables. Locals will tell you what's piled on these tables is a better calendar than a calendar: the market always knows the month.

The name came later. For fifty years this was simply the Marché du Nord; in 1983 the city renamed it for Jean Talon, the first intendant of New France — the seventeenth-century administrator who conducted what's often called the first census in North America, counting every household, farm, and cow in the colony back in 1666. There's something fitting in that: the man whose job was knowing exactly what this land could produce now has his name over the place where the land shows up in person, every morning, to prove it.

And notice where you are. The market sits in the middle of Little Italy, and the neighbourhood soaked into it long ago — which is why, between the Quebec farm stalls, you'll find espresso counters, fresh pasta, and produce sellers who can give you three ways to cook an eggplant without writing anything down.

[beat]

Nearly a century on, this place is still doing precisely the job it was built for in one of the hardest years the city ever had: putting the countryside and the city face to face, and letting them work it out over a pile of tomatoes.
