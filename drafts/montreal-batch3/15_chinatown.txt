Four monumental gates, one square block. That ratio tells you nearly everything about this neighbourhood.

You're in Montreal's Chinatown, best experienced from the pedestrian stretch of La Gauchetière Street, which runs gate to gate through the heart of it. The ornate arches at the district's corners are called paifang, and Montreal has more of them than any Chinatown in Canada — four grand ceremonial entrances guarding a territory you can walk across in about five minutes.

The community is far older than the gates. The first Chinese immigrants reached Montreal in 1877, many after brutal years building the country's railways, and by the 1890s these blocks held their laundries, groceries, and clan associations. In 1923, Canada's Exclusion Act shut the door on Chinese immigration for a generation — a plain fact of this district's story, worth knowing as you stand in it. And in the layered Montreal manner, the ground remembers earlier tenants too: before Chinatown, these same streets were the heart of the city's Yiddish-speaking Jewish quarter.

Then, for half a century, the city built against it. A convention centre rose on one flank, a federal office complex on another, expressways and redevelopment carved away streets, and the neighbourhood that once stretched blocks further was compressed to the square you see — while towers and rising rents pressed on what remained. Which makes what happened in 2022 remarkable: Quebec granted Chinatown formal heritage protection, the first Chinatown in the province — indeed in Eastern Canada — to be written into law that way. After a century of being built around, the neighbourhood finally got walls the developers can't move.

The gates, in other words, aren't decoration. They're a boundary a community fought to hold, and the smallest neighbourhood in this city may be the one with the strongest sense of exactly where it begins and ends.

Now do the thing the district actually wants you to do: eat. Duck into a bakery or a dumpling counter on La Gauchetière, take it outside, and stroll the car-free stretch — a five-minute neighbourhood that takes a whole afternoon if you do it properly.
