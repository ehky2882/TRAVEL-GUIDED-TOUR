If this building looks familiar, that's entirely deliberate. You are looking at St. Peter's Basilica — Rome's, the Vatican's — rebuilt at a quarter of the size on a downtown corner in Montreal.

This is Mary, Queen of the World Cathedral, the seat of the city's Catholic archdiocese, facing Dorchester Square. The copy is faithful right down to the details: the colonnaded portico, the great green dome, and inside, over the main altar, a twisting gold-and-copper canopy modelled on Bernini's baldachin in Rome.

Start with the roofline, though, because that's where the copy quietly becomes a Montreal original. On St. Peter's, the statues along the facade are the apostles. Up here, the thirteen figures are the patron saints of the Montreal parishes that paid for them — Saint Patrick for the Irish parish, Saint John the Baptist for the French, and on down the line. It's a donor wall in copper, hiding in plain sight, and a fair portrait of who this church actually answered to.

Now the reason it exists. After the old Catholic cathedral burned in 1852, Bishop Ignace Bourget decided its replacement would be a miniature St. Peter's — and he decided to plant it here, in what was then the city's Protestant, English-speaking west end, about as far from his French Catholic parishioners as he could get. His own architect, Victor Bourgeau — the man who shaped the interior of Notre-Dame — studied the original and refused, saying it couldn't be scaled down. Bourget simply sent a priest to Rome to secretly measure the basilica and build a model, and construction went ahead anyway. The building was never just a church; it was a flag.

It's worth standing here with that in mind, because the world that flag announced has gone. Within a lifetime of its consecration in 1894, Quebec's Quiet Revolution moved schools, hospitals, and daily life out of the Church's hands — one of the fastest secularizations anywhere — and the cathedral now sits ringed by office towers, a quarter-scale Rome in a city that quietly stopped reporting to it.

Step inside if it's open. The marble slabs carry names in gold leaf, the dome hushes the traffic, and the whole argument of the nineteenth century stands there in miniature — magnificent, and complete.
