Remember the rapids. Down at the Old Port, you learned that Montreal exists because furious white water forced every canoe and every ship to stop here, whether they liked it or not. The calm ribbon of water in front of you is what happened when the city finally refused to accept that.

This is the Lachine Canal — fourteen and a half kilometres of hand-dug waterway, opened in 1825 to slip boats past the Lachine rapids at last. Thousands of labourers cut it with picks and shovels, and a staircase of locks lifted vessels the height of a four-storey building between the harbour and the lake upstream.

Then came the twist nobody fully planned. The canal's water didn't just carry boats — it could turn wheels. Factories crowded onto its banks to drink the hydraulic power, and within a couple of generations this corridor held the most diversified concentration of industry in the country: sugar refineries, textile mills, machine works, foundries. If Canada's industrial revolution has a birthplace, you're walking through it. The neighbourhoods around you — Saint-Henri, Little Burgundy, Griffintown — were all built to house the hands.

It ended fast. The Saint Lawrence Seaway opened in 1959 and made the canal obsolete almost overnight; by 1970 it closed to shipping entirely, and the corridor spent decades rusting. The comeback took just as long as the decline: Parks Canada took the site over, the corridor was named a National Historic Site, and in 2002 the locks opened again — this time for kayaks and pleasure boats. The bike path along the banks is now among the most celebrated urban rides anywhere, and the old mills have been reborn as lofts, their painted ghost-signs still fading on the brick.

So the rapids made this city twice. First they stopped the boats, and a trading post became Montreal; then they forced this canal, and Montreal became an industrial capital. And when the water finally retired from working life, the city did the most Montreal thing imaginable — it learned to play in it.
