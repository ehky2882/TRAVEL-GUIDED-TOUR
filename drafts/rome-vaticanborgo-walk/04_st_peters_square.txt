Let the street release you into the square, and stop just inside it.

Ahead is the facade of the basilica, the dome rising behind it; sweeping out to either side of you, two curved colonnades, four columns deep, carrying a parade of stone saints along their roofline. Depending on the day, barriers may channel the space — go as far toward the middle as they allow, and then turn slowly, all the way around.

Bernini, who built the colonnades in the mid-seventeenth century, described them himself as arms — reaching out from the church to gather people in. Stand still and you can feel the idea working. The great buildings of Rome tend to confront you; this one surrounds you. The square is less a forecourt than an embrace, scaled for crowds of hundreds of thousands and somehow not unkind to a crowd of one.

At the centre stands an Egyptian obelisk that has been on this spot since the 1580s. And beneath it all — under the high altar, deep below the dome — lies what tradition holds to be the grave of Peter: a Galilean fisherman, executed in Rome in the first century, whose tomb this entire church was built to crown.

[beat]

Which closes the circle of this walk. You began at an emperor's tomb, and you've ended at a fisherman's. Hadrian's monument had to become a fortress to survive; Peter's became the largest church on earth, its arms open to whoever walks up the street.

Stay inside the colonnade a while. Pilgrims have been finishing journeys on this spot for something like two thousand years, most of them on foot, many of them along the exact line you walked today. However you arrived, and whatever you came for, the square holds room for you too.
