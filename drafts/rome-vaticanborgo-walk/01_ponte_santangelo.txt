Look down the line of angels.

Ten of them, five to a side, each caught mid-gesture on the parapet — and each carrying something: a crown of thorns, a handful of nails, a lance, a sponge raised on a reed. These are the instruments of the Passion, the objects of the crucifixion story, and Gian Lorenzo Bernini designed the series in the late 1660s so that crossing this bridge became a kind of procession. Pilgrims bound for St. Peter's walked the story, object by object, on the last stretch of their journey — the bridge doesn't just deliver you to the basilica, it prepares you for it.

The bridge itself is far older than its angels. Hadrian built it as the approach to his own mausoleum — the round fortress ahead of you — and the three arches beneath the middle of the span are still the Roman originals. For most of the centuries since, this was the main crossing for anyone bound for St. Peter's: the last bridge of a thousand pilgrim roads.

[beat]

The bridgehead behind you, where you stepped onto the span, was for centuries one of the city's places of public execution. The name Rome remembers most is Beatrice Cenci — a young noblewoman put to death here in 1599, condemned with her family for the killing of her violent father, after a trial the whole city argued over. The verdict has never stopped being argued, and her story has been carried ever since in paintings, plays and Roman legend.

An emperor's approach road, a gallery of Baroque devotion, a place of punishment — this bridge has carried nearly every mood the city has ever had. Cross the rest of it now, and come off onto the wide open square in front of the fortress. Castel Sant'Angelo, up close, is next.
