This walk runs between two tombs.

At one end stands the drum-shaped fortress across the river — built eighteen centuries ago as the mausoleum of the emperor Hadrian. At the other, out of sight for now, rises the largest church on earth by most measures, raised over what tradition holds to be the grave of a Galilean fisherman. Between the two: a bridge, a castle, and a single straight street. Rome has been walking this line for most of its history, and pilgrims by the million have finished their journeys along it.

You should be standing at the southern end of Ponte Sant'Angelo, on the city side of the Tiber, with the bridge and its white marble angels stretching away toward the round castle on the far bank. The full route is only about a kilometre and a quarter, flat the whole way — and since the traffic in front of the castle was sent underground, you can now walk it end to end without crossing a single road.

This is a walk about faith and fear, and about what each of them builds: an emperor's tomb turned fortress, a hidden corridor for popes in flight, a grand avenue for arrivals — and at the end of it all, an embrace.

Walk out onto the bridge, and stop when you reach the middle.
