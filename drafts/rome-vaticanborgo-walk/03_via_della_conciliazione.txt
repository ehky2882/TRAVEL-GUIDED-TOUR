The name of this street means "reconciliation" — and the street itself is the youngest thing on this walk.

Stand at its eastern end, the castle at your back. Ahead of you, framed dead centre above the rooflines, is the dome of St. Peter's, and you'll have it in view the whole way down.

In 1929, Italy and the papacy signed the Lateran Pacts, ending a standoff that had lasted since Italian troops took the city from the popes six decades earlier. This street commemorates that peace — but it was built by demolition. Until the 1930s, the space you're looking down was filled by the spina di Borgo, a wedge of medieval streets and houses running from here toward the basilica. The regime levelled it in 1936 and 1937 to cut a ceremonial approach it judged worthy of a capital newly reconciled with its church, and the paving wasn't finished until 1950.

[beat]

Rome gained a vista and lost a neighbourhood, and which mattered more is an argument the city has never quite finished.

As you walk, look at the buildings flanking the street. A few of the palaces are centuries older than the road they now stand on — Renaissance survivors of the old Borgo, some left on their original ground, at least one taken down stone by stone and rebuilt to fit the new alignment. The avenue looks all of a piece; it isn't. Pieces of the old quarter are hiding in plain sight along it.

Walk toward the dome. The street runs about five hundred metres, flat the whole way, and when the buildings finally stop and the space opens wide around you, you've arrived. St. Peter's Square is next — and last.
