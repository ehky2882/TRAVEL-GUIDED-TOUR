Look down the line of angels.

Ten of them, five to a side, each caught mid-gesture on the parapet — and each carrying something: a crown of thorns, a handful of nails, a lance, a sponge raised on a reed. These are the instruments of the Passion, the objects of the crucifixion story, and jahn loh rent soh bair nee nee designed the series in the late sixteen sixties so that crossing this bridge became a kind of procession. Pilgrims bound for Saint Peter's walked the story, object by object, on the last stretch of their journey — the bridge doesn't just deliver you to the basilica, it prepares you for it.

For most of the centuries since, this was the main crossing for anyone bound for Saint Peter's: the last bridge of a thousand pilgrim roads.

The bridgehead behind you, where you stepped onto the span, was for centuries one of the city's places of public execution. The name Rome remembers most is beh ah tree cheh chen chee — a young noblewoman put to death here in fifteen ninety-nine, condemned with her family for the killing of her violent father, after a trial the whole city argued over. The verdict has never stopped being argued, and her story has been carried ever since in paintings, plays and Roman legend.
