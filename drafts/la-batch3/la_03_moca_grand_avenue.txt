The most interesting building on Grand Avenue is the one doing its best not to be noticed.

You should be on Grand Avenue between Third and Fourth Street, looking at a low composition of red sandstone — an arched entrance, a barrel-vaulted roof, a cluster of glass pyramids. Up the block, Disney Concert Hall is exploding in stainless steel, and The Broad is wearing its white honeycomb. This is MOCA Grand Avenue, and most of it is below your feet.

Start with what's missing. When the building opened in 1986, critics complained that the architect, Arata Isozaki, had given Grand Avenue a blank wall — no grand staircase, no glass lobby, barely a front door's worth of ceremony. That was the point. Isozaki sank the galleries below street level and turned the museum inward, around a sunken courtyard, so the art lives in quiet, top-lit rooms while the towers carry on overhead. Seven levels, and only four of them above the street — you walk down into it, like the collection is being kept somewhere safe.

And it's some collection. MOCA was founded in 1979 — the only museum in Los Angeles founded by artists — and it built one of the great holdings of art made since 1940 anywhere in the world: Rothko, Rauschenberg, the whole postwar story, roughly seven thousand works. General admission is free, thanks to a single donor's gift, which makes this one of the best free rooms in the city.

Here's the detail worth keeping. A share of the money that built MOCA came from a redevelopment rule requiring Bunker Hill projects to set aside one and a half percent of their budgets for public art. This hill was once a dense Victorian neighborhood; it was cleared in the 1960s to make room for the towers around you. The towers, in a small way, paid for the museum at their feet.

[beat]

So the newest-looking street in Los Angeles keeps its conscience underground — a red sandstone box holding the era's art, funded by the very rebuilding that erased what came before. Disney Hall gets the photographs. This one plays the long game. Step down into the courtyard and go in — it costs you nothing but the descent.
