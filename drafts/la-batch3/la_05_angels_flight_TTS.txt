The whole ride is two hundred ninety-eight feet. It might be the best dollar fifty in Los Angeles.

You should be on Hill Street, across from Grand Central Market, looking at a steep track climbing Bunker Hill, with two orange-and-black wooden cars on it and a boh zar archway at the sidewalk reading Angels Flight. The cars have names — ol ih vet and sy ny, after two biblical mountains — and they've been climbing this hill, on and off, since nineteen oh one.

Watch them move first. The two cars are each other's counterweights: they share a cable, so as one climbs, the other descends, and they pass in the middle like a slow-motion handshake. It's billed as the shortest railway in the world, and it's one of only about half a dozen inclined railways still operating anywhere in the United States.

Now the part you can't see. When Angels Flight opened, Bunker Hill was a neighborhood — a dense hilltop of Victorian mansions gone to boarding houses — and the fyoo nik yuh ler was how residents got down to the shops and streetcars without taking a long flight of stairs. In the nineteen sixties the city declared the hill blighted and cleared it — houses, hotels, whole streets — to make room for the towers up there now. Angels Flight was dismantled in nineteen sixty-nine and put into storage, and it stayed in crates for twenty-seven years before being rebuilt half a block from its original spot in nineteen ninety-six. It has had stops and starts since, but it's running — daily, morning to night.

Which makes this little railway the only piece of Victorian Bunker Hill still doing its job. Everything it was built to serve is gone; the hill it climbs has been rebuilt from the dirt up; and the two cars just keep trading places, the oldest moving things downtown. Ride it — a dollar fifty, less with a transit card, and the trip takes under a minute. You board in nineteen oh one and step off in the twenty-first century, which is more time travel than a dollar fifty usually buys.
