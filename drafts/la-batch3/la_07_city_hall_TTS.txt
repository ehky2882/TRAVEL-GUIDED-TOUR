For nearly forty years, this was the tallest building in Los Angeles — not because nobody could build higher, but because it was against the law to.

You should be near the corner of First and Spring Streets, looking up at a white tower rising in tiers to a stepped pyramid at the top. This is Los Angeles City Hall, finished in nineteen twenty-eight — four hundred fifty-four feet, thirty-two floors, designed by a committee of the city's three leading architects and somehow better for it.

Here's the legal part. Since nineteen oh five, the city had capped every building at one hundred fifty feet — thirteen stories — in the name of keeping the skyline harmonious. City Hall, as a public building, was exempt. So for decades the government tower stood three times taller than anything private money was allowed to build — the skyline, all by itself — until voters repealed the limit in the late nineteen fifties and the banks finally caught up in the following decade.

You already know this building, whether you realize it or not. It's stamped on every Los Angeles police badge, and it spent the nineteen fifties playing the Daily Planet in the Superman television show — a Los Angeles tower moonlighting as Metropolis. At the dedication in nineteen twenty-eight, President Coolidge pressed a button in the White House and lit the aircraft beacon on the tower's crown by telegraph, from the other side of the country.

One more fact, for the earthquake-minded. After a retrofit finished around the turn of this century, City Hall rests on more than five hundred base isolators — giant shock absorbers between the building and the ground — making it the tallest base-isolated building in the world. The whole tower is designed to float through a major quake.

Think about what this building says. A boom city, barely seventy years past being a small town, wrote its own government into the sky and made that supremacy law — civic ambition literally written into the zoning code. The towers eventually overtook it, but on the badge, on the screen, in the establishing shot, this is still what Los Angeles looks like. And it's still yours: on weekdays you can walk in, clear security, and ride up to the free observation deck on the twenty-seventh floor — the view the height limit once guaranteed, open to anyone.
