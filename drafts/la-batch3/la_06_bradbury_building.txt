From the sidewalk, this is the least promising landmark in Los Angeles.

You should be at the corner of Third and Broadway, looking at a five-story box of brown brick and sandstone. Grand Central Market is across the street; Angels Flight is two blocks away. Nothing about the outside suggests you should care. Go in anyway — the entrance is on Broadway, and it's free.

Inside is the Bradbury Building, finished in 1893, and one of the most beautiful interiors in America: a narrow, dim lobby that opens into a five-story court flooded with daylight from a glass roof. Rose-colored glazed brick, staircases of Belgian marble, floors of Mexican tile, and everywhere black wrought iron so fine it looks grown rather than forged — ironwork made in France and shown at the 1893 Chicago World's Fair before it was installed here. The two open-cage elevators still run on their original hydraulics, rising through the atrium like birdcages on wires.

Now the story, which is better than it has any right to be. Lewis Bradbury, a dying gold-mining millionaire, wanted a monument. He rejected the design of an established architect and handed the job to George Wyman — a thirty-two-year-old draftsman in that same architect's office, with no formal training. Legend says Wyman only accepted after a session with a ouija-style planchette spelled out a message promising the building would make him famous. That's legend, and worth exactly that much — but his inspiration is documented: an 1887 science-fiction novel that imagined the office building of the future as a vast hall full of light. Wyman built the year 2000 the way a Victorian dreamed it. Bradbury died months before the doors opened.

And the future kept agreeing. This is the most filmed interior in Los Angeles — film noir found it in the 1940s, and a famous 1982 science-fiction dystopia made it immortal. A building designed out of a science-fiction book ended up starring in one.

[beat]

Visitors can go as far as the first landing, and that's enough. Stand there, look up through five stories of iron and light, and remember the plain brick face you just walked past. Los Angeles keeps its best face turned inward more often than you'd think. This is the building that teaches you to check.
