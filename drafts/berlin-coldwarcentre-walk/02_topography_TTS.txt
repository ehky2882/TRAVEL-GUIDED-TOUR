This one corner holds both of the twentieth century's German dictatorships in a single eye-line.

Walk along nee der keersh ner shtrah suh beside the Wall segment — one of the longer battered stretches still standing in the city centre, its concrete gnawed down to the reinforcing rods by souvenir hunters in the early nineties. Then look past it, and down. The open ground behind the Wall, with its gravel field and excavated cellar walls, is where the headquarters of the geh shtah poh, the SS leadership and the Reich Security Main Office stood. In those buildings, the persecution and murder carried out across Nazi-occupied Europe were planned, ordered and filed. The buildings are gone; the ground was left deliberately raw, and an open-air exhibition now runs along the excavated cellars, free to anyone who walks in.

Take in the layering, because nowhere else states it this plainly: the Wall of one dictatorship standing directly on top of the foundations of another. Two systems, forty years apart, stacked in a few vertical metres of one unremarkable street.

You can walk the site now, or note it for a longer visit — it deserves hours, and this walk won't pretend to give it them. Either way, when you're ready, continue east along nee der keersh ner shtrah suh as it becomes tsim mer shtrah suh. Watch the ground again: the cobblestone line runs with you along this street, block after block, keeping you company on the old border itself. Several minutes' walking brings you to a crowded corner with a small white hut in the middle of the road. That's Checkpoint Charlie.
