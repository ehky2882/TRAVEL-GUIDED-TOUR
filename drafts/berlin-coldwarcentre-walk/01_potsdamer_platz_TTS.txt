Almost nothing you can see from here is older than the fall of the Wall.

Stand near the station entrances at pots dahm er plats and turn a slow circle: towers of glass and brick-coloured tile, a tented roof glowing over the Sony Center, traffic pouring through in every direction. In the nineteen twenties this was one of the busiest intersections in Europe — the square that got the continent's first traffic lights, so the story goes, and a replica of that odd little traffic tower stands near the station to make the point. Then the war levelled it, and the border finished the job: the Wall ran straight through here, and for thirty years one of Europe's great crossroads was a silent field of rubble and rabbits between two concrete lines. When the Wall fell, the city suddenly owned an empty prairie in its exact centre — and filled it, in a single furious decade of cranes, with everything around you now. The whole quarter is younger than many of the people walking through it, and it still has the faint self-consciousness of new shoes.

Before you leave the square, two things. First, near the station entrance, six upright slabs of the Wall itself stand in a row along its old course, studded with visitors' chewing gum — an undignified fate, and somehow a very Berlin one. Second, the ground: the double line of cobblestones you were promised crosses the square right here, running under the traffic without breaking stride. Step over it once, deliberately. You'll be doing that all afternoon.

Now walk south along shtray zeh mahn shtrah suh, keeping the railway side on your right, and take the left turn into nee der keersh ner shtrah suh. Ahead, you'll see a long, battered run of the Wall itself, standing above an open excavation. That's the Topography of Terror — the hardest ground on this walk, and the most necessary.
