NORTH BEACH — Stop 4: Coit Tower

You made it. This is Coit Tower — two hundred and ten feet of pale concrete at the top of Telegraph Hill, and one of the most misunderstood landmarks in the city.

Here's the misunderstanding. The money for the tower came from Lillie Hitchcock Coit, a wealthy and famously eccentric San Franciscan who, as a girl, had adored the city's volunteer firefighters — she chased their engines, became a mascot of one of the companies, and signed her name with a little number five, for her beloved Engine Company Number Five, for the rest of her life. So when she left a fortune to beautify the city and this tower went up in 1933, everyone decided it must be built to look like the nozzle of a fire hose, in her honor. It's a wonderful story. It also isn't true — the architects always insisted the shape was simply a clean fluted column, and any resemblance is in the eye of the beholder. But the story is so good the city has never let go of it.

Step inside before you go up, because the ground floor is the real treasure, and it's free. The walls are covered in murals painted in 1934 by dozens of artists put to work by a Depression-era government program. They show California labor — people in the fields, on the docks, in the factories — in a bold, social-realist style, and a few of the details were political enough to cause a small scandal when they were first unveiled. They're some of the finest public murals in the country, and most visitors march straight past them to the elevator.

[beat]

So go up too, if you like — there's a small fee, and the view from the top is the whole bay at once, both bridges included. But take a second down here first.

Think about what you just climbed through. A bookstore that fought for free speech, an Italian square with the wrong man on its statue, a staircase full of parrots and one woman's garden, and now a tower paid for by a firefighter-obsessed heiress and decorated by out-of-work painters. Not one piece of this was built by the people who usually get monuments. North Beach is a neighborhood that made its own landmarks, on its own terms, all the way up the hill — and you just climbed the whole story. Enjoy the view. You earned it.
