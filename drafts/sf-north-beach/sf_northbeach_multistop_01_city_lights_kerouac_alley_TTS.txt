NORTH BEACH — Stop 1: City Lights & Kerouac Alley (TTS-safe)

You're standing in front of the bookstore that went to court — and won.

This is City Lights, opened in nineteen fifty-three by the poet Lawrence ferlinghettee and a partner, and it was the first bookstore in America to sell nothing but paperbacks. That sounds ordinary now. At the time it was close to radical — paperbacks were considered cheap, throwaway things, and a serious bookshop selling only those, staying open late, and letting people sit and read without buying a thing, was a genuinely new idea about what a bookstore could be.

Then it became something bigger. In nineteen fifty-six, City Lights published a slim book of poetry by Allen ginzberg called Howl. The poem was raw, furious, and frank about sex and drugs in a way that got ferlinghettee arrested the next year for selling obscene material. The trial that followed, in nineteen fifty-seven, became a landmark: the judge ruled that the poem was not obscene because it carried real social value, and that decision helped clear the way for publishing serious work that earlier could have been banned outright. A bookstore on this corner, in other words, helped widen what Americans were legally allowed to read.

Now look to the side of the building, at the narrow lane running alongside it. That's Jack keruwack Alley, named for the author of On the Road, who drank and argued just across the way at vuhsoovio, the bar still standing on the far side. Lines from keruwack and other writers are set right into the pavement under your feet, so down here it's worth looking at the ground as much as the walls.

This little corner — a bookstore, an alley, a bar — is where the Beats turned San Francisco into the capital of the American counterculture, a full decade before the hippies took over the Haight. It started with cheap paperbacks and one furious poem.

When you're ready, walk up kohlumbus Avenue a few blocks to the open green of Washington Square, the living room of Italian North Beach, where two white spires are waiting.
