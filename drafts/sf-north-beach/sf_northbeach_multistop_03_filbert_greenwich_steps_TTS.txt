The sidewalk just ended and turned into a staircase. You're now climbing the Filbert Steps, and this is one of the strangest, loveliest corners of San Francisco.

Telegraph Hill is steep — too steep, in places, for ordinary streets — so on its eastern face the roads simply give up and become wooden staircases threading up through gardens. As you climb, notice the cottages tucked along the steps, some of them small wooden houses from the eighteen sixties and seventies. They survived the great fire of nineteen oh six for a blunt reason: this hillside was too steep and too awkward for the flames, and for the crews dynamiting buildings to make firebreaks, so a pocket of the old wooden city lived on right here while grander neighborhoods burned to the ground.

The gardens you're walking through aren't an accident either. For decades much of this slope was a neglected dumping ground, until a woman named Grace marchunt moved in around nineteen forty-nine and spent the next thirty years clearing the trash and planting by hand, turning the hillside into the lush public garden that still carries her name. You're climbing through one person's stubbornness, basically.

And then there's the sound. Listen — and look up. Telegraph Hill is home to a flock of wild parrots, bright green with flashes of red, descended from escaped and released pet birds that found each other, bred, and never left. They're loud, they're fast, and they became famous enough to end up in a documentary. If a squadron of green birds tears overhead screaming, you've just met the locals.

One more thing, about the name. There's no telegraph up there now, but in the eighteen fifties the hilltop held a signal station with a wooden semaphore that told the city below which kind of ship was sailing in through the Golden Gate. The hill was San Francisco's early-warning system, back when a ship full of news or gold could change everything.

Catch your breath here — the view is already good, and it's about to get better.

Keep climbing. The steps deliver you almost to the foot of coyt Tower, the great pale column at the summit, which is our last stop.
