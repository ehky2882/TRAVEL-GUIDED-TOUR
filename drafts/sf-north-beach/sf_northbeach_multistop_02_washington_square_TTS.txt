NORTH BEACH — Stop 2: Washington Square & Saints Peter and Paul (TTS-safe)

This is Washington Square, and it's the closest thing North Beach has to a town square — the neighborhood's shared front yard.

Come in the morning and you'll find people doing tai chi on the grass; come in the afternoon and it's sunbathers, dog walkers, and old men arguing in Italian. But here's the first surprise: the statue in the middle isn't George Washington. It's Benjamin Franklin, donated in the eighteen seventies by a wealthy dentist and temperance crusader who wanted to give San Franciscans something to drink other than alcohol — which is why the base once ran with fountains of free water. A Washington Square with a Franklin standing in it, and the city never bothered to fix the mismatch.

Now look up at the church. Those two white spires belong to Saints Peter and Paul, finished in nineteen twenty-four and long known as the Italian Cathedral of the West — the spiritual center of North Beach back when this was a dense neighborhood of Italian fishermen and immigrants. The most famous of those Italian sons was the baseball player Joe dimadjeeoh, who grew up just a few blocks from here. When he married the movie star Marilyn Monroe in nineteen fifty-four, the couple came straight to these steps to be photographed afterward, and you'll still see wedding parties doing exactly the same thing today — which is part of how the place picked up its reputation as a marriage church.

Stand here a moment and take the measure of it. Down at the bottom, the Beats; up the hill ahead, the parrots and the tower; and right here in the middle, the ordinary Italian village that both of those stories grew up around. This is the everyday heart of the whole thing.

When you're ready, we start climbing. Head east, toward the green hill rising in front of you, and find the base of the Filbert Steps, where the sidewalk gives up and turns into a garden staircase. This is where the walk gets wild — in every sense of the word.
