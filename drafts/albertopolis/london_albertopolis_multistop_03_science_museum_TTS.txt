The Science Museum. After a museum of art and a museum of nature, this is the third panel — a museum of what people have made and figured out. The making of the modern world, it calls one of its great halls, and that's about right.

Two things in here are worth planning your visit around, because they're not copies. They're the actual objects.

The first is Stephenson's Rocket. Not a model, not a replica: the real locomotive that, in eighteen twenty-nine, more or less invented the railway age — small, battered, sitting quietly in a case. Almost everything about how the modern world moves traces back to that one machine.

The second is up in the space gallery: the Apollo ten command module. The genuine capsule, scorched from re-entry, that carried three astronauts around the Moon in nineteen sixty-nine — the dress rehearsal, two months before the first landing. It went to the Moon and back, and now it sits in South Kensington, and you can stand a few feet from it.

That's the gift of this whole place: the real things, not the story of them.

And it completes Albert's idea. Walk it back in your head — art and design at the vee and ay, the natural world next door, human invention here. Three museums, side by side, making one argument: that beauty, nature, and knowledge belong together, and belong to everybody. That was the whole point of the money from eighteen fifty-one.
