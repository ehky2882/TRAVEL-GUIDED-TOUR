Most great cities grow by accident. This corner of London was planned — on purpose, by one man, as a kind of argument.

You're in South Kensington, standing on Exhibition Road, with the Victoria and Albert Museum on the corner ahead of you. Over the next ten minutes or so we're going to walk a tight little cluster: three enormous museums in a row, then a concert hall and a memorial just to the north. It's the most compact walk in this set — you could throw a stone across most of it.

Here's the idea that built all of it. In 1851, Britain threw the Great Exhibition — a vast show of art, industry and invention, housed in a glass palace in Hyde Park, just up the road. Six million people came. It made a fortune. And the man behind it, Prince Albert, Queen Victoria's husband, had a plan for the money: buy the land down here, and build a permanent district devoted to art, science and learning. For everyone, not just the wealthy. The papers nicknamed it Albertopolis.

Then, in 1861, Albert died. He was forty-two. He never saw most of this finished.

So that's the thread to hold as we walk. Nearly everything here was built after the man whose idea it was had gone — completed in his memory, by a grieving country and a grieving wife. It's a whole neighbourhood built as remembrance, dressed up as progress. And it ends, fittingly, at a memorial to him.

Start with the building on the corner. Walk to the main entrance of the V&A, on Cromwell Road, and we'll begin there.
