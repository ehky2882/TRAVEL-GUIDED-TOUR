The Victoria and Albert Museum — the vee and ay. You're at the Cromwell Road entrance, under the great carved doorway, with the tall fluted tower rising above you.

This is the world's leading museum of art and design — and that last word is the key to it. Not a museum of masterpieces behind rope, but a museum of making: furniture, fashion, ceramics, ironwork, jewellery, wallpaper, whole rooms lifted out of old buildings and rebuilt inside. Around two and a quarter million objects, spanning five thousand years. You can find a Raphael cartoon and a Vivienne Westwood dress under the same roof, and the vee and ay would tell you they belong together.

Now, the gift. The vee and ay was the first museum in the world to have a proper restaurant — a place to sit, eat, and stay a while. That sounds small. It wasn't. The whole idea here was that ordinary people, after a day's work, might come in, look at beautiful things, learn how they were made, and go home wanting to make better ones themselves. Feed them, and they'll stay longer. The refreshment rooms are still there, still serving — three rooms by three different designers, and worth a look even if you're not hungry.

That mission is the vee and ay's real exhibit. Most museums of this age were built to show off — empire's spoils, a nation's wealth. This one was built to teach: to raise the standard of British design by letting anyone study the best of it, free. It's a school disguised as a palace.

The foundation stone for the front you're looking at was laid by Queen Victoria herself, in eighteen ninety-nine — her last great public appearance. An old woman, laying the cornerstone of the museum that carries her dead husband's name alongside her own.

When you've had your look, come back out and turn right. The next building along Cromwell Road is impossible to miss — it looks like a cathedral. That's where we're headed.
