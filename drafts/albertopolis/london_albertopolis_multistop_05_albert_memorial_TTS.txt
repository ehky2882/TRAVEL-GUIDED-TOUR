Here it is. Stand at the foot of the steps and look up.

This is the Albert Memorial, finished in eighteen seventy-two, and it is unashamedly a shrine. A tall, pointed, gilded canopy — like a spire lifted off a cathedral and set down over a single seated figure. And that figure, gold from head to foot, glinting when the sun catches him, is Albert. The man whose idea you've spent the last ten minutes walking through.

Look at what he's holding. In his hands, resting on his knee, is a book — and it's not a Bible, or a sceptre, or anything you'd expect a prince to be shown with. It's the catalogue of the Great Exhibition of eighteen fifty-one. The very thing that started all of this. He's sitting here, for ever, holding the programme of his own great show, facing the hall that bears his name, with the three museums his money built just behind it. The whole district arranged around him like an offering.

The man who designed this was George Gilbert Scott — and if the name rings a bell, it should. He also built the great red Gothic front of St Pancras station across town. And his grandson, Giles Gilbert Scott, went on to design Battersea Power Station, the Tate Modern building, and the red telephone box. One family, three generations, scattered right across this city.

Around the base, at the four corners, sit groups representing the four continents — a very Victorian, very imperial idea of the world gathering at Albert's feet. Worth seeing, and worth seeing clearly for what it is.

But mostly, just stand here a moment. Everything you've walked past today — the art, the bones, the rockets, the music — was the living version of an idea. This is the man who had it, who didn't live to see it built, sitting in gold at the centre of the city that finished it for him. A whole neighbourhood, raised as a kind of thank-you note. Not many people get one of those.

That's the end of the walk. Take your time here before you go.
