The Royal Albert Hall. Stand back far enough to take in the shape — a vast oval drum, red brick and terracotta, with a frieze running right around the top like a ribbon. It opened in eighteen seventy-one, and it's been London's great room ever since: concerts, boxing, tennis, Churchill, the Beatles, and every summer the Proms — weeks of nightly concerts that end in the famous, flag-waving last night.

But the gift here is about a flaw. For its first century, this beautiful room had a terrible secret — the echo. The domed ceiling bounced sound back so badly that people used to joke the Albert Hall was the only place a British composer could be sure of hearing his work twice. They tried everything. Finally, in nineteen sixty-nine, they hung a constellation of large fibreglass discs from the ceiling — saucer-shaped, floating up there in the dome. The mushrooms, everyone calls them. They scatter the sound before it can echo, and they fixed in an afternoon what had defeated the engineers for a hundred years. Look up next time you're inside; they're still hanging there.

Now the thread. This is the first building in the whole district to actually carry Albert's name — Royal Albert Hall. Victoria laid the stone and came to the opening, but she was so overcome with grief, still, years after his death, that she couldn't bring herself to speak. Someone else declared it open on her behalf.

Which brings us to the last stop, and it's just across the road. Turn and face north, away from the hall, and look across Kensington Gore into the park. You'll see something tall and gold glinting under a pointed canopy. Walk towards it. That's the Albert Memorial — and it's where this whole walk has been heading.
