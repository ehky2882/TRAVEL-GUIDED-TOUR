This is the Natural History Museum, and the first thing to do is just stand back and look at it, because it doesn't look like a museum. It looks like a cathedral.

That was the point. The architect, Alfred Waterhouse, designed it in the 1870s as a temple — but a temple to nature instead of God. Two great towers, a vast rounded doorway, the whole front faced in pale terracotta tile. And here's the gift, once you know to look for it: the building is covered in animals. Carved and moulded into the stone, everywhere. Look at the west side, to your left, and the creatures are living ones — monkeys, birds, things still walking the earth. Look at the east side, to your right, and they're extinct — pterodactyls, ancient beasts. Living on one wing, dead on the other. The building sorts the natural world the way the collection inside does.

And what a collection. Around eighty million specimens — plants, animals, fossils, rocks, meteorites — making this one of the most important records of life on earth anywhere.

Step inside, even just into the entrance, and you're in Hintze Hall, the great central space under the arched roof. For decades a diplodocus skeleton stood here, nicknamed Dippy, and a couple of generations of British children grew up greeting him on the way in. A few years ago he was retired, and now a real blue whale skeleton hangs from the ceiling above you, diving through the air — the largest animal that has ever lived, where the dinosaur used to be.

Here's the quiet thing about this building. It was finished in the same decades Britain was arguing hard about evolution — about whether nature needed God at all. And the answer the museum gave wasn't a sermon. It was this: build nature a cathedral, and give it the grandeur we used to reserve for the divine.

Behind this building, on Exhibition Road, is the third of the three museums. Head round to the Science Museum.
