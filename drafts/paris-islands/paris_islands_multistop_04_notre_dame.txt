Two towers, flat-topped and square, with a long row of carved figures across the front and a great round window in the centre. You're on the parvis — the wide open square in front of Notre-Dame. Find a spot where you can take in the whole west front, and let the crowd flow around you.

Before you look up, look down. Somewhere near the centre of the square, set into the ground, there's a small bronze marker — a star, with an inscription. That's Point Zéro: the exact spot from which every road distance in France is measured. When a sign out in the country tells you Paris is so many kilometres away, this is the point it means. The whole country is measured outward from the front step of this cathedral, on the island where the city began. The centre of France is, officially, right under your feet.

Now the cathedral. They began building it in 1163 and kept at it for the better part of a century — those towers, the rose window, the carved kings across the front. For more than eight hundred years it stood here, the heart of the city, until one evening a few years ago when the world watched it burn. The fire took the spire and tore through the roof — and that roof had a name. They called it the forest: a lattice of oak beams, hundreds of trees felled in the Middle Ages, each one shaped by hand, holding the lead roof above the stone.

*[beat]*

And then France did something that wasn't guaranteed. It put the cathedral back. The forest was rebuilt the medieval way, with fresh oak and old tools, the spire went up again, and the stone was scrubbed of centuries of soot in the process. In December 2024 the doors reopened, and what you walk into now is brighter than anything in living memory — a building going on nine hundred years old that came back from the edge of being lost, and somehow looks new.

You've now walked the length of the older island, from its western point to here. One short bridge remains, just behind the cathedral, and it leads to the other island — the quiet one. Cross it, and the noise drops away. That's the Île Saint-Louis.
