Before there was a Paris, there was an island.

Two thousand years ago, a tribe called the pareezee-ee settled a teardrop of land in the middle of the senn — defensible, easy to cross to either bank, impossible to ignore. The Romans took it and called the place looteesha. Everything you think of as Paris — the boulevards, the towers, the twenty aron-deesmon spiralling outward — grew from this one island and the river around it.

This walk stays where the city started. It's short: a little under a kilometre and a half end to end, flat the whole way, along and across two islands that sit in the river one behind the other. You can do it in an afternoon with time to spare.

We'll begin at the western tip of the eel duh la seetay, on the oldest bridge in Paris — which is, confusingly, the one called the New Bridge. From there we walk east along the island: past a medieval palace that became a prison, into a chapel built to hold a crown of thorns, and out to the cathedral that nearly burned down and came back. Then we cross one last short bridge to the eel san loo-ee — the quiet island, the one where Paris stops performing and just lives.

The old coat of arms of the city is a ship riding the water, with a motto underneath: tossed by the waves, but never sunk. Stand on these islands long enough and you understand why. The river has flooded them, fire has taken parts of them, revolutions have emptied their palaces — and they're still here, still in the middle of everything.

Start at the pon nuhf. Find the western point of the island, where the bridge crosses and a king sits on a bronze horse, looking downstream.
