You found it — that slender spire rising out of the cluster of law-court buildings. From the outside the sant shapell barely shows itself; it's hemmed in now by the courts that grew up around it. Don't judge it from here. Go in, climb to the upper level, and look up.

What you'll be standing in is, in effect, the inside of a jewel. Fifteen windows, each about fifteen metres tall, run almost floor to ceiling all the way around, so the stone seems to vanish and the whole upper chapel becomes a cage of coloured glass — well over a thousand scenes from the Bible, glowing whenever the sun is behind them. On a bright afternoon the light comes through red and blue and lands on the floor in pools.

Here's why it exists. In the twelve forties the king — loo-ee the Ninth, later made a saint — got hold of the most precious object in the Christian world: the Crown of Thorns, the one from the Passion, bought from the emperor in Constantinople, who was short of money. And here's the detail that tells you everything: the relics cost the king far more than this building did. Several times more. The sant shapell isn't a church that happens to hold a relic — it's a reliquary you can walk inside, a glass case built around a crown, sized to the thing it was made to protect.

Think about what that means. A king took a sum that could have paid for fortresses or armies, spent it on a ring of thorns, and then wrapped it in the most beautiful room he could commission — not to hide it behind walls, but to flood it with light. That's not decoration. That's a thirteenth-century argument about what's worth the most, made in stone and glass.

The crown itself isn't here any more. It was moved long ago to the great cathedral at the other end of the island — and it was carried out of that cathedral by hand, in the dark, the night the building caught fire a few years back.
