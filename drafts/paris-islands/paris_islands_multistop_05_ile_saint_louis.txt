You've crossed the Pont Saint-Louis, and you can probably feel the difference already. The crowds thin, the noise from the cathedral fades behind you, and you're on a single straight street running the length of a much smaller island. This is the Île Saint-Louis, and it's the quietest famous place in Paris.

Here's what makes it strange. Almost everywhere else in the city grew in layers, century on century. This island didn't. For most of its life it was muddy pasture — two little islets where cows were grazed — until, in the early 1600s, someone had the idea to join them together, throw a grid of streets across the result, and sell the plots. The whole island went up more or less at once, in one style, over a few decades, and then it essentially stopped. The tall pale houses lining this street are the originals.

So while the rest of Paris was torn up and rebuilt — most dramatically in the 1800s, when Baron Haussmann drove his wide boulevards through the medieval city — the Île Saint-Louis was left almost completely alone. No grand avenue cuts across it. To this day there's no Métro station on the island; you arrive on foot, over a bridge, the way you just did.

*[beat]*

And the thing this island is most famous for, after centuries of being a discreet address for people who could afford the quiet, is ice cream. A shop called Berthillon has been making it here since 1954, and on a warm afternoon you'll see the evidence in every direction — people walking the bridges with a cone, in no hurry at all.

Which is the right note to end on. You started this walk at the prow of the island where Paris was born, among kings and relics and a cathedral that came back from the dead — and you finish it here, on the small island that chose, very deliberately, to be nothing grander than a good place to live. Find a spot on the stone edge above the water, sit down, and watch the river go by. It's been doing that since before there was a city. Tossed by the waves, never sunk.
