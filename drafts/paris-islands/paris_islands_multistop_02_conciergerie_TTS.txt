The towers are the konsyairzhuree. Stand on the walkway above the river so you can see them straight on — four of them, pale and round and capped with dark cones, rising right out of the embankment, and on the corner a square tower with a clock set into it.

That clock is the first public clock the city ever had, hung here in the thirteen seventies so ordinary Parisians could tell the time without listening for church bells. But the clock is the gentle part of the story.

For around four hundred years, this was the centre of France — not a museum, not a courthouse, but the palace where the kings actually lived, the seat of royal power on the island where the city was born. When the kings finally moved out to grander places, they left the machinery of the state behind: courts, offices, and a prison in the lower floors of the old palace.

Then came the Revolution, and the prison became something the building's architects could never have imagined. In the seventeen nineties the konsyairzhuree was the last address of the condemned — the place where prisoners waited, sometimes for a single night, before the cart came to carry them to the guillotine. Thousands passed through these rooms on that one-way trip. The most famous was the queen, maree antwanett, held here through the autumn of seventeen ninety-three in a small guarded cell, tried in the building, and taken out to her execution in October. Later, under the kings who returned to power, a chapel was built on the spot where her cell had stood.

So hold the whole arc of it in your head, standing here: the same walls that were once the proudest expression of French royalty became the place where French royalty was, quite deliberately, taken apart. Power and the end of power, in one building, on one island.

There's a single room left from the palace at its medieval height, and you don't have to imagine it — it's just inside, and it leads us to the next stop. Step into the complex behind these towers, among the law courts, and look for the slender spire rising above them. That's the sant shapell.
