You're standing on the pon nuhf, the bridge that crosses the western tip of the island in two unequal spans. Walk out to the middle, to the point where the two halves meet and a king sits on a bronze horse. That's your spot.

First, the name. pon nuhf means New Bridge — and it's the oldest one in Paris, finished in sixteen oh seven. The joke writes itself, but stay with why the name stuck: when it opened, it genuinely was new, in a way the city had never seen. Every other bridge in Paris was lined with houses, shops stacked along both sides, so you crossed the river through a kind of tunnel and never saw the water. This one was built bare. No houses — just the span, the open sky, and, for the first time in the city, raised walkways set aside for people on foot, so you could cross without being run down by a cart.

So Parisians did something they'd never done on a bridge before: they lingered. They strolled out to the middle, watched the river, bought a song or a tooth-pulling from the hawkers who set up along it. The pon nuhf didn't just cross the senn — it invented the bridge as a place to be, rather than a thing to get over.

The king on the horse is onree the Fourth, who finished it. Look closely and you're looking at a replacement: the original statue was melted down for cannon during the Revolution, and this one was cast in eighteen eighteen to put him back where he belonged.

Now look past him, off the downstream point of the island, where the land narrows to nothing like the bow of a ship. There's a small park down at water level there — the skwair dew vair galon. That shape, that prow, is the reason the old seal of Paris is a ship riding the current. You're standing at the front of it.
