For most of its life, this park was not yours.

The Buen Retiro began in the sixteen-thirties as a private pleasure ground for Philip the Fourth — a second palace on the edge of the city, with a lake dug for staged naval battles and gardens kept for a court that did not share them. The public got in on sufferance, on the right days, dressed correctly. It was only in 1868, after a revolution pushed out a queen, that the gates were thrown open for good and the royal garden became the city's.

That's the thread of this walk: a private royal indulgence, handed over piece by piece until it belonged to everyone. We'll start at a king's triumphal gate stranded in the traffic, walk down to a lake where anyone can now rent a boat beneath a monument built for a king, step inside a glass palace raised for an empire and now free to wander, pass a rose garden one gardener built as a gift to the city, and finish just outside the railings at a row of book stalls where, for a century, a book cost about the same as an anchovy.

It's an easy walk — flat park paths most of the way, shade in summer, a gentle slope down to the books at the end. Start at the northern corner, at the Puerta de Alcalá. We'll head into the park from there.
