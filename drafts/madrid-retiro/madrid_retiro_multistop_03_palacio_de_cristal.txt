In front of you is a pavilion made of glass and iron, sitting at the edge of a small pond that doubles it in reflection. The Palacio de Cristal — the Crystal Palace — a great greenhouse of a building, light pouring straight through it, ducks on the pond, turtles if you're lucky.

It was built in 1887, and it's worth knowing what for. That year Madrid held an exhibition devoted to the Philippines, then a Spanish colony, and this glass house was raised to display what had been brought over from the islands — including, in a practice common to such exhibitions of the time, people. That part of its history is real and it isn't comfortable; the building is beautiful, and it was made to put a colony on show. Both things are true, and worth holding together rather than choosing between.

What it is now is something else entirely. The glass house belongs to the Reina Sofía museum, and it's used for contemporary art — large installations that change through the year, the kind of work that needs a flood of natural light and a lot of empty air around it. There's no ticket. You step in off a park path, into whatever an artist has filled it with this season, and walk out again.

A pavilion built to exhibit an empire, now a bright empty room anyone can enter for nothing — the same democratic turn the whole park keeps taking.

When you're ready, head south and west, toward the quieter side of the park. There's a garden ahead that one man spent years getting right — the Rosaleda, the rose garden.
