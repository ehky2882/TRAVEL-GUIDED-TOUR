You should be standing at the edge of the big rectangular lake — the Estanque — with a great curved colonnade rising on the far side and a king cast in bronze on horseback at its centre. That's Alfonso the Twelfth, and the monument is deliberately enormous: a half circle of stone columns wrapped around the water like an open arm, paid for by public subscription after he died young.

Here's the thing to hold in your head while you look at it. This lake was dug for a king's amusement — Philip the Fourth had it made in the sixteen-thirties for staged naval battles and floating concerts, a private toy on a scale most cities couldn't have managed. For two centuries it stayed essentially royal. Then, in 1868, the crown lost the park for good and it passed to the city.

So look at what's on the water now. Boats — dozens of them, anyone can rent one by the half hour, couples pulling badly on the oars, children trailing their hands in the green water, all of it directly beneath a monument the size of a cathedral front built to glorify a dead king. Nobody planned that contrast, and nobody could have designed it better: imperial grandeur as the backdrop for a cheap afternoon on a boat.

That's the whole park in one view — the grand gesture and the ordinary pleasure sharing the same frame, the second having quietly inherited the first.

When you've taken it in, keep walking south, deeper into the park along the tree-lined paths. There's a building ahead made almost entirely of glass, and it's worth seeing up close — the Palacio de Cristal.
