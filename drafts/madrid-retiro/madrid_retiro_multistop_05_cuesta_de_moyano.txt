You've come out of the park, and you're standing at the top of a short sloping street lined on one side with a row of low wooden stalls — grey-blue, shuttered like little cabins, each one a second-hand bookshop. This is the Cuesta de Moyano, and it has been exactly this, in more or less this spot, since 1925.

Around thirty stalls, open every day, many of them run for generations by the same families. They sell old books, out-of-print books, first editions, faded magazines, maps — the things you can't easily find anywhere else. There's a statue of the writer Pío Baroja near the top of the slope, and the politician it's named for, Claudio Moyano, at the bottom; he passed the law that first put schools across Spain.

Here's the detail to take away. In its early years a writer nicknamed this the Feria del boquerón — the anchovy fair — because a book here cost about what an anchovy cost. That was the whole point: reading made cheap enough for anyone. Through the Civil War the stalls barely closed, and in the censored years that followed, this slope quietly became a place to find the books you weren't supposed to read.

It's still here, still cheap, still open in the rain — and as of early 2026 it's formally protected, recognised as part of the city's living heritage.

Which is a fitting place to end. We started at a king's gate and a king's lake, walked through a palace built to show off an empire, and we finish on a public slope where the city's idea of a luxury is a row of cheap books anyone can stop and buy. The garden was handed to everyone; so, in its own way, was this. Stay a while. Browse something you didn't come for.
