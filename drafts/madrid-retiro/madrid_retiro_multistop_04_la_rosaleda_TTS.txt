This is the rosalayda, the rose garden — an ellipse of beds and clipped hedges with two fountains and a run of metal arches that the climbing roses are trained up and over. If you've come in May or June you've timed it perfectly; the rest of the year it's a quiet, pretty corner you'll have largely to yourself.

It exists because of one man. Around nineteen fifteen the city's head gardener, thaytheelyo rodreegeth, decided Madrid should have a proper rose garden to rival the famous ones in Paris, and he went to Paris himself to choose the first roses. His timing was terrible — the First World War broke out while he was away, and he had a genuinely hard journey getting himself and his roses back to Spain. He planted them here anyway, on a spot that had held a private greenhouse and, before that, a pond people skated on in winter.

Then the garden was destroyed — the Civil War tore through the park and the roses with it. It was replanted in nineteen forty-one, more than four thousand bushes, hundreds of varieties, and that's essentially what you're standing in now: a thing built once as a gift to the city, lost, and deliberately made again.

It's a small idea with a lot behind it — that ordinary people should have somewhere beautiful to walk for nothing, and that it's worth a gardener crossing a continent in wartime to get it right.
