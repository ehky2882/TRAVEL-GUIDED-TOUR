You're at the Plaza de la Independencia, and the gate marooned in the middle of the roundabout is the Puerta de Alcalá. Find a spot on one of the corners where you can take in the whole thing — five openings, pale granite, lions and trophies along the top, traffic streaming around it on every side.

Charles the Third put it here in 1778. He had come from ruling Naples to ruling Spain, he thought Madrid was a shabby capital for a great empire, and he set about fixing that — fountains, boulevards, this gate. It marked the eastern edge of the city, the road in from Alcalá de Henares, and it was built to impress whoever arrived: a triumphal arch with no battle behind it, just the confidence of a king who wanted the entrance to his city to look like Rome.

Now look at the two main faces. They aren't the same — different arrangements of columns and carving on each side, because the design was argued over and the architect, Sabatini, more or less built both versions at once. Most people walk past without ever noticing they're looking at two facades on one gate.

What's changed is everything around it. The city long ago swallowed the edge this gate marked; the threshold of Madrid now stands on an island in a roundabout, circled by cars, belonging to no one and everyone. A grand royal gesture, quietly demoted to a traffic landmark — which is a fitting way to begin, because the whole garden ahead went the same way.

From here, cross into the park and walk down toward the water. We're heading for the lake, and the king on horseback who watches over it.
