Find a spot on the slope facing northeast, looking downhill toward the city. On a sunny weekend this is the most San Francisco scene there is: a green hillside packed shoulder to shoulder, music drifting from a dozen directions, the downtown skyline framed by palm trees, and the whole sloping lawn tipped toward the view like a giant set of bleachers. This is Dolores Park — the city's communal backyard.

It works because of the tilt. The land rises as it runs west, which turns the whole park into natural seating angled at the skyline. People sort themselves across it almost by neighborhood: families and dogs lower down, the sunny southwest corner long known as a gathering spot for the city's queer community, everybody arranged on the grass like a crowd that showed up early for a show that never quite starts.

But the ground under all this leisure has a heavier past, and it's worth knowing. For decades these two blocks were Jewish cemeteries — until the city, running out of room, relocated nearly all its dead to the south early in the twentieth century. And in 1906, when the earthquake and fire drove tens of thousands from their homes, this hillside became a refugee camp, rows of army tents covering the slope where the picnic blankets are now.

[beat]

That's the quiet thing to hold while you sit here. San Francisco has a habit of turning solemn ground into a place to lie in the sun — of letting a hillside that once held graves and then held the homeless become, a century later, the city's favorite spot to do absolutely nothing on a Saturday. It isn't disrespect. It's more like the city's deepest reflex: take what was hard, and make it a place to gather.

So stay a while. Watch the skyline catch the late light, listen to the overlapping music, let the slope do its job. You're sitting in the city's living room, on ground that has been a graveyard and a disaster camp and is now, simply, where San Francisco comes to be together. Few places carry their history this lightly — or this well.
