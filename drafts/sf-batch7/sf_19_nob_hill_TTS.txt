You've made it to the top of Nob Hill, and notice it actually is a climb, which is the whole reason this place exists. In the eighteen seventies, when cable cars finally made these slopes livable, the men who'd just gotten very rich building the transcontinental railroad raced to the top and put their mansions where everyone below could see them. The hill's name is a clipped version of nabob, an old word for someone newly, conspicuously wealthy. Locals had a sharper version: Snob Hill.

The kings of the hill were the so-called Big Four, Crocker, Huntington, Hopkins, Stanford, railroad barons whose wooden palaces sprawled across these blocks in a competition of pure ostentation. And then, in nineteen oh six, the earthquake and the fire that followed erased nearly all of it. The wooden mansions burned to their foundations in an afternoon.

Look across to the brownstone building that's now the Pacific-Union Club. That one survived, it was the Flood mansion, built of Connecticut stone instead of wood, and its walls were about the only thing on the hilltop the fire couldn't take. It's the last of the barons' houses left standing.

Here's what's interesting about what came next. The families didn't rebuild their private palaces. The empty lots became hotels, the Fairmont, the Mark Hopkins, the Huntington, and Grace Cathedral rose on land the Crocker family gave away. The fire turned a hill of private wealth into a hill of public luxury. Where you once needed to be a railroad baron to get past the gate, you can now ride a cable car up, walk into the Mark Hopkins, and take the elevator to the Top of the Mark, a sky-high bar where, during the Second World War, sailors shipping out to the Pacific drank a last round and their wives watched the convoys sail under the Golden Gate. They called one window Weepers' Corner.

So stand here a moment and read the hill correctly. It was built as a monument to keeping people out, then rebuilt, by accident of disaster, as a place that lets people in. Ride the cable car down when you're done. It's the same machine that made the climb worth it in the first place.
