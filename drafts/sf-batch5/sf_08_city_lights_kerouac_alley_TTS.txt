There's a small bookstore on the triangular corner ahead of you, where Columbus cuts across the grid, and it looks like exactly what it is: three crammed floors of paperbacks, a poetry room up top, hand-lettered signs telling you to sit down and read. What it doesn't look like is a place that changed American law. It did.

City Lights opened in nineteen fifty-three, founded by the poet Lawrence ferlinghettee and a partner named Peter Martin, as the country's first all-paperback bookstore, a genuinely radical idea back when paperbacks were treated as cheap and disposable. That alone would have earned it a footnote. But in nineteen fifty-six, City Lights published a thin book of poems by a young writer named Allen ginzberg, and the book was called Howl.

It was raw, furious, and sexually frank, and the authorities came for it. In nineteen fifty-seven the store's manager was arrested and ferlinghettee was charged with selling an obscene book. The trial that followed wasn't really about one poem. It was about whether the government got to decide what Americans were allowed to read.

ferlinghettee won. The judge ruled that a work with, in the court's phrase, redeeming social importance could not be banned as obscene, no matter how much it offended. That single ruling cracked the door open for nearly everything that came after it: the books that could finally be printed, sold, and argued about in the open.

And that's the reframe worth standing here for. People treat this corner as a shrine to the Beat poets, and it is one; ginzberg, keruwack, and the whole restless crowd drank across the alley at vuhsoovioh and argued here for years. But the monument isn't the poetry. The monument is the verdict. City Lights matters less as a clubhouse than as the place that went to court for the right to sell a difficult book, and won.

Step into the alley on the north side of the store. It's named for Jack keruwack, and the pavement is set with quotes pressed into the stone. Then go inside, climb to the poetry room, and pull something off the shelf. You're allowed to stand there and read the whole thing, the signs say so, and a courtroom once said so too.
