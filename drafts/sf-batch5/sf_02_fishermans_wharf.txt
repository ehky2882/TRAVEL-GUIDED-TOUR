Stand at the foot of Taylor Street, where the sidewalk runs out and the harbor begins. Most people come to Fisherman's Wharf for the things behind you — the t-shirt racks, the wax museum, the sourdough shaped like a crab, the guy who's been hiding behind a bush startling tourists since the Reagan administration. Turn the other way.

In front of you is a working harbor. Not a reproduction of one. A real one, still going, though smaller every year.

Look at the boats first. The squat wooden ones with the high curved bows are Monterey hulls — "Monterey clippers" — the design Italian fishermen brought to this coast more than a century ago, built to take a beating from the Pacific and still come home loaded. Many of the boats in front of you go out before dawn. The catch that built this place, and still defines it, is Dungeness crab — the season opens in November, and when it's running, the docks here smell like salt and diesel and the cold, sweet funk of fresh shellfish.

The fleet that fishes out of here was founded by Genoese and Sicilian families, and before them by Chinese fishermen who were pushed out by law in the late 1800s. The Italian names on the old restaurants — Alioto, Tarantino, Sabella — were fishing families first and restaurant families second. They sold their catch off the back of the boat, then opened a stand, then a dining room. The trap, in other words, grew out of the trade.

Here's the thing worth standing here for. Everyone calls Fisherman's Wharf a tourist trap, and they're not wrong. But underneath the souvenir city is the actual reason it exists, still happening, just out of frame: a few dozen real boats, a real catch, a real fleet hanging on against rising costs and shrinking waters. The spectacle didn't replace the fishery. It grew on top of it, like a barnacle — and now it's the thing keeping the barnacle's host alive.

[beat]

So before you buy the crab, walk down to Fish Alley, behind the restaurants on Jefferson Street, where the boats actually unload. That's the Wharf the postcards forgot — and it's the only part that was ever really here.
