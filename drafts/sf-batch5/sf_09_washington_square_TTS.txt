Find a bench in the open green and look across at the church with the two white spires. This is Washington Square, one of the oldest parks in the city, and almost nothing here is quite what its name promises.

Start with the statue near the center. In a square named for Washington, you'd reasonably expect George Washington. It's Benjamin Franklin. He's stood here since the eighteen seventies, put up by a dentist and temperance crusader who, fittingly, plumbed the base to dispense free drinking water. The square kept Washington's name and got somebody else's monument: the first of several small switches you're surrounded by.

Now the church. Saints Peter and Paul, finished in nineteen twenty-four, the twin-spired heart of what was once a densely Italian North Beach. They called it the Italian Cathedral of the West, and it anchored a neighborhood of jenoeez and sisillian families, often the same families who fished the wharf a few blocks downhill.

Here's the detail people come looking for. In nineteen fifty-four, Joe dimadjeeoh and Marilyn Monroe stood on the steps of this church and were photographed by what felt like every newspaper on earth. It became one of the most famous wedding-day images of the century, except they weren't married here. dimadjeeoh had been divorced, so the church wouldn't perform the ceremony; they'd married that morning at City Hall, and came here only for the pictures. Another substitution. The image everyone remembers is of a wedding that never happened on these steps.

And that's the quiet truth of this square, a place of stand-ins and overlaps, layered rather than fixed. The Italian neighborhood that built the church has largely moved on, out to the avenues, while Chinatown presses in from the south.

So on a given morning you'll see the result: rows of people, many of them Chinese, moving slowly through tie chee on the grass of a square named for Washington, in front of an Italian church, watched over by a statue of Franklin. Stay a few minutes and watch the green fill up. Nothing here matches its label, and somehow it all fits together anyway, which is about as San Francisco as a single block gets.
