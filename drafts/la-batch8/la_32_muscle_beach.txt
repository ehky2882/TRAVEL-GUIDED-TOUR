Before there were gyms on every corner of America, there was a patch of sand where working out was a public performance — free to do, free to watch.

You should be on the beach just south of the Santa Monica Pier, where rings, ropes, and bars rise out of the sand. This is the original Muscle Beach, and it began in 1934 as a Depression relief project: the Works Progress Administration installed exercise equipment here to put people to work and give the public something wholesome to do. What grew instead was a phenomenon. Acrobats, gymnasts, wrestlers, Hollywood stunt performers, and bodybuilders made this stretch of beach their open-air stage, stacking human pyramids and pressing barbells while weekend crowds packed the sand to watch. Fitness in America had mostly been a private, faintly eccentric pursuit; here it became a spectacle, a community, and eventually an industry.

The roll call from this sand is remarkable. Jack LaLanne trained here decades before television made him the country's coach. Joe Gold worked out here long before the first Gold's Gym opened. And Pudgy Stockton — a five-foot-one secretary turned barbell star — became the face of women's strength at a time when much of the country insisted lifting was unladylike. Then, in 1959, Santa Monica abruptly shut the whole thing down and bulldozed the platform. The lifters simply migrated two miles south along the sand to a modest city weight pen in Venice — which inherited the crowds, the champions, and eventually the name: it was officially dedicated as Muscle Beach Venice in 1987, the added word a nod of respect to this spot, the original.

Santa Monica came around in the end, restoring and rededicating this beach as the birthplace of the American fitness boom. The equipment is still free, and on most afternoons somebody is on the rings, working on something difficult in full public view — which was always the whole idea.
