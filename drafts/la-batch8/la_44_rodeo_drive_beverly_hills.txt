RODEO DRIVE & BEVERLY HILLS

These three blocks are a performance, and everyone here — the stores, the cars, the window-shoppers — is in on it.

You should be on Rodeo Drive, somewhere in the three famous blocks between Wilshire and Santa Monica Boulevard: flagship boutiques behind spotless glass, palm trees in formation, and a steady traffic of people photographing storefronts the way people elsewhere photograph cathedrals. Which is the point. Rodeo doesn't just sell luxury goods; it sells the *idea* of Los Angeles wealth, and the idea outsells the goods.

It wasn't born this way. Into the 1960s this was an ordinary shopping street in a well-off suburb — hardware, groceries, a bowling alley in the neighborhood mix. The transformation began when a handful of merchants, most famously a boutique owner named Fred Hayman, started treating retail as theater: champagne for customers, valets, celebrity clienteles cultivated like gardens. The luxury houses of Europe followed, and by the 1980s the street had become shorthand, worldwide, for a particular gilded fantasy of California — a fantasy the movies were happy to amplify, most famously in a certain 1990 romantic comedy built around a shopping spree here.

The street's honest masterpiece is at the Wilshire end: Via Rodeo, a curving, cobbled European lane that climbs past storefronts to a little piazza. It looks centuries old. It was built in 1990 — a brand-new antique street, a stage set you can walk on, and the most candid thing Rodeo ever did, because it admits the whole enterprise is scenery. And for an architectural footnote, look for the odd little zigzag building at number 332: Anderton Court, a late work by Frank Lloyd Wright, holding its corner among the marble flagships like an eccentric relative at a formal dinner.

[beat]

Here's the way to enjoy it: don't ask whether it's authentic. Nothing in Los Angeles answers that question well, and Rodeo least of all — it's a street performing wealth the way the city's main industry performs stories, professionally and in public. Admission to the show is free, the light is always good, and nobody checks whether you can afford the merchandise. Window-shopping was invented for exactly this.
