THE GETTY CENTER

You take a driverless tram to get here, which is the first clue that someone thought very hard about the experience of arriving.

You should be at the top of the hill in Brentwood, on the arrival plaza of the Getty Center, with the white stone campus spreading around you and — on a clear day — the whole city laid out below, from downtown to the ocean. Six pavilions of galleries, gardens, and terraces, opened in 1997, designed by Richard Meier, and paid for by the strangest problem in the museum world: too much money.

When the oil billionaire J. Paul Getty died in 1976, he left his fortune to his small museum trust — which by law had to spend a share of it, every year, forever. The result was an institution that became one of the wealthiest in art, could outbid nearly anyone, and eventually built itself this billion-dollar acropolis. In the galleries hangs the collection that spending spree assembled, including Van Gogh's Irises. And in keeping with the founder's terms, admission is free — you pay to park, but the art costs nothing.

Now touch the walls. The campus is clad in travertine, a warm Italian stone quarried near Rome, and the panels were split along their natural grain rather than sawn smooth — so look closely and you'll find fossilized leaves and branches pressed into the surface. The building's skin is full of fifty-thousand-year-old gardens. The living garden below the plaza is its counterpoint: the Central Garden, designed not by a landscape architect but by the artist Robert Irwin, who conceived it as a sculpture you walk through, and it changes with the seasons like any artwork made of plants must.

The hilltop site is also a fortress, quietly. Stone skin, sealed air systems, its own water reserve, cleared brush — the Getty was engineered to defend its collection from wildfire, and in January 2025, when fire moved through these hills with terrible cost to the city, the design held and the art sheltered safely in place.

Museums usually borrow grandeur from the past. This one built its own hill town from scratch — then let everyone in for free. Take the view; it's included.
