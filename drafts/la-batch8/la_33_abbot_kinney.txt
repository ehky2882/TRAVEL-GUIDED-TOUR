Every city has a street like this now — the one with the murals and the single-origin coffee and the lines outside small doors. This is the one they're all copying.

You're on Abbot Kinney Boulevard, a mile-long diagonal through Venice, named for the tobacco millionaire who founded the neighborhood in 1905 as his "Venice of America." The name is recent, though. For most of the twentieth century this was West Washington Boulevard — auto shops, storefront churches, cheap commercial space — and that cheapness was the making of it. When Venice was at its lowest, artists took over the empty storefronts and garages for studios; the street developed a genuine, unplanned bohemian ecosystem; and around 1990 the neighborhood renamed it after its founder, less as marketing than as a memorial.

The marketing came anyway. Locals half-jokingly date the turn to the arrival of a frozen-yogurt chain in 2007; the tech campuses of Silicon Beach landed nearby around the same era; and in 2012, GQ pronounced this the coolest block in America — a compliment from which no street fully recovers. Rents climbed, the galleries and repair shops gave way to fashion flagships, and the very word that made the boulevard famous became the engine that priced out many of the people who had made it cool in the first place.

Walk it with that double vision and it's a richer street. The murals are real, and so are the brand strategies behind some of them. There are still working artists here, and there are shops selling the idea of the artist's life at a considerable markup. Whether this street is Venice's revival or its replacement is argued at every table on the block — and both sides are describing the same mile.

[beat]

Kinney named his city for a place he loved and built an imitation of it. A century later, the street with his name sells an imitation of old Venice, California. He'd at least recognize the business model.
