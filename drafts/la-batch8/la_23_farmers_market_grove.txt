Nothing in Los Angeles is supposed to last — least of all a Depression stopgap made of stalls and awnings. And yet here it is.

You should be at the corner of Third Street and Fairfax Avenue, under or near the white clock tower — the landmark of the Original Farmers Market since 1948. The market itself is older. In July 1934, with the Depression grinding on, a dozen farmers paid fifty cents a day to park their trucks on the Gilmore family's idle oil land and sell produce straight off the tailgates. The stopgap stuck: within months the trucks had become permanent stalls, and the stalls never closed. This market has fed the city every single day since — through the war, the freeways, and every reinvention Los Angeles has thrown at itself.

Step inside and notice the shape of the place. The aisles are low, narrow, and slightly crooked, because nobody ever master-planned any of this — it grew the way real villages grow, one stall at a time, by appetite. More than a hundred vendors now, selling gumbo, doughnuts, hot corned beef, and produce, some of them run by the same families for generations. The story goes that the market's first restaurant began when a shopkeeper's wife named Blanche Magee saw hungry farmers at their trucks and simply started filling a picnic hamper.

Now look next door. The Grove, opened in 2002, is an outdoor mall designed with enormous care to feel like a small-town square — a trolley, a dancing fountain, music in the air. Which gives this corner something rare: an actual accidental village and a meticulous imitation of one, side by side, sharing a trolley line. You can debate which is the real Los Angeles, but this city has always sold the invented version and the genuine article from adjacent storefronts.

Pick an aisle and order something you've never tried. A place this improvised has outlasted almost everything planned around it — which, in this town, is its own kind of monument.
