Every red carpet on earth — every premiere, every velvet rope, every searchlight sweeping a night sky — traces back to one address on Hollywood Boulevard, and it isn't the one with the handprints.

You're at number sixty-seven twelve Hollywood Boulevard, on the south side of the street, looking into a long open forecourt lined with palm trees and framed by heavy columns painted with hieroglyphs. At the far end stands a stage-set of a facade — carved heads, a winged scarab, doors fit for a pharaoh's tomb. This is the Egyptian Theatre, opened in October 1922: the first great movie palace in Hollywood, built by the showman Sid Grauman five years before his more famous Chinese Theatre up the block.

Two things happened that October worth knowing. First, the theater opened just weeks before archaeologists discovered King Tut's tomb — Egyptomania was already in full swing, and the news made Grauman's timing look prophetic. Second, and more important: for opening night, the premiere of Robin Hood starring Douglas Fairbanks, Grauman invented the Hollywood premiere. Searchlights. Crowds held behind ropes. Ushers in Egyptian costume. Guards pacing the rooftop, calling down the minutes until showtime. And a red carpet running to the doors — an idea he's said to have lifted from the VIP carriage of a train. The movie was the product, but the arrival was the show, and a century later that is still the industry's basic math.

The building's own story turned out to be a survival epic. Eclipsed by the Chinese, it slid into long decades as a re-run house, closed in 1992, and was badly damaged by the 1994 earthquake — after which the city sold it to a nonprofit film organization for a single dollar, on the condition that they restore it and reopen it as a cinema. They did. And when the money ran thin a generation later, a streaming company bought the theater and spent a fortune restoring it again, down to the 1922 details. It still shows films the old way — including, a few nights a year, rare nitrate prints, a stock so volatile that only a handful of theaters in the country are equipped to run it.

The temple of the movies, saved twice — most recently by the very format that was supposed to finish movie theaters off. Grauman would have appreciated the plot twist. He might even have staged a premiere for it.
