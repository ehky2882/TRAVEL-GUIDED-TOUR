Union Station

They finished this place in nineteen twenty-seven — just in time, more or less, for everyone to start driving.

Stand out on Front Street, facing the long stone front, and look at the row of columns running almost the width of the block. There are twenty-two of them, each about twelve metres tall and weighing something like seventy-five tonnes — a single colonnade longer than a city block, built to make you feel small in a useful way. Now step inside, into the Great Hall.

This is the room. A barrel-vaulted ceiling about twenty-six metres up, light pouring in through four-storey windows at either end, the floor laid in Tennessee marble. It took the better part of fourteen years to build, slowed by a world war and a shortage of stone, and when the Prince of Wales opened it with a pair of gold scissors, he is said to have remarked that Canada built its train stations the way Britain built its cathedrals. He wasn't wrong. That was the idea.

Here's the gift, and it's easy to walk straight past. Run your eye along the top of the walls, just below the ceiling — carved into the stone is a long list of place names. Those are the towns and cities the railway reached, from the Maritimes out to the Pacific, spelled around the room in order. The whole country, engraved in a single hall. To arrive here was to step into the middle of a map.

And the timing is the quiet tragedy of it. This was a temple raised to the railway at the precise moment the railway began to fade — the cars were coming, the Depression was about to empty the trains, and within a few decades this hall full of cathedral light was, for a long stretch, mostly a place people hurried through on the way to a hamburger counter.

But here's the turn. Around sixty-five million people now move through Union Station every year, more than any other transit hub in the country — most of them commuters, on the very trains the city eventually came back to. The temple outlasted the prophecy. Stand in the middle of the hall a moment, look up, and let everyone flow around you.
