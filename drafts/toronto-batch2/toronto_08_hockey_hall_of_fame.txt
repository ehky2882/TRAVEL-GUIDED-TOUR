Of all the places to keep the most sacred trophy in the country, somebody chose a bank vault. And it turns out that's exactly right.

You should be standing at the corner of Yonge and Front Streets, looking up at a grand old stone building — arched windows, carved detailing, a rounded corner crowned with a dome — tucked into the base of a modern glass complex that grew up around it. Out front, there's a bronze sculpture of players leaping over the boards. This is the Hockey Hall of Fame, but it's the old building that's worth slowing down for.

Look at the stone first, before you think about what's inside.

It was built in 1885 as the head office of the Bank of Montreal, back when banking was about the most important business in a young country, and a bank wanted you to feel that the moment you walked in. It's one of the very few buildings in this part of downtown to survive the Great Fire of 1904, which flattened most of the blocks around it. Step inside and you'll find the old banking hall topped by a stained-glass dome about fourteen metres up, full of mythological figures and provincial emblems — built to glorify money, now hanging over a collection of trophies.

Because the museum moved in here in 1993, and its centrepiece is the Stanley Cup. The original Cup, along with the oldest trophies in the sport, is kept in the building's actual bank vault — a small alcove off the great hall. The vault that once guarded a bank's gold now guards the game's most coveted object.

There's even a resident ghost, by reputation — a former teller named Dorothy, said to turn up in an upstairs window.

A building made to impress you with the weight of money, quietly repurposed to carry the weight of a game. Same instinct, really — both are about what a culture decides is worth locking away and lining up to see. Stand under that old dome a moment, whether or not you follow the sport, and notice the trade the city made: one kind of temple for another, and they kept the vault.
