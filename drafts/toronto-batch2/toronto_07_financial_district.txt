You're standing on the most expensive piece of ground in Canada — and somebody decided to leave most of it empty.

You should be at the corner of King and Bay Streets, on the open granite plaza of the Toronto-Dominion Centre, in the heart of the Financial District. Around you, a cluster of tall black towers — flat-topped, dark, set back from the sidewalk on that wide stone plaza. The low glass box on the corner is the banking pavilion. And under your feet, though you can't see it yet, there's a second city.

Start with the towers.

These are the work of Mies van der Rohe, the German-American architect whose whole philosophy fit into three words: less is more. No stone gargoyles, no carved entrances, no decoration of any kind — just black-painted steel, bronze-tinted glass, and a grid so strict that everything you can see lines up with everything else. The first tower went up in 1967, and at the time it was the tallest building in the country. It was one of the last things Mies ever designed; he died two years later.

Now look down at the grass. There's a small herd of seven life-size bronze cows lying on the plaza. That's not an accident — it's a quiet joke about all this open space sitting on the priciest real estate in Canada.

To build it, the city cleared more than a hundred older buildings, including an ornate 1913 bank that had stood right here. Its stone face wasn't destroyed — it was taken apart and rebuilt in a park out in Scarborough, where you can still visit it. Stone temple, swapped for a glass box.

And the second city? It's beneath you. The PATH — a network of underground walkways that began with a single department-store tunnel in 1900, and grew, tower by tower, into roughly thirty kilometres of passages linking more than seventy buildings. More than two hundred thousand people move through it every day, most of them never coming up for air in winter.

The towers and the tunnels come from the same idea: that the weather, and even the street itself, were things Toronto could simply improve upon.

[beat]

Stand here on a cold morning and you'll feel both at once — the plaza above, empty and severe, and below you, warm and crowded and humming. Two ideas of a city, stacked one on top of the other.
