This block has been a market for longer than there's been a Canada. Since 1803 — same spot, same purpose, while everything around it changed.

You should be standing on Front Street East, between the two market buildings. On the south side, the big yellow-brick hall with the tall arched windows and the high roof — that's the South Market. Across the street, the newer building with the glass-and-orange front is the North Market, only just rebuilt. Head toward the doors of the south building.

Go inside — but keep the upstairs in mind.

Because this 1845 building was never only a market. The upper floor was Toronto's city hall; the council met up there for more than half a century. The same building held the city's police offices and its jail. You can still climb to the old council chamber — it's kept as a small gallery now. So the room where the young city governed itself sat directly above the stalls where it bought its dinner. Government and groceries, under one roof.

Then come back down for the food. More than a hundred and twenty vendors fill the hall — cheesemongers, butchers, bakers, fishmongers. The thing to eat is the peameal bacon sandwich, a genuinely local invention built on a cure you won't find done quite this way anywhere else. And if you want a sense of the market's reputation, National Geographic once called it the best food market in the world. Take that with a grain of salt — but notice that the claim gets made about a place where you buy your lunch.

Now cross to the North Market. Every Saturday since 1803, farmers have brought produce to this exact corner — the oldest continuous market day in the city. The building they used was torn down and rebuilt, and in 2025 the farmers came home to it. New shell, identical ritual.

[beat]

Most of a city's great buildings ask you to look up — at domes, at columns, at spires. This one asks you to look down at a table. The oldest institution Toronto has is also its most ordinary: a place to buy a chicken and a loaf of bread. Pick up something small, find a corner to eat it in, and you're taking part in the single longest-running habit the city has.
