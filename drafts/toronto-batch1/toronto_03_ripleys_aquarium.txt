Ripley's Aquarium of Canada

Right at the foot of the tower, tucked under all that height, is a building full of sharks.

From outside it's easy to miss — a long, low roofline beside the CN Tower, shaped to look like a slab of earth lifted up to show the water underneath. Inside is Canada's largest aquarium: nearly six million litres of it, marine and freshwater, from the cold Pacific kelp off British Columbia to the reefs of the tropics.

The thing everyone comes for is the Dangerous Lagoon. You step onto a slow-moving walkway, and it carries you through a glass tunnel laid along the floor of the biggest tank — around ninety-six metres of it, the longest underwater viewing tunnel in North America — while sand tiger sharks, sawfish and sea turtles slide by overhead and on either side. You don't really walk through it so much as get gently delivered, standing still while the ocean moves around you.

Here's the small twist. None of this was meant for Toronto. The plan, back in the 2000s, was to build it up at Niagara Falls, among the wax museums and the haunted houses — the natural habitat for a Ripley's attraction. That deal fell through, and the aquarium landed here instead, at the base of the city's tallest landmark, on a slice of old railway land.

[beat]

Which tells you something about this corner of downtown. Inside one short block you've got the tallest tower, the stadium with the roof that opens, and a tunnel full of sharks — the city's biggest crowd-pleasers, stacked together at the water's edge where the trains used to run. It would be easy to be a bit sniffy about all of it. Easier, honestly, to just enjoy it.
