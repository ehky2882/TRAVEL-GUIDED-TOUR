Rogers Centre

Look at the roof. That's the whole reason this building mattered.

You're standing beside it, in the shadow of the CN Tower — a low, pale dome that, from the tower above, looks like a tortoise shell set down beside all that glass. When it opened in nineteen eighty-nine, it was the first stadium anywhere with a fully retractable roof that actually moved. Not a dome you sat under. A roof that slid open to the sky in about twenty minutes, and closed again when the weather turned — which, this being Toronto, it reliably does.

The engineering is the gift here. Four enormous panels, three of them rolling along rails until they nest behind the fourth, the whole assembly weighing something like ten thousand tonnes and held together with a quarter of a million bolts. When it's open, almost every seat sits under open sky — and from the observation deck of the tower next door, you can look straight down into the game.

The name is pure Toronto. They held a public contest to name it, took in thousands of entries, and settled on "SkyDome." The woman who suggested it won lifetime seats, which she reportedly still holds, even though the place was sold and renamed Rogers Centre in two thousand and five. Most people here call it the SkyDome anyway.

It's worth remembering how strange this once was. The summer it opened, they slid the roof shut in the middle of a ball game for the first time — and that became the first game in the sport's history to start outdoors and finish indoors. It felt like a postcard from the future. Now retractable roofs are everywhere, from Miami to Seattle, and nobody blinks. This is the one that went first.

When you move on, glance up at that roofline once more, and picture it splitting open down the middle.
