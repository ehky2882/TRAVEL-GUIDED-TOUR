CN Tower

Here's the part that never makes the postcard: this was built to fix the television.

You're standing near the base, on the south side, with your neck already starting to complain. Look for the three curved legs at the bottom — the whole thing rests on a wide concrete Y — and follow the shaft up as it tapers to the round pod near the top, with a thin needle of antenna above that. Five hundred and fifty-three metres, from the sidewalk to the tip.

Start with that number, because it held a record for a long time. When it was finished in 1976, this was the tallest free-standing structure in the world, and it stayed that way for thirty-two years, until Dubai's Burj Khalifa passed it in 2007. "Free-standing" is the phrase that matters: no guy-wires, no cables holding it up — just concrete and its own weight, balanced on a footing that reaches only about fifteen metres into the ground.

And the way that concrete went up is the gift here. The shaft was poured into a mould that slowly climbed itself, hydraulic jacks lifting the form roughly six metres a day as the concrete below it set, the form shrinking as it rose to give the tower that taper. Over the full height, it leans away from true vertical by less than three centimetres — the length of your hand, across more than half a kilometre.

Now, the reason it's here at all. By the 1960s, downtown Toronto was finally growing tall, and all those new towers were bouncing radio and television signals around, scattering them before they reached anyone's set. Canadian National — the railway — owned a wide stretch of empty rail land right here, and put up an antenna tall enough to clear every roofline in the city. That's the "CN." Not a monument. A very large fix for a fairly boring problem.

[beat]

Which is the quiet joke of the place. The city never set out to build a symbol — it set out to get a clearer picture on the evening news, and ended up with the thing it now puts on every souvenir, every skyline shot, every "we made it" photo. An accident of engineering the whole city decided to love. It takes around seventy-five lightning strikes a year, and barely notices.

Stay a moment, and look up once more before you head in. Worth the sore neck.
