Toronto Islands

First thing you notice, stepping off the ferry: no cars. None. You can hear the lake, and your own footsteps, and not much else — which, fifteen minutes from one of the busiest downtowns in the country, is a genuinely strange feeling.

Stand on Ward's Island, near the boardwalk, with the little wooden cottages behind you and the skyline laid out across the water. This is the largest urban car-free community in North America: about two hundred and sixty homes, reached on foot or by bike, groceries hauled up from the dock in wagons. People actually live here, year-round.

That they live here at all is the story worth knowing. Back in the nineteen fifties the city decided the islands would make better parkland than neighbourhood, and started clearing the cottages — bulldozers, year after year, until more than three hundred homes were gone. The residents of the last two hundred and fifty, on Ward's and neighbouring algonkwin Island, simply refused to leave.

It came to a head on a July morning in nineteen eighty. The city sent a sheriff across with eviction notices, and the islanders were ready for him — a siren went up, a phone chain ran through the community, and by the time he reached the bridge to algonkwin Island a crowd was standing there in the rain, waiting. They talked him into turning around. The next year the province passed a law letting them stay, and the leases now run decades into the future.

There's a longer memory here too. This was a single sandy peninsula until a storm tore it loose from the mainland in eighteen fifty-eight, and long before that the missisawgaz knew it as a place of trees standing out of the water — somewhere you crossed over to, deliberately, leaving the shore behind. Which is more or less what you've just done.

So look back at those towers, close enough to count the floors, and then turn around and look at the cottages. That gap — fifteen minutes of water and a whole different idea of how to live — is the thing people fought to keep. Stay on this side of it a while.
