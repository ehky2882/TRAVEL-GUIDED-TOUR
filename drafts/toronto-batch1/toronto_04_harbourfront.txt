Harbourfront Centre & Queens Quay

Stand at the railing and look out at the lake — then notice how much work it took to let you do that.

You're on Queens Quay — spelled "quay," said "key" — the promenade along the water's edge, with the boardwalk and the boat slips in front of you and the squat brick box of the Power Plant gallery off to one side. Behind you, an elevated expressway hums. That expressway, and the rail tracks beside it, are the whole point of this stop.

For most of a century, Toronto turned its back on its own lake. The original shoreline ran blocks north of here, up around Front Street — everything south of that is landfill, pushed out into the water to make room for railways, grain silos and warehouses. The waterfront became a working edge of the city: walled off behind trains and industry, the kind of place you didn't stroll. You shipped things from it.

Then, in 1972, the federal government bought up about a hundred acres of that industrial harbour and set out to hand it back to the public — partly inspired by Granville Island, over in Vancouver. The old power station became a contemporary art gallery. A vast 1927 cold-storage warehouse, which in its day held the world — walnuts from Spain, coffee from Sri Lanka, figs from Algeria — was turned into shops and condominiums and a dance theatre.

[beat]

And there's a quiet thread back to where you started today. In 1975, while the CN Tower was going up, the very top section of its antenna sat down here at Harbourfront before it was flown into place — and the city invited schoolchildren to come and sign it. So somewhere above your earlier view, thousands of kids' names are riding the top of the tower.

Linger at the water a while. Watch a streetcar slide past on the grass tracks behind you, and a sailboat cross in front. This is the lake the city spent a hundred years ignoring, and then decided, deliberately, to come back to.
